All the above refers exclusively to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions. For them, one cannot guarantee anything and one cannot fully automate the training of neural networks. However, along with iterative training algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms with very high stability that allow for complete automation of the training process. Testing the adequacy of training

Even in the case of seemingly successful training, the network does not always learn exactly what the creator intended. There is a known case where a network was trained to recognize tank images from photographs, but later it was discovered that all the tanks were photographed against the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of "learning" to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what was required of it, but rather what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be conducted on examples that did not participate in its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be greater the higher the quality of training. If the error probability of the neural network is close to one in a billion, then confirming this probability would require a billion test examples.

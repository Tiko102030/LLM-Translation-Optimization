The center is operated by approximately 15,000 employees and contractors. The history of the center began in 1962, when NASA started acquiring land to expand launches. In the same year, the territory was named the Launch Center, and in 1963, after the assassination of President John F. Kennedy, it was renamed the Kennedy Space Center. The lunar program consisted of three stages: Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo. The Mercury program aimed to put a man into orbit, study his work in space, and test the return. The first flights were conducted using rockets Redstone and Atlas. The Gemini program used two-person capsules and allowed the first extravehicular activities in space. Within this program, 12 missions were conducted. The Apollo program used the Saturn V rocket. For this, launch complex 39 was built with a large-scale infrastructure, including a vertical assembly building and transport systems.

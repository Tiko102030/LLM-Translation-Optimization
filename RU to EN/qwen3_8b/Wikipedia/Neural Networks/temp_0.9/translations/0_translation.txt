Forecasting

The ability of a neural network to forecast directly follows from its ability to generalize and identify hidden dependencies between input and output data. After training, the network is capable of predicting future values of a sequence based on several previous values and (or) certain existing factors at the present moment. Forecasting is possible only when previous changes to some extent truly determine the future. For example, forecasting stock prices based on prices from the previous week may be successful (or may not be), whereas forecasting the results of tomorrow's lottery based on data from the past 50 years almost certainly yields no results. Approximation

Neural networks can approximate continuous functions. The general approximation theorem has been proven: using linear operations and cascade connections, one can obtain a device capable of computing any continuous function with a specified accuracy. This means that the nonlinear characteristic of a neuron can be arbitrary: from sigmoidal to arbitrary wave packets or wavelets, sine, or polynomials. The choice of nonlinear function may affect the complexity of a specific network, but with any nonlinearity, the network remains a universal approximator, and with the correct choice of structure, it can sufficiently accurately approximate the functioning of any continuous automaton. Data Compression and Associative Memory

The ability of neural networks to detect relationships between different parameters allows expressing data of large dimensions more compactly if the data are closely interrelated. The reverse process—reconstructing the original dataset from partial information—is called (auto)associative memory.

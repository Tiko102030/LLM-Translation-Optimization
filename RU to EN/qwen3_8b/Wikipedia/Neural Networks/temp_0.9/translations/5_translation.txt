All the above applies only to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions. For them, nothing can be guaranteed, and it is impossible to fully automate the training of neural networks. However, along with iterative training algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms with very high stability and allowing full automation of the training process. Testing the adequacy of training

Even in the case of seemingly successful training, the network may not learn exactly what the creator intended. There is a known case where a network was trained to recognize tank images from photographs, but later it was found that all tanks were photographed against the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of "learning" to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what was required of it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be conducted on examples that were not part of its training. The number of test examples should be greater the higher the quality of training. If the error probability of the neural network is close to one billionth, then a billion test examples are needed to confirm this probability.

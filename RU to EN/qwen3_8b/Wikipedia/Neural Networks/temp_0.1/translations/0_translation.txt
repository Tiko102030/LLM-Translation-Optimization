Prediction

The ability of a neural network to predict directly follows from its ability to generalize and identify hidden dependencies between input and output data. After training, the network is capable of predicting a future value of a sequence based on several previous values and (or) certain existing factors at the current moment. Prediction is only possible when previous changes to some extent truly determine the future. For example, predicting stock prices based on prices from the previous week may be successful (or may not be), whereas predicting the results of tomorrow's lottery based on data from the last 50 years is almost certainly not going to yield any results. Approximation

Neural networks can approximate continuous functions. The generalized approximation theorem has been proven: using linear operations and cascade connections, one can obtain a device that computes any continuous function with a given accuracy. This means that the nonlinear characteristic of a neuron can be arbitrary: from sigmoidal to arbitrary wave packets or wavelets, sine, or polynomials. The choice of nonlinear function may affect the complexity of a specific network, but with any nonlinearity, the network remains a universal approximator, and with the correct choice of structure, it can accurately approximate the functioning of any continuous automaton. Data compression and associative memory

The ability of neural networks to identify interrelationships between different parameters allows expressing data of large dimensions more compactly, if the data are closely interrelated. The reverse process—reconstructing the original dataset from partial information—is called (auto)associative memory.

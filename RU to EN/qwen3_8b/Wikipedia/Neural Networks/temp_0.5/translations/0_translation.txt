Forecasting

A neural network's ability to forecast directly follows from its ability to generalize and identify hidden dependencies between input and output data. After training, the network is capable of predicting a future value of a certain sequence based on several previous values and (or) some existing factors at the current moment. Forecasting is only possible when previous changes to some extent truly determine the future. For example, forecasting stock prices based on prices from the previous week may be successful (or may not be), whereas forecasting tomorrow's lottery results based on data from the last 50 years is almost certainly going to yield no results. Approximation

Neural networks can approximate continuous functions. The general approximation theorem has been proven: using linear operations and cascade connections, one can obtain a device capable of computing any continuous function with a predetermined accuracy from an arbitrary nonlinear element. This means that the nonlinear characteristic of a neuron can be arbitrary: from sigmoidal to an arbitrary wave packet or wavelet, sine, or polynomial. The choice of a nonlinear function may affect the complexity of a specific network, but with any nonlinearity, the network remains a universal approximator and, with the correct choice of structure, can accurately approximate the functioning of any continuous automaton. Data compression and associative memory

The ability of neural networks to detect relationships between different parameters allows expressing data of large dimensions more compactly if the data are closely interrelated. The reverse process—reconstructing the original dataset from partial information—is called (auto)associative memory.

Other networks (for example, Kohonen networks), as well as networks trained with a teacher, repeatedly review the dataset; one full pass through the dataset is called an epoch of training. In supervised learning, the set of source data is divided into two parts—the actual training data and test data; the principle of division may be arbitrary. Training data is presented to the network for learning, while test data is used to calculate the network's error (test data is never used for training the network). Thus, if the error on the test data decreases, the network is indeed generalizing. If the error on the training data continues to decrease while the error on the test data increases, it means the network has stopped generalizing and is simply "memorizing" the training data. This phenomenon is called network overfitting or overfitting. In such cases, training is usually stopped. During training, other problems may arise, such as paralysis or the network falling into a local minimum of the error surface. It is impossible to predict in advance the occurrence of any particular problem or to provide definitive recommendations for resolving them. All the above refers only to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions.

In the early 19th century, state authorities striving to promote industry and trade launched a campaign to build roads and canals. While constructing the state infrastructure, direct costs, expenses related to damage to neighboring property, and losses in the value of undiminished property were incurred. According to the legal principle "use your own property so as not to harm another's" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would bear responsibility for indirect costs, significantly increasing the expenses of financing inventions. By altering the meaning of this principle, i.e., by exempting independent entrepreneurs from responsibility for the consequences of their actions regarding adjacent property, the costs of constructing state infrastructure would sharply decrease. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calender v. Marsh," establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calender filed a lawsuit for damages caused to him during street repair, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

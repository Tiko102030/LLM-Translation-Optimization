Citing the Bible, Locke argues that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property also limits the extent of this property. "God gave us all in abundance" – this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But why did he give us this? For our enjoyment. A man has a right to appropriate as much property as he can use for the needs of his life, before the subject is spoiling. Whatever goes beyond these limits exceeds his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's disappointment, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportionate and unequal ownership of land," to wield power over others. Therefore, there is a need for limitations on the accumulation of property to ensure that all have access to the wealth of society. This part of Locke's theory of property contributed to the emergence of discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation. Later, other authors joined these considerations, including the well-known American radical, Thomas Paine.

These views were prevalent in the American republic, and Locke's arguments continued to be cited by judges of the late nineteenth century opposing public regulation of property rights (see Ellickson 1998). Others, however, argue about property from the standpoint of instrumentalism. For them, property rights ought to be protected, i.e., these rights serve as a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, convincing is the point that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not elevate it as an end in itself, an inalienable right that exceeds all others. Instead, the problem of property becomes now paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines the protection of all property rights.

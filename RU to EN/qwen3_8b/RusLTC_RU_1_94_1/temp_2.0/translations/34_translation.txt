In the late 19th century, expressing opposition to the notion of "a numerous body of discontented citizens," attorneys and judges aimed to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tiedemann, they demanded limiting the full authority of the state by establishing boundaries for the police's jurisdiction. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to determining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. People" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law took effect, all those who had sold this liquor had their property seized. At the same time, the procedure of property confiscation did not follow the established legal procedure. For the first time recognizing that the concept of "established legal order" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative authority cannot wholly abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thus subjecting property to total vanishing."

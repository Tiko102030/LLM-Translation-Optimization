First, he became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of New York State (1805), where a property owner situated downstream brought a claim for damages. The dam, situated upstream, collapsed and flooded his home. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: "the defendants had the same rights regarding their territory... the principle 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others' should be limited to reasonable extent to prevent a person from enjoying the use of his property merely because of some arbitrary inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law which provided for some inconveniences of this sort; therefore, a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether he owned the adjacent banks or not... the public, whose interests should not be overlooked, would be deprived of a part of the profit that it could have obtained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, resulting in both longstanding and new residents having the same privileges. Thus, wealth redistribution took place in favor of entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents being prohibited from limiting entrepreneurial activities. At the same time, by removing the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from legal proceedings regarding indirect damages, these cases actually undermined existing property rights. Thus, the transformation of the rule 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property proposed by J. Locke. The new approach was neither an overall critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights.

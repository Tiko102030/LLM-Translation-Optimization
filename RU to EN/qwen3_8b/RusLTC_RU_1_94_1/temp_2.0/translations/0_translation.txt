INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and social practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ based on the definition they provide for the concept of property, and often the boundaries are difficult to determine. Some believe that the right of ownership extends to items such as land and trees, including the most temporary objects such as living nature and flowing water. Others expand this concept by asserting that the right of ownership also extends to objects such as ideas (patents), and some even include humans as slaves in this category. Societies also differ depending on the rights that property owners possess; sets of privileges concerning responsibility for actions relating to other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because every use of it affects the property of others. Property has dimensions because it affects human relationships; I have the right to manage my property, which reduces your right to be fully free. For example, when the environment is polluted, the neighbors' rights to clean air and water significantly decrease; rights prohibiting environmental pollution limit the rights of the given property, yet protect the neighbors' property. Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to the property of neighbors.

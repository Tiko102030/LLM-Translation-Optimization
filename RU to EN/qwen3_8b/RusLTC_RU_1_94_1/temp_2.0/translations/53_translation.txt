Conclusion: the development process of property rights Regardless of Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Note remains in effect as a law. As it was submitted for discussion, the Supreme Court approved almost all legislation regulating property rights, acknowledging that powers of selected members of the legislative body must be "as vast as the nation's economic needs." At the same time, the Supreme Court affirmed itself as a guardian of private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. It equated the right to pollute one's territory or to engage in discrimination based on sex, race, or religion with these rights, which now supersede the property owners' claims. Considering property as a social institution, the court implied that property rights stand on two pillars: the entity of harm and property as an instrument, not a sacred right. Acknowledging the dual nature of injuries, courts assess any asserted "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," all of which must be balanced. After approving the principle of balancing interests from Holmes, the Court was to examine each asserted "right" to property in terms of instrumental realization, as alternating interests would lead to achieving more global public purposes. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute and must never be the sole objective.

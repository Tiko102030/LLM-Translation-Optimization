But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on state regulation. For example, in the case of Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required that the property owner in South Carolina be compensated for the harm caused to his property after a new "Beachfront Management" law halted construction on his land. Again expressing distrust toward elected legislators, Scalia cautioned, as in the case of the Nollan's, that judges must carefully scrutinize the regulations in order to protect themselves from "the risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in the case of Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from the property owners to the government. The case was filed when the City of Tigard, Oregon administration stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only if she donated part of her land to create a public park and pedestrian and bicycle path in order to reduce traffic congestion on roads. Prepared for the Court by Chief Justice William Rehnquist, argued that the city administration "did not dispute the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle paths at Dolan's expense was a result of the city administration's need to improve the transportation network." The decisions in the Nollan and Dolan cases signal the arrival of a new era of intensified scrutiny of regulatory property controls. This may lead to property rights returning to the level they were at before the New Deal.

According to the court ruling: "A landowner has no absolute and unrestricted right to alter the natural composition of soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe upon the rights of others and which could not have been achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that "environmental protection zoning regulations must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violation of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "eminent domain," as in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was because "the use of property without changing the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Advocates of property rights achieved greater success in litigation regarding "eminent domain" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Under the leadership of Reagan's lawyer, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already ruled on several matters in 1987. For instance, in the case "First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court ordered the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the regional flood control organization prohibited the restoration of a flood-damaged church campsite. The Court notified the Los Angeles County administration that "a regulation that deprives the owner of the right to use their property must be necessary in order to 'grant them the right to damages.'"

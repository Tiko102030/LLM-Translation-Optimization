Examining more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Wait acknowledged several limitations on state regulation. "For the use of regulated property, you may receive compensation," and Wait claimed that the final decision on compensation must be made by a legislative body. "It is assumed that any law," he stated, "is constitutional. Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it is clearly evident. If there is doubt, the expressed will of the legislative body should be supported by a majority." Acknowledging that this authority could be misused, he encouraged citizens to seek redress "at polling stations rather than in court." Property rights defenders were stunned by the open interpretation of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field thought differently and said that "the principle on which the majority opinion relies... undermines private property rights." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state would come under the control of the majority of members of its legislative body." The ruling in the Mann case demonstrated that property rights are now also considered one of society's interests, another tool for achieving public objectives, and no constitutional guarantee is required anymore.

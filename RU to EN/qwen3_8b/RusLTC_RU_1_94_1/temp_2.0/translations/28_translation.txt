The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed some property in comparison with other property. According to one legal historian, William Hirst, this approach is one of the working principles of the American legal order—"the liberation of the full creative energy of mankind." Despite the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remaining on the shoulders of entrepreneurs, they still have the possibility of proceeding with profitable projects. In theory, even if the cost of a project was higher than the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the damage caused. However, in practice, all the burden is shifted to the affected party, which only further encourages the number of projects by entrepreneurs, and all the risk and operating costs are placed on the shoulders of neighbors. If property rights were more strictly enforced legally, then high operating costs would hinder the implementation of entrepreneurial plans. Thus, by saving on the costs of risk and operating costs for entrepreneurs, the new principle has even more strongly contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

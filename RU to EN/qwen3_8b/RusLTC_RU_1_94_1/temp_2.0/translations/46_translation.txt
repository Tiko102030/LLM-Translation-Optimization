"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938) and "United States v. Darling" (1941) became prototypes of judicial rulings from the 1930s, which directly contributed to the spread of judicial pragmatism and the instrumental approach to property rights. Most significantly, the 1934 ruling in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell" saw the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, approve the state's regulatory measures regarding property. The Court upheld a Minnesota law which imposed a moratorium on the right to redeem mortgaged property in the context of an economically adverse situation established by statutory provisions. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, yet it remains one of the key provisions of the Constitution, the interpretation of which can only occur in the presence of additional information. He further noted that the Court should interpret this article "only in conformity with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its residents." "When assessing the effectiveness of the legislative body's actions, one should not consider whether the influence exerted on the terms of the agreement is direct or indirect, but rather whether the decision made was lawful and whether it was reached by reasonable and appropriate methods." This indicates a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which in turn became transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status for private property, Hughes concluded that:
the economic situation in the country can be the result of temporary restrictions on the enforcement of contract terms being coordinated with the true meaning and purpose of the article, thus not exceeding the state's reserved right to protect vital public interests. Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful restriction on the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority. In this regard, whether the legislative body pursues a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will no longer be involved.

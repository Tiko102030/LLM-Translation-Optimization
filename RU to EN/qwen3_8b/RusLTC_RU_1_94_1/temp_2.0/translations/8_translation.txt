A specific form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but will never be necessary. However, Locke himself does not defend property rights as forcefully as one might assume from the well-known passage quoted above (see Horn 1990; Ketsnelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he addresses the question of the right of Stuart English monarchs to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament. But beyond this issue, Locke also acknowledges the social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke argued that every person must have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be limited to ensure that all can share the generosity of nature, leaving "sufficient and in the same state" for others. Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature that thus grants us property also limits the extent of this property.

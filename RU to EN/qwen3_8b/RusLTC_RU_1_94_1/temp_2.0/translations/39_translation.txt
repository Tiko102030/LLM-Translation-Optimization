A similar situation arose in the Jacobson case, and Justice Rufus W. Peckham of the Supreme Court argued that personal freedom included not only the absence of "his physical restrictions" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wished; to earn a living by any legal means; to seek out his chosen profession and for that purpose enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned purposes." After the court's decision in the Allgeier case, eight years later in the Lochner v. New York case (1905), the court declared New York's law unconstitutional because, according to the law, bakery workers' hours were reduced. When speaking before the court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law inevitably infringes upon the right to enter into a contract between employer and employee," which constitutes a violation of "the general right to enter into a contract relating to the operation of his business," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Acknowledging that police regulation of property is justified because it protects "health or morality," Peckham maintained that there was "no justifiable reason for interfering with the freedom of the individual or his right to enter a free contract by reducing the working hours for the position of a baker." In conclusion, Peckham stated that the question was merely which of the evils was less: the state's power to enact laws or the individual's right to personal freedom and freedom of contract. We believe that the limits of police power were reached and fully examined in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner Doctrine could logically be defined based on other judicial decisions affirming property rights.

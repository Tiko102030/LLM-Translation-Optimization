However, the most significant aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote of Judge Stone's opinion, in which the majority of the Court merged the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in matters related to economic legislation, the Court intends to follow the presumption of the constitutionality of laws, with selected legislative members being accountable for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. As these rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not view it as necessary to balance economic interests with legislative measures. Stone, on the contrary, warned that careful examination would be conducted regarding violations of personal freedoms such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and the counter-action of the "taking" procedure. Following the ruling in the Carolyn Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body broad rights in selecting objects of social policy, even though they had imposed limitations on property owners' actions. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued within this context. The "Just v. Marinett County" 1972 Wisconsin case clearly illustrates how the judicial power easily applied the provisions from the Carolyn Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just filed a lawsuit opposing the cancellation of regulations preventing them from occupying the designated marshy areas for constructing their homes. The Wisconsin Supreme Court rejected the family's claims, stating that legislative activity constitutes a lawful exercise of police power. Later, it aimed to approve a broader list of restrictions on property rights.

Thus, on the one hand, the protection of property rights is viewed as an expression of the owner's individuality; on the other hand, the rights of the majority to control economic activity, define social goals, and protect property owners from the consequences of actions by polluters are considered. However, such a general comparison of property rights can be misunderstood, since these rights, to a large extent, limit the rights of other people. Property rights grant individuals state-sanctioned privileges to monopolize resources, the ability to appeal to authorized police authorities to restrict the activities of others. Therefore, property significantly limits some of these advantages due to the fact that government authorities, to a greater extent, support property owners rather than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a debate on the principles of natural liberty and justice, but rather the discussion of property regulation that forces a choice about whose property should be protected and whose property rights are superior in the struggle for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so complex because, within the legal system structure of the United States, legal issues are repeatedly debated at various governmental levels. Thus, under the federal system, a single Federal Government is established, while each state has its own legal system. Additionally, at each level of administration, there is a clear separation of executive, legislative, and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure compliance with the Constitution, in which they find their interpretation.

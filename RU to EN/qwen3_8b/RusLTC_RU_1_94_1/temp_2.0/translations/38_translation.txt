The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, prompting attorneys to fear growing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion is more prominent in memory than Judge Miller's majority opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that fundamental rights to free labor had been violated in the state of Louisiana. Citing Adam Smith, Field based his defense of property rights on John Locke's teachings that free labor is the ultimate source of property, thus making property "the fundamental part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." Field took twenty years to fully grasp the Supreme Court's decision in the case of "Sloter-House and Mann" before his views were adopted by the Court. In 1897, in the case of "Allgeyer v. Louisiana," the Court declared the state's law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company without a commercial license in Louisiana, which in essence amounted to a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation arose in the Jacobs case, where Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that a person's freedom included not only the absence of "physical restrictions" but also encompassed the right "to work where he chooses; to earn a living by any lawful means; to pursue the vocation he desires and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts which may be appropriate, necessary, and essential to achieve the aforementioned goals."

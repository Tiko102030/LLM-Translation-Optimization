The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and therefore the attorneys feared the increasing pressure regarding the expansion of the regulatory measures, redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has left a stronger impression in memory than Judge Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field asserted that in the state of Louisiana, fundamental rights to free labor had been violated. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's teachings that, since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the essential part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." It took Field twenty years to fully grasp the court's decision in the case of "Sloter-House and Mann" before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case of "Allgeier v. State of Louisiana," the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not hold a license to conduct commercial activities in the state of Louisiana, which in essence constituted a violation of freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was present in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that freedom of a person included not only the absence of "his physical restraint," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wills; to earn his living by any lawful means; to pursue his preferred profession and for that purpose to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives."

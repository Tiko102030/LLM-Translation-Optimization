There exists an "inalienable" right of property for the laborer, because: although the earth and all lower creatures belong collectively to all people, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, according to the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever a person may take from that state in which nature created and preserved the object, he combines it with his labor and adds to it something that belongs to him personally, thus making it his own property... For since this labor is the laborer's inalienable property, no person other than himself can have a claim to anything to which he once added his labor, at least in those cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it is to use someone else's labor, that is, to make a person a slave. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, property owners form societies and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

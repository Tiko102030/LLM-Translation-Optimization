In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment enshrined in the United States Constitution the principle of property confiscation. According to this amendment, the confiscation of enslaved people's property redistributed nearly 3 billion U.S. dollars, half of the total wealth of Southern states. As a result, the emancipation of enslaved people in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Government intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widespread belief that government has a moral obligation to foster a thriving economy and support the poor citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted the trade of food products, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and also limited the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Hendlin and Hendlin 1947; Berджin 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right of compulsory expropriation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal v. Holmes, Holmes more acted as a Trojan Horse rather than as a winner - defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "expropriation principle" on the basis of establishing a balance between competing interests. Restitution will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should be constantly "reasonably" used, but the notion of what "reasonably" means remains vague. If actions go "too far," in such cases, compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it will be the judges, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully assess and balance the competing interests of both sides. Property owners, in turn, should not provoke everyone against themselves. Holmes argued that "government will hardly be able to continue its activities further if to some extent the question of property rights is not resolved."

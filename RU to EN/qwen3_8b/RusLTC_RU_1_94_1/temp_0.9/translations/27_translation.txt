For the first time, he became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property downstream initiated a claim for damages. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same right regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your property so as not to harm others'] should be limited to a reasonable extent to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for some inconvenience of this kind, so that a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be overlooked, would be deprived of the portion of profit that could have been gained through competitive rivalry. This approach democratized property rights, resulting in both long-standing and new residents possessing the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs by prohibiting established residents from restricting the actions of entrepreneurs. However, at the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases only undermined existing property rights. Therefore, the transformation of the principle 'use your property so as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property advanced by John Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

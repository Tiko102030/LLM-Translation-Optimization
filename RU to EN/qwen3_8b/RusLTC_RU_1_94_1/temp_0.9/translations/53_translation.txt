Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Amendment remains in effect. Since it was discussed, the Supreme Court approved almost all legislation regulating property and recognized that the powers of elected members of the legislative body must be "as vast as the country's economic needs." At the same time, the Supreme Court affirmed its role as a guardian of private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. It equated the right to pollute one's territory or engage in discrimination based on sex, race, or religion with these rights, which are now above the claims of property owners. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that the right to property stands on two pillars: the nature of harm and property as an instrument, not a sacred right. Acknowledging the dual nature of harm, courts view any asserted "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," all of which must be balanced. After the principle of Holmes' balance of interests was approved, the Court must consider each asserted "right" to property in instrumental terms, as the alternation of interests would lead to achieving more comprehensive public goals. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute and should never be an end in themselves.

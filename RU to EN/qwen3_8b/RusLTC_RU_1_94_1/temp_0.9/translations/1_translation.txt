Society, on one hand, does not prohibit an owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it may support the affected neighbors and limit the activities of this owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, this reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of the loss of clean air and water or the cost of payment that would have to be made to the firm to stop it from discharging waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer without the privileges they possess, because they would have to reduce the level of pollution, or they would have to buy the right to pollute from their neighbors. Some question the fact that property rights have a significant influence on the distribution of income, while others argue in favor of its effectiveness. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social outcome, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment purchase the right to do so from their neighbors, or should the neighbors pay those who pollute the environment to stop doing so? Pollution will occur where the costs of reducing its negative effects are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and it will cease when the costs of reducing the negative effect are less. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

The Supreme Court of Louisiana adopted a law stating that "loss of private rights in this case is irrelevant due to the overall profit and trade turnover of this commercial entity" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230-31). The meatpackers of New Orleans brought their case before the U.S. Supreme Court, where their interests were represented by John A. Campbell, a predecessor of Field, a Democrat and assistant war minister (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court prior to the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the principles of labor legislation to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property ownership. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the city ordinances of New Orleans had brought us back to a time when "the government's prying eye followed the meatpacker to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The meatpackers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power.

Instead, the issue of property has now become the primary concern, and the instrumental approach itself undermines the protection of all property rights. A certain form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke himself does not defend property rights as resolutely as one might understand from the well-known passage cited above (see Horn 1990; Ketznelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he refers to the question of the right of the Stuart monarchs of England to impose taxes without the consent of Parliament. But beyond this question, Locke acknowledges the social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke argued that each person should have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be limited to ensure that everyone can share the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and as good" for others. Referring to the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature that in this way gives us property equally limits the extent of this property.

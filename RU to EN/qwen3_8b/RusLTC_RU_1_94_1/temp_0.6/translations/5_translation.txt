The new paradigm of property rights acknowledges various definitions of property, however, it still considers "property rights" as relationships between people and objects (see Barceló 1997; Leibknecht 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As relationships between people and objects, property exists without human society; for instance, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Just as relationships between people and objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it serves as a reward for labor, which forms the non-human material world. As John Locke says, "labor, at first, gave the right to property" (see J. Locke 1952, p. 27 [par. 45]).

This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation. Later, other authors joined these discussions, including the well-known American radical, Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret the so-popular in society conclusions of Locke. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the perspective of relations between people and objects, it can also serve as a socially neutral factor: the accumulation of the results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is accessible to others. However, recognizing the social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person may harm others, for example, in the case of monopolizing resources previously accessible to all. Just as social relations, the theory of property becomes the subject of various disputes (see Fried 1998, p. /think).

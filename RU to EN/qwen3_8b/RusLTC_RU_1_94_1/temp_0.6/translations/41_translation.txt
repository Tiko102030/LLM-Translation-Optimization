It appears that the basis of the aforementioned case lies in John Locke's views on absolute property rights. The Mann case's final decision was also mentioned, and it was rendered by Chief Justice Wait. However, attention must be drawn to the last statement considered earlier: "this is merely a question of degree of severity." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously re-established an instrumental vision of property rights, which declared the previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. Holmes had already expressed his opinion in the Lochner case. Beginning his speech with the assertion that "the decision was rendered according to the principles of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy most of the country's population," he added: "The right of each person to act as they wish for as long as they please, provided, of course, that they do not infringe upon the right of others to do the same, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal management, or the laws of any of the states or other municipal institutions that take their money to achieve goals that seem necessary, regardless of whether they want it or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner, which despotically protects intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require achieving a balance. After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

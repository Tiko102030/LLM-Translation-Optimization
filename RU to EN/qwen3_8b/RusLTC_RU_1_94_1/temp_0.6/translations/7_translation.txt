Such views prevailed in the American republic, and the reasoning of Locke continued to be cited by judges of the Supreme Court in the late nineteenth century, who opposed public regulation of property rights (see Elly 1998). Others, however, argue about property from the perspective of instrumentalism. For them, property rights should be protected, that is, these rights serve as an incentive for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, the convincing point is that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an indisputable right that surpasses all others. Instead, the problem of property becomes the primary issue, and the instrumental approach undermines the protection of all property rights.

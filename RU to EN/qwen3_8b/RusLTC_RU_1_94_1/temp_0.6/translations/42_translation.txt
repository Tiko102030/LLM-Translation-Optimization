After losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case based on an alternative legal theory. Instead of relying on the majority's opinion of the legislative assembly regarding natural rights or on the convictions of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "Social Statics" by Spencer. Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that more likely signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than the protection of a sacred conception of free labor. According to the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "General Law" (1881), he thoroughly discussed how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "Law does not even attempt to compensate a person for all the harm inflicted upon him... Anyone may start any business, and later, in order to survive in competitive struggle, he will steal customers from his competitor, and possibly thereby even ruin him." In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible according to the principles of "such a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p.)

CalleNDER filed a claim for damages caused during street repairs, which resulted in damage to the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited English laws, common law precedents, and this principle to seek compensation. However, the defense rejected the use of English precedent with an instrumental presentation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim," which CalleNDER had brought, "were to be satisfied, then it would be necessary to examine the correctness of repairs of all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed CalleNDER's claim for compensation. Then, the Massachusetts state court adopted the state's offer of market interpretation of the case to avoid similar subsequent damage claims, which was decidedly bad news for people whose property was directly adjacent to public structures. "Those who purchase property adjacent to a roadway," the court stated, "should take into account future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." For, in such a case, the compensation amount would have doubled compared to the original cost. Similar conclusions were drawn in New York in 1828, in the case "Lansing v. Smith Walter Quackenbush," where a claim was brought against the state of New York for damages due to the fact that docks fell in price after the construction of a new harbor in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "CalleNDER v. Marsh" to reject Quackenbush's claims.

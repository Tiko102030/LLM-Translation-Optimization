In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy that has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Criminal Intent, and Design," Holmes built upon the aforementioned understanding of the harm caused by competition. Thus, he aimed to demonstrate the general necessity of an instrumental approach and the "principle of balancing interests," especially in circumstances where judges weigh competing rights instead of relying on syllogistic reasoning (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130–35). At the 1903 Supreme Court session, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glu v. United States Glu." "In modern society," he stated, "every part of it is closely connected with another, and if something affects one of them, the others will more or less feel it. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the end of legislative authority is reached, it would not be sufficient merely to show that some progress has been made in the development of the law, that is, when it will already be possible to intervene in trade or existing contracts."

The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed certain property in comparison with another property. According to one legal historian, Willard Hurst, this approach is one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America—"the release of the full creative energy of man." Despite the fact that the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on the entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. In theory, if the cost of a project were higher than the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the damage caused. However, in practice, all the burden is shifted onto the injured party, which only further encourages the number of projects undertaken by entrepreneurs, while all the risk and operational costs are placed on the shoulders of the neighbors. If property rights were respected to a greater legal extent, high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by economizing on the costs of risk and operational expenses for entrepreneurs, the new principle has even more significantly contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

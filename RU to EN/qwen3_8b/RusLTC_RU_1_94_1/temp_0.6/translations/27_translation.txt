For the first time, it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream initiated a lawsuit for damages. The dam situated upstream collapsed and flooded his home. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same right regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others'] should be limited within reasonable limits to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely due to some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law that provided for some inconvenience of this nature, so that a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of the portion of profits they could have obtained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both long-time and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, a redistribution of wealth occurred toward entrepreneurs at the expense of long-time residents, whose actions were prohibited from restricting entrepreneurs' activities. At the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turned out that the transformation of the rule 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property introduced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights.

The iron and steel industry, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, could only ensure the developing railroad network. At the apex of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system, established in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Incensed workers and farmers saw the true aims of the bankers and owners of the iron and railroad companies, whose ownership was precisely sponsored by governmental authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand the public's authority in matters of regulating property. They achieved remarkable success in securing the eight-hour workday law and the law establishing railroad tariffs. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were sent for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of the popular radicalism of that time. The plaintiffs claimed that the law, by voiding existing contracts, violated the Constitution's contract clause, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference.

According to the court's ruling: "A landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of others and which could not have been achieved with its original composition." The court recognized that environmental protection zone regulations "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing the violation of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "taking" of property, as occurred in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was because "the use of property without altering the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding "takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Under the leadership of Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already issued a series of decisions in 1987. For example, in the case of "First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the local flood control agency prohibited the restoration of a flood-damaged church campground. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a regulation that prevents a property owner from using their property must be necessary in order to 'grant them the right to compensation for damages.'"

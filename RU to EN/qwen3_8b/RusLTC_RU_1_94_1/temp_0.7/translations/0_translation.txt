INTRODUCTION: The concept of property in economic theory and social practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ based on the definitions they provide for the concept of property, and often, the boundaries can be difficult to define. Some believe that the right of ownership extends to such items as land and trees, including the most perishable objects such as living nature and flowing water. Others expand this concept by asserting that the right of ownership also applies to such objects as ideas (patents), and some even include humans as slaves within this category. Societies also differ based on the rights that property owners possess; the set of privileges concerning responsibility for actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because every use of it affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, since it involves human relationships; I have the right to manage my property, which reduces your right to be fully free. For example, when the environment is polluted, the neighbors' right to clean air and water significantly decreases; rights prohibiting pollution limit the rights of that property, but protect the property of the neighbors. Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to the property of neighbors.

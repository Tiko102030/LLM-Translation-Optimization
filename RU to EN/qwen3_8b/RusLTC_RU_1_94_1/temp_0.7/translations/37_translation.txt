The Supreme Court of Louisiana adopted a law establishing that "the loss of private rights in this case is irrelevant due to the overall profit and trade turnover of this commercial enterprise" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230–31). The meatpackers of New Orleans brought their case to the U.S. Supreme Court, where their interests were represented by John A. Campbell, Field's predecessor, a Democrat and assistant war minister (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court prior to the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the core principles of labor legislation to persuade the Court to protect a citizen's right to property. Drawing on the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the city of New Orleans' ordinances had brought us back to the time when "the government's pesky eye followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The meatpackers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure that regulations would be expanded and that income, property, and power would be redistributed.

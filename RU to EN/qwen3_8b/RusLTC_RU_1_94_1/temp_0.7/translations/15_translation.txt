In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment enshrined in the Constitution of the United States the principle of property confiscation. According to this amendment, confiscation without compensation of slave property redistributed nearly $3 billion, half of the total wealth of the southern states. Consequently, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Governmental intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widely held belief regarding the moral obligation of government to promote a thriving economy and care for the poor. For example, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited the trade of food products, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and restricted the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Hendlin and Hendlin 1947; Berджин 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right of compulsory alienation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

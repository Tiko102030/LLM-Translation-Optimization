The New York Court employed an instrumental approach used in the Massachusetts case "Calhoun v. Marsh" to dismiss Quicken's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement necessarily, more or less, affects the comfort and property of people; and when the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court ruled: "this case is based on the principle that the public good must prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise  
Not prohibiting the government from undertaking state improvement at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedent law, as in the case of "Lansing v. Calhoun," shifted the obligation of covering costs associated with achieving public goals onto some individuals (see Horvitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case "Torpe v. Railroad Company 'Rutland and Ballston' (1855)," Chief Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to protect their tracks, because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas).' The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property rights.

In the late nineteenth century, expressing opposition to the opinion of the "numerous discontented citizens," attorneys and judges intended to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tidemann, they demanded limiting the full extent of state power by establishing boundaries for the authority of the police. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to the determination of private property. Even before the beginning of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. Pippel" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared a law prohibiting the sale of liquor unconstitutional. After the law went into effect, all those who had sold this liquor lost their property. However, the procedure of expropriation did not follow the established legal order. For the first time, recognizing that the concept of "established legal order" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative power cannot entirely eliminate commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thus subjecting property to complete disappearance."

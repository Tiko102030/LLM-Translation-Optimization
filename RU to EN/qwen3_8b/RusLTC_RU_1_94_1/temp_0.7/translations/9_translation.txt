Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property equally limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly"—this is the voice of reason, confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did he give us this? For enjoyment. A person has a right to convert as much of it into their own property as they can use for their own needs before the thing is spoiled. Anything beyond these limits exceeds their share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoil or destruction. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportionate and unequal ownership of land," gaining power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee everyone access to society's wealth. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, later joined by other authors, including the radical American thinker Thomas Paine.

In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of a "condition regarding the grant of a land use permit" does not constitute an "expropriation" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general land use "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "is therefore unconstitutional, and compensation must be provided." In conclusion, Scalia spoke of the new standard: Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying reason for restricting construction, the new purpose transforms into something different, unlike what it was before. Now, the government's main task is to obtain general land use rights without paying any compensation... if the condition specified in the land use permit is not considered a prohibition on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a valid land use regulation, but merely "a present taking." This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's requirement to review the opinion of the legislative body members. They argued this because "the proper standard for consideration... is whether the government can rationally determine if the adopted measure can achieve the public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on governmental regulation.

On the other hand, the right of the majority to control economic activities, determine public goals, and protect property owners from the externalities caused by polluters is considered. However, such a general comparison of property rights can be misunderstood, as these rights, to a greater extent, limit the rights of other people. Property rights grant individuals state-sanctioned privileges to monopolize the management of resources and the ability to appeal to authorized police authorities to restrict the activities of other people. Therefore, property rights significantly limit certain advantages because government agencies support property owners more than other citizens. Thus, the debate is not about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather about property regulation, which forces a choice regarding whose property should be protected and whose property rights have the advantage in the struggle for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so unclear because legal issues in the structure of the United States legal system have repeatedly been discussed at various levels of government. Thus, according to the federalism system, one Federal Government is established, and each state has its own legal system. Additionally, at each level of governance, there is a clear separation of executive, legislative, and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure the observance of constitutional norms, which they interpret.

The plaintiffs argued that by voiding existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Recently enacted to protect civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment is now considered an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights that protected private property from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court affirmed and even expanded past regulatory powers. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people must use their property, but only when necessary for the public good." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice of England Hale's statement that when property "touches the interests of society, it is by law no longer a subject of private regulation alone." Remembering the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when an owner uses property in such a way that the public acquires an interest in it, in fact, he himself wants this, and until that interest disappears, he must accept that now society controls his property, since it is necessary for the increase of public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply cease its use. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

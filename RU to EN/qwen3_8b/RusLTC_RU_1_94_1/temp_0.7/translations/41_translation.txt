It appears that the aforementioned case is based on John Locke's views on absolute property rights. The case also mentioned the final decision in the Mann case, issued by Chief Justice Wait of the Supreme Court. However, attention should be drawn to the last statement previously considered: "this is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously re-established an instrumental vision of property rights, which declared the previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. Holmes had already expressed his opinion in the Lochner case. Beginning his speech with the idea that "the decision was rendered according to the principles of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy most of the country's population," he added: "The right of each person to act as they wish for as long as they do not infringe upon the rights of others to do the same does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal management, laws of any of the states, or other municipal institutions that take his money to achieve goals that seem necessary, regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike how Lochner despotically protects intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require achieving a balance. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

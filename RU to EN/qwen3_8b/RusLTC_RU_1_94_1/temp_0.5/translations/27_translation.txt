For the first time, it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" in the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property downstream brought a claim for damages. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same right regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others'] should be limited to a reasonable extent to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely due to some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law that provided for some inconvenience of this nature, so that a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose the portion of profit that it could have gained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and as a result, both the long-standing and new residents possessed the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth toward entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents whose actions were restricted from limiting the activities of entrepreneurs. At the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases only weakened existing property rights. It turned out that the transformation of the principle 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property introduced by John Locke. The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

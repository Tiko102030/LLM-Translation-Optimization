The plaintiffs claimed that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts, which also violated the recently enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Recently enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment is now an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property protecting private property rights from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court members. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Speaking about the statement that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens toward each other and how people ought to use their property, but only when it is necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice of England Hale's statement that when property "touches the interests of society, it is already by law no longer a subject of mere private regulation." Remembering the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when a property owner uses his property in such a way that the public develops an interest in it, in fact, he himself desires this, and until that interest disappears, he must merely accept that now society controls his property, since it is necessary for the purpose of increasing public welfare. If he does not wish society to control his property, he must simply cease its use. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

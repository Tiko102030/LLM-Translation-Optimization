"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938), and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of judicial decisions from the 1930s, which, in turn, contributed to the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, upheld the state's actions regarding property regulation. The Court affirmed the law of the state of Minnesota; this law imposed a moratorium on the forced sale of mortgaged property under conditions of an adverse economic situation established by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information. He added that the Court should interpret this article "only in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its citizens." "The efficiency of legislative action should not be assessed by whether it directly or indirectly affects the conditions of the contract, but rather by whether the decision was lawfully made and whether it was achieved through reasonable and appropriate methods." This reflects a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that: "The economic situation in the country may be the result of how a temporary limitation on the enforcement of contract conditions is reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not exceed the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unlawful restriction of the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority.... At the same time, whether the legislative body conducts a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not concern itself with that."

In the 1875 case "Kohler v. United States," it was stated that the right to eminent domain over private property is "a result of political necessity and cannot be separated from sovereign power." The right to eminent domain over private property was one example of the use of governmental power to promote the development of "a well-organized society," to enhance the level of "public welfare" or "well-being" through regulations involving intervention in property rights. These regulations reflected principles of common law such as: "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). These main principles, often cited in court rulings, serve as "principles of common law governance in a well-regulated society," providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property in order to reflect mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

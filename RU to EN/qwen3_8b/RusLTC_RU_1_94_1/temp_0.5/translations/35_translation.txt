In the precedent case of Jacobson (1885), the Appellate Court of New York State declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in leased premises, because it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use his property," thus "depriving him of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in a broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual servitude... but also the right of a person to use his abilities in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or restrict these rights... constitute violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The Legislative Assembly claimed that the ruling was based on the state's police powers, and the measures taken were necessary to "improve the health system of the country." For defenders of state regulation, it was not promising that the court would consider this requirement, and in the end, it concluded that it lacked the necessary amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law "did not actually concern health issues at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police powers, there can be no arbitrary intrusion into personal rights and private property." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an unprecedented judicial activism in the protection of property rights. For example, in the case of Godcharles v. Widjmen (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (rather than company stocks). The court asserted that "the law is a violation of the equal rights of employer and employee, more than that, it is an offensive attempt to subject an unskilled worker to legislative oversight," because it hinders workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

The Mann case decision showed that property rights were now one of society's interests, another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantees. Amid growing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and caused a greater surge in feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events at Haymarket Square in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings had not allowed limitations on police powers. The spread of the non-interference policy reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled throughout all civilized worlds. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to determine what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the development of universal suffrage, conservatives feared even more that an autocracy more despotic and more unwise than any that had existed before might emerge. In the late nineteenth century, when expressing opposition to the views of "the numerous army of dissatisfied people," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

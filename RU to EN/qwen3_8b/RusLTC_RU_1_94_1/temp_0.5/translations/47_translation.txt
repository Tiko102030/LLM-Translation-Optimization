Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that: the economic conditions in the country may result from how a temporary restriction on compulsory enforcement of contractual terms is reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of the article, thus not exceeding the state's reserved right to protect vital societal interests. Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful restriction of the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in a manner that does not violate legislative authority. In this regard, whether the policy is reasonable or unreasonable, the legislative body will conduct it, and the Court will not interfere. Already in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon the evaluation of economic legislation and the adjudication of cases concerning property rights, the legislative body had already declared the necessity of "protecting vital societal interests." After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "move" the Court in 1937, the Court had practically fully agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach in the Court's activities as the case United States v. Carolene Products (1938). In this specific case, the company was sued under the 1923 Milk Act, which prohibited the interstate sale of milk with added fat and oil, considering it as the sale of "intentionally spoiled food product harmful to health." The company's attorneys argued that the law unconstitutionally restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court considered the law valid, as it viewed it as a fair instrument of state regulation necessary for protecting public health. The decision itself would have been an ordinary, simple confirmation of the established police powers. However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, according to which the majority of the Court shared the understanding of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

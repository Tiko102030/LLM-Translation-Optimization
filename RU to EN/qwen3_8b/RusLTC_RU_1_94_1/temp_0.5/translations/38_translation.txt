The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, prompting attorneys to fear increasing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has left a stronger impression in memory than Judge Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the fundamental rights to free labor had been violated in the state of Louisiana. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's prescriptions that, since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the essential part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." It took Field twenty years to fully grasp the court's decision in the "Slotter-House and Mann" case before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case "Allgeyer v. Louisiana," the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company without a license to conduct commercial activities in Louisiana, which essentially constituted a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation arose in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham demonstrated that freedom of the individual included not only the absence of "physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn a living by any lawful means; to pursue the chosen profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives."

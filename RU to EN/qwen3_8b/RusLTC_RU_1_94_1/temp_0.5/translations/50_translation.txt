The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a resolution depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary in order "to give them the right to compensation." The court's decision in the First Anglican Church case, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles, merely affirming the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nolans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to replace their aging bungalow with a new three-bedroom home similar to other homes in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only under the condition that the Nolans agreed to grant a common right of use over the land between the tide line and the dike behind their home. This way, people could easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply strolling leisurely along the sand. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling the land," which "people would no longer subconsciously realize that there is a coastline nearby and that they have the full right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Co. v. Mahon, Holmes more likely acted as a Trojan Horse rather than as a winner-defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "principle of taking" property on the basis of balancing competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should be constantly "reasonably" used, but the notion of what constitutes "reasonable" remains still vague. If actions go "too far," in such cases, compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it will be the judges, taking into account the presence of public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully evaluate and balance the competing interests of both sides. Property owners, in turn, should not set all against themselves. Holmes claimed that "government will hardly be able to continue its activities further if, to some extent, the issue of property rights is not resolved."

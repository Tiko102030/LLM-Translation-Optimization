On the other hand, the right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect property owners from the consequences of the external impact of polluters is considered. However, such a general comparison of property rights can be misunderstood, since these rights, to a greater extent, limit the rights of other people. Property rights grant individuals government-sanctioned privileges to monopolize resources, the ability to appeal to authorized police authorities to limit the activities of other people. Thus, property rights significantly limit some advantages because government authorities support owners to a greater extent than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a debate about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather discussions on property regulation that force a choice about whose property needs to be protected and whose property rights have an advantage in the struggle for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so unclear because legal issues have been repeatedly discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the U.S. legal system. Thus, according to the federalism system, one Federal Government is formed, while each state has its own legal system. Also, at each level of governance, there is a clear separation of executive, legislative, and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure the observance of constitutional norms, which they interpret.

The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now also one of the interests of society, becoming another tool for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of increasingly growing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked a stronger outburst of feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings did not allow limiting police powers. The spread of the policy of non-interference reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled throughout the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to determine what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the conditions of universal suffrage, conservatives became even more afraid that a despotism more oppressive and less reasonable than any that had existed before might emerge. In the late 19th century, when expressing opposition to the opinion of "the numerous army of dissatisfied people," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

The New York court employed the instrumental approach used in the Massachusetts case "Calender v. Marsh" to dismiss Quicken's claims. The court asserted: "Any significant public improvement necessarily, more or less, affects the comfort and property of people; and in the case where the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is foreseeable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "This case is based on the principle that the public good must prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise  
The precedent, as established in the case "Lansing v. Calender," does not prohibit the government from improving the state's infrastructure at the expense of adjacent property owners, shifting the obligation to some individuals to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horowitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case "Torp v. Rutland and Burlington Railroad" (1855), Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to protect their tracks, because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas)." The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property rights.

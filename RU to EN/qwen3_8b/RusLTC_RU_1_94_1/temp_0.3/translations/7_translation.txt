Such views spread within the American republic, and arguments by Locke continued to be cited by late nineteenth-century Supreme Court justices opposing public regulation of property rights (see Elly 1998). Others, however, discuss property from the perspective of instrumentalism. For them, property rights should be protected, that is, these rights serve as a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see Northh 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an inalienable right surpassing all others. Instead, the issue of property becomes now primary, and the instrumental approach undermines the protection of all property rights.

In reality, the Thirteenth Amendment embeds in the United States Constitution the principle of property confiscation. According to this amendment, the confiscation without compensation of enslaved people's property redistributed nearly $3 billion, half of the total wealth of the Southern states. As a result, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Government intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property affairs, thereby once again confirming the widely held belief in the government's moral obligation to promote a thriving economy and support the poor citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited the trade of food products, regulated wage levels and the possibility of employment, as well as limited the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Henderson and Henderson 1947; Berger 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right to compulsory expropriation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

The U.S. Supreme Court did not rush to criticize state regulation, but Judge Stephen Field cast doubt on the idea of the police power as early as 1872 in the case of "Charitable Association of New Orleans Butchers v. Crescent City Livestock Landing & Slaughterhouse." This case arose after the state legislature of Louisiana imposed restrictions on livestock slaughter in New Orleans. Since the public's health was not threatened, this law was similar to those previously enacted in many other cities. Citing the ruling of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court's chief justice, Shaw, in the case of "State of Massachusetts v. Algar" (1851), the Louisiana Supreme Court upheld the law, stating that "the loss of private rights in this case is of no significance in view of the overall profit and commercial turnover of this commercial entity."

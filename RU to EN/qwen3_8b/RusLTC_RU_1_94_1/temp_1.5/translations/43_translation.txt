In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible according to the principles of "a policy that has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Criminal Intent, and Design," Holmes based his argument on the aforementioned conception of harm caused by competition. Thus, he aimed to show the general necessity of an instrumental approach and the "principle of balancing interests," especially in situations where judges weigh competing rights rather than resorting to syllogistic reasoning (Horwitz 1992, p. 130-35). At the 1903 Supreme Court session, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glu" vs. "United States Glu." "In modern society," he stated, "every part of it is closely connected with another, and if something affects one of them, the others will more or less feel it. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the legislative authority ends, it will not be sufficient merely to show that some progress has been made in the development of the law, that is, when it will already be possible to interfere with commercial or existing contracts."

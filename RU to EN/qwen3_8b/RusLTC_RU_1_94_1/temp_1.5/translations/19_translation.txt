Local authorities regularly confiscated houses used for unlawful activities, dumped factory wastewater into sewers, banned the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private homes and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, and others of economic significance not related to healthcare, such as the rights to trade and purchase, to sell and produce (see Newak 1996, pp. 84-94). When regulation proved insufficient to achieve public goals, officials began to take matters into their own hands. For instance, in 1872, the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the fertilizer plant owned by the firm "Manhattan Fertilizing" and commenced "mitigating" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying its machinery and taking all the necessary components with them (see Newak 1996, p. 226).

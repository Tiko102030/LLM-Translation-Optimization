A similar situation was in the court case of Jacobus, where Justice Rufus W. Peckham of the Supreme Court argued that freedom of an individual included not only the absence of "his physical restraint," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn his living by any lawful means; to pursue the profession he loves and for that purpose to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned purposes." After the court's decision in the case of Allgeyer, the peak of judicial activism in articulating motives was established eight years later in the case of "Lochner v. State of New York" (1905), where the Court declared New York's law unconstitutional, because according to this law, the working hours for bakers were reduced. Speaking in court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law compels interference with the right to contract between employer and employee," which constitutes a violation of "the general right to enter into contracts relating to the operation of his business," which in turn is "protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Acknowledging the fact that the actions of the police in regulating property are justified because they protect "health or morality," Peckham argued that in this case, "there is no necessary justification for interfering with the freedom of an individual or his right to form a free contract by reducing the working hours for the position of a baker." In conclusion, Peckham stated that this was merely a question of which of the evils is lesser: the power of the state to enact laws or the individual's right to personal freedom and freedom of contract. We believe that the limits of police power have been reached and fully addressed in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner Era can logically be defined based on other judicial decisions that affirmed property rights.

Rejecting any privileged status for private property, Hughes concluded that:
The country's economic conditions may result from how a temporary limitation on compulsory enforcement of contractual terms is reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of the article, thus not exceeding the state's reserved right to protect vital societal interests. Since this contractual article is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful restriction on the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to infringe upon legislative authority. In this regard, whether the policy is reasonable or unreasonable, the legislative body will conduct it, and the Court will not concern itself with that. Already in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon its evaluation of economic legislation and its adjudication of cases on property rights, the legislative body already then asserted the necessity of 'protecting vital societal interests.' After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempts to 'move' the Court in 1937, the Court had practically fully agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach in the Supreme Court's operations as the case 'United States v. Carolene Products Co.' (1938). In this particular case, the company was sued under the 1923 'Milk Act,' which prohibited the interstate sale of milk with added fat and oil, interpreting this as the sale of 'deliberately spoiled food product harmful to health.' The company's counsel argued that the law unconstitutionally restricted the freedom of contract. Dismissing the arguments in favor of property rights, the Court considered the law valid, as it saw it as a fair instrument of state regulation necessary for protecting public health. In itself, the decision would have been an ordinary, simple confirmation of the established police powers. However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, according to which the majority of the Court equated property rights with the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

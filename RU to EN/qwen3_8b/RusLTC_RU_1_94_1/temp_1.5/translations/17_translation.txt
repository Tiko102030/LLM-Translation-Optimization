In the 1875 case "Koch v. United States," it stated that the right to eminent domain over private property is "the result of political necessity and cannot be separated from sovereign power." The right to eminent domain over private property was an example of the government's use of power to promote the development of "a well-organized society," to raise the level of the "public good" or "welfare" through regulations involving interference with property owners' rights. These regulations reflected common law principles such as: "the welfare of the people shall be the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own property in such a way as not to harm others" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). These key principles, often cited in court rulings, serve as "common law management programs in a well-regulated society," providing the legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property to reflect the mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

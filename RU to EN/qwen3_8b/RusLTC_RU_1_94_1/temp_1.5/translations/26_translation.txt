"Use your own so as not to harm another." Application of this principle ensures access to the social interpretation of property rights. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights, because, as he said, actions of one owner affect others even when carried out on one's own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests of different people are involved and both sides claim that the neighbor's interference in their territory (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Issues concerning the subject of property rights were re-evaluated in the 19th century to facilitate the development of entrepreneurial activities, even at the expense of causing harm to other people's property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained, but now it was used to protect entrepreneurs' right to act in accordance with the requirements of passive neighbors who wanted their property to remain untouched. Now the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of wrongful acts against another's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own property. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by the use of their property. This approach first became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Milligan" of the state of New York (1805).

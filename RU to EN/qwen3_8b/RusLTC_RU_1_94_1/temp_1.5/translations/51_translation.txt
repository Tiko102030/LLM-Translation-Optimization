In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of a "condition regarding the grant of land use permission" is not an "exemption" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general land use "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "thus constitutes an illegality, and damages must be awarded." In conclusion, Scalia discussed the new standard: Because there is no connection between the condition and the original justification for the construction restriction, the new purpose transforms into something different, unlike what it was before. Now, the government's main task is to acquire general land use rights without paying any compensation... if the condition set forth in the land use permit is not considered a construction ban and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the construction restriction will not be a lawful land use regulation but merely "a current exaction." This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a separate opinion. They rejected Scalia's requirement to reconsider the legislators' opinion. They argued this because "the proper standard for consideration... is whether the state can rationally determine whether the adopted measure can achieve the public purpose." However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed additional restrictions on government regulation.

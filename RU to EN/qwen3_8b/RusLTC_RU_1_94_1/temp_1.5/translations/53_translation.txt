Nevertheless, a significant portion of legal and judicial practice and precedent still opposes property rights defenders. Conclusion: the process of the development of property rights. Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Note remains in effect. Since it was submitted for discussion, the Supreme Court approved nearly all legislation regulating property and acknowledged that the powers of elected members of the legislative body must be "as vast as the country's economic needs." At the same time, the Supreme Court has affirmed its role as the watchdog of private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. It equated the right to pollute one's territory or engage in discrimination based on gender, racial, or religious characteristics, which are now above all claims of property owners. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that property rights rest on two pillars: the nature of harm and property as an instrument, not as a sacred right. Acknowledging the dual nature of damages, courts view any asserted "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," all of which must be balanced. After the principle of Holmes' balance of interests was adopted, the Court had to consider each asserted "right" to property in an instrumental manner, as the alternation of interests would lead to achieving more comprehensive public goals. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute and should never be ends in themselves.

The plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated the Constitution's provision on contracts, which in turn violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights against state interference. Recently enacted to safeguard the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property protecting private property rights against state interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court members. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee concerning property, the Court affirmed and even expanded past regulatory powers. Referring to the statement that "full powers belong to any supreme authority," the Court decided that "the government may regulate and direct the behavior of its citizens towards one another, and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited the English Chief Justice Hale's statement that "when property 'touches the interests of society, it ceases by law to be subject to private regulation only.' Recalling the principle 'the welfare of the people is the supreme law' (lat. salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added that when a property owner uses their property in a manner that gives rise to public interest, in fact, they themselves desire such an outcome, and until the interest disappears, they must simply accept that now society manages their property, since this is necessary for the increase of public welfare. If, however, they do not wish for society to manage their property, they should simply cease its use. Considering more thoroughly the limits of public power in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on state regulation.

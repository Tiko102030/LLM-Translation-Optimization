However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Judge Stone, according to which the majority of the Court members equated the concept of property rights with the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone's claims, in cases concerning economic legislation, the Court intends to apply the principle of the presumption of constitutionality of laws, and selected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and limitations on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative actions. Stone, on the contrary, warned that a careful investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religious choice, will proceed. Environmental protection and the counter-procedure of "expropriations." After issuing the decision in the Caroline Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body to have broad rights in choosing objects of social policy, even though they limited the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued in this context. The court case "Just v. Marinette County, Wisconsin, 1972" clearly illustrates how the judicial power easily applied the position from the Caroline Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just filed a lawsuit regarding the cancellation of regulations that prevented them from occupying the designated marshy areas for building homes. Rejecting the Just family's claims, the Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate use of police powers. Later, it wanted to approve a broader list of limitations on property rights.

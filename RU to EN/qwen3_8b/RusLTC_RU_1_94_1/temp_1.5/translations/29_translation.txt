The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which liberated the country from slavery. Reformers, however, wanted even more. For example, the labor rights advocate George Macneil warned that America must "imprint republican principles on our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to the train conductor C.F. Breassie, "The Declaration of Independence was not created with the purposes that trust companies today attempt to impose on us. The result of the 1861 Civil War was the emancipation of black slaves, yet today workers are nothing other than slaves of consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124–125). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. Massive sums of money were needed for military actions, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed that significant financial and industrial capital was required to sustain government activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products benefited from protective tariffs, were the only ones capable of supporting the developing railroad network.

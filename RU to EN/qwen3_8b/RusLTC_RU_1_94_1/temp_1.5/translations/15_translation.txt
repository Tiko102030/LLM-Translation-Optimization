In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment solidified the principle of eminent domain within the United States Constitution. According to this amendment, uncompensated confiscation of slave property redistributed nearly three billion dollars, accounting for half of the total wealth of Southern states. Consequently, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Governmental intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widespread belief that government has a moral obligation to foster a thriving economy and support the welfare of the poor. For example, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and also restricted the activities of alcohol sellers, taverns, and prostitutes (see Henderson and Henderson, 1947; Burgin, 1989; Novak, 1996; Lori, 1999; Levi, 2000). Local authorities also used the right to compulsory transfer of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

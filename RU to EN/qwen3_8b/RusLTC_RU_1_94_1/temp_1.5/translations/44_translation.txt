In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Mahon, Holmes more closely played the role of Trojan Horse rather than as a victor—defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "taking principle" of property on the basis of balancing competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should constantly be "reasonably" used, but the understanding of what "reasonably" entails remains vague. If actions go "too far," in such cases, compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it is the judges, taking into account the presence of public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully assess and balance the competing interests of both sides. Property owners, in turn, should not alienate everyone against themselves. Holmes argued that "the government will hardly be able to continue its activities if to some extent the issue of property rights is not resolved."

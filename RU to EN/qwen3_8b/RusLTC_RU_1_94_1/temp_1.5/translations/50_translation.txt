The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of his property" was necessary so that "it would give him the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely confirmed the central holding of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case of the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation because it established a new standard in relation to old rules. Thus, holding a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, the Nollans wanted to build a new three-bedroom home in place of their run-down bungalow, similar to other homes in that county. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only upon the condition that the Nollans give consent to grant a common right of use over the territory between the line of high tide and the dike behind their home. Thus, people could easily reach the parks located both in the north and south directions by simply strolling leisurely along the sand. The commission explained its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "landfilling," which "people would unconsciously fail to realize that there is a shoreline nearby and that they fully have the right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

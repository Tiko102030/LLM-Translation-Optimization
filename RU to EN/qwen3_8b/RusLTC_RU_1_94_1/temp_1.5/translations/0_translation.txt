INTRODUCTION: The Concept of Property in Economic Theory and Social Practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ based on the definition they provide for the concept of property, and often the boundaries are difficult to delineate. Some believe that ownership rights extend to such items as land and trees, including the most ephemeral objects such as living nature and flowing water. Others expand this concept by asserting that ownership rights also extend to such objects as ideas (patents), and some even consider humans as property in this context. Societies also differ depending on the rights that property owners possess; the set of privileges relating to responsibility for actions toward other people. These rights prevent us from viewing the law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because every use of it affects other people's property. Property has dimensions, because it involves human relationships; I have the right to dispose of my property, which diminishes your right to be completely free. For example, when the environment is polluted, neighbors' rights to clean air and water significantly decrease; rights prohibiting pollution limit the rights of this property, but protect the property of neighbors. Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using land and water resources, even if it may cause harm to neighboring property.

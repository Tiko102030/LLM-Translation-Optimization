It appears that the aforementioned case is based on John Locke's views regarding absolute property rights. The Mann case's final decision was also mentioned, which was handed down by Chief Justice Waite. However, attention should be drawn to the previous statement made: "this is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., consciously reestablished an instrumental vision of property rights, which declared the previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. Holmes had already expressed his opinion in the Lochner case. Beginning his speech by stating, "the decision was based on the principles of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy most of the country's population," he added: "The right of each person to act according to his own will for as long as he wishes, provided, of course, that it does not violate the right of others to do the same, does not conform to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal service management, the laws of any of the states, or other municipal institutions that take his money to achieve objectives that seem necessary, regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner's despotic protection of intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require achieving a balance. After losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

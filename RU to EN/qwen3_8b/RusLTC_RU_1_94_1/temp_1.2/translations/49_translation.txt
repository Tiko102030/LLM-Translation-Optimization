According to the court's ruling: "The owner of the land has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of others and that could not have been achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that environmental zoning rules "must be reasonable," but "we consider that preventing violations of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "taking of property," as in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was due to the fact that "the use of property without altering the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding "takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already adopted several decisions in 1987. For example, in the case of "First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. County of Los Angeles (1987)," the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the regional flood control organization banned the restoration of the church's flood-damaged campsite. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a regulation that deprives the owner of using their property" is necessary to "grant them the right to compensation for damages."

Calhander filed a claim for damages caused during street repairs, which resulted in damage to the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited English laws, common law precedents, and this principle to seek compensation. However, the defense side rejected the use of English precedent with an instrumental approach to the case. They explained that due to the circumstances in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim," which Calhander brought, "is granted, then we would have to review the correctness of repairs of all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Calhander's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's proposal for a market interpretation of the case to avoid similar subsequent damage claims, which was decidedly bad news for people whose property directly abutted public structures. "Those who buy property adjacent to a roadway," the court stated, "must take into account future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." For in such a case, the compensation amount would have doubled compared to the initial cost of repairs. Similar conclusions were reached in New York in 1828. In the case "Lancing v. Smith Walter Quickenbush," a claim was brought against the state of New York for damages due to the drop in the price of docks after the construction of a new harbor in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach applied in the Massachusetts case "Calhander v. Marsh" to dismiss Quickenbush's claims.

"Use your own so as not to harm another." The application of this principle ensures access to the consideration of a social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his view, actions performed by one owner affect others even if they occur on their own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm, not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests arise and both parties claim interference by a neighbor (see Holmes, 1894, p. 11). Issues concerning the subject of property were reevaluated in the 19th century to promote the development of entrepreneurial activity, even at the expense of harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained, but now it was used to protect the right of entrepreneurs to act in accordance with the requirements of passive neighbors who want their property to remain undisturbed. Now, the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of unlawful acts against another's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own property. This new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for harm caused by the use of their property. This approach was first established in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" in the state of New York (1805).

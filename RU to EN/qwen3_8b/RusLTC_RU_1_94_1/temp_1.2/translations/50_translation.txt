The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving an owner of the use of their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the First Anglican Church case, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nollan v. the California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nollans, who owned a coastal parcel of land between public parks to the north and south, sought to build a three-bedroom house in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other houses in the area. The California Coastal Commission granted the necessary permit only on condition that the Nollans agree to allow a common right of use over the territory between the line of high tide and the seawall behind their home. Thus, people could easily reach the parks located both to the north and south by simply strolling leisurely along the beach. The Commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger house would contribute to "filling in the area," which "people would subconsciously not realize that they are near the shoreline and that they have a complete right to spend time there" (see Igle 1996, p. 255).

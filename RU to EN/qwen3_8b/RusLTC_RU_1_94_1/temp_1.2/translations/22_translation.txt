After the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, African Americans from the Southern states gained the right to own property, however, their rights could change depending on the political climate of the state and the extent of African American participation in it. The same instrumental approach that the governments of the Northern states used to limit property rights concerning clean air and water was also used in the Southern states to maintain African American dependence on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public goals, although the goals differed. Redistributing property rights in order to stimulate economic growth
By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach, which he had used in the Court to invalidate the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge Company. He did this to independently manage the only bridge across the Charles River, which connects Boston and Cambridge (see the case Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge). The defense of the Charles River Bridge Company experienced some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... which had been invested in the construction of railroads and canals along the expressways... We would be pushed back a century to the inventions of that era and would be forced to remain motionless."

For the first time, it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream brought a claim for damages. The dam situated upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same rights regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your property in such a way as not to injure others'] should be limited to a reasonable extent to prevent a person from being deprived of the enjoyment of their property merely due to some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law providing for some inconvenience of this kind; thus, a person who could first construct a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of the portion of profit that could have been obtained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both long-standing and new residents possessed the same privileges. Thus, there occurred a redistribution of wealth toward entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents whose actions were prohibited from restricting entrepreneurs' activities. At the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases only weakened existing property rights. Thus, the transformation of the rule 'use your property in such a way as not to injure others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property introduced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights.

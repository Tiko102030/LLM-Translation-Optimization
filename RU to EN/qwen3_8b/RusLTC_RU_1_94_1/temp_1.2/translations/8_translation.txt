A particular form of ownership as a tool may be convenient, but never necessary. However, Locke himself does not defend property rights as resolutely as one might understand from the well-known passage cited above (see Horn 1990; Kecskesnelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he addresses the issue of the right of the Stuart monarchs of England to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament. But beyond this issue, Locke also acknowledges the social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke argued that each person should have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be limited to ensure that all can share the generosity of nature, leaving "enough and in the same state" for others. Referring to the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature, which in this way gives us property, equally limits the extent of this property.

The new paradigm of property rights acknowledges various definitions of property, however, has so far regarded "property rights" as relationships between humans and objects (see Barceló 1997; Loubké 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As relationships between humans and objects, property exists without human society; for instance, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Like relationships between humans and objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it is a reward for labor that forms the non-human material world. Labor, as John Locke says, "in the beginning, gave the right to property" (see J. Locke 1952, p. 27 [par. 45], p. /think)

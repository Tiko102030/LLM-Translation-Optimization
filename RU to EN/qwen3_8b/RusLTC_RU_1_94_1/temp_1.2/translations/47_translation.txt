Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:
The economic conjuncture in the country may result from how temporary restrictions on compulsory enforcement of contract terms are reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of the article, and thus will not exceed the state's reserved right to protect vital societal interests. Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful limitation on the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority. In this regard, whether the policy is reasonable or unreasonable, the legislative body will carry it out, and the Court will not interfere. Already in 1934, when the Court was prepared to abandon the evaluation of economic legislation and the adjudication of property rights in court cases, the legislative body had already then asserted the necessity of "protecting vital societal interests." After Roosevelt's re-election to the presidency in 1936 and his attempt to "move" the Court in 1937, the Court had practically fully agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach in the activities of the Supreme Court as the case "United States v. Carolene Products" (1938). In this specific case, the company was sued under the "Dairy Products Act" of 1923, which prohibited the interstate sale of milk with added fat and oil, and it was considered the sale of "deliberately spoiled food product harmful to health." The company's attorneys argued that the law unconstitutionally restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting the arguments in favor of property rights, the Court found the law valid, as it considered it a fair instrument of state regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would have been a routine, simple confirmation of the police power's established authority. However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, according to which the majority of the Court members shared the concept of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

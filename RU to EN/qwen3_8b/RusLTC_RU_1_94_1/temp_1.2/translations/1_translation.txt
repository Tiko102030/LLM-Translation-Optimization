Society, on one hand, does not prohibit an owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it may support the affected neighbors and limit the activities of that particular owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of the loss of clean air and water or the cost of the payment that would have to be made to the firm to stop it from dumping waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be significantly poorer if they did not have these privileges, because they would have to reduce the level of pollution or they would have to buy a pollution permit from their neighbors. Some question the fact that property rights significantly influence the distribution of income, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase claimed that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear definition of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social outcome, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment purchase the rights from their neighbors, or should the neighbors pay those who pollute the environment to stop doing so? Pollution of the environment will occur where the cost of reducing its negative consequences is greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and it will be stopped when the cost of reducing the negative effect is less. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

The ironworking and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, could only ensure the developing network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently established centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Irritated workers and farmers saw the true goals of bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose property was precisely sponsored by government agencies. They wanted new laws that would expand the public's authority in regulating property. They achieved remarkable success in securing the law establishing an eight-hour workday and a law setting railroad tariffs. There were also objections from many large businessmen, and as a result, these bills were sent for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case of Mann v. State of Illinois greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of the popular radicalism of that time. The plaintiffs claimed that the law, by voiding existing contracts, violated the constitutional clause on contracts and also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from government interference.

There exists an "inalienable" right of property for the laborer, because: Although the land and all lower creatures belong collectively to all mankind, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, according to the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever a man may take from that state in which nature has created and preserved the subject, he combines it with his labor and appends to it something that belongs exclusively to him, thereby making it his property... For since this labor is the laborer's inalienable property, no one else can have a right to that to which he has once attached it, at least in those cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it is to use someone else's labor, that is, to reduce a person to servitude. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, property owners form society and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

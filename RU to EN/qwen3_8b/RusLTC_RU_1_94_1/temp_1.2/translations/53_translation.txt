Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Amendment remains in effect. Since it was discussed, the Supreme Court approved nearly all legislation regulating property and recognized that the powers of elected legislative members must be "as vast as the country's economic needs." At the same time, the Supreme Court affirmed its role as a watchdog of private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. It equated the right to pollute one's territory or to engage in discrimination based on gender, race, or religion with these rights, which are now above the claims of property owners. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that property rights rest on two pillars: the nature of harm and property as an instrument, not as a sacred right. Acknowledging the dual nature of damages, courts view any asserted "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," all of which must be balanced. After the principle of Holmes' interest balance was approved, the Court should consider each asserted "right" to property in an instrumental interpretation, as the alternation of interests would lead to achieving more comprehensive public goals. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute and should never be an end in themselves.

However, the most significant aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, in which he stated that the majority of the Court equated the concept of property rights with a bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in matters of economic legislation, the Court intends to apply the presumption of constitutionality of laws, and selected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative actions. On the contrary, Stone warned that there will be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religious choice. Environmental protection and challenge to the 'taking' procedure. After the decision in the Caroline Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body broad authority in choosing objects of social policy, even though they had restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main objectives of the policy pursued in this context. The 1972 case "Just v. Marinette County," Wisconsin, clearly illustrates how judicial power easily applied the position from the Caroline Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just filed a lawsuit against the repeal of regulations that prevented them from occupying the designated swampy areas for building homes. The Wisconsin Supreme Court rejected the Just family's claims and ruled that legislative activity is a lawful use of police powers. Later, it sought to approve an even broader list of property rights restrictions.

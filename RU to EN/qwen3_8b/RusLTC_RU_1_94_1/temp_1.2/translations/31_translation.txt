The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitution's contract clauses by invalidating existing agreements, thereby also violating the recently enacted Fourteenth Amendment, which protects citizens' rights from governmental interference. Originally enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the Southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment is now regarded as an essential civil right and a constitutional guarantee of property rights, safeguarding private property from governmental encroachment. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property rights, the Court upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards one another, and how people should use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice Hale of England, stating that "when property 'touches the interests of society, it is by law no longer subject only to private regulation.' Remembering the principle 'the welfare of the people is the supreme law' (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: 'When a property owner uses his property in such a way that the public acquires an interest in it, he actually desires that, and until that interest ceases, he must accept that now society controls his property, since this is necessary for the public's welfare. If he does not want society to control his property, he must simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.'

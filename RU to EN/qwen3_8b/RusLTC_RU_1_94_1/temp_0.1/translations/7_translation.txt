Such views spread in the American republic, and the arguments of Locke continued to be cited by judges of the Supreme Court in the late nineteenth century, who opposed public regulation of property rights (see Elly 1998). Others, however, discuss property from the perspective of instrumentalism. For them, property rights should be protected, i.e., these rights serve as an incentive for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an indisputable right that surpasses all others. Instead, the problem of property is now paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines the protection of all property rights.

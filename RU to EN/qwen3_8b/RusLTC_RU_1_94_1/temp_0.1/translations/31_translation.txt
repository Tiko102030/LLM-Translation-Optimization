The plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from state interference. Recently enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the Southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights, protecting private property rights from state interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property rights, the Court affirmed and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people ought to use their property, but only when it is necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Wight cited Lord Chief Justice Hale of England, stating that when property "touches the interests of society, it is by law no longer subject to private regulation alone." Remembering the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Wight added: when an owner uses his property in such a way that the public develops an interest in it, in fact, he himself desires this, and until that interest disappears, he must simply accept that now society controls his property, since it is necessary for the increase of public welfare. If he does not wish for society to control his property, he must simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Wight acknowledged several limitations on state regulation.

For the first time, it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property downstream initiated a claim for damages. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same right regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your property so as not to harm others'] should be limited within reasonable limits to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely due to some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for some inconvenience of this nature, so that a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose part of the profit they could have gained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, resulting in both old and new residents possessing the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents whose actions were prohibited from restricting entrepreneurs' activities. At the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases only weakened existing property rights. Therefore, the transformation of the rule 'use your property so as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property introduced by John Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

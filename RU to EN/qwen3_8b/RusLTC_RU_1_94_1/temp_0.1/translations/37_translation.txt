The Louisiana Supreme Court adopted a law stating that "losses of private rights in this case are irrelevant due to the overall profit and commercial turnover of this commercial entity" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230-31). The butchers of New Orleans brought their case to the US Supreme Court, where their interests were represented by John A. Campbell, a legal predecessor of Field, a Democrat and assistant war minister (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court prior to the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the core principles of labor legislation to persuade the Court to protect a citizen's property rights. Drawing on the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans city ordinance had brought us back to a time when "the government's intrusive gaze followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5-4, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power.

Considering more closely the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Wait acknowledged several limitations on state regulation. "For the use of regulated property, you may receive compensation," and Wait argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "It is assumed," he said, "that any law is constitutional. Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it is clearly obvious. If there is doubt, the expressed will of the legislative body should be supported by a majority." Acknowledging that this power could be misused, he urged citizens to seek redress "at polling stations, not in court." Defenders of property rights were shocked by the open reading of the police's authority in the Mann case. Judge Steven Field thought differently and said that "the principle on which the majority's opinion relies... undermines private property rights." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be under the control of the majority of its legislative body members." The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights are now also one of society's interests, another tool for achieving societal goals, and no longer require any constitutional guarantee.

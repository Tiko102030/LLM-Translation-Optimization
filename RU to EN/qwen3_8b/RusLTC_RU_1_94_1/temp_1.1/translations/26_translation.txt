"Use your own so as not to harm another." Application of this principle ensures access to the consideration of the social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his view, actions of one property owner affect others even if they occur on their own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm, not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests of different people are involved and both parties allege interference with their neighbor's territory (see Holmes, 1894, p. 11). Issues related to the subject of property were re-evaluated in the 19th century to promote the development of business activities, even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained, but now it was used to protect entrepreneurs' rights to act according to the requirements of passive neighbors who wish their property to remain undisturbed. Now, the concept of "damage" was considered not as a series of wrongful acts against another's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own property. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by the use of their property. This approach first became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of New York (1805).

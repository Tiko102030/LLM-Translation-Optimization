The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed certain property in comparison to another property. According to one historian of law, Ward Hirst, this approach is one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America—"the release of the entire creative energy of a person." Despite the fact that the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on the entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. In theory, if the cost of the project were higher than the amount of the caused damage, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the caused damage. However, in practice, all the burden is shifted to the injured party, which only to a greater extent encourages the increase in the number of entrepreneurs' projects, and all the risks and operational costs are placed on the shoulders of the neighbors. If property rights were respected to a greater legal extent, high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by saving on the costs of risk and operational costs of entrepreneurs, the new principle has further contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

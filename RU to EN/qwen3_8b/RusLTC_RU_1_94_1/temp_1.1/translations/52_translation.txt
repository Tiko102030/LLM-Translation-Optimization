But after these liberal icons retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional limitations on government regulation. For example, in the case Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required that property owners in South Carolina be compensated for damages to their property after a new "Coastal Line Organization Act" halted construction on their land. Again expressing distrust of elected legislators, Scalia warned, as in the Nollan case, that judges must carefully examine regulations to avoid the risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, would be used for public purposes. The Court refined its new standards in the case Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from property owners to the government. The case arose when the City of Tigard, Oregon administration stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only if she gave up part of her land for creating a public park and pedestrian and bicycle trails to reduce traffic congestion. Writing for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city "did not contest the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle trails at Dolan's expense was a result of the city's necessity to improve transportation infrastructure." The rulings in the Nollan and Dolan cases signal the arrival of a new era of intensified scrutiny of property regulation. This could lead to property rights returning to the level they were at before the New Deal.

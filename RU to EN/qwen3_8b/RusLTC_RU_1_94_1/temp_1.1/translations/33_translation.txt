The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now one of society's main concerns, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the background of increasing social unrest, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and provoked a stronger outburst among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events at Haymarket Square in 1886, prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings had not allowed the limitation of police powers. The spread of the non-intervention policy reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government interference in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unrestrained throughout the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to determine what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their evident power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the conditions of universal suffrage, conservatives were even more afraid that such an autocracy might appear, which would be more despotic and more unwise than any that had existed before. In the late nineteenth century, when expressing opposition to the opinion of "the numerous army of dissatisfied people," advocates and judges aimed to sanctify private property.

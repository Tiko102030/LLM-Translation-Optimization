Local authorities routinely confiscated homes used for unlawful activities, dumped factory sewage into waterways that "endangered people's health," prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock processing, as well as others with economic significance unrelated to health, such as rights to trade and purchase, sale and production (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials resorted to coercive measures. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the premises of the Manhattan Fertilizing fertilizer plant and began "reducing" the harm caused by the factory's operations, destroying machinery and removing all necessary components (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, prompting attorneys to fear increasing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissent is more memorable than Justice Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the state of Louisiana had violated fundamental rights to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, Field based his defense of property rights on John Locke's teachings that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the essential part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." It took Field twenty years to fully grasp the Supreme Court's decision in Sloter-House v. Mann before his views were adopted by the Court. In 1897, in the case Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company without a license to conduct commercial activities in Louisiana, which essentially constituted a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacob case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that freedom of the individual included not only the absence of "physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he pleases; to earn his living by any lawful means; to pursue the desired profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned objectives."

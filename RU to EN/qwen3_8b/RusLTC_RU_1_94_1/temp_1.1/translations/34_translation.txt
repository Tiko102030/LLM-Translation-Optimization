In the late nineteenth century, expressing opposition to the opinion of the "numerous dissatisfied," attorneys and judges aimed to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tideman, they demanded that the full extent of state power be limited by establishing boundaries for the authority of the police. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhimer v. People" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the sale of liquor. After the law took effect, all those who had sold this liquor lost their property. At the same time, the procedure for seizing property did not follow the established legal order. For the first time, the court recognized that the concept of "established legal order" protected property, and it ruled that "legislative power cannot entirely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thereby subjecting property to total disappearance."

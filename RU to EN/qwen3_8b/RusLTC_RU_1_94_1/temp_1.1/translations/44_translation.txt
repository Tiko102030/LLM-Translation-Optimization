In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Co. v. Holmes, Holmes rather acted as a Trojan horse than as a defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "taking principle" of property based on balancing competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on circumstances and will be a non-mandatory procedure. According to Holmes, property must constantly be "reasonably" used, but the notion of what constitutes "reasonable" remains vague. If actions go "too far," in such cases, compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it will be the judges, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully evaluate and balance the competing interests of both sides. Property owners, in turn, should not incite all against themselves. Holmes asserted that "government will hardly be able to continue its activities any further if, to some extent, the issue of property rights is not resolved."

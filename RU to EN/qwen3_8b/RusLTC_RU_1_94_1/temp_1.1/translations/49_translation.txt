According to the court's ruling: "A landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of soil to use it for purposes that infringe upon the rights of other people and that could not be achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that zoning regulations for environmental protection "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "eminent domain" taking of property, as in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Co. This was because "the use of property without altering the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding "eminent domain" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already issued several rulings in 1987. For example, in the case of "First Anglo Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the local flood control district prohibited the restoration of a flood-damaged church campsite. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a regulation preventing the owner from using their property is necessary to 'give them the right to compensation for damages.'"

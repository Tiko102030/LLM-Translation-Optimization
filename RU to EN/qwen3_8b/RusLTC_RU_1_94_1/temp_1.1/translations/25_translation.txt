The New York Court used an instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Calhoun v. Marsh" to dismiss Quakenbush's claims. The court asserted: "Any significant public improvement necessarily and to some extent affects the comfort and property of people; and in the case when the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of the damage itself should be considered part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." The court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the public good should prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise  
Without prohibiting the government from carrying out state improvements at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedent law, as in the case of "Lansing v. Calhoun," imposed an obligation on some people to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental principles became private cases. For example, in the case "Torp v. Rutland and Burlington Railroad" (1855), Chief Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to protect their tracks, because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas)." The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property rights.

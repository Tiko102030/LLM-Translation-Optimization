Chancellor Kent of New York declared in 1826 that "every person possesses such an amount of freedom to acquire, use, and dispose of his or her property, which may be compared to an acceptable state of property and mutual rights of others... Although, thus, property is protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use to the extent necessary to prevent abuse that may lead to violation of the rights of another person or of society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the entire 19th century. For example, in 1837, the legislative body in the state of Illinois lacked three pages to list all the powers that the Chicago city administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation and restricting "swinging a hoop, playing ball, or launching paper kites... capable of annoying people," the new city acquired the power to "compel the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle store, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, outhouse, sewing factory, or any other dilapidated, unpleasant building or house to clean, remove, or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). Authorities in other cities also assumed broad powers regarding property.

 The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor an overall affirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it only asserted some ownership in comparison to other ownership. According to one of the legal historians, Willard Hurst, this approach is one of the working principles of the rule of law in the United States of America - "the release of all human creative energy." Despite the fact that the burden of compensating neighbors for indirect losses remains on the shoulders of entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to start profit-generating projects. In theory, if the cost of the project were higher than the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of damage caused. However, in practice, the whole burden is shifted to the injured party, which only further contributes to an increase in the number of entrepreneurs' projects, and all the risk and operating costs fall on the neighbors. If property rights were observed to a greater legal extent, then high operating costs would impede the implementation of the entrepreneur's plans.

 The new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of ownership, however, it still views "property rights" as relationships between people and objects (see Barzel 1997; Liebke 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relationship between people and the objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it is a reward for labor that shapes the non-human material world. Labor, as John Locke says, "at first gave rise to a right to property" (see J. Locke 1952, p. 27 [par. 45]).

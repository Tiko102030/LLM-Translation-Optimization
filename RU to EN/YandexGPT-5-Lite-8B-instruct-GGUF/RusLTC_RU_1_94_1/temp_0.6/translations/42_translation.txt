 Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to launch a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the opinion of the majority of legislators about natural rights and the beliefs of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that rather signifies the end of productivity and prosperity, not the protection of the sacred notion of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of a different social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "The Common Law" (1881), he talked in detail about how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the prerequisites for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even seek to compensate a person for all the harm done to him... Anyone can found any firm, and further on, to survive in the competition, he will poach his competitor's clients, and perhaps thereby even ruin him." In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm brought by competition is permissible according to the principles of "a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881).

Chancellor Kent of New York in 1826 declared that "every person possesses such an amount of freedom to acquire, use and dispose of his property as may be commensurate with a tolerable state of property and the mutual rights of others... Although property is thus protected... the legislature has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use to the extent that it may be necessary in order to prevent abuse of the right, which could lead to violation of the rights of another person or society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in Illinois, the legislature lacked three pages to catalog all the powers that the Chicago city administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and limiting "the spinning of hoops, playing ball or flying kites... capable of annoying people", the new city acquired the power that "will force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, tallow candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, lavatory, clothing factory or any other shabby, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). The authorities of other cities also assumed broad powers over property issues. Thus, in New York, certain norms were established for the transportation of goods across the East River, for example, the price for cargo transportation varied from twenty-five cents for well-fed buffaloes or bulls to fifty cents per cord of hickory wood, one cent for empty milk bottles, twenty-five cents for child corpses and fifty cents for adult corpses (see Novak 1996).

 Referring to the Bible, Locke argues that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property also limits the extent of that property. "God has given us everything abundantly"—this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But why did he give it to us? For enjoyment. A person has the right to convert through his labor into his own property as much as he can use for some of the needs of his life, before this item is spoiled. And what goes beyond these limits exceeds his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's chagrin, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportionate and unequal ownership of land," to have power over others. Therefore, restrictions on the accumulation of property are necessary in order to guarantee everyone access to the wealth of society. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as restrictions on individual accumulation, later other authors joined these reasoning, including the famous American radical, Thomas Paine.

Although the earth and all lower creatures belong to all people in common, yet ... his body's labor and the work of his hands most strictly belong to him. Whatever then a man draws from that state wherein nature hath placed it and kept it, he mixes with his labour something belonging to himself alone, and so makes it his property... For since his labour is the undeniable property of the worker, no one but him can have a right to what once he has joined to it, at least in cases where enough and the same quality remains for others' common use. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it means to use someone's labour, i.e., to turn a person into a slave. It follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, the owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

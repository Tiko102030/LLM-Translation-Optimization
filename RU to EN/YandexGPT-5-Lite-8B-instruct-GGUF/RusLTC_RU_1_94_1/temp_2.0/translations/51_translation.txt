Writing an opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of a "condition regarding the granting of land-use permission" is not a "taking" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general land use "does not serve the intended purpose to protect people's visual access to the beach" and "therefore it is unlawful, and compensation must take place." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard: Because there is no connection between the condition and the root cause of the construction restriction, the new goal transforms into something different from what it was before. Now the main task for the government is to obtain general land use rights without paying any compensation.... if the condition provided in the nature management permit is not considered as a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a legitimate land use rule, but rather "mere extortion." This time justices Brennan and Marshall expressed dissenting opinions. They rejected Scalia's demand to revise the views of legislative members. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration... is whether the state can rationally address the issue of whether the adopted measure can achieve a public goal." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed additional limitations on government regulation.

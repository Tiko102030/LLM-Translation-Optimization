 The new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of ownership, however, it still considers "property rights" as relationships between people and objects (see Barzel 1997; Leibke 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relationship between people and the objects they possess, ownership exists without human society; after all, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Just like relationships between people and the objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it is a reward for labor that shapes the non-human material world.

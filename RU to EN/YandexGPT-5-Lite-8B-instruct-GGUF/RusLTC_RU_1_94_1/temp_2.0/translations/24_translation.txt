 Callender filed a claim for damages caused to him during the street repair, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle in order to seek compensation. But the defense side rejected the use of the English precedent with an instrumental presentation of the case. She explained that due to circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim" presented by Callender "is satisfied, then it will be necessary to check the correctness of the repair of all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Callender's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts court accepted the state's proposal for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public constructions. "Those who buy property next to the roadway," the court выдвинул, "should take into account the future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." After all, in this case, the amount of compensation would double compared to the original cost. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case of "Lansing vs Smith Walter Queкенбуш," a claim was filed against the state of New York for damages due to the fact that docks fell in price after a new bay was built in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach used in the case of "Callender v Marsh" of Massachusetts in order to dismiss Quakenbush's demands.

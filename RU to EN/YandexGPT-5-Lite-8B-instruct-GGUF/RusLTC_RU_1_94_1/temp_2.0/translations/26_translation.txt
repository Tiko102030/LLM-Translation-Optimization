 The use of your own must not injure others... Application of this principle provides access to the consideration of social interpretation of property. Justice Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his words, actions taken by one owner affect others even though they take place on their own property territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is only a norm and not a rule; it should not be used in most cases where the property interests of different people collide and where both parties complain about their neighbor's interference with their territory (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Issues related to the subject of property were revised in the XIX century in order to promote the development of business activities, even at the expense of damaging the property of others. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" survived, but it was now used to protect the right of entrepreneurs to act in accordance with the demands of passive neighbors who want their property left untouched. Now the concept of "damage" was seen not as a series of illegal actions against someone else's property, but as an inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case of "Palmer v. Mulligan" of New York State (1805).

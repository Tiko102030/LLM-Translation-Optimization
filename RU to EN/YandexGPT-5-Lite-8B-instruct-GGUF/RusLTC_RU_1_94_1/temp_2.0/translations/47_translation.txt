 By denying any privileged status to private property, Hughes concluded that:
The economic situation in the country could be the result of how the temporary limitation on forced compliance with the terms of the contract would be согласован with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus would not go beyond the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unauthorized restriction on the protectionist policies of the state, the legislature must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority.... At the same time, whether the legislative body will pursue reasonable or unreasonable policies, the Court will no longer be concerned. Back in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon the assessment of economic legislation and разбирательства in court cases on property rights issues, the legislature had already declared the need to "protect the vital interests of society". After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court practically fully agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach in the деятельности of the Supreme Court as was done in the case of "United States v. Carolene Products" (1938). In this particular case, the company was sued on the basis of the «Low-fat Milk Act» of 1923, which prohibited the interstate sale of milk with added fat and oil, considering it as the sale of a "deliberately spoiled food product harmful to health". The representatives of Carolene Products claimed that the law unlawfully limited freedom of contract. By rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court considered the law valid, because, in its opinion, it was a fair tool of state regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would have been an ordinary, simple confirmation of the established police powers. However, what was most important in this case was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the Court had divided the concepts of property rights and a bunch of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

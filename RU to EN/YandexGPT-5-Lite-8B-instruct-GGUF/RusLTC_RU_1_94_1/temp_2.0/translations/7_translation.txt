Such views were widespread in the American Republic, and Locke's reasoning continued to be quoted by judges of the Supreme Court of the late nineteenth century who opposed public regulation of property rights (see Elie 1998). Others discuss property from the position of instrumentalism. For them, property rights must be protected, i.e., these rights are an incentive for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an indisputable right that exceeds all others. Instead, the problem of property ownership is now paramount, and the instrumental approach itself undermines the protection of all property rights.

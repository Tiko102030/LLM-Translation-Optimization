There is an "undeniable" right of property of the worker because: Although the land and all lower creatures belong collectively to all people, nevertheless...the labor of his body and the work of his hands, by the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever then a person may extract from that state in which nature has created and preserved the subject, he combines it with his labor and joins to it something belonging personally to him and thereby makes it his property....After all, since this labor is undeniable property of the worker, no one but him can have the right to what he once joined to it, at least in cases when a sufficient quantity and same quality remains for general use by others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it is to use someone's labor, i.e., to turn a person into a slave. It follows from this that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, the owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

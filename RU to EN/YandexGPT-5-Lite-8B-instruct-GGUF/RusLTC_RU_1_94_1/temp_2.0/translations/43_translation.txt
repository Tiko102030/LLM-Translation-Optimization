 In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm brought by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Criminal Intent and Design," Holmes built on the aforementioned idea about the harm that competition brings. Thus, he wanted to demonstrate the general need for an instrumental approach and a "principle of balance of interests," especially in conditions where judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At a session of the Supreme Court in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Mine" versus "United States Glue". "In modern society," he declared, "each part is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, then the others will more or less feel it. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the end of legislative power comes, it will not be enough simply to show that some progress has been made in the development of the law, i.e., when it will already be possible to intervene in ongoing or existing transactions."

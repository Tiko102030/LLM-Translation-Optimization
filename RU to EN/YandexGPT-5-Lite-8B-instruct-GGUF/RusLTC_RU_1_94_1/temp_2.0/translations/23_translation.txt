In relation to this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public goals, were immediately abolished once they obstructed these goals. In the early XIX century, state authorities, striving to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads, railways, and canals. Indirect costs were incurred during the construction of infrastructure in the state, expenses associated with damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of underdeveloped properties. Under the legal principle «use your own so as not to harm another» (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would be held responsible for indirect costs that significantly increase the cost of financing inventions. By modifying this principle, i.e., by avoiding responsibility for independent entrepreneurs' actions regarding adjacent property, the costs of constructing state infrastructure would decrease dramatically. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case «Callender v. Marsh», which set a precedent for Massachusetts. Callender filed a claim for compensation for damage caused to him during street repair, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston.

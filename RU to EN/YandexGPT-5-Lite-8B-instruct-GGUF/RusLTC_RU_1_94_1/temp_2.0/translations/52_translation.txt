 But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation. For example, in the case «Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council» (1992), the Court required that a real estate owner in South Carolina be compensated for damage to his property after a new "Coastal Zone Management Act" suspended construction on his land. Again expressing its distrust of elected legislators, Scalia warned, as in the Nollan case, that judges should carefully examine regulations in order to insure themselves against «the risk that private property will be used for public purposes under the guise of reducing serious harm to society». The Court refined its new standards in the case "Dolan v. City of Tigard" (1994), where the courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from owners to the government. The case was filed when the administration of Tigard, Oregon, said that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only on condition that she give up part of her land for a public park, pedestrian and bike paths in order to reduce traffic congestion. Writing an opinion for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration «did not dispute the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle paths at Dolan's expense was a consequence of the need for the city administration to improve the transportation interchange». The decisions in the Nollan and Dolan cases indicate the onset of a new era of increased scrutiny of property regulation. This could lead to the situation where property rights are again at the level they were before the New Deal.

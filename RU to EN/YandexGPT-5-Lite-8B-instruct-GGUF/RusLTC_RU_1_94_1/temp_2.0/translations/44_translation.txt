Instead of considering the right to property as a natural right, Holmes justified the "principle of expropriation" of property based on establishing an equilibrium between competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on the circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must be constantly used "reasonably," but the idea of what is "reasonable" remains blurred. If actions go "too far," then compensation for damage will be necessary in this case; however, it will be up to judges, taking into account the existence of public needs and special circumstances, to determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully evaluate and balance the competing interests of both parties. Owners should not, in turn, antagonize everyone. Holmes argued that "the government could hardly continue its activities if the issue of property were not resolved to some extent."

 For the first time it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of New York state (1805), where the owner of property located downstream filed a claim for damages. The dam located upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: 
the defendants had the same right with regard to their territory... [the principle "use your own so as not to harm another"] must be limited within reasonable limits in order not to deprive a person of the pleasure of using his property simply because of some silly inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for certain inconveniences of this nature, thus, a person who could first build a dam or factory on any public or navigable river eventually acquires an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether he owns the adjacent shores or not.... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of that part of the profit it could gain as a result of competition. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs due to the fact that established residents were forbidden from restricting the actions of entrepreneurs. But at the same time, by eliminating the established monopoly power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation over indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" did not depend at all on J. Locke's ideas about sacred private property. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

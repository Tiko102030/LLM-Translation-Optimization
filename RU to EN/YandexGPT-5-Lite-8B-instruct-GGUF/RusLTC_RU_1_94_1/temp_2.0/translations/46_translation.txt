The most remarkable was the court decision of 1934. In the case "Home Building & Loan Association against Blaisdell", the Supreme Court, in connection with the prevailing economic instability in the country, approved the actions of the state regarding regulation of ownership. The court upheld a Minnesota state law; this law imposed a moratorium on foreclosure in conditions of an unfavorable economic situation established by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the article of the Constitution limiting government regulation should "not be considered with literal accuracy as any mathematical formula, but it still remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information." He added that the Court should interpret this article only "in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its inhabitants." 
"The effectiveness of a legislative body's activity should be assessed not by the direct or indirect impact it has on contract terms, but by whether the decision made is lawful and whether it was achieved by reasonable and appropriate methods." This speaks to the social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Denying any privileged status for private property, Hughes came to the conclusion that:
The economic situation in the country may result from how the temporary restriction on enforcement of contract terms will be reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this article of the treaty is not an unconditional and completely unlawful limitation of the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body should make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative legitimacy.... At the same time, whether the legislative body pursues a reasonable or unreasonable policy, it will no longer be the Court's concern.

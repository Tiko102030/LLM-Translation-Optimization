 Judge Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court supported the legislative power addressing property regulation issues in Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting building construction outside this pier, and spoke about police powers as follows:

We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, regardless of whether he has exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all implied responsibility so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their own property use, nor the whole society as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules necessary to achieve the common good and universal welfare can be applied to it. Property rights, like all other social and conventional rights, are subject to appropriate restrictions necessary to ensure that others have not suffered from the use of this property, and to legally established rules, since the legislative power... can assume that this is a necessary and expedient solution.

In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and its sources of power than to set boundaries or limits to its manifestation." Indeed, using universal, typical phrases like "public interest", "internal structure of the state", "implied right and duty of higher power to protect everyone" and "common good and universal welfare", Shaw established that police authorities could have a large set of instruments to achieve their goals. Southern states' governments used police assistance in order to enact laws limiting slaves' property use, for example, they prohibited slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after abolition, they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as the introduction of strict racial segregation policies, which restricted the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activity in certain professions. The application of laws ensuring unequal protection of African American property and labor once again demonstrates what understanding of property there was at that time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichtenstaine 1996; Winer 1978; Woodward 1955).

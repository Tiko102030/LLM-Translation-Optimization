Local authorities regularly confiscated houses where immoral activities took place, засыпали wastewater from factories that "jeopardized people's health", prohibited the production of hazardous chemicals and put out fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, and did not pay any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of sectors of the economy, including those related to healthcare, such as the production of medicines and slaughtering livestock, and others that were economically significant and did not concern health issues, such as the right to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation failed to achieve a public goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of a fertilizer plant owned by "Manhattan Fertilizing" and proceeded to "reduce" the harm caused by the factory's activities by destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

Back in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon the assessment of economic legislation and proceedings in court cases on property rights issues, the legislature had already declared the need to "protect the vital interests of society". After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court practically agreed with his reform program. No other decision has illustrated so clearly the new approach in the activities of the Supreme Court as it was done in the case "United States v. Carolyn Products" (1938). In this particular case, the company was sued on the basis of the "Low-Fat Milk Act" of 1923, which prohibited the interstate sale of milk with added fat and butter, considering it as the sale of a "deliberately adulterated food product harmful to health". The attorneys for Carolyn Products argued that the law had improperly restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court considered the law valid because, in its opinion, it was a fair instrument of state regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would have been an ordinary, simple confirmation of established police powers. However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the members of the Court distinguished between property rights and a bunch of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

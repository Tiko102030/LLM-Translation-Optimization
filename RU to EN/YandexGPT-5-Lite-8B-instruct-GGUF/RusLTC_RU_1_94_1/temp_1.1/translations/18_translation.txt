Chancellor Kent of New York in 1826 announced that "every person has as much freedom to acquire, use and dispose of his property as can be compared with an acceptable state of property and the mutual rights of others... Although thus the property is protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use to the extent that it may be necessary in order to prevent abuse of the right, which could lead to violation of the rights of another person or society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in Illinois, the legislative body did not have enough three pages to enter into the catalog all the powers that the Chicago city administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and limiting "the act of spinning hoops, playing ball or flying kites... capable of annoying people", a new power appeared in the city that "will force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, tallow candle shop, soap factory, leather workshop, stable, barn, lavatory, clothing factory or any other shabby, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Nowak 1996, pp. 3-6). Authorities in other cities also assumed broad powers over property issues. Thus, in New York, certain norms were established for the transportation of goods on the East River, for example, the price for transporting cargo varied from twenty-five cents for fattened buffaloes or bulls to fifty cents per cord of hickory wood, one cent for empty milk bottles, twenty-five cents for child corpses and fifty cents for adult corpses (see Nowak 1996).

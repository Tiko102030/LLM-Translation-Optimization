In this regard, in the Pennsylvania Coal case, Holmes rather acted as a Trojan Horse rather than a winner — defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering the right to property as a natural right, Holmes обосновал the "principle of seizure" of property based on establishing an equilibrium between competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on the circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should be used "reasonably" all the time, but the idea of what is "reasonable" remains vague for now. If actions go "too far", then in this case compensation for damage will be necessary; however, it will be the judges who, taking into account the existence of public needs and special circumstances, will determine what "going too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully evaluate and balance the competing interests of both sides. Owners should not, in turn, alienate everyone. Holmes claimed that "the government can hardly continue its activities if the issue of ownership is not resolved to some extent".

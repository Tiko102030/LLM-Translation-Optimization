 The iron and steel industries, for whose products preferential tariffs were established, could only provide for the developing network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the real goals of bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose property was sponsored by government authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand the powers of the public in regulating property. They achieved striking success in getting a law passed on an eight-hour working day and a law establishing railway tariffs. There were objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were submitted for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's decision in 1877 in the case "Mann v. Illinois" greatly alarmed the business circles of the state, who were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of radicalism that was popular at the time. The plaintiffs argued that by canceling existing transactions, the law violated a provision of the constitution on transactions, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from governmental interference.

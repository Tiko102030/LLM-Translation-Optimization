Writing an opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia admitted that imposing "a condition regarding granting permission to use land" is not a "taking" according to the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for public use "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting people's visual access to the beach" and "thus is unlawful, and compensation must take place." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard: Because there is no connection between the condition and the root cause of the construction restriction, the new goal transforms into something else, dissimilar from what it was before. Now the main task for the government is to obtain a general right to land use without paying any compensation.... if the condition provided in the nature management permit is not considered as a building ban and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the construction restriction will not be a lawful land use rule, but rather "a real extortion." This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall stated a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's demand to revise the view of members of the legislative body. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration.... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure adopted can achieve a public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

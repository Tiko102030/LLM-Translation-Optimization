 The property of the British was even less respected: debts to English creditors were summarily erased; some states, including Virginia, closed the cases assigned for hearing regarding claims filed by British subjects (see Elie 1998, p. 35). Widespread confiscation of the property of opponents of independence from England did not bode well for the security of property in the new republic. The major rebels struggled to curb what they saw as "an excess of democracy" (see Tomlins 1993, p. 60). Ensuring the security of property, the "defender of any right" began to concern the creators of the first constitutions of all the states (see Elie 1998, p.).

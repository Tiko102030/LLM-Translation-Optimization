 In the precedent case of Jacobs (1885), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in leased premises because it "prevented the owner from making a profit and freely using his property," and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of individual freedom and property rights protection, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in the broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual slavery... but also a person's right to use their abilities in all legal ways in order to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or constrain these rights... are violations of his fundamental freedom rights. The legislative assembly argued that the ruling in this case was based on state police powers, and the measures taken were necessary in order to "improve the country's healthcare system." For advocates of government regulation, it did not bode well when the court began to consider this requirement, subsequently concluding that it lacked the required amount of evidence. It was determined that the law did not "actually address health issues at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the guise of police instructions, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily infringed upon." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of a time of unprecedented judicial activity in defense of property rights. For example, in Godcharles v. Weijman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal tender money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law violated the equal rights of employer and employee, moreover, it is an insulting attempt to subject an unskilled worker to legislative care," because it prevents workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

The most remarkable was the court decision of 1934. In the case "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," the Supreme Court approved government actions regarding regulation of property in connection with the prevailing economic instability in the country. The court upheld a Minnesota state law; this law imposed a moratorium on foreclosing on mortgaged property under conditions of an unfavorable economic situation established by legislative order. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the article of the Constitution limiting government regulation should "not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, but it still remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information." He added that the court must interpret this article only "in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its residents." "It is necessary to assess the effectiveness of the legislative body not by whether it has a direct or indirect influence on contractual terms, but by whether the decision made is legitimate, and whether it was achieved in reasonable and appropriate methods." This indicates a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, has been transformed into a constitutional principle. Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that: The economic situation in the country may result from how the temporary restriction on enforcement of contractual terms will be reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this article of the agreement is not an unconditional and completely unlawful limitation of the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative legality.... At the same time, whether the legislative body will pursue reasonable or unreasonable policies, it will no longer be the court's concern.

 The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, serving as another tool for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the background of increasingly growing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused a greater explosion of feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent legal scholar Christopher Tiedemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of God's anointed one did not allow limiting police powers. The spread of laissez-faire policy reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by the government." But now:
government intervention in human rights is declared вне закона, and it is now needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant throughout the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to establish what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous army of discontented and their obvious power in that they, especially with the development of universal suffrage, can forcefully implement their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, conservatives feared even more that there might appear such an autocracy that is more despotic and more unreasonable than any that existed before. In the late XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of the "numerous army of discontented," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

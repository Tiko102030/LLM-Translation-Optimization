 For the first time, it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream filed a claim for damages. A dam located upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: defendants had the same right regarding their territory... [the principle "use your own so as not to harm another"] should be limited within reasonable limits in order not to deprive a person of the pleasure of using his property simply because of some stupid inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for some inconveniences of this nature, so that a person who could first build a dam or factory on any public or navigable river eventually acquires an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether he owns the adjacent shores or not.... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of the part of the profit it could have gained as a result of competition. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents being prohibited from restricting the actions of entrepreneurs. But at the same time, eliminating the existing monopoly power and freeing entrepreneurs from legal proceedings regarding indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" did not depend in any way on J. Locke's ideas about sacred private property. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

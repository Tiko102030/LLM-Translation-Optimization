 The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that a "ruling depriving an owner of using his property" is necessary in order to "give him the right to compensation for damage." The court decision on the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles but merely confirmed the main provisions of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case of the same year, "Nollan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different case because it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, owning a coastal plot of land located between public parks to the north and south of each other, the Nollans wanted to build a new three-bedroom house in place of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in the area. The California Coastal Commission granted the necessary permission only on the condition that the Nollans would agree to grant general public access rights to the territory between the tide line and the dam located behind their house. Thus, people will be able to easily reach the parks located both to the north and south by leisurely walking along the sand. The committee explained its requirement by saying that a larger house would contribute to "development of the area," that "people would no longer subconsciously realize that there is a coast nearby and that they have every right to spend time there" (see Eagle 1996, p. 255).

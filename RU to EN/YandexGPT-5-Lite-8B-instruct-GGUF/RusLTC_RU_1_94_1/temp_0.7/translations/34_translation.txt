 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "numerous army of dissatisfied people", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tideman, they demanded to limit the full extent of state power by establishing boundaries for police powers. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the outbreak of the Civil War, in the case of "Weinhumer v. People" (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who sold this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for изъятие собственности did not take place in accordance with the law. For the first time discovering that the concept of "procedure prescribed by law" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislature cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance."

Writing for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the presentation of "conditions regarding the granting of land use permits" does not constitute an "eminent domain taking" according to the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for public land use "does not serve the supposed purpose of protecting people's visual access to the beach" and "thus is unlawful, and compensation must take place." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard: due to the lack of connection between the condition and the root cause of the construction restriction, the new goal transforms into something else, unlike what it was before. Now the main task for the government is to obtain a general right to land use without paying any compensation.... if the condition provided in the nature management permit is not considered as a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a lawful land use rule, but rather "a real extortion." This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall expressed dissenting opinions. They rejected Scalia's demand to revise the opinion of legislative members. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration.... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure adopted can achieve a public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional limitations on government regulation.

Local authorities regularly confiscated houses where immoral activities took place, засыпали the wastewater of factories that "endangered people's health", banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, and did not pay any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of sectors of the economy, including those related to healthcare, such as the production of medicines and slaughter, and others that were economically significant and did not relate to health issues, such as the right to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve a social goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the fertilizer plant owned by "Manhattan Fertilizing" and proceeded to "reduce" the harm caused by the factory's activities, destroying the machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

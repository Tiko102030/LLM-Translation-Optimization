Although the earth and all lower creatures belong collectively to all people, nevertheless ... the labor of his body and the work of his hands in the strictest account belong to him. Whatever a person then extracts from that state in which nature has created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and attaches to it something belonging personally to him and thereby makes it his property... After all, since this labor is the undeniable property of the worker, no one but him can have the right to what he once attached to it, at least in cases where a sufficient quantity and the same quality remain for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it means to use someone's labor, i.e., to turn a person into a slave. It follows from this that the rights of ownership owe nothing to society; however, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

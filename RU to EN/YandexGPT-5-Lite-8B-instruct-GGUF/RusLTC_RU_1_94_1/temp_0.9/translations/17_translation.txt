The US Supreme Court also pointed out that the right to forcibly отчуждать private property is based on public benefit and instrumental considerations, and in the case of Kohl v. United States (1875), it ruled that the right to forcibly alienate private property is "the result of political necessity, and it cannot be separated from sovereign power." The right to forcibly alienate private property was one example of the use of governmental power to promote the development of a "well-organized society," to increase the quality of "public good" or "welfare" through rulings pertaining to interference with the rights of property owners. These rulings reflected common law principles such as: "the welfare of the people shall be the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Frequently cited in court rulings, these main principles serve as "common law programs for governance in a well-regulated society," providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property in order to reflect the mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

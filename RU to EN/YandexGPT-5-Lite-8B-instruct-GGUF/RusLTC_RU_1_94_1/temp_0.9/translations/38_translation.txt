 The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increasing pressure that the regulatory framework would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissent is more memorable than Justice Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that fundamental rights to free labor were violated in Louisiana. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on J. Locke's precepts that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "an essential part of freedom... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to understand the court decision in the Slaughter-House and Mann case before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not receive an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in Louisiana, which was essentially a violation of freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn a living in any legal way; to pursue his favorite profession and, for this purpose, to enter into all transactions that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals."

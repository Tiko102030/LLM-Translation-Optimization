 In the precedent case of Jacobs (1885), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in rented premises because it "prevented the owner from making a profit and using his property freely," and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in the broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual slavery... but also a person's right to use their abilities in all legal ways in order to live and work.... Therefore, any laws that weaken or restrain these rights... violate his fundamental rights to freedom. The State Assembly argued that the ruling in this case was based on the police powers of the state, and the measures taken were necessary in order to "improve the healthcare system in the country." For defenders of government regulation, it did not bode well when the court began to consider this requirement, later concluding that it lacked the required amount of evidence. It was established in conclusion that the law actually "did not touch on health issues at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the guise of police instructions, one cannot arbitrarily intrude into personal rights and private property." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of an unprecedented period of judicial activity in protecting property rights. For example, in Godcharles v. Wyman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law violates the equal rights of employer and employee, moreover, it is an offensive attempt to subject unskilled labor to legislative custody" because it prevents workers from selling their labor on any terms they were willing to accept.

 A similar situation occurred in the Jacobs case trial, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus V. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn a living in any legal way; to seek his favorite profession and for this purpose to enter into all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals." After the Allgeyer court decision, a peak of judicial activity in setting out motives was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared New York law unconstitutional because, according to this law, bakers' working hours were reduced. Speaking in court, Justice Peckham stated emphatically that "the law obligatorily affects the right to conclude a contract between employer and employee," which is a violation of "the general right to enter into a transaction that relates to the activities of his firm," which in turn "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution." Recognizing the fact that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary reason to interfere with human freedom or his right to negotiate a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham spoke about how it was just a matter of which evil is less: the state's power to legislate or an individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limit of police powers has been achieved and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and Lochner's New Deal could logically be defined based on other court decisions that asserted property rights.

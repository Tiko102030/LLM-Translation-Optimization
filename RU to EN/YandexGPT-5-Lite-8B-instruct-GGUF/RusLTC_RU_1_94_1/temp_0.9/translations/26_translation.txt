 The use your own so as not to harm another's... Application of this principle provides access to the consideration of social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognition of limitations on property rights, because, in his words, actions taken by one owner affect others even though they take place on their own property territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where the property interests of different people collide and where both parties complain about a neighbor's interference with their territory (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Issues related to the subject of ownership were revised in the XIX century in order to promote the development of entrepreneurship, even at the expense of harming other people's property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" was preserved, but now it was used to protect the right of entrepreneurs to act according to the requirements of passive neighbors who want their property to remain untouched. Now the concept of "damage" was seen not as a series of unlawful actions against someone else's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of New York State (1805).

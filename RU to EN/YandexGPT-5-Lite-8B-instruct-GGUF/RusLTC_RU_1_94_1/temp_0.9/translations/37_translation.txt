Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the Supreme Court of the United States, where they were represented by Field's predecessor, John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant secretary of war (see Foner 1988, p. 530). While serving on the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell had ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used basic labor law principles to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property rights. Citing the fight against feudalism, Campbell argued that the ordinances from New Orleans took us back to a time "when the meddling eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The New Orleans butchers lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the justices, and lawyers therefore feared increased pressure that regulatory measures would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed.

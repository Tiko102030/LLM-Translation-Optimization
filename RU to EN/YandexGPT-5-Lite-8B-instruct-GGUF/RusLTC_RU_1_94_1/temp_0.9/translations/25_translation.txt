 The New York court used the instrumental approach used in the case of Callender v. Marsh of Massachusetts to dismiss Queckenbush's claims. The court argued: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect more or less the comfort and property of people; and in a case where preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself must be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In the end, the court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the common good must prevail over private interests." State support for private entrepreneurship.

Without prohibiting the government from improving the state at the expense of adjacent property owners, case law, as in Lancing v. Callender, shifted the responsibility for covering costs associated with achieving public goals onto some people (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became special cases. For example, in Thorpe v. Rutland and Burlington Railroad Company (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield upheld a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "protecting the public order of the state implies protecting the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and tranquility of all people, and protecting all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (Latin sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Applying this principle ensures access to consideration of the social interpretation of property.

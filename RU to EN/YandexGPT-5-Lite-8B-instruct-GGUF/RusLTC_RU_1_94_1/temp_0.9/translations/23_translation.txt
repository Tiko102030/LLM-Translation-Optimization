In relation to this case, as well as regarding all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public goals, were immediately abolished as soon as they interfered with the fulfillment of these goals. At the beginning of the 19th century, government authorities, seeking to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads, railways, and canals. The construction of state infrastructure had to bear indirect costs, expenses associated with damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of underdeveloped property. Under the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would be responsible for indirect costs that significantly increased the cost of financing inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding liability for independent entrepreneurs' consequences regarding neighboring property, the costs of building state infrastructure would have drastically decreased. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Callender v. Marsh", setting a precedent for Massachusetts. Callender filed a claim for compensation for damage caused to him during street repairs, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston.

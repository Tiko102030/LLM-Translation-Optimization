 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "large army of dissatisfied people", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Citing Tideman often, they demanded to limit the full power of the state by establishing the boundaries of police authority. State courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the outbreak of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhummer v. People" (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that banned the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who sold this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for seizing property did not take place in accordance with the legal procedure. For the first time discovering that the concept of "legal procedure" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative branch cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance."

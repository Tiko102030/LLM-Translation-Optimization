McCarter" (1908), referred to as "restrictions applicable to property for the benefit of the whole society". The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that "By public policy and economy I mean the proper regulation and internal constitution of government, whereby those who live under it, like members of a well-regulated family, are obliged to observe rules of decency and good manners, assist their neighbors, and be honest, industrious, and orderly in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, pp. 4, 162). In the case "Vanderbilt v. Adams" in 1827, Judge Woodworth of New York described police powers in more detail, saying: "The supreme power in society can and must prescribe how private property rights may be used.... Police powers are based on implied rights and the duty of the highest authority to protect everyone through established law regulations so that the welfare of the whole society is increased.... Such state power should exist in every well-regulated society." In the case "Commonwealth v. Algar" (1851), Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court upheld the legislative authority dealing with property regulation in Boston Harbor and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this wharf, and spoke about police powers as follows: We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every owner of property, regardless of whether he possesses exclusive rights to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility so that its use does not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their property or the whole society as a whole.

 This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as restrictions on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these arguments, including the well-known American with radical views, Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret Locke's popularly held conclusions. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, admits that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for protecting property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the standpoint of relations between people and objects, it can act as a socially neutral party: accumulating the results of one's own labor activity does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. But recognizing the social parameters of property also allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person can harm others, for example, in the case of monopolization of resources previously accessible to all. Just like social relations, the theory of property becomes the subject of всевозможное disputes (see Fried 1998).

 The concepts of property, instrumentalism and Lochner's New Deal could be logically defined based on other court decisions that asserted the rights of property owners. In the case of "Adkins v. Oliver Wendell Holmes and Pennsylvania Coal
Shortly before considering the Adkins case, the Supreme Court made another historic decision that seemed to completely relate to protecting property rights. In the case of "Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Mahon" (1922), the Court declared a Pennsylvania law unconstitutional because this law limited the activities of coal mining companies in order to preserve the subsoil. The Court ruled that it would be generally accepted that regulation of property can only be carried out to a certain extent, and if it goes too far, then the taking of property will take place.... It remains unclear why an individual's failures or needs would justify shifting responsibility for harm onto someone else.... and the strong desire of society to improve its situation would not be enough to guarantee that this goal can be achieved by a shorter, than constitutional way to buy off charges.... it is only a matter of degree. Mentioned more than a hundred thousand times over the next seventy-five years, the Pennsylvania Coal case is still in effect. It contributed to the emergence of a new area of law on takings.

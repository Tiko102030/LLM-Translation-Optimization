 The use thy own so as not to injure another's... The application of this principle provides access to the consideration of social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, he said, actions taken by one owner affect others even though they take place on their own property territory. However, the principle "use your own in such a way as not to harm another" is only a norm, not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where the property interests of different people collide and where both parties complain about their neighbor's interference with their territory (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Issues related to property were revised in the XIX century in order to promote the development of entrepreneurship, even at the expense of harming other people's property. Thus, the principle "use your own in such a way as not to harm another" remained, but now it was used to protect the right of entrepreneurs to act according to the demands of passive neighbors who want their property left untouched. Now the concept of "damage" was seen not as a series of illegal actions against someone else's property, but as an inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by the use of their property. It first became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of the State of New York (1805).

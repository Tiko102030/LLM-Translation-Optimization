 After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to launch a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the majority opinion in the legislature about natural rights and John Locke's beliefs, he accused them of copying Spencer's "Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that rather signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than protecting the sacred notion of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, a different instrumental vision, or the selection of a different social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal rationale used to protect property rights. In his book "The Common Law" (1881), he spoke at length about how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the prerequisites for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even seek to compensate an individual for all the harm caused to him... Anyone can found any firm, and later, in order to survive in competition, they will poach their competitor's clients, and perhaps thereby even ruin them."

 The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the case «Callender v. Marsh» of Massachusetts to reject Queckenbush's claims. The court stated: «Any significant public improvement must necessarily more or less affect the comfort and property of people; and in a case where the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself must be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement». In the end, the court concluded: «this case is based on the principle that the common good should prevail over private interests». Government support for private enterprise.

Without prohibiting the government from carrying out state improvement at the expense of the owners of adjacent property, precedent law, as in the situation «Lancing v. Callender», shifted the responsibility for covering the costs associated with achieving public goals to some people (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became particular cases. For example, in the case «Thorpe v. Rutland & Barlinghton Railway Company» (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because «the protection of state order in the state implies the protection of lives, limbs, health, comfort and tranquility of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas).» The application of this principle provides access to consideration of social interpretation of property.

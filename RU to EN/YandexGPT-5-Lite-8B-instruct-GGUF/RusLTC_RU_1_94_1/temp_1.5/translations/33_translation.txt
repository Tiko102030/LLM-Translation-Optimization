 The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, serving as another instrument for achieving social goals, and no constitutional guarantee was required any longer. Against the background of increasing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and aroused greater feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that occurred on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent legal scholar Christopher Tiedemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings did not allow limiting the police's powers. The spread of laissez-faire policies reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by the government." But now:
government intervention in human rights is declared вне закона, and it is now needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant throughout the civilized world. The state is призвана to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to establish what wage a worker should receive for their labor, and how many hours they should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the large army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power in that they, especially with the development of universal suffrage, can forcefully implement their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, conservatives were even more afraid that there could appear such an autocracy that is more despotic and more unreasonable than any that has existed before. At the end of the 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of the "large army of dissatisfied," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

 Representative Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judiciary supported the legislative power dealing with property regulation issues in Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this wharf, and spoke about police powers as follows:
We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, whether or not he has exclusive right to it, manages it with all implied responsibility so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their own property or society as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules necessary for the sake of achieving the common good and universal welfare can be applied to it. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are also subject to appropriate restrictions necessary to ensure that others have not suffered from using this property, and subject to established legal rules, since the legislative power... can assume that this is a necessary and expedient decision. Shaw added in conclusion: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and its sources of power than it is to establish boundaries or limits for its manifestation." Indeed, when using universal, typified phrases such as "public interest", "internal structure of the state", "implied right and duty of higher authority to protect everyone" and "common good and general welfare," Shaw established that police authorities could have a wide range of instruments at their disposal in achieving their goals. Southern state governments resorted to the assistance of the police in order to introduce laws limiting the use of slaves' property, for example, they prohibited slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after abolition they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, for instance, introduced a policy of rigid racial segregation that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activities in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of the property and labor of African Americans once again demonstrates what the understanding of property was at that time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955).

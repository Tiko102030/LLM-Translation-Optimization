 Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to the property of neighbours. On the other hand, it can support affected neighbours and limit the activities of that owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the actual income of neighbours by the amount of loss of clean air and water or the cost of payment that the enterprise would need to make so that it no longer discharges waste into the environment. Factories that actively pollute the environment would be much poorer if they did not have privileges, because they would have to reduce pollution levels, or they would have to buy permission to pollute from their neighbours. Some doubt that property rights have a significant impact on income distribution, while others argue in favour of its effectiveness. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same effective social result, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment buy the right to do so from their neighbours, or should neighbours pay those who pollute the environment to stop doing so? Environmental pollution will take place where the cost of reducing its negative effects is greater than the value of clean air for neighbours, and it will be stopped when the costs of reducing the negative effect are less. A free trade market will lead to the same effective result regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

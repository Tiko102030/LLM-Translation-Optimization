 The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increasing pressure over the expansion of the regulatory framework and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion is more memorable than Justice Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that basic rights to free labor had been violated in Louisiana. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on J. Locke's prescriptions that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "an essential part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." It took Field twenty years to sort out the court's decision in the Slaughter-House and Mann case before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not get an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in Louisiana, which essentially violated the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacobs case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that a person's liberty included not only the absence of "his physical constraint," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn his living in any legal way; to seek his beloved profession and for this purpose to conclude all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals."

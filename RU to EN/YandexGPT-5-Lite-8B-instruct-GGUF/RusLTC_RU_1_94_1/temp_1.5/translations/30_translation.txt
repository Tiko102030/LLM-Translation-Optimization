 The iron and steel industries, for whose products preferential tariffs were established, were the only ones that could support the developing railway network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was the recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benezel 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the real goals of the bankers and owners of ironworking and railroad companies, whose ownership was sponsored by government authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority in property regulation matters. They were remarkably successful in achieving a law on an eight-hour working day and a law establishing railway tariffs. There were objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result these bills were referred to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The decision of the Supreme Court in 1877 in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, who were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of popular radicalism at that time. Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing transactions, the law violated a constitutional clause on transactions, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference.

 The plaintiffs argued that by abolishing existing transactions, the law violated the Constitution's provision on deals, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect the rights of citizens from state interference. Recently issued in order to protect the civil rights of slaves freed in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment now became an inherent civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property that protected the rights of private ownership from state intervention. However, this opinion was supported by a minority of members of the Court in 1877. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Saying that "powers are inherent to any sovereign authority", the Court ruled that "the government can regulate both the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England in stating that when property "affects the interests of society, it legally ceases to be subject to private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme right" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when an owner uses his property in such a way that the public has an interest in it, he actually wants this himself, and until the interest disappears, he must simply accept that society now manages his property because it is necessary to increase public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should just stop using it. Considering in more detail the boundaries of social power in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on state regulation.

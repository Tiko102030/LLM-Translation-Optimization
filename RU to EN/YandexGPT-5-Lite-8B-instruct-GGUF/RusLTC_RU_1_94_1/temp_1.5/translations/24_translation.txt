 Callender filed a lawsuit for compensation for damage caused to him during the street repair, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited quotations from English laws, common law precedents, and this principle in order to achieve compensation. But the defense side rejected the use of the English precedent with an instrumental presentation of the case. She explained that due to the circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a lawsuit" as filed by Callender "is satisfied, then it will be necessary to check the correctness of repairing all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Callender's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts court accepted the state's proposal for a market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damage compensation, which was undoubtedly bad news for people whose property was directly adjacent to public constructions. After all, in this case, the amount of compensation would double compared to the original amount of expenses. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case of "Lansing against Smith Walter Quackenbush," a lawsuit was filed against the state of New York for damage due to docks falling in price after building a new bay in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach used in the case of "Callender vs. Marsh" in Massachusetts in order to dismiss Queckenbush's claims.

 In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, pp. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894, "Privilege, Criminal Intent, and Design," Holmes based himself on the aforementioned idea of the harm caused by competition. By doing so, he wanted to show the general need for an instrumental approach and the "principle of balance of interests," especially in conditions where judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At a Supreme Court session in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Milling Company v. United States". "In modern society," he declared, "each part is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, then the others will more or less feel it. Therefore, until everything is banned and the end of legislative power comes, it will not be enough just to show that there has been some progress in the development of the law, i.e., when it will already be possible to interfere with trading or existing transactions."

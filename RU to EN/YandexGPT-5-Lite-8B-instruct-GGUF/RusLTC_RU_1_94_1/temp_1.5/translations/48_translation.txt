 However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote of Justice Stone, according to which the majority of Court members divided the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of presuming the constitutionality of the law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, напротив, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and the backlash of the "takings" procedure After the decision in the Carolyn Products case, the Supreme Court allowed democratically elected members of the legislative body wide powers to choose social policy options, even though they restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued in this context. The Just v. Marinneth County judicial case of 1972 in the state of Wisconsin наглядно illustrates how the judiciary easily applied that provision in the Carolyn Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just sued to cancel regulations preventing them from occupying the wetlands intended for them to build houses. Rejecting the claims of the Just family, the Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate use of police powers. Later it wanted to approve an even broader list of restrictions on property rights.

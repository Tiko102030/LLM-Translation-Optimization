 In the landmark case of Jacobs (1885), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in rented premises because it "interfered with the owner's ability to profit and freely use his property", thus "depriving him of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of individual freedom and property rights protection, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in the broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual slavery... but also the human right to use their abilities in all legal ways in order to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or restrain these rights... are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The State Assembly argued that the ruling in this case was based on the state police powers, and the measures taken were necessary in order to "improve the country's healthcare system." For defenders of government regulation, it did not bode well that the court began to consider this requirement, subsequently concluding that it lacked the necessary amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law actually "did not deal with health issues at all," and Judge Earl notified the legislature that "arbitrarily encroach on personal rights and private property under the pretext of police instructions." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of an unprecedented period of judicial activity in protecting property rights. For example, in Godcharles v. Wajgman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (rather than company stock).

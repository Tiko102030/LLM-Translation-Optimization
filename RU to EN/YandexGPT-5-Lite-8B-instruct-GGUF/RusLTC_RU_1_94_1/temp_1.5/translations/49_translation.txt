According to the court ruling: "The owner of the land has no absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of other people and could not be achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that zonal environmental protection rules "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no " изъятие" of собственности, as there was in the case of Pennsylvania Coal. This was due to the fact that "the use of property without changing its natural composition is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding " takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the court made a number of decisions already in 1987. For example, in the case "First American Lutheran Evangelical Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the district flood control organization banned the restoration of the flood-damaged church tent camp. The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using his property" is necessary in order "to give him the right to compensation for damage."

 In the precedent case of Jacobs (1885), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in rented premises because it "prevented the owner from making a profit and freely using his property," and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and the protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in the broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual slavery... but also a person's right to use their abilities in all legal ways in order to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or restrain these rights... violate his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislature argued that the ruling in this case was based on the state police powers, and the measures taken were necessary in order to "improve the health system in the country." For defenders of government regulation, it did not bode well when the court began to consider this requirement, subsequently concluding that it lacked the required amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law actually "did not concern health issues at all," and Judge Earl notified the legislature that "under the pretext of police instructions, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily infringed upon." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of a time of unprecedented judicial activity in defense of property rights. For example, in Godchaux v. Wyman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal tender money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law is a violation of equal employer-employee rights, moreover, it is an offensive attempt to subject unskilled workers to legislative guardianship" because it prevents workers from selling their labor under any terms they were willing to accept.

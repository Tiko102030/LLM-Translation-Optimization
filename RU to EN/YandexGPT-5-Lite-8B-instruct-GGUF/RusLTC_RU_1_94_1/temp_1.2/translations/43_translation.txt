 In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm competition brings is permissible in accordance with the principles of "a policy that has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Criminal Intent, and Design," Holmes relied on the aforementioned notion of the harm competition brings. By doing so, he sought to demonstrate the general necessity for an instrumental approach and a "balance of interests" principle, especially in situations where judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At the Supreme Court session in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Mill vs. United States Glucose." "In modern society," he stated, "each part is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, then the rest will more or less feel it. Therefore, until everything is forbidden and the end of legislative power comes, it will not be enough just to show that there has been some progress in the development of the law, i.e., when it will already be possible to interfere with trade or existing transactions."

Although the earth and all lower creatures are jointly owned by all people, yet ... his body's labour and the work of his hands belong to him by the most exact reckoning. Whatever a person then extracts from that condition in which nature has created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and attaches to it something belonging personally to him and thereby makes it his own property... After all, since this labor is the undeniable property of the laborer, no one but him can have the right to what he once attached to it, at least in cases where a sufficient quantity and the same quality remain for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labour. Property is an important natural right; to use it means to use someone's labor, i.e., to turn a person into a slave. It follows that property rights owe nothing to society; however, proprietors form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

The most notable was the court decision of 1934. In the case "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," the Supreme Court, due to the prevailing economic instability in the country, approved the actions of the state regarding regulation of property. The Court upheld a Minnesota statute; this law imposed a moratorium on foreclosing mortgaged property in conditions of unfavorable economic situation established by legislative order. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the article of the Constitution limiting government regulation should "not be considered with literal accuracy as any mathematical formula, but it still remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information." He added that the Court should interpret this article only "in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its residents." "To assess the effectiveness of a legislative body's activities, it is necessary not by the direct or indirect impact it has on contractual terms, but by whether the decision made is legal and whether it was achieved in reasonable and appropriate methods." This speaks to the social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that: The economic situation in the country can be the result of how temporary restrictions on the enforcement of contractual terms are reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the agreement is not an unconditional and completely illegitimate restriction of the state's protectionist policy, the legislature must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative legitimacy.... At the same time, what reasonable or unreasonable policy the legislative body will pursue, it will no longer be the Court's concern.

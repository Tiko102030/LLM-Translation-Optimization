 The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, serving as another instrument for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the background of growing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused greater emotion among those who feared that the expanded powers of the police would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent legal scholar Christopher Tiedemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of God's anointed one did not allow limiting the powers of the police. The spread of laissez-faire policies reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by the government." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government intervention in human rights is outlawed, and now it is needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled throughout the civilized world. The state is призвано to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to establish what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous army of discontented and their obvious power in that they can forcefully implement their ideas about a civic state in the civilized world, especially under the development of universal suffrage, conservatives feared even more that there might appear such an autocracy that is more despotic and unwise than any that existed before. In the late nineteenth century, speaking out against the opinion of the "numerous army of discontented," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

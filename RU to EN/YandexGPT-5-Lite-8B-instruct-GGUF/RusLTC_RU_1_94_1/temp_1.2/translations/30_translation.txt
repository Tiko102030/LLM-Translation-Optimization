 The ironworking and steel industries, on whose products preferential tariffs were set, could only provide for the developing network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the real goals of bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose property had been sponsored by state authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand the powers of the public in regulating ownership. They achieved striking success in securing a law on an eight-hour workday and a law establishing railroad tariffs. There were objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were submitted for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court decision of 1877 in the case «Mann v. State of Illinois» greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of popular radicalism at that time. Plaintiffs argued that by canceling existing transactions, the law violated the constitutional provision on transactions, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference.

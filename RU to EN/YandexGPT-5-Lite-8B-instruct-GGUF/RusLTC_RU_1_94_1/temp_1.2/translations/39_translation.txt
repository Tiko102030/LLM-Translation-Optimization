 A similar situation occurred in the Jacobs case trial, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn a living in any legal way; to seek his favorite profession and, for this purpose, to enter into all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for successfully achieving the aforementioned goals." After the court's decision in the Allgeyer case, the peak of judicial activity in stating the motivations was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared New York law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers according to this law. Speaking in court, Justice Peckham strongly stated that "the law obligatorily affects the right to enter into a contract between an employer and an employee," which is a violation of "the general right to conclude a deal that relates to the activities of his firm," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution." Recognizing the fact that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary basis for interfering with human freedom or his right to negotiate a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham said that it was just a matter of which evil is less: the power of the state to legislate or the individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to make contracts. We believe that the limit of police powers has been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and Lochner New Deal could be logically determined based on other court decisions that asserted property rights.

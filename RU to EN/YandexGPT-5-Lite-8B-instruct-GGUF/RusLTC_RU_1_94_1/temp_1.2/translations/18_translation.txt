Chancellor Kent of New York declared in 1826 that "every person has so much freedom to acquire, use and dispose of his property as may be commensurate with an acceptable state of property and the reciprocal rights of others... Although thus protected... the legislature has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as far as it may be necessary to prevent abuse of the right that could lead to violation of the rights of another person or society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in Illinois, the legislature lacked three pages to catalog all the powers that the Chicago City Administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transport, and limiting "the act of spinning hoops, playing ball or flying kites... capable of annoying people", the new city had a power that would "force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, tallow candle shop, soap factory, leather tannery, stable, barn, privy, clothing factory or any other ramshackle, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it, or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). The authorities of other cities also assumed broad powers on property issues. Thus, in New York, certain standards were established for transporting goods across the East River, for example, the price for transportation varied from twenty-five cents for fattened buffaloes or bulls to fifty cents per cord of walnut wood, one cent for empty milk bottles, twenty-five cents for children's corpses and fifty cents for adult corpses (see Novak 1996).

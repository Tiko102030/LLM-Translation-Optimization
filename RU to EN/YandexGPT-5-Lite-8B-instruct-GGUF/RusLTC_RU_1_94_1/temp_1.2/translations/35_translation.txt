 In the precedent case of Jacobson (1885), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in rented premises because it "prevented the owner from making a profit and freely using his property," and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in the broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual slavery... but also a person's right to use their abilities in all legal ways in order to live and work... Therefore, any laws that weaken or restrain these rights... are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislative assembly argued that the ruling in this case was based on the state police powers, and the measures taken were necessary in order to "improve the healthcare system in the country." It boded ill for advocates of government regulation that the court began to consider this requirement, and later concluded that it did not have the required amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law actually "did not touch on health issues at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the guise of police instructions, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily invaded." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an unprecedented period of judicial activity to protect property rights. For example, in Godcharles v. Wiegmann (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal tender money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law is a violation of the equal rights of employer and employee, moreover, it is an offensive attempt to subject unskilled workers to legislative care" because it prevents workers from selling their labor on any terms they are willing to accept.

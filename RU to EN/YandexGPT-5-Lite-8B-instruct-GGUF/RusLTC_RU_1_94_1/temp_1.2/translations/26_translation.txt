 The use your own so as not to injure another's... The application of this principle provides access to the consideration of social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his words, actions taken by one owner affect others even though they take place on their property territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to injure another" is only a norm, not a rule; it should not be used in most cases where the property interests of different people clash and both parties complain about a neighbor's encroachment on their territory (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Issues related to the subject of ownership were revised in the XIX century in order to promote the development of entrepreneurship, even at the expense of causing damage to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to injure another" remained, but it was now used to protect the right of entrepreneurs to act in accordance with the demands of passive neighbors who want their property left untouched. Now the concept of "damage" was considered not as a series of illegal actions against someone else's property, but as an inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case of Palmer v. Mulligan in the state of New York (1805).

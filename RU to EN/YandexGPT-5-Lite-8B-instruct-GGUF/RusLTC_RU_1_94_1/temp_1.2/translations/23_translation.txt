In this case, as with all American law, property rights, originally necessary to achieve public goals, were immediately abolished once they hindered the fulfillment of these goals. At the beginning of the 19th century, state authorities, striving to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads, railways, and canals. The construction of state infrastructure entailed indirect costs, expenses associated with damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of underdeveloped property. Under the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would be responsible for indirect costs that significantly increased the cost of financing inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding responsibility for the consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property by independent entrepreneurs, the costs of building state infrastructure would sharply decrease. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Callender v. Marsh", establishing a precedent for the State of Massachusetts. Callender filed a claim for compensation for damage caused to him during street repairs, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston.

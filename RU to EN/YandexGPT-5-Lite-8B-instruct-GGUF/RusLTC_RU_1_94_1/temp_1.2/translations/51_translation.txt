Recording a speech for the court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that presenting "a condition regarding the granting of land use permission" is not an "taking" according to the Fifth Amendment. The general public use requirement "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting people's visual access to the beach" and "thus it is unlawful, and compensation must take place." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard: because there is no connection between the condition and the root cause of the construction restriction, the new goal transforms into something different, dissimilar to what it was before. Now the main task for the government is to obtain a general land use right without paying any compensation.... if the condition provided in the nature management permit is not considered as a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a legitimate land use rule, but only "a real extortion." This time Justices Brennan and Marshall stated a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's demand to revise the views of members of the legislative body. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure taken can achieve a public goal." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

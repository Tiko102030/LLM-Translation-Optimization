 The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increasing pressure that the set of regulatory measures would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion is more memorable than Judge Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and further supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the fundamental rights to free labor had been violated in Louisiana. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on J. Locke's precepts that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "an essential part of freedom... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to understand the decision of the court in the Slaughter-House and Mann case before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the Allgeyer v. Louisiana case, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in Louisiana, which was essentially a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacobson court, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also covered the right "to work where he wants; to earn a living in any lawful way; to seek his beloved profession and for this purpose to enter into all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the above-mentioned goals."

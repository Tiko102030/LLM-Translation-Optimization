 Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:
The economic situation in the country may result from how the temporary limitation on the enforcement of contractual conditions will be aligned with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the reserved right of the state to protect vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the treaty is not an unconditional and completely illegitimate restriction on the protectionist policies of the state, the legislature must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative legality.... At the same time, whether the legislature will pursue reasonable or unreasonable policy, it will no longer be the Court's concern. Back in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon assessment of economic legislation and разбирательство in cases involving property rights, the legislature had already declared the need to "protect vital interests of society". After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court practically fully agreed with his reform program. No other decision has illustrated so clearly the new approach in the деятельности of the Supreme Court as it was done in the case "United States v. Carolyn Products" (1938). In this particular case, the company was sued based on the law "On Skimmed Milk" of 1923, according to which the interstate sale of milk with added fat and butter was prohibited, it was considered as the sale of a "deliberately spoiled food product harmful to health". The attorneys for Carolyn Products argued that the law unlawfully restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court considered the law valid because, in its opinion, it was a fair tool of government regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would be an ordinary, simple confirmation of the established police powers. However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the members of the Court distinguished between property rights and a bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

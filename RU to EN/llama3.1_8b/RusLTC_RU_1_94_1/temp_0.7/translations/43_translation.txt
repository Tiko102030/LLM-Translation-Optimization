In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is allowed according to the principles of "such a policy as has nothing in common with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894 "Privilege, Criminal Intent and Purpose", Holmes based himself on the aforementioned presentation of the harm caused by competition. In doing so, he wanted to show that there is a general need for an instrumental approach and the "principle of balance of interests" especially in situations where judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, p. 130-35). At a hearing before the Supreme Court in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case of "Diamond Glucose Co. v. United States Glucose Co." In modern society, he declared, each of its parts is closely connected with another, and if something affects one of them, the others will feel it to some extent. Therefore, until all is prohibited and the end of legislative power arrives, it is insufficient simply to show that there has been some progress in the development of law, i.e., when intervention can already be made into trade or existing contracts.

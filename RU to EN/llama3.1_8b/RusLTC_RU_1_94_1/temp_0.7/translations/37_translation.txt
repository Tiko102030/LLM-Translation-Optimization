The Supreme Court of Louisiana had enacted a law that established "that the loss of private right in this case is without any significance because of the general profit and trading turnover of this commercial organization" (see Novak 1996, p. 230-31). Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the US Supreme Court, where they were represented by John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant Secretary of War (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell had ruled in Dred Scott that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later he used fundamental tenets of labor law to persuade the court to protect a citizen's property rights. Citing battles against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans ordinances took us back to a time when "the prying eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and lawyers therefore feared growing pressure for an expanded regulatory complex with redistributed income, property, and power.

The First Anglican Church case, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rely on constitutional principles but rather confirmed the basic facts of Pennsylvania Coal. However, the 1987 case "Nollan v California Coastal Commission" was quite another matter, because it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, owning a coastal plot of land situated between public parks to the north and south of each other, Nollans wanted to build a three-bedroom house similar to others in this district instead of their dilapidated bungalow. The California Coastal Commission granted the required permit only on condition that Nollans would agree to allow common right of way over the territory between the high tide mark and the seawall located behind their house. Thus, people could easily reach parks located both north and south of them simply strolling leisurely along the beach. The Commission explained its demand by saying that a larger home would contribute to "territorial development", with people becoming "unconsciously unaware" that they had coast nearby and were entitled to spend time there (see Eagle 1996, p. 255).

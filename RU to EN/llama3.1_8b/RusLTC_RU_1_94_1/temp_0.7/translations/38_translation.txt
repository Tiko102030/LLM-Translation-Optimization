Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges and lawyers feared growing pressure for expanded regulation and redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has lingered in memory more than Miller's majority opinion which was later buttressed by arguments on police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that Louisiana had infringed upon labor rights protected by the Constitution. Citing Adam Smith, he founded his defense of property on John Locke's precepts that free labor is a final source of property and thus property is "the chief part of liberty... in the American sense." It took Justice Field twenty years to comprehend the court's decision in Sloughter-House & Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, the Court ruled the Louisiana statute unconstitutional in Allgeyer v. Louisiana.

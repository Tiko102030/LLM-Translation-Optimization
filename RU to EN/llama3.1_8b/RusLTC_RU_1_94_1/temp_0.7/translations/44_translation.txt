In the case of Pennsylvania Coal Holmes was more of a Trojan Horse than a winner-defender of sacred property rights. Instead of regarding property right as a natural right, Holmes founded the "principle of expropriation" based on establishing equilibrium between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must constantly be used in a "reasonable" way, but what is considered "reasonable" remains vague at this time. If actions go "too far", then in this case damages will need to be paid; however, it is the judges who taking into account public needs and special circumstances will determine what means "too far". Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case judges must carefully weigh and balance competing interests of both parties. Owners should not, in turn, get everyone against them. Holmes claimed that "the government can hardly continue its work if in some way a question of property is not resolved."

This part of Locke's theory of property contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limits on individual accumulation, which later led other authors to join in, including the well-known American radical Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property had a reason to regret the very popular conclusions drawn by Locke in society. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it can also represent a socially neutral aspect: accumulation of results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. However, recognizing social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person may harm others, for example, in cases of resource monopolization that were previously accessible to all.

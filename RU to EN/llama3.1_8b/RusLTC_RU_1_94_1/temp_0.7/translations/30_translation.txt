The iron-processing and steel industries, which had favorable tariffs imposed on their products, were the only ones able to supply a developing network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was recently centralized banking system, founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984 g., 1990 g.). Enraged workers and farmers saw the real goals of bankers and owners of iron-processing and railroad companies, whose property was just being supported by government agencies. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority in questions of regulating property. They achieved a remarkable success in obtaining legislation establishing an eight-hour workday law and a law setting railway tariffs. There were objections from many large entrepreneurs as well, which led to these bills being sent for review to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court was still approving comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court decision in the case "Mann v Illinois" in 1877 strongly disturbed business circles in that state, which were concerned about growing interest in radical ideas popular at the time.

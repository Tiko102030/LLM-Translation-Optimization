Local authorities regularly seized homes in which immoral acts were being carried out, buried the sewers of factories that "posed a threat to human health", banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, without paying any compensation whatsoever. Officials gained access to dozens of industries, including those related to healthcare, such as drug manufacturing and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance but not related to public health, like the right to trade and purchase, sale and production (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation failed to achieve its intended goal, officials began picketing. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the property of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and began "reducing" the harm caused by the factory's activities by destroying machinery and taking with them all necessary parts (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

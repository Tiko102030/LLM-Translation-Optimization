A certain form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke does not defend property rights so resolutely as might be understood from the well-known passage quoted above (see Horn 1990; Ketznelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he addresses the question of whether the Stuart monarchs of England have a right to levy taxes without Parliament's consent. However, apart from this specific issue, Locke recognizes and acknowledges the social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that each person should have as much as they can use.

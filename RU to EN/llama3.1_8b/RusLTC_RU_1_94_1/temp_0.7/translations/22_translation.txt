Cohen 1991; Liechtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955). Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as that owned or used by Europeans in America. After the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, Negroes from southern states gained the right to own property, but their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and the level of participation of African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach used by governments of northern states to restrict ownership rights regarding clean air and water was also used in southern states to maintain dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, ownership rights were defined to achieve public goals, but with different purposes. Redistribution of property ownership for the purpose of stimulating economic growth 

Sponsoring public infrastructure construction through redistributive property ownership, Chief Justice of the United States Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to instrumental approach which he used in Court to annul contract rights of Charles River-Bridge company. He did this in order to personally manage the only bridge over the Charles River connecting Boston and Cambridge (see case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of Charles River Bridge company experienced some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... invested in building railroads and canals on high-speed lines... We will be pushed back a century to inventions of that age and we will be forced to remain stationary".

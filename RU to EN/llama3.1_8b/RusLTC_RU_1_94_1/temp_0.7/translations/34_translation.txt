At the end of the 19th century, advocating against the "numerous army of disgruntled" opinion, lawyers and judges aimed to consecrate private property. Frequently citing Thielemann, they demanded that the fullness of state power be limited by establishing a boundary on police authority. The state courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for the sacred approach, rather than an instrumental one, to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Weinhammer v. People" (1856), the Appellate Court of New York State declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law took effect, all those who were selling this liquor at the time lost their property. During this procedure, the process of confiscation did not follow the established legal order. For the first time, finding out that the concept "established legal order" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative authority cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thus subject property to complete disappearance."

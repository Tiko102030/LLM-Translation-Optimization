The purpose of the public organization is that property becomes its own goal. Such views were spread in the American republic, and Locke's reasoning continued to be cited by end-19th century Supreme Court justices who opposed public regulation of property rights (see Eli 1998). Others reason about ownership from an instrumentalist position. For them, property rights must be protected, i.e., these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it its own goal, an unconditional right that surpasses all others. Instead, the problem of property has now become primary, and the instrumental approach undermines protection of all property rights.

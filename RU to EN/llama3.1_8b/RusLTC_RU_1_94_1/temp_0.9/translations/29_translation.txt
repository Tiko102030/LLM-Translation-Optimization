Civil war served as the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full authority of the national government which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers wanted more. For example, the defender of workers' rights, George McNeill, warned that America should "inject republican principles into our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F. Brace: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes they are now trying to impose on us by trust companies." The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, but it is precisely workers who today are nothing other than slaves of combined trust companies" (see Fonner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, civil war also contributed to workers uniting and resisting these new demands. Large sums of money were required for military action, as well as the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus, which once again confirmed that a significant financial and industrial capital was needed to support government activity. The iron and steel industries, on whose products preferential tariffs were set, were the only ones who could provide the developing network of railways.

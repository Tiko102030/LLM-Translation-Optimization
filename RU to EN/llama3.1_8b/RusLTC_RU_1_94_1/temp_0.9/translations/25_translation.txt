The New York Court used an instrumental approach employed in the case "Calder v. Jones" of Massachusetts to dismiss Quakenbush's demands. The court held: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily, more or less, affect people's comfort and property; and when the preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation for damage will be recognized, but the damage amount itself should be considered as part of the price paid for future benefits of this public improvement." The court concluded at the end: "This case is based on the principle that the general welfare must prevail over private interests."

Government support for private entrepreneurship
Without prohibiting the government from carrying out state improvements at the expense of adjacent property owners, a judicial precedent, as it was in the situation "Lansing v. Calder," shifted some people's obligation to cover expenses related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). In subsequent cases such instrumental norms became private instances. For example, in the case "Torp v. Ranelagh and Burlington Railway Company" (1855) the Chief Justice of Vermont State Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of lives, limbs, health, comfort, and tranquility of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas)... The application of this principle provides access to a social interpretation of property.

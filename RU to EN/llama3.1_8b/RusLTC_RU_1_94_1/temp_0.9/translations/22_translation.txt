Property belonging to or used by African Americans did not have the same level of inviolability as property belonging to or used by Americans of European origin. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from southern states received the right to own property, however their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and the participation of African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach used by northern state governments to limit property rights with regard to clean air and water was also used in southern states to maintain the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public goals, but with different purposes. Redistribution of property for stimulating economic growth By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property, Supreme Court of the United States President Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to the help of an instrumental approach used by him in court to annul the contractual rights of Charles River Bridge Company. He did this in order to personally manage the only bridge over the Charles River, which connects Boston and Cambridge (see case "Charles River Bridge v Warren Bridge"). The defense side of Charles River Bridge Company experienced some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... invested in the construction of railways and canals on high-speed lines... We will be pushed back a century to inventions of that age and will have to remain immobile".

A similar situation occurred in the Jacobson v. Massachusetts case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right to "work wherever he chooses; earn his living by any lawful means; pursue the calling he may prefer; and with this end in view to contract and agree with others to associate himself with them under a common label and symbology, as a co-partner." Following the Allgeyer v. Louisiana decision, there was an eight-year hiatus before the court established precedent for expressing motives in Lochner v. New York (1905). The Supreme Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. In this case, Justice Peckham resolutely stated that "the statute necessarily interferes with the right to make contracts by imposing an obligation on both the employer and the employee," violating the "general right to enter into contracts which relate to his business" protected by the 14th Amendment of the U.S. Federal Constitution. Acknowledging that police actions regulating property are justified as protecting "health or moral welfare," Peckham argued that in this case, there is no necessary reason for interfering with human freedom or the right to enter into a free contract by reducing working hours for bakers. In conclusion, Peckham stated that it was simply a question of which of the two evils was lesser: state power to issue laws or human individual liberty and the right to form contracts. We believe that the limits of police authority were reached and fully considered in this case.

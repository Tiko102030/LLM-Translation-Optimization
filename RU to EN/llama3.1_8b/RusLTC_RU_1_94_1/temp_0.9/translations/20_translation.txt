Similar actions were justified by the concept of "police power" of the state or what Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. called "property restrictions in the interest of the community" when writing for the Supreme Court in the case "Hudson County Water Company v. McCarter" (1908). The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s William Blackstone wrote that by state police and economy he meant the proper regulation and internal arrangement of the state, through which people in the country, as well as members of a well-organized family, are obliged to follow rules of propriety and good breeding, help neighbors and be orderly, industrious and unassuming in their intentions (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case of "Vanderbilt v. Adams" (1827), Judge Woodworth of New York more detailed described police power by saying: "The supreme authority in society may and must prescribe how private rights in property shall be exercised.... Police powers are based on implicit rights and duties of the highest authority to protect all through law-established regulations so as to increase the general welfare of the community.... Such governmental authority should exist in every well-regulated society". In the case "Commonwealth v. Alger" (1851), a Massachusetts Supreme Court representative Lemuel Shaw supported legislative power dealing with property regulation on Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting building structures outside this dock, stating about police powers: We hold that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each owner of property, regardless whether they have or do not have exclusive right to it, manages it with all implied responsibility so that its use should not harm either other people having similar rights to enjoy their properties or the community as a whole.

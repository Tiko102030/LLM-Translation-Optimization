According to the court ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of other people and which could not be achieved by its original composition." The court found that zoning regulations protecting the environment "must be reasonable", but "we believe that preventing a violation of public rights by introducing restrictions on the use of private property in its original purpose is justified." There was no "taking" of property, as there was in the case of Pennsylvania Coal. This was because "using property without changing the natural composition of its soil is allowed by law". Property rights advocates achieved greater success in court cases regarding "takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's counsel Antonin Scalia, the Court had already made a series of decisions as early as 1987. For example, in the case "First English Evangelical Lutheran Church v. County of Los Angeles" (1987), the Court ordered the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the county flood control district prohibited the reconstruction of a church's damaged tent camp following a flood. The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using their property" was necessary in order to "give them the right to compensation for damage".

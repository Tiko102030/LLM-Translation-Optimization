Examining the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society more closely, Wait acknowledged several limitations to state regulation. "You can get compensation for using regulated property," and Wait maintained that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "Any law is assumed to be constitutional," he said. "Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it's clear, of course. If there is a doubt, then the expressed will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." Recognizing that this power could be abused, he encouraged citizens to pursue compensation "in voting booths, not in court". Property rights defenders were shocked by the open reading of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field had different thoughts and said that "the principle on which the majority opinion... undermines property rights." "If it becomes a law," he warned, "then all property and commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of the legislative body's majority members." The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now also an interest of society, another tool for achieving public objectives, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee.

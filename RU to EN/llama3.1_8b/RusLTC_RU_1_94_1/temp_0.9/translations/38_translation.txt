Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but the arguments of Campbell struck a chord with judges and therefore lawyers were concerned about growing pressure for the expansion of regulatory measures and redistribution of income, property and power. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissent has left a stronger impression than Justice Miller's opinion which was supported by a majority vote and later further justified by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that Louisiana had violated basic rights to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property on John Locke's prescriptions that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "the main part of liberty...in the American sense of the word." It took Field twenty years for the Supreme Court to accept his views in the case of Slotterhouse v. Mann before they were accepted by the Court. In 1897 in the case Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain insurance from a company without a license to conduct business in the state of Louisiana, effectively violating freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was before the court in Jacobson, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only "the absence of his physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he will; to earn a living by any legally recognized calling; to seek his livelihood in whatever lawful way he may prefer; and by means necessary to accomplish these purposes, to make such contracts as shall be suitable, proper and necessary for that end."

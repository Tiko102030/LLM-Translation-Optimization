Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully selected words to start a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the majority opinion of lawmakers about natural rights and J. Locke's convictions, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their protection of property as an instrumental choice that would rather mean the end of productivity and prosperity than the protection of a sacred notion of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory or instrumental view or choice of another public goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged existing models of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he elaborated that the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even attempt to compensate individuals for all the harm caused them... Anyone can found any company, and in the future, to survive in competition, they will poach their competitor's clients, and may even bankrupt him by doing so".

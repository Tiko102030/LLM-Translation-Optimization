Instead, the problem of property is now paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines the protection of all property rights. A certain form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke does not protect property rights as firmly as one might understand from the well-known passage cited above (see Horn 1990; Ketznelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage he addresses the question of the right of the English Stuart monarchs to tax without parliamentary consent. But apart from this specific issue, Locke acknowledges and recognizes a social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that everyone should have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be restricted in order to ensure that all can share the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and the same" for others. Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature which thus gives us property just as strictly limits the size of this property.

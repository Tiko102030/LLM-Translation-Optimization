There exists an "indisputable" right to property of the working person because:
Although the earth and all lower beings belong in common to all people, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, counted strictly by the rule, belong to him. Whatever man may derive from the condition in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his own labour and adds something that belongs particularly to himself and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is indisputable property of the working person, no one but he can have a right to what he once joined to it, at least when there is enough of the same quality left for general use by others. Thus Locke consecrates property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone's labour, i.e., turning a man into a slave. From this follows that the rights of ownership should owe nothing to society; nevertheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

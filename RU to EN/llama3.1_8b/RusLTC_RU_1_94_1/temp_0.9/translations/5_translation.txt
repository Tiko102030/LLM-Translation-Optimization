A new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of property, however, it still views "property rights" as a relationship between people and objects (see Barzel 1997; Liebkep 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relationship between people and objects, property exists without human society; for Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Like the relationships between people and the objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic value because it is a reward for the labor that shapes the non-human material world. Labor, as John Locke says, "at first gave the right to property" (see J. Locke 1952, p. 27 [par. 45], etc.

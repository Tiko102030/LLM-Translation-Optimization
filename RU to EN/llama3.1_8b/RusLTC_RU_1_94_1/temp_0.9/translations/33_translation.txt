The Mann decision showed that property rights were now also one of the interests of society, and another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of increasing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and sparked a greater explosion of emotions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After events took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, a prominent jurist, Christopher Tiedeman, (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of God did not allow for limiting police powers. The spread of non-interference policies reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms". But now: 
The political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unconstitutional, and now it is seen as a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant throughout the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, establish what wages workers should receive for their labor, and how many hours they should work daily... 
Considering these unusual demands of a large army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power in that they can, especially in conditions of universal electoral rights development, coercively impose on society their ideas about civil state in a civilized world, conservatives feared even more that such an autocracy could emerge which would be more despotic and less reasonable than any existing before. In the late 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of "a large army of dissatisfied people", lawyers and judges intended to consecrate private property.

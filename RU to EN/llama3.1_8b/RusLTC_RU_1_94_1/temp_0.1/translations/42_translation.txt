Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to start a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the opinion of the majority of lawmakers about natural rights and the beliefs of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that means the end of productivity and prosperity rather than protection of the sacred idea of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental view or the choice of another public goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he detailed that the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even attempt to compensate a person for all the harm caused to him... Anyone can found any company, and later, in order to survive in competition, he will poach his competitor's clients, and may even bankrupt them".

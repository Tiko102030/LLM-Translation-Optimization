Calender filed a lawsuit for damages caused to him during the repair of the street, as a result of which his house's foundation in Boston was damaged. His attorney cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle to seek compensation. But the defense side rejected the use of the English precedent with an instrumental explanation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a lawsuit" filed by Calender "is satisfied, then we will have to check the correctness of repairs for all highways in our country". This argument found resonance with the judges who dismissed Calender's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's proposal for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent lawsuits for damages, which was undoubtedly bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public facilities. "Those who buy property next to a road - the court said - must take into account future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." Because in such a case the compensation amount would double compared to the original cost of expenses. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case "Lansing v. Smith W. Cuykendall", a lawsuit was filed against the state of New York for damages due to the fact that docks fell in price after building a new bay in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach, used in the case "Calender v. Marsh" of the Massachusetts state to dismiss Cuykendall's demands.

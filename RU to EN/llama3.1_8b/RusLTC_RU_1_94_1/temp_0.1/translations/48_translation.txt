However, the most important aspect of this matter was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Judge Stone, according to which the majority of the Court shared the concepts of property rights and sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in economic legislation cases, the Court intended to be guided by the principles of constitutional presumptions of law, while elected members of the legislative body would be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. Stone, on the other hand, warned that there would be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and the countermotion procedure "exemptions"

After the decision in Carolene Products, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body to have broad powers in choosing social policy objects, even though they restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policies conducted in this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case "Justice v. Marinette County" vividly illustrates how judicial power easily applied the Carolene Products provision. Ronald and Catherine L. Justice brought an action regarding the repeal of ordinances that prevented them from occupying designated wetlands for building homes. Rejecting the Justice family's claims, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate exercise of police powers. Later, he wanted to approve an even broader list of restrictions on property rights.

The butchers of New Orleans lost their case by a vote of five to four, but Campbell's arguments struck a chord with the judges, and lawyers were concerned that pressure was building over what additional regulations would be added, income redistributed, property and power re-distributed. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's special dissent is remembered better than Judge Miller's opinion supported by the majority which in turn has been supplemented with arguments on police authority. Like Campbell, Field argued that the state of Louisiana had infringed basic rights to free labor; referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property on John Locke's dictates regarding how, as the ultimate source of property is free labor, so too is property "the fundamental aspect of liberty... in an American sense." It took Judge Field 20 years to grasp the court's ruling on Slote r v. Manchester before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgier vs Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional which prohibited obtaining insurance from companies without commercial licenses to operate in Louisiana thereby breaching the freedoms granted by the Fourteenth Amendment. The situation was also evident in Jacobs's court case; and Justice Ruther V. Peckham demonstrated that a person's freedom included not just their "physical liberty," but encompassed also the right "to work where they wish; earn their livelihood any legal means; seek employment in a desired profession by concluding such appropriate, necessary and reasonable agreements to achieve the mentioned goals."

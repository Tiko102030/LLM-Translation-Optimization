The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that a "judgment depriving an owner of using their property" is necessary in order to "give him a right to recover for damage." The decision of First English Church v. Los Angeles, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles; it simply upheld main provisions of Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from that year, Nolan vs. County of California Coastal Commission (1987), was completely different because it set a new standard with regard to old rules. Thus, owners of a coastal land plot located between public parks to north and south of each other, Nolans, wanted to build in place of their old bungalow a house of three bedrooms similar to others situated in the county. The California Coastal Commission gave necessary permit only if Nolans agreed on allowing easement to common right on territory between the line of high tide and dike located behind their house.

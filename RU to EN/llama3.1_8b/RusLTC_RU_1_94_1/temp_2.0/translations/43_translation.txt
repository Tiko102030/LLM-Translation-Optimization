In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition was tolerated in accordance with principles of "policy which has no relation to morals" (Holmes 1881, pp. 115, 128). In his essay "Privilege, Mens Rea and Intent" from 1894, Holmes based his argument on the concept of harm resulting from competition mentioned earlier. Thus, he wanted to demonstrate the general necessity of an instrumental approach and a principle of balancing interests especially in situations where judges weigh competing rights instead of relying on syllogistic reasoning (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At the Supreme Court hearing in 1903 in the case "Diamond Glucose Company v United States Glucose Company," Holmes applied this approach, stating: "In modern society, - he said, - every one of its parts is closely connected with another and if something affects one part, others will be affected as well to a greater or lesser extent. So, until everything is prohibited and the authority of legislation comes to an end, it will not suffice simply to demonstrate some progress in law development i.e. once it would be possible to interfere in trade or already existing agreements."

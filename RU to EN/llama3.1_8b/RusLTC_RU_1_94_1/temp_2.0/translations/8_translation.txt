Instead, the issue of property rights is now paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines protection for all forms of property ownership. A certain form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but never necessary. However, Locke protects property rights not so resolutely as might be understood from the well-known excerpt quoted above (see Hume 1990; Kateson 1998, p. 77). In this excerpt, he addresses the issue of English Stuart monarchs' right to levy taxes without parliamentary consent. Beyond that question, Locke acknowledges a social definition of property, according to which property rights should be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that each person must have as much as they can use. Accumulation should be limited so that others too can share in nature's bounty, leaving "enough for all to have what the same thing shall afford" and ensuring others are left in their former condition. Citing Scripture, Locke asserts that the same natural law which gives us property equally limits its size.

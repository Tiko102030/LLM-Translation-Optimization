A new approach was not a general criticism of property rights or their affirmation, but rather a redefinition of the distribution of these rights by arguing that some properties are more valuable than others. According to one legal historian, Villard Hallstead, this approach is one of the working principles of US law: "the freedom of all human creative energy". Despite the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect losses remaining on entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to carry out projects that bring profits. In theory, if a project's value exceeds the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of damages. However, in practice, all the burden falls on the injured party, which only contributes to an even greater number of entrepreneurial projects and puts all the risk and operational costs on the neighbors' shoulders. If property rights were observed to a greater legal extent, high operating costs would hinder entrepreneurs' schemes. By saving on the cost of risks and operational expenses, this new principle has contributed to entrepreneurship in the United States even more greatly.

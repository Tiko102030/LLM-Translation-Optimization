According to the court ruling: "The landowner does not have any absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil so that he can use it for purposes that violate the rights of other people and cannot be achieved at its initial composition." The court recognized that zonal environmental protection rules "must be reasonable", but "we believe that preventing infringement on public rights by introducing restrictions on the use of private property in its original purposes is justified." There was no "taking" of property, as occurred in Pennsylvania Coal. This was due to the fact that "use of property without changing the natural composition of its soil is allowed by law." Property rights defenders achieved greater success in the lawsuit regarding "takings" after Ronald Reagan became President in 1980. Led by Attorney General Scalia, appointed by Reagan, The Court had already taken several decisions as early as 1987.

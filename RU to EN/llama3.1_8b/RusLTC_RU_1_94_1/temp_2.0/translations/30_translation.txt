The iron-processing and steel-manufacturing industries, for whose products preferential tariffs were established, alone could ensure the developing network of railroads. At the top of this financial pyramid sat a recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzell 1984 g., 1990 g.). Angered workers and farmers saw the true purposes of the bankers and owners of iron-processing and railway companies, whose property was financed by the state authorities themselves. They wanted new laws that would expand the public's authority over questions of property regulation. They achieved remarkable success in achieving a statute establishing an eight-hour workday and a law setting railroad tariffs.

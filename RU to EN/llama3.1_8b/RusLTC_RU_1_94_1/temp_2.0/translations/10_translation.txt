This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, which were later joined by other authors, including the well-known American radical Thomas Paine. Contemporary defenders of sacred property rights had a reason to regret such popular public deductions made by Locke. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in biblical interpretation, Richard Epstein, for example, admits that Locke created "no small problem" in defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). Considering property from the perspective of human relationships and objects can take the form of a socially neutral side: accumulating the results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others.

Examining the boundaries of public power in a democratic society more closely, Waite acknowledged several limitations to state regulation. "You can receive compensation for using regulated property," and Waite claimed that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative authority. 

"It is assumed that any law— declared him—- is constitutional. Judges cannot declare what laws are unconstitutional, unless, of course, it is clear enough. If there's doubt, the expressed will of the legislative authority must be supported by a majority." Recognizing that this power could be abused, he implored citizens to seek compensation for injury "at polling places, not at court". Property rights defenders were stunned by the open reading on police powers in Mann v. Wisconsin. Judge Stephen Field disagreed and said that "the principle upon which it is thought to turn… undercuts property rights." 

"If so, it becomes a law—- he warned—- then all property and commercial activities in the state will be at the discretion of its legislative majority members." The Mann case decision showed that now private property rights were also one of society's interests, another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer needed any constitutional guarantee.

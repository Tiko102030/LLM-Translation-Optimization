Property rights, as established by the courts (Horwitz 1992, Demsetz 1964; Dillon 1895), are viewed from one perspective as an expression of individual property ownership and from another perspective as the right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect property owners from the consequences of external pollution. However, this general comparison of concepts can be understood incorrectly because these rights restrict the rights of others to a great extent. Property rights give people government-approved privileges to monopoly management of resources, the ability to turn to authorized police bodies to limit the activities of other people. Thus, property significantly limits certain benefits precisely because state agencies support owners' property more than those of other citizens. Therefore, this is not a dispute about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather discussion on immovable asset regulation requires consideration for whose property needs protection and which property rights possess advantages in fighting over them.

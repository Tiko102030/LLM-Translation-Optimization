The new paradigm of property rights acknowledges different definitions of property, but still considers “property rights” as a relation between people and things (cf. Bartels 1997; Liebke 1989). In this way, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relation between humans and things, property exists regardless of human society; even Robinson Crusoe has property rights. Like the relations between humans and things that they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic value because it is a reward for labor that shapes an unhuman material world.

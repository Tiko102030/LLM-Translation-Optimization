However, the most important aspect in this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Judge Stone, according to which a majority of the Court equated the concepts of property rights and the sacred civil liberties, which were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. As claimed by Stone, the Court intended to be guided by the principles of presumptions of constitutional law in economic legislation cases, while the chosen members of the legislative body would be responsible for evaluating economic measures and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established by instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. Stone, instead, warned that there would be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and countermarch of "takings procedure" 

After the decision in Caroll Products, the Supreme Court allowed chosen democratic members of the legislative body wide discretion in choosing social policy objects, even though they had limited property owners' actions. Environmental protection was one of the main goals pursued by policies implemented in this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case "Justice v. Marinette County" clearly illustrates how judicial authority easily applied the decision of Caroll Products to practice. Ronald and Katherine L. Justice claimed suit due to repeal of regulations impeding their taking up wetlands designated for them to build homes on. Rejeting family Justice's request, The Wisconsin Supreme Court held that legislative action was a legitimate exercise of police powers, then went even further to approve an already wider list of restrictions on property rights.

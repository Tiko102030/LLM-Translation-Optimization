Representative of the Supreme Court of Massachusetts Leumiel Shaw supported legislative authority in matters of regulating property on Boston Harbor grounds and prohibiting construction beyond these bounds, and spoke to the rights of police as follows: 

We consider this to be a established norm emerging from the very nature of a well-organized civil society where each property owner, regardless if they possess exclusive right to it or not, exercises control over it with all implied responsibility so that its use should not harm others who have an equal right to enjoy their property, nor harm the entire community in general. All property in this society... is fully or partially owned by the government and subject to common rules necessary for achieving the common good and general welfare. Rights of ownership, like all other social and conventional rights, fall under restrictions corresponding to ensuring that others are not harmed by use of given property and also fall under laws set by the legislative authority as this power... may assume is a reasonable decision needed in its name. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much simpler to feel and understand life and sources of her authority than to establish boundaries or limits of their manifestations." Indeed, with using universal and generic phrases like 'public interest', 'state internal constitution', 'implied right', "the highest power protecting all" and 'common good and general welfare' Shaw established that police can dispose of a large set of tools for achieving their goals. Governments of southern states resorted to police assistance in imposing laws limiting property use by slaves, for example, prohibiting slave owners from teaching slaves how to read.

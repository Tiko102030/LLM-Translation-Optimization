In connection with this, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Co v Holmes, more likely played a role Trojan Horse rather than the winner - defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering ownership as an inherent right, Holmes justified the "principle of expropriation" on the basis of establishing a balance between competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on circumstances and be a discretionary procedure. According to Holmes, property should be used constantly in a "reasonable" manner; however, the representation that what constitutes "reasonable" remains unclear at this time. If actions go "too far", compensation would be necessary in this case, but it is judges who, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, will determine what "going too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case judges should carefully assess and balance competing interests of both sides. Property holders must not, in turn, set everyone against them. Holmes argued that the "government would hardly be able to continue its activity if, in any way, question of property was not resolved."

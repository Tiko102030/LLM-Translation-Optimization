A similar situation existed in the Jacobson case before the Supreme Court, and Judge Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom was not limited to the absence of physical restrictions, but also included the right "to work where one desires; earn a living by any lawful means; seek one's chosen occupation and contract with others as may be necessary and proper for the attainment of those objects." Eight years after the Allgeyer decision set a benchmark for judicial elaboration of reasoning in this context, it was established in Lochner v. New York (1905) that New York State's law limiting baker hours to 60 hours per week was unconstitutional. Peckham argued forcefully that "the law inevitably involves an infringement of freedom of contract," violating the individual's common-law right to make contracts concerning his business that are necessary, proper and suitable for the successful pursuit of those ends; which is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution. Acknowledging that state regulation in other areas of property was justified because it was designed to protect "health or decency", Peckham maintained that in this case there was no valid reason for government intervention into human freedom or into contract between individual and employer by limitation of baker hours. In conclusion, he described the question at issue as whether lesser evil - a state's power to enact laws or an individual's personal liberty and right of making contracts - prevailed.

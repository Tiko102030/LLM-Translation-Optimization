In this case, as well as for all American rights related to property, originally necessary for achieving public goals were immediately cancelled once they prevented the accomplishment of those goals. At the beginning of the XIX century, state authorities seeking to stimulate industry and trade conducted a campaign to build highways and railways, canals. When constructing state infrastructure had to bear indirect costs, expenses connected with damage to neighboring property and losses in value of unsalvaged goods. In accordance with the legal principle "use yours so as not to harm another" (Lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would bear responsibility for indirect costs significantly increasing financing costs of inventions. When altering the meaning of this principle, i.e. when excluding independent entrepreneurs from responsibility for consequences of their actions in respect to neighboring property, the costs of state infrastructure construction have greatly reduced. The change began in 1823 in case "Calder v. Jones", establishing a precedent for Massachusetts state. Calder has brought an action against for reimbursement of damage resulting from renovation on street which resulted in damaging foundation of his house in Boston.

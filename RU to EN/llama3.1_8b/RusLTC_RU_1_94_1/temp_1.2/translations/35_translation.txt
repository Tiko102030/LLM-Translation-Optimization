In the landmark Jacobson v Massachusetts case (1885), the New York State Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the manufacture of cigars on leased premises because it "prevented an owner from receiving profit and freely using his property," and thus, "deprived him of his property and personal liberty."  Comparing concepts of individual freedom and property rights, Judge Earl stated that, in broad sense and as understood in our country, freedom means the right not only to be free from actual slavery... but also for man to use his faculties in all legal ways to live and work. Therefore any laws weakening or limiting these rights are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislative assembly contended that the ruling was based on police powers, and the measures taken were necessary to "improve public health across the country." For advocates of state regulation there was nothing to fear as long as the court considered the requirement, and subsequently concluded it lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law in fact "did not deal with public health issues at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the guise of police power one cannot arbitrarily infringe on personal rights and private property." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an era of unprecedented court activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the 1866 Godcharles v. Wigeman case, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring wages to be paid in cash (not company stock). The court argued that "the law is an invasion of equal labor and employee rights, more so, it's an affront on attempting to place unqualified workers under legislative guardianship," because it prevents workers from selling their labor at any conditions they are willing to accept.

Examining the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society in more detail, Waite acknowledged several restrictions on government regulation. "You can get compensation for using regulated property," and Waite claimed that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "Any law is assumed to be constitutional," he said. "Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it is clear and obvious." If there was any doubt, the expressed will of the legislative authority must be supported by a majority. Admitting that this power could be abused, he encouraged citizens to pursue compensation "at the polls rather than in court". The defenders of property rights were shocked by the open reading of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field thought otherwise and said that the principle relied on by the majority... undermines private property rights. "If it becomes a law," he warned, "then all property and commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of the legislative majority."

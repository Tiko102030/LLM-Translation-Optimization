In this case, Holmes acted more like a Trojan horse than a victor-protector of sacred property rights in the Pennsylvania Coal Case. Instead of considering property right as natural law, Holmes justified the "expropriation principle" based on establishing equilibrium between competing interests. Compensation for losses would depend on circumstances and would be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must always be used "reasonably", but the notion of what is "reasonable" remains ambiguous. If actions go "too far," in this case compensation will be necessary; however, it is up to judges, taking into account public needs and specific circumstances, to determine what constitutes "going too far". Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; each time judges must carefully evaluate and balance competing interests of both sides. Owners should not, on their turn, set everyone against them.

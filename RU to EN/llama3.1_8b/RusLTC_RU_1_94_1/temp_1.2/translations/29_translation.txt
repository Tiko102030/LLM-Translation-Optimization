The Civil War served as the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers wanted more. For example, labor rights advocate George McNail warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to conductor K.F. Breyse: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that are now being imposed on us by trust companies. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, but now it is workers who are nothing more than slaves of combined trust companies" (see Fonner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the civil war contributed to the fact that workers united and spoke out against these new demands. Large sums of money were needed for military operations, as well as a tightly linked expansion and centralization of government institutions, which once again confirmed that a significant financial and industrial capital was needed, one that could ensure the activity of the government. The steel and metal industry, whose products had preferential tariffs, alone could supply the developing network of railroads.

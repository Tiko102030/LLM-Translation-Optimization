As the use of this principle provides access to consideration of the social interpretation of property, Judge Redfield urged that the limits of property rights be recognized, because, as he said, actions taken by one owner affect others even if they occur on their own territory. However, the "use your own in such a way that you do not harm another" principle is only a norm and not a rule; it should not guide most cases where the interests of different parties are at stake and both sides complain about each other's encroachment (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions regarding property items were reviewed in the 19th century to promote business activity even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the "use your own in such a way that you do not harm another" principle remained, but it was now used to protect entrepreneurs from acting in accordance with passive neighbors' requirements who want their property left untouched. Now, the concept of "damage" is considered not as a series of wrongful acts against someone else's property, but rather an inability for owners to use their own. A new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by using their property. It became a precedent for the first time in New York State case "Palmer vs Mulligan" (1805).

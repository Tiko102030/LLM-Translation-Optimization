However, the most important aspect of this matter was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth note, according to which a majority of the Court divided concepts of property rights and sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in economic legislation cases the Court intends to be guided by the principles of presumptive constitutionalism of law, and selected legislative members will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and limitations on property rights. Since such rules are established due to instrumental causes, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. Stone, on the other hand, warned that a thorough investigation of personal freedoms violations would take place, such as freedom of speech or the freedom of choice of religion. Environmental protection and counter-procedure "exemptions"

After the Carolene Products decision, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic legislative members to have broad rights in choosing objects of social policy, even though they limited property owners' actions. Environmental protection was one of the main goals pursued in this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case of Just v. Marquette County vividly illustrates how the judicial authority easily applied the Carolene Products doctrine. Ronald and Catherine L. Just brought an action against ordinances that hindered them from occupying plots intended for them, which were swampy lands where they wanted to build homes. Denying the Just family's requests, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activities constitute a lawful exercise of police powers.

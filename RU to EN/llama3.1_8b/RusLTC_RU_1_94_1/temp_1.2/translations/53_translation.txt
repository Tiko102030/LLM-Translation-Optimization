Conclusion: The process of developing property rights Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth footnote remains in effect as a law. As it was under discussion, the Supreme Court approved almost all legislation regulating ownership, acknowledging that the powers of elected members of the legislative body must be "as large as the economic needs of the country." At the same time, the Supreme Court has established itself as a watchdog over private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. To these, it has equated the right to pollute one's territory or engage in discrimination based on gender, racial, or religious markers, which are now above all property owners' claims. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implicitly suggested that the right to property stands on two columns: the essence of harm and property as an instrument, not a sacred right. Recognizing the dual nature of harm, courts consider any "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," all of which must be balanced. After Holmes' interest balancing principle was approved, the Court had to consider each claimed "right" to property as an instrumental implementation, since conflicting interests would lead to achieving more global public goals. Instrumental and competing "rights" to property can never be absolute; they should never become an end in themselves.

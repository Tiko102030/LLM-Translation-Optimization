A certain form of ownership as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke protects property rights not so resolutely as one could understand from the well-known excerpt, quoted above (see Horn 1990; Kateslon 1998, p.77). In this excerpt he addresses the issue of the right of the English Stuart monarchs to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament. However, in addition to that question, Locke acknowledges the social definition of property, according to which property rights should be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that each person must have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be restricted so that all can share in the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and in the same condition" for others.

In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy as has nothing to do with morals" (Holmes 1881, pp. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894 "The Privilege, Criminal Intent and Design", Holmes based himself on the aforementioned representation of the harm caused by competition. By doing so he wanted to demonstrate the general necessity for the existence of an instrumental approach and "the principle of balance of interests" especially in conditions when judges weigh competing rights rather than resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At a Supreme Court hearing in 1903 Holmes applied this approach in the case of "Diamond Glucose Co v United States Glucose Co". "In modern society", he stated, "each part is closely related to another and if something affects one it will affect others somewhat too." So as long as everything isn't prohibited and the end of legislative power hasn't come yet, it's not enough to simply show that there has been some progress in legislation, i.e. when you can already interfere with trade or existing deals.

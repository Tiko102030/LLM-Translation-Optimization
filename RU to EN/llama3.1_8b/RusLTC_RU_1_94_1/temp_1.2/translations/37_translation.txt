The Louisiana Supreme Court had established a precedent that "the loss of private right in the case is deemed to have no significance whatever, due to the overall profit and trade volume of the business organization" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230-31). Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the US Supreme Court, where it was argued by John A. Campbell, a Democrat and former Assistant Secretary of War (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working at the Supreme Court during the war, Campbell had ruled in Dred Scott v. Sandford that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later he used labor law principles to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property rights. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans city ordinances sent us back to "times when 'the galling hand of government' was 'shoved into every corner and recess of life'." The butchers lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found an echo among the justices, and lawyers therefore feared growing pressure for a more extensive complex of regulatory measures, to redistribute income, property and power.

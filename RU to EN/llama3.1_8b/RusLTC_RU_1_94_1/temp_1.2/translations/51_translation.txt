In the case of Nollan vs California Coastal Commission, the Supreme Court ruled that the commission's decision was unconstitutional and established a new criterion for judicial review of land use determinations. Recording the speech to the court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that "the condition regarding the granting of the right of way" did not constitute a "taking" pursuant to the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general beach access was found to be "unrelated to the purpose it purports to serve...thus rendering it unlawful and requiring restitution." Concluding with a new standard, Scalia said:

Because there is no nexus between the condition imposed and the cause of the limitation on construction, what had previously served one end now serves an entirely different objective. The primary goal of government has shifted from obtaining a general right of way without paying any compensation...If the condition prescribed in the land use determination is not considered as limiting development and is serving the same governmental purpose, then limiting development would not be a valid land use rule but would simply "be extortion in its pure form." On this occasion Justices Brennan and Marshall dissented. They rejected Scalia's call for re-evaluation of legislative intent, arguing that "the proper standard for review...is whether the state can rationally determine whether an adopted measure will achieve public purpose." However, after these icons of liberalism left office in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional constraints on regulatory government.

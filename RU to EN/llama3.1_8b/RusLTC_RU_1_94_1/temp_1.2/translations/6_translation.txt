There exists an "undeniable" right of property to the worker, because:
Although the land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, still... the work of his body and the labor of his hands, strictly counted, belong to him. Whatever man he might then extract from that condition in which nature created and preserved this thing, he combines it with his own labor and adds something belonging to himself, and thus makes it his property... For since this labor is the undeniable property of the worker, no one but him can have rights to what he once combined with it, at least in those cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for use by others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone's labor, i.e., turning a person into a slave. It follows that property rights should not benefit society; nevertheless, property owners form societies and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

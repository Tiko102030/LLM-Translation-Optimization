Coen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955). Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of sanctity as property owned or used by Americans of European descent. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, negroes from southern states acquired the right to own property; however, their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and how involved African Americans were in it. The same instrumental approach that northern states governments used to restrict property rights regarding clean air and water was also used in southern states to preserve African American dependence on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined for public purposes, but with different objectives. Redistribution of property rights for the purpose of stimulating economic growth

Supporting construction of public infrastructure through redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Roger Taney in 1837 used an instrumental approach that he used in Court to annul contract rights of Charles River Bridge Company. He did this to manage the single bridge over Charles River which connects Boston and Cambridge (see case Charles River Bridge vs Warren Bridge). The defense side of Charles River Bridge experienced some difficulties. The company might lose "millions of dollars... invested in building railroads and canals on high-speed main lines... We will be thrown back a century to inventions of the last century and will be forced to remain immobile."

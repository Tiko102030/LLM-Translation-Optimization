In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy as has no connection with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894, "The Privilege, the Criminal Intent, and the Design," Holmes based this conception of the harm caused by competition. In doing so, he sought to demonstrate the general necessity for the instrumental approach and the "balance-of-interest principle" especially in situations where judges weigh competing rights instead of relying on syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At the US Supreme Court hearing in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case of "The firm 'Diamond Glue' against the United States Glue company." "In modern society," he declared, "each of its parts is closely connected with another, and if something affects one of them, the others will feel it to a greater or lesser extent. So long as everything has not been forbidden and an end has not come to legislative power, it will not be enough merely to show that there is some progress in the development of law, i.e., at a time when it is already possible to intervene in trading agreements or existing contracts."

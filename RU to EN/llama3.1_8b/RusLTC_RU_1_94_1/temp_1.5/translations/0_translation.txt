Introduction: the concept of property in economic theory and public practice 
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ in their definition of the concept of property, and it is often difficult to define boundaries. Some consider the right to property to extend to objects such as land and trees, including very ephemeral objects like living nature and running water. Others add that the right to property extends to such objects as ideas (patents), while some also consider a person in bondage a part of this concept. Societies differ depending on the rights owned by the owner of property; sets of privileges concerning responsibility for performing certain actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because every use of it affects the property of others. Property has dimensions since it affects relationships between people; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when pollution affects the environment, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; the rights prohibiting pollution limit those property rights but protect neighbors' property. Society allows a property owner to use their land and water resources even if it may cause harm to neighboring properties.

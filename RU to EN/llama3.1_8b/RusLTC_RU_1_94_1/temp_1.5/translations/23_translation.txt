Regarding this case, as well as regarding all American law, property rights, initially necessary for achieving public goals, were immediately abolished as soon as they hindered the achievement of these goals. In the early 19th century, government agencies, aiming to stimulate industry and trade, embarked on a campaign to build highways and railroads, canals. During the construction of state infrastructure, indirect costs had to be borne, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in value of unimproved properties. According to the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would bear responsibility for indirect costs significantly increasing financing expenses on inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by exempting independent entrepreneurs from liability for consequences of their actions concerning neighboring property, the costs of building state infrastructure were sharply reduced. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case of "Calder v Marsh", setting a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calder filed a lawsuit to compensate for damage caused during street repairs, as a result of which his house's foundation was damaged in Boston.

The Chancellor of New York Kent in 1826 announced that "each person has a degree of freedom to acquire, use and manage their property that can be comparable to an acceptable state of property and mutual rights of others… Although thus protected... the legislative organ has the right to prescribe the way and model its use as much as it may be necessary to prevent abuse of the right which could lead to the infringement on the rights of another person or society in general." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments took broad powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in the state of Illinois in 1837, the legislative organ did not have three pages to record all the powers that would be possessed by the administration of the city of Chicago concerning property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and restricting "ring spinning, ball games or launching paper snakes... capable of disturbing people", a new power appeared in the city which "would oblige the owner or tenant of any dry goods store, wine cellar, soap box shop, laundry factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, toilet, sewing factory or other old, detestable building or house to clean it out, move it, or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, p. 3-6). The authorities of other cities also took broad powers concerning property issues.

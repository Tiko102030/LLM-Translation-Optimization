The Civil War served as the beginning of a new era in American politics, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers sought more. For example, labor defender George McNeil warned that America must "instill republican principles into our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F. Breasye: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that are being forced upon us by trust companies today. The result of the Civil War of 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, but now it is workers who are nothing other than slaves of combined trust companies" (Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124-5). Furthermore, the civil war also contributed to the fact that workers united and protested against these new requirements. Large sums of money were needed for military operations, and closely related to this were the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus, which once again confirmed that a significant financial and industrial capital was required that could ensure the operation of the government.

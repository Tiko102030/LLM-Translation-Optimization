But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional limits on government regulation. For example, in Lucas v Coastal Commission (1992), the Court required the property owner to be compensated for damage to his property when a new "Coastal Zone Management Act" law suspended construction on his land. Again expressing skepticism towards elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as he did in Nollan, that judges must carefully review regulations to insulate themselves from "the risk of private property being used under the guise of mitigating significant societal harm for public purposes". The Court refined its new standards in Dolan v City of Tigard (1994), where it ordered a partial shifting of the burden of proof from government to landowners. The case was brought when the City of Tigard, Oregon, said it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only if she gave part of her land for the creation of public park and pedestrian-bicycle path to alleviate traffic congestion. Recounting the Court's decision for Scalia, Chief Justice William Rehnquist maintained that "the city has not denied the fact that the construction of a pedestrian-bicycle trail at Dolan's expense is a consequence of what the city needed to improve transportation relief". The decisions in Nollans and Doleans mark a new era of heightened scrutiny of property regulation. It may lead to private property rights being restored to their pre-New Deal level.

In the landmark case of Jacobson (1885), the Appellate Court of New York State declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on leased premises because it interfered with an owner's ability to gain profit and freely use their property, thus depriving them of their property and personal liberty. 

Judge Earl compared the concepts of individual freedom and protection of property rights, stating that in its broadest sense and as understood in this country, liberty means not only freedom from actual servitude but also a human right to utilize one's talents by all lawful means for living and working. 

Therefore, all laws that weaken or fetter these rights are violations of their basic right to freedom. The legislature argued that the case rested on the police power of the state and that the measures taken were necessary "to improve public health". 

For advocates of government regulation, there was no hint of impending trouble when the Court took up the question; however it eventually ruled that the law did not have sufficient evidence, concluding in the end that the statute was not relevant to issues of public health at all, and Judge Earl informed the legislature that personal rights and private property could not be arbitrarily infringed upon under the guise of police guidance. 

The Jacobson case marked the beginning of a period of unprecedented court protection of property rights. For example, in Gutchell vs Waidmen (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment by lawful money (not company shares) and argued that the law - a violation of equal rights of employer and employee - was a disparaging attempt to subject unskilled labor to legislative tutelage.

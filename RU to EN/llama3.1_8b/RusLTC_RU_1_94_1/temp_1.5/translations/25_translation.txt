The New York Court of Appeals used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Calendář v. Marsh" to dismiss Quakenbush's claims. The court claimed: "Any significant public improvement must, by necessity, more or less affect people's comfort and property; and when damage is preserved with a remote causal connection and predictability, no compensation for damage is recognized, and the amount of damage should be considered as part of the cost paid for future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "This case is based on the principle that the general good must prevail over private interests." State Support for Private Entrepreneurship Not preventing the government from beautifying the state at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedent law, as it was in the situation of Lansing v. Calder, transferred to some individuals the obligation to cover expenses related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Such instrumental norms later became private cases. For example, in the case Torp v. The Ratonand Barlington Railroad Company (1855) the Chief Justice of the State of Vermont, Isaac Redfield supported the law requiring railway companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of public order in the state implies the protection of human lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace for all people, as well as protecting all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (Latin. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas).

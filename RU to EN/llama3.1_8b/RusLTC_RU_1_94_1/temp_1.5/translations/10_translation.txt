This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limits on individual accumulation, and other authors subsequently connected these ideas, including the radical American Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property had reason to lament so popular in society the conclusions drawn by Locke. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in his interpretation of the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke created "no slight difficulty" to the defense of property rights (see Epstein 1985, p.10). Considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and things it may act as a socially neutral side: accumulation of results of their own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others.

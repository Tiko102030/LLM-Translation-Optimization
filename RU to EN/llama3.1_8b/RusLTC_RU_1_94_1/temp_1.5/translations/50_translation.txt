The church's decision, supported by liberal judges Brannon and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles but simply reaffirmed the basic rules of Pennsylvania Coal Case. However, in a 1987 case, Nolan v California Coastal Commission, it was a completely different matter because it established a new standard with regard to old rules. Having coastal land located between public parks north and south of each other, Nolans wanted to build a three-bedroom house like the others in this district instead of their dilapidated bungalow. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on the condition that Nolans agree to give up easement for common use over the territory between the high tide line and the dike located behind their house.

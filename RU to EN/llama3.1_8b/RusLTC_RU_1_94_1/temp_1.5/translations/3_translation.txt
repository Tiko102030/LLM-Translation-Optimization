The concepts of property rights are examined in two ways: one side views the protection of property rights as a manifestation of the owner's individuality, while the other considers the right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect property owners from the consequences of external pollution influences by the guilty parties. However, such a general comparison of these concepts can be misunderstood, since these rights mainly restrict the rights of others. Property rights give people government-authorized privileges for monopolistic control over resources and the opportunity to address authorized police agencies in order to limit the activities of other people. As a result, property significantly limits certain benefits due to the fact that government institutions more often support property owners than other citizens. Therefore, this is not about debating the principles of natural freedom and justice but rather discussing issues of real estate regulation which leads to choosing whose property to protect and whose property rights have an advantage in fighting for them. The discussion on democratic rights and their regulation has become so obscure because legal questions are repeatedly discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the U.S. legal system. In this way, according to the federalism system, one Federal Government is formed, and for each state its own legal system is established. Additionally, there is a clear division of executive, legislative, and judicial powers at every level of administration, with each intended to respect and ensure compliance with the norms of the Constitution in which they are also interpreted.

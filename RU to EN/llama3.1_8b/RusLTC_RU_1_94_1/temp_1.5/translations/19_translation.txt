Local authorities regularly seized houses where immoral acts took place, filled in factory ditches which posed a threat to people's health, prohibited the production of hazardous chemicals and put out fires, destroying private homes and warehouses in the process, and paying no compensation for this. Officials gained access to dozens of sectors of the economy including those related to healthcare such as drug manufacturing and livestock processing, and others with economic value but unrelated to public health issues like trade, purchase, sale, and production rights (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve a public goal officials began striking, for example in Jersey City in 1872 the police commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the property of the fertilizer plant owned by Manhattan Fertilizing Company and "reduced" the damage caused by the factory's activities, destroying machine equipment and taking needed parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

The Chancellor of New York Kent in 1826 declared that "every person has such an amount of freedom to acquire, use and dispose of their property as can be comparable with the acceptable state of other people's property and mutual rights... Although, thus, property is protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as much as it may be necessary to prevent abuse of the right which could lead to a violation of another person's or society's rights in general." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in 1837, the Illinois legislative body did not have three pages to list all the powers that would be held by the administration of Chicago regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transport, and limiting "the spinning top, playing ball or launching paper snakes... capable of annoying people", a new power appeared in the city which "would force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, outhouse, sewing factory or any other old and filthy building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, p. 3-6).

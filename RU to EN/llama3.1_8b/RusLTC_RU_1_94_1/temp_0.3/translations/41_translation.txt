It appears that the underlying views of J. Locke regarding absolute property rights are at the basis of this case. The Mann decision was also mentioned in this case, and it was rendered by Chief Justice Waite. However, attention should be drawn to the last statement considered earlier: "it is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously re-established an instrumental view of property rights that declared unconstitutional the previous fifty years of judicial practice. In the Lochner case, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Beginning his speech with the fact that "the decision was made in accordance with the principles of economic theory, which however, does not please a large part of the population," he added: The right of each person to act as they see fit for as long as they wish, provided, of course, that the rights of others are not violated, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal management, or laws of any state or other municipal institution which take their money to achieve goals that seem necessary regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. In contrast to how Lochner despotically defends intensive property rights, Holmes considered that competing interests require a balance to be achieved. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words in order to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

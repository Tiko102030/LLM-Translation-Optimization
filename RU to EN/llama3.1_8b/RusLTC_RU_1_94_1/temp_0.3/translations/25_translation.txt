The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the case of "Calder v. Massachusetts" to dismiss Quackenbush's claims. The court argued: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect, more or less, the comfort and property of individuals; and when the preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, but rather the amount of the damage should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "this case is based on the principle that the general good must prevail over private interests". State support for private entrepreneurship

Not prohibiting the government from carrying out state beautification at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedent law, as in the situation of "Lansing v. Calder", shifted the burden to some individuals to cover expenses related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case of "Torp v. The Rutland and Burlington Railroad" (1855), Chief Justice of the Vermont Supreme Court Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle provides access to a social interpretation of property.

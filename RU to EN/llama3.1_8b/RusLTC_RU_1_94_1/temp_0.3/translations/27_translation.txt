For the first time, it became a precedent in the case of "Palmer v. Mulligan" (1805) in New York state, where the owner of property downstream filed a lawsuit for damages against the dam owner upstream, which had collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: The defendants had the same right to their territory... the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" should be limited in reasonable bounds so that a person may enjoy the use of his property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for certain inconveniences of this character, such as a person who could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river eventually acquiring exclusive rights, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether he owned the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of that part of the profit it might have obtained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and as a result both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents who were prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. However, at the same time, by removing the existing monopoly power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits over indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It appears that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" in no way depended on J. Locke's ideas about sacred private property. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general affirmation of them.

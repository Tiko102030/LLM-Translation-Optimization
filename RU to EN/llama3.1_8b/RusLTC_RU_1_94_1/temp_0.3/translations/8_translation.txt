A certain form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke defends property rights not as resolutely as might be understood from the well-known excerpt quoted above (see Horn 1990; Kateslon 1998, p. 77). In this excerpt he addresses the question of the right of the Stuart monarchs of England to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament. But besides this particular issue, Locke recognizes and accepts a social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that each person should have as much as they can use.

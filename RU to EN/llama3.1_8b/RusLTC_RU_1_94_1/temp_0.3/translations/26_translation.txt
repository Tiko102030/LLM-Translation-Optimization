As the principle is applied, access to consideration of social interpretation of property is provided. Judge Redfield called for recognition of limitations on property rights because, as he said, actions taken by one owner can affect others even if they occur on their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests are at stake and both parties complain about interference from neighbors (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions regarding the subject of property were re-examined in the 19th century to promote entrepreneurial activity even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained but was now used to protect entrepreneurs from acting according to passive neighbors' requirements that their property remain untouched. Now the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of wrongful acts against someone else's property but rather as an inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for harm caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case of Palmer v. Mulligan (1805) in New York State.

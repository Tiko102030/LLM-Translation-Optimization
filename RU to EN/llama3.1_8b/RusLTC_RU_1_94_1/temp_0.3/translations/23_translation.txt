Regarding this case, as with all American law, rights of property, initially necessary for achieving public goals, were immediately abolished as soon as they interfered with these goals. In the early 19th century, state authorities, seeking to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads and railroads, canals. When building the state's infrastructure, indirect costs had to be borne, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved assets. Pursuant to the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would have been responsible for indirect costs significantly increasing the costs of financing inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding independent entrepreneurs' responsibility for the consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property, indirect costs on building state infrastructure were sharply reduced. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Callender v. Marsh", establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Callender filed a lawsuit to recover damages caused to him during street repairs, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

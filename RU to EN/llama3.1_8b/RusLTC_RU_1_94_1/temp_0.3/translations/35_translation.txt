In the landmark case of Jacobson (1885), the New York State Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on leased premises because it "interfered with the owner's right to earn a profit and freely use his property," and thus "deprived him of his property and personal liberty." Comparing concepts of individual freedom and property rights, Judge Earl stated that, in its broadest sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only the freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of man to employ his faculties in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, any law which weakens or fetters these rights is a violation of his basic right to liberty. The legislature claimed that the decision was based on state police powers and that measures taken were necessary to "improve public health." For advocates of government regulation, it boded ill that the court would even consider this requirement, and subsequently concluded that it lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was found that the law in fact "did not concern itself with issues of public health at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the guise of police power, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily invaded." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of a time of unprecedented judicial activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the case of Godcharles v. Wigeman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment by legal tender (and not company stock). The court held that "the law was an infringement on equal rights of employer and employee, more than that, it was an insulting attempt to subject unskilled labor to legislative tutelage," because this interfered with workers selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

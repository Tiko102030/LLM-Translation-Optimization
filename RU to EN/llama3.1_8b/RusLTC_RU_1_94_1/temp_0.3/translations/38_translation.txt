Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance with the judges, and lawyers were therefore concerned about growing pressure for expanded regulatory measures and redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has stuck in memory more than Judge Miller's majority opinion, which was later supported by additional arguments on police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the state of Louisiana had violated fundamental rights to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's prescriptions that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "the primary part of liberty... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to understand the court's decision in the case of Slotterhouse v. Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct commercial activities in the state of Louisiana, which essentially constituted a violation of freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was present in the case of Jacobson, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical constraint," but also encompassed the right "to work where he pleases; to earn a living by any lawful means; to pursue his chosen profession and for this purpose to enter into all contracts which may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the above-mentioned goals."

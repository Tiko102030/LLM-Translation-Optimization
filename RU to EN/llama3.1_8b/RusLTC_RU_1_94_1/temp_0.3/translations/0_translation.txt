INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and public practice
Society has property. Economic systems differ in how they define the concept of property, and often it is difficult to determine boundaries. Some believe that the right to property extends to objects such as land and trees, including the most ephemeral objects, such as living nature and running water. Others supplement this concept by stating that the right to property also extends to objects such as ideas (patents), and some also include a person in slavery. Societies also differ depending on the rights possessed by owners of property; sets of privileges related to responsibility for actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because each use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, as it involves human relationships; I have a right to manage my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when polluting the environment, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; laws prohibiting pollution limit the rights of this property but protect neighboring property. Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to neighboring property.

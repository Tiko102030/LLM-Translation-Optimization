A similar situation was in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical restraint," but also encompassed the right to "work where he pleases; earn a living by any lawful means; pursue his chosen profession and for this purpose enter into all contracts which may be proper, necessary and essential to the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives." After the Allgeyer decision, the peak of judicial activity in explaining motives was established eight years later in the Lochner v. New York case (1905), where the Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. In court, Justice Peckham resolutely stated that "the law necessarily involves the right to make a contract between employer and employee," which is a violation of "the general right to enter into contracts related to his business" which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Federal Constitution." Recognizing that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior", Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary ground for interfering with human freedom or his right to make a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham spoke about which of the two evils was less: state power to issue laws or human right to personal freedom and freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limits of police powers were reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner can be logically identified from other court decisions that asserted property rights.

Representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Court Lemuel Shaw supported legislative authority dealing with issues of regulating property in Boston Harbor and prohibiting building structures outside this harbor, and regarding police rights he said: We believe that this is a settled norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every owner of property, regardless of whether they have or do not have exclusive right to it, manages it with all implied responsibility so that its use should not cause harm to other people who also have the right to enjoy their property, nor to the entire community. All property in this association... belongs either fully or partially to the government, and can be subject to those general rules necessary for achieving common good and overall well-being. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to corresponding restrictions necessary to confirm that others have not been harmed by its use, and to established law rules, since legislative authority... may assume it is a necessary and reasonable decision. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits on its manifestations." Indeed, when using universal and typical phrases such as "public interest", "internal organization of the state", "implied right" and "duty of higher authority to protect all" and "common good and overall well-being", Shaw established that police authorities can dispose of a large set of tools in achieving their goals. Governments of Southern states resorted to police help to introduce laws limiting the use of property by slaves, for example, prohibited slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they introduced unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activity in certain professions. The application of laws ensuring unequal protection of property and labor for African Americans once again demonstrates what understanding of property was at the time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichterstein 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955).

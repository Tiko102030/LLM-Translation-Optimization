In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Holmes was more like a Trojan Horse than a winner - defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property right as natural law, Holmes justified "the principle of taking" property based on establishing balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should constantly "reasonably" be used, but the idea of what is "reasonable" remains vague so far. If actions go "too far", then in this case compensation would be necessary; however, it is exactly judges who, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, will determine what means "too far". Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case judges must carefully evaluate and balance competing interests of both parties. Owners should not, in turn, set everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "the government is hardly likely to be able to continue its activities further if the question of property is not resolved to some extent".

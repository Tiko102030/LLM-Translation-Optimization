The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that a "resolution depriving an owner of using their property" was necessary in order to give them "the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rely on constitutional principles but simply confirmed the basic facts of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the 1987 case of Nollan v. California Coastal Commission was a completely different case because it established a new standard for old rules. Nollans owned a coastal plot of land located between public parks to the north and south of each other and wanted to build a three-bedroom house similar to others in the area instead of their dilapidated bungalow. The California Coastal Commission granted the necessary permit only on condition that Nollans agree to grant an easement for common use of the territory between the high tide line and the dike located behind their home. Thus, people would be able to easily reach parks located both in the northern and southern directions simply by strolling along the sand without a care. The Commission explained its requirement by stating that a larger house would contribute to "territorial development," which would make people "unconsciously not realize that there is a coastline nearby and that they have the right to spend time there" (see Eagle 1996, p. 255).

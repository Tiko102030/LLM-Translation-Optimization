But after these liberal icons retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed additional restrictions on government regulation. For example, in Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required a property owner in South Carolina to be compensated for damage to his property after a new "Coastal Zone Management Act" halted construction on his land. Again expressing distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as he had in Nollan, that judges must carefully scrutinize regulations to protect themselves from the risk that private property will be used under the guise of reducing serious harm to society for public purposes. The Court refined its new standards in Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were instructed to shift some of the burden of proof from owners to government. The case was brought when the city administration of Tigard, Oregon, stated that it would only approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store if she gave up part of her land for a public park and pedestrian/bike path to reduce traffic congestion. Recording Scalia's opinion for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration "did not dispute the fact that construction of the pedestrian/bike path at Dolan's expense was a consequence of the city's need to improve transportation flow." The Nollan and Dolan decisions signal the advent of an era of enhanced scrutiny of regulatory takings. This could lead to property rights being restored to the level they were before the New Deal.

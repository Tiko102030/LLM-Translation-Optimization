Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as property owned or used by Americans of European descent. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from southern states gained the right to own property, but their rights could change depending on the political climate in the state and the level of participation of African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach that northern states' governments used to limit property rights regarding clean air and water was also used in southern states to maintain the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined for achieving public goals, but with different objectives. Redistribution of property for stimulating economic growth

By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through redistribution of property, Chief Justice of the United States Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach that he used in Court to annul the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge Company. He did this in order to personally manage the only bridge over the Charles River, which connects Boston and Cambridge (see case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge Company faced some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... invested in building railroads and canals on high-speed main lines... We will be pushed back a century to the inventions of that era and will be forced to remain immobile".

In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is justified in accordance with the principles of "such a policy as has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894, "Privilege, Criminal Intent and Design", Holmes relied on this presentation of the harm caused by competition. By doing so, he wanted to show the general necessity of an instrumental approach and the "principle of balancing interests" especially in circumstances when judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, p. 130-35). At a Supreme Court hearing in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case of "Diamond Glucose Co v United States Glucose Co". "In modern society," he said, "each of its parts is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, then the others will feel it more or less. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the end of legislative power comes, it will not be enough just to show that there has been some progress in the development of law, i.e., when we can already intervene in trade or existing contracts."

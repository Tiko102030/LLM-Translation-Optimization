Similar actions were justified by the concept of "police powers" of the state or what Oliver Wendell Holmes called "restrictions applicable to property in the interest of the whole community". The concept of police powers has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that by "public police and economy I mean due regulation and internal management of the state, whereby persons within the country, as well as members of a well-governed family, are bound to conform to the rules of decency and good breeding, assist their neighbours, and be orderly, industrious, and obedient in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case of Vanderbilt v Adams (1827) Judge Woodworth of New York more thoroughly described police powers saying: "The supreme power in society may, and must, prescribe how private rights to property shall be exercised... Police powers are founded on implied rights and duties of higher authority to protect all by means of established statutory regulations so as to increase the well-being of the whole community.... Such state authority must exist in every well-regulated society". In the case of Commonwealth v Alger (1851) Massachusetts Supreme Court Justice Lemuel Shaw supported legislative power dealing with questions of property regulation on Boston Harbor and prohibiting buildings from being constructed outside this harbor, saying about police powers: "We think it is a settled rule arising out of the very nature of a well-ordered civil society, where every owner of property, whether he has or has not an exclusive right to it, exercises it with all implied responsibility so that its use shall not injure other persons having like rights to enjoy their own property, nor the community at large".

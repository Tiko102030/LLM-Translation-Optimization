It appears that the basis of the aforementioned case is rooted in John Locke's views on absolute property rights. The Mann case was also mentioned, with the decision being made by Chief Justice Waite. However, attention should be drawn to the last statement considered earlier: "it is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously established an instrumental view of property rights, which declared unconstitutional fifty years of previous judicial practice in the Lochner case. Holmes had already expressed his opinion on the matter. Beginning his speech with the statement that "the decision was made according to economic theory principles, however, which do not satisfy a large portion of the population," he added: The right of each individual to act as they please for as long as they desire, provided that the rights of others are not infringed upon, does not conform to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal administration, or laws of any state or municipal institution, which take their money to achieve goals deemed necessary regardless of whether one wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment takes no account of Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner's despotic defense of intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require a balance to be achieved. After losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words in order to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

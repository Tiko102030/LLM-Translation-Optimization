The Chancellor of New York, Kent, in 1826 declared that "every person has such an amount of liberty to acquire, use and dispose of their property as may be comparable to the acceptable state of other people's property and mutual rights... Although thus protected by law, the legislative authority has a right to prescribe the way and manner of its use to whatever extent it may be necessary to prevent abuse of this right that could lead to violation of another person or the entire society." In order to protect public welfare and consider the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed extensive powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in Illinois in 1837 the legislative body did not have enough space on three pages to list all the powers that would be exercised by the administration of Chicago over property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transport, and limiting "the spinning top, ball play or launching paper snakes... capable of irritating people", a new power emerged in the city that "would compel the owner or lessee of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, privy, sewing mill or other old and disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, p. 3-6).

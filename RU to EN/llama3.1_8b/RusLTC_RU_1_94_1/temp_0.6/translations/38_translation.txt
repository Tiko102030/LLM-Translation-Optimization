Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found an echo among the judges and therefore his lawyers feared growing pressure for expanding regulatory measures and redistributing income, property, and power. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has stuck in memory more than that of Judge Miller's which was supported by a majority vote and later reinforced with arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the state of Louisiana had violated its fundamental right to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on Locke's prescriptions that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "the primary part of freedom... in an American sense." It took Judge Field twenty years to understand the decision by the court in the Slotterhouse and Mann case before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897 in the Allgeyer v. Louisiana case, the Court ruled that a state law was unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company without a license to conduct business in the state of Louisiana, which effectively constituted an infringement on freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacobson case and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham proved that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical restraint", but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; earn a living by any lawful means; pursue his desired profession and for this purpose conclude all agreements which may be proper, necessary and important to achieving the aforementioned goals".

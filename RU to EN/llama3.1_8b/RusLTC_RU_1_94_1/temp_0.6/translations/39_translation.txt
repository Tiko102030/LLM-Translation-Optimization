A similar situation occurred in the case of Jacobson v. Massachusetts, and Chief Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human liberty included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right to "work where he pleased; earn his living by any lawful calling; engage in the pursuit of a desired trade or profession, and for that purpose make all contracts which might be proper, necessary, and essential to the successful attainment of those ends." After the Allgeyer decision, the peak of judicial activism in explicating motives was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it mandated a reduction in working hours for bakers. In that case, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the statute in question compels the exercise of a right on the part of an employer to make a contract with his employee," which was an infringement of "the general right to make contracts relating to his business" that was protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. While acknowledging that police regulation of property was justified as protecting "health or morals," Peckham argued that in this case there was no necessary ground for interfering with human liberty or its right to a free contract through reducing working hours for bakers. In conclusion, Peckham said it was simply a question of which evil was the lesser: state power to issue laws or individual freedom and the freedom to make contracts. We believe that the limits of police power had been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner could be logically deduced from other court decisions that upheld property rights.

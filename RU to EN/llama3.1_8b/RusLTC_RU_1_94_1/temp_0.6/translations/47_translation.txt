Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes came to the conclusion that:

The economic situation in the country can be a result of how a temporary restriction on the enforcement of contract conditions is coordinated with the true meaning and purpose of its articles, and thus will not exceed the reserved right of the state to protect vital public interests.... Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely lawless restriction of the protectionist policy of the state, then the legislative body should make decisions in such a way as not to violate the legislative legality.... In doing so, reasonable or unreasonable policies will be conducted by the legislative body, and this will no longer concern the Court. As early as 1934, when the Court was ready to refuse to evaluate economic legislation and to consider cases on property rights, the legislative body had already declared the need to "protect vital public interests." After Roosevelt's re-election in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court had almost completely agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach of the Supreme Court as it was done in the case "United States v. Carolene Products" (1938). In this specific case, a company was sued under the 1923 "Non-Fat Milk Act," which prohibited interstate sales of milk with added fat and oil, considering them to be the sale of "intentionally spoiled food products harmful to health." The representatives of the "Carolene Products" company claimed that the law unfairly restricted contract freedom. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court found the law valid, believing it to be a fair instrument of state regulation necessary for protecting public health.

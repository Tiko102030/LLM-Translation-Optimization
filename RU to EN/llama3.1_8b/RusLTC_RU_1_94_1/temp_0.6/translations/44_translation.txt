When it will be possible to intervene in trading or existing transactions is unclear. The scope of activity must be defined and quantitative differences established. In the case of Pennsylvania Coal Holmes played more the role of a Trojan Horse than a victor-defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property as a natural right, Holmes based "the principle of expropriation" on establishing equilibrium between competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on circumstances and be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must be constantly used in a "reasonable" way, but the notion of what is "reasonable" remains vague so far. If actions go too far, then compensation will be necessary; however, it is up to judges to determine what "too far" means, taking into account public needs and special circumstances. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case judges must carefully weigh and balance competing interests of both sides. Owners should not provoke everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "the government will hardly be able to continue its activities if property issues are not resolved at least to some extent".

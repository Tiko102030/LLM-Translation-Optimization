The iron and steel industries, which had been granted favorable tariffs on their products, were the only ones that could supply the developing network of railroads. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzell 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the true intentions of bankers and owners of iron and steel companies, whose property was being subsidized by state authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand public control over issues of private property regulation. They achieved a remarkable success in passing an eight-hour workday law and a railroad tariff law. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, which led to these bills being sent for review to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of private property rights. The Supreme Court's decision in Mann v. Illinois (1877) greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about growing interest in ideas of popular radicalism at the time. The plaintiffs argued that by invalidating existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional clause on contracts and also violated the newly enacted 14th Amendment protecting citizens from government interference.

Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the majority opinion of lawmakers about natural rights and J. Locke's convictions, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that would rather mean the end of productivity and prosperity than protection of the sacred notion of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the principles of another economic theory, another instrumental vision or choice of another public goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged existing models of legal justification used to defend property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he extensively discussed how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even attempt to compensate a man for all the losses caused by damage done to him... Anyone can establish any company, and in further competition to survive, he will be able to lure clients away from his competitor's business, and thus possibly even bankrupt it."

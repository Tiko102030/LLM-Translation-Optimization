The Mann decision showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against a backdrop of increasingly heightened social unrest, this approach was increasingly alarming to conservatives and sparked an even greater explosion of emotions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). Following the events on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent legal scholar Christopher Tiedemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of God's anointed did not allow for restrictions on police authority. The spread of non-interventionist policy reduced the number of "human rights violations by the government". But now:
The political pendulum swings back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights is declared unconstitutional, and now it is needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil threatening the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant throughout civilized nations. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the strong, establish what wage workers should receive for their labor, and how many hours they must work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of a large army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in a civilized world, especially under conditions of universal electoral suffrage, conservatives feared even more that such an autocracy might emerge, which would be more despotic and less reasonable than any existing before. At the end of the 19th century, expressing opposition to the views of "a large army of dissatisfied people", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

In relation to this case, as with all American law, property rights initially necessary for achieving public goals were immediately abolished as soon as they hindered the achievement of these goals. At the beginning of the 19th century, state authorities seeking to stimulate industry and trade launched a campaign to build roads and railroads, canals. During the construction of state infrastructure, indirect costs had to be borne, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in value of unimproved property. In accordance with the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would bear responsibility for indirect costs significantly increasing funding costs for inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding independent entrepreneurs' responsibility for consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property, construction costs for state infrastructure would have been sharply reduced. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calder v. Marsh", setting a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calder brought an action for damages caused to him during street repair work, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

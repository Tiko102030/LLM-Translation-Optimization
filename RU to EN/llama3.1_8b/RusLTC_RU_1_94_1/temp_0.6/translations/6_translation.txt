There exists an "uncontested" right to property of the worker because: Although the land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, nonetheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands strictly speaking belong to him. Whatever he extracts from that state in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and adds something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is uncontested property of the worker, no one else can have a right to what he once combined it with, at least in cases where there remains sufficient quantity and quality for others' common use. Thus, Locke consecrates property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone's labor, i.e., turning a person into a slave. It follows that the rights of property should owe nothing to society; nevertheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

However, the most important aspect of this matter was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, according to which the majority of the Court shared the concepts of property rights and sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in economic legislation cases, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of constitutional law presumption, while elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. On the other hand, Stone warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and countering the "takings" procedure

After the decision in Carolene Products, the Supreme Court allowed elected members of the legislative body to have wide discretion in choosing social policy objectives, even though they restricted property owners' actions. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policies conducted within this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case of Justice v. County of Marquette graphically illustrates how judicial power easily applied the provision of Carolene Products. Ronald and Catherine L. Justice brought an action against the revocation of regulations that prohibited them from occupying designated wetlands for building homes. Rejecting the Justices' claims, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate exercise of police powers. Later it wanted to approve an even broader list of property rights restrictions.

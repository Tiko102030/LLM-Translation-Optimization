The Supreme Court of Louisiana upheld a law that stated "the loss of private right in this case is of no consequence due to the general profit and trade turnover of the commercial organization" (see Novak 1996, p. 230-31). Butchers from New Orleans appealed their case to the United States Supreme Court, where they were represented by John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant Secretary of War (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working at the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell had ruled in Dred Scott that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later he used the fundamental principles of labor law to convince the court to protect a citizen's right to property. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans ordinances sent us back "to the time when the inquisitive eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery".

At the end of the 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of a "numerous army of dissatisfied", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Thiedeke, they demanded that the fullness of state authority be limited by establishing a boundary on police powers. State courts took the initiative in determining new bases for the sacred, rather than instrumental approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhamer v People" (1856), the Appellate Court of New York declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into effect, all properties were deprived of those who had just sold this liquor. At the same time, the procedure for seizing property did not pass through the legally established order. For the first time discovering that the concept "established by law" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance".

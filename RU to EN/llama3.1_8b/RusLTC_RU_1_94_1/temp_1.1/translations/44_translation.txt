When can intervention in trade or already existing transactions be made. The area of activity should be delineated and quantitative differences must be determined. In this case, Pennsylvania Coal Holmes functioned more as a Trojan Horse than a champion of sacred property rights - instead of considering the right to property as natural law, Holmes based the "principle of takings" on establishing balance between competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must be used in a constantly "reasonable" manner but the representation of what is considered "reasonable" remains vague. If actions go too far, damage compensation will be required; however, it is the judges who will determine this by taking into account public needs and special circumstances. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; each case must carefully weigh and balance competing interests from both sides. Property owners should not provoke everyone against themselves.

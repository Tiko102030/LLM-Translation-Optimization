In the case of Nollan v California Coastal Commission, the Supreme Court ruled that the decision by the committee was unconstitutional and established a new criterion for judicial review of zoning regulations. Recording his speech to the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia recognized that presenting "the condition for permission of coastal access" is not an "exaction" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general coastal access is "not serving the purported goal of protecting people's visual access to the beach" and "is thus improper and compensation must be made". In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard:

Because there is no connection between the condition and the reason for limiting development, the new purpose is converted into something different from what it was before. Now, the main task of the government is to obtain a general coastal access right without paying any compensation.... if the condition specified in the coastal usage permit is not considered a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental goal, then the restriction on construction will not be a proper zoning regulation but only "a naked extortion". On this occasion, Justices Brennan and Marshall presented a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's proposal to reconsider the legislative branch's views. They argued that "the appropriate standard for review... is whether the government can rationally decide if the adopted measure can achieve a public goal". However, after these liberal icons retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on state regulation.

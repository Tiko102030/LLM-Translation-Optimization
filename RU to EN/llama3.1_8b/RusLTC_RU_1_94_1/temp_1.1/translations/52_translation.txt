But after these icons of liberalism had retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed additional restrictions on state regulation. For example, in Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Commission (1992), the Court required compensation to be paid to a property owner in South Carolina for damage to their property when the newly enacted Beachfront Management Act suspended construction on their land. Again expressing his distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia cautioned, as he had done in Nollan, that judges must carefully review ordinances to safeguard against "the risk that private property will be used under the guise of reduction of serious harm to society for public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were required to shift part of the burden of proof from owners to government.

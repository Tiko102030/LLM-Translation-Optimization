However, the most important thing in this matter was not the decision itself, but the fourth note of Judge Stone's statement that a majority of the Court shared the concepts of property rights and sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, the Court intends to follow principles of presumptive constitutionality of laws in cases of economic legislation, while elected members of the legislature will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and limitations on property rights. Since such rules are established by instrumental causes, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, on the other hand, warned that a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion, would be conducted.

After the decision in Carolene Products, the Supreme Court allowed elected members of the legislature to have broad rights in selecting objects of social policy, even though they restricted actions of property owners. Protection of the environment was one of the main objectives of the policies implemented in this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case of Justice v County of Marinette graphically illustrates how judicial power easily applied the Carolene Products doctrine. Ronald and Catherine L. Just brought an action challenging ordinances that prohibited them from occupying marshy tracts intended for them to build houses. Dismissing the Just family's claims, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate exercise of police powers. Later it wanted to approve an even broader list of property rights limitations.

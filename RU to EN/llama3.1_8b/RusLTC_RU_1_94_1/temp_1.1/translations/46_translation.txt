The case of Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell (1934) was a milestone in the development of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. The Supreme Court ruled that states have reserved powers to protect the vital interests of their citizens, even if it means temporarily limiting or modifying contractual obligations during periods of economic instability. Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that Article X of the Constitution, which restricts state regulation, should not be interpreted literally but rather in light of the reserve power of the state to protect its citizens' vital interests.

He further emphasized that courts should evaluate the effectiveness of legislative decisions based on whether they are lawful and arrived at by reasonable and proper means. By denying any privileged status to private property rights, Hughes concluded that economic conditions within a country can be seen as a result of how a temporary limit on enforcement of contractual obligations is coordinated with the true meaning and purpose of its provisions.

As such, it will not exceed the reserved power of the state to protect vital community interests. Since the provision does not constitute an absolute and completely improper limitation on the protectionist policy of the state, the legislative body should make decisions that do not violate its legitimate authority.... The court's attention would not be drawn to a rational or irrational policy being conducted by the legislative body.

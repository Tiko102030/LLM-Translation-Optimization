The Civil War served as the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers wanted more. For example, defender of workers' rights, George McNeil, warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor, C.F.Bracey: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes which are now being imposed on us by trust companies." The outcome of the Civil War in 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, but it is now workers who have become nothing more than slaves of united trust companies" (see Fonner 1998 p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the civil war also facilitated workers to come together and resist these new demands. Large sums of money were required for waging wars, as well as the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus closely related to it, which once again proved that a significant financial and industrial capital was needed to ensure the activity of the government. The iron and steel industry, which had preferential tariffs on its products, was the only one able to provide the developing network of railways.

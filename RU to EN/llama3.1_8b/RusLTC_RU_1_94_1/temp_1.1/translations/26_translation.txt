As the application of this principle provides access to consideration of social interpretation of property, Judge Redfield urged recognition of limitations on the right of property because, in his words, actions undertaken by one owner affect others even if they take place on their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is only a norm and not a rule; it should not be guiding in most cases where conflicting interests are at stake between different persons and both sides complain of interference by each other's neighbors (see Holmes 1894 p. 11). Questions relating to matters of property were re-examined in the 19th century with a view to promoting entrepreneurial activity even if it involves harm to neighboring property rights. Thus, while the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained, it was now used to protect entrepreneurs acting according to passive neighbors' demands that their property remain untouched. At this point, the concept of damage was no longer regarded as a series of improper actions against other people's property but rather as the inability of owners to use their own.

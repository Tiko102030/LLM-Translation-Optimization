The Chancellor of New York, Kent, in 1826 declared that "every man has such an amount of liberty to acquire, use and dispose of his property as may be comparable with a reasonable state of the property and reciprocal rights of others... Although thus protected by law... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and mode of its use as much as it may be necessary to prevent abuse of the right which may lead to an infringement on the right of another person or the community in general." In order to protect public welfare and consider the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments took upon themselves broad powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in the state of Illinois the legislative body did not have enough space for three pages to list all the powers that would be held by the administration of Chicago regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation and limiting "turning a hoop, playing ball or launching paper snakes... capable of annoying people", the new city had authority which "would force the owner or lessee of any dry goods store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, privy, sewing factory or any other old and disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, p. 3-6).

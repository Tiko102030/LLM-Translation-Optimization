The Jacobson case (1885) set a precedent when the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on rented premises because it "interfered with the owner's right to profit and freely use his property," thus depriving him of his property and personal liberty. Comparing concepts of individual freedom and property rights, Judge Earl stated that in its broadest sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only the freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of man to employ all his faculties in a lawful manner to live and work... Therefore, any laws which weaken or fetter these rights are violations of his basic right to liberty. The legislature claimed that the decision was based on police power and that the measures taken were necessary "to improve public health in the country." For defenders of state regulation, it boded ill that the court would consider this demand; however, it concluded that it lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law did not actually address any questions of public health, and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that one could not arbitrarily infringe on individual rights and private property under the guise of police power. The Jacobson case marked the beginning of a time of unprecedented judicial activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the Godcharles v. Wigeman case (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in lawful money (not company stock).

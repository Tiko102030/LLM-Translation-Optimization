The New York Court used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case of "Calder v. Marsh" to dismiss Quigley's demands. The court argued: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect, more or less, people's comfort and property; and when the preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, but rather the amount of damage should be considered as part of the price paid for future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "this case is based on the principle that the general good must prevail over private interests." State support for private entrepreneurship 

Not prohibiting the government from carrying out state beautification at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedential law, as in the situation of "Lansing v. Calder", shifted the responsibility to cover expenses related to achieving public goals onto some individuals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case of "Torrey v. The Rutland and Burlington Railroad" (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies protecting the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and tranquility of all people, and protecting all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle provides access to social interpretation of property.

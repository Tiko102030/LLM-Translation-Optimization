INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and public practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ in how they define the concept of property, and it is often difficult to establish boundaries. Some believe that property rights extend to items such as land and trees, including the most ephemeral objects, such as living nature and running water. Others supplement this definition by stating that property rights also apply to objects such as ideas (patents), some also include a person in this category as a slave. Societies also differ depending on the rights of property owners; sets of privileges related to responsibility for actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection of property, because each use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, as it involves human relationships; I have a right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, pollution of the environment significantly reduces the neighbors' right to clean air and water; rights that prohibit pollution limit the rights of this property, but protect the neighbors' property. Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause harm to neighboring properties.

Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional clause on contracts which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment was now an indispensable civil right and constitutional guarantee of property, shielding private property from state intervention. However this view was supported by a minority of court members in 1877. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee of property, the Court upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. The Court stated that "powers are inherent to any supreme authority," ruling that "government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards one another, and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Hale, chief justice of England, in a statement that "when property 'affects public interests' it by law ceases to be subject solely to private regulation".

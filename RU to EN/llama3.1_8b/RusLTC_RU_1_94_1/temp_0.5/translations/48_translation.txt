However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote of Judge Stone's opinion, according to which a majority of the Court equated property rights with the sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in matters of economic legislation, the Court intended to be guided by the principles of constitutional presumptions of law, and elected members of the legislative body would be responsible for assessing economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established by instrumental causes, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. Stone, on the other hand, warned that there would be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and the counter-procedure "exemptions"
After the decision in Carolene Products, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body to have broad powers to choose objects of social policy, even though they restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policies conducted in this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case of Justice v. Marinette County illustrates how judicial power easily applied the Carolene Products provision. Ronald and Catherine L. Justice filed a lawsuit over the revocation of ordinances preventing them from occupying designated wetlands for building homes. Rejecting the Justices' claims, the Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled that legislative activity is a lawful exercise of police powers.

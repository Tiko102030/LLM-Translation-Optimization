The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using their property" is necessary for it to provide a right to compensation for damage. The court's decision in the case of First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles but simply confirmed the basic provisions of Pennsylvania Coal. However, the 1987 case of Nolan v. California Coastal Commission was quite different because it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, owning a coastal plot of land situated between public parks to the north and south of each other, Nolans wanted to build a three-bedroom house instead of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in this district. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on condition that the Nolans agree to grant an easement for common use of the territory between the high tide line and the dike located behind their house. Thus, people would be able to easily reach parks situated both north and south of it simply by strolling leisurely along the sand. The Commission explained its requirement by saying that a larger house would promote "territorial development" so that "people will unconsciously cease to perceive the coastline nearby and fully realize they have the right to spend time there" (see Eagle 1996, p. 255).

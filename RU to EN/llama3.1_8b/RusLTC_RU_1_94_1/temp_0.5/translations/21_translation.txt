Representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Court, Lemuel Shaw, supported legislative authority dealing with issues of harbor property regulation and prohibiting building outside this harbor area, and spoke about police rights as follows: 

We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very nature of a well-organized civil society, where every owner of property, regardless of whether they have exclusive right to it or not, exercises control over it with all implied responsibility so that its use must not cause harm to other people who also have the right to enjoy their own property, nor to the entire community as a whole. All property in this community... belongs fully or partially to the government and is subject to those general rules necessary for achieving the common good and overall well-being. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to corresponding restrictions necessary to confirm that others have not been harmed by its use, and to the established law rules, since legislative authority... can assume it is a necessary and reasonable decision. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It's much easier to feel and understand life and its sources than to establish boundaries or limits on its manifestations." Indeed, using universal, typical phrases such as "public interest", "internal arrangement of the state", "implied right" and "duty of the highest authority to protect all" and "common good and general well-being" Shaw established that law enforcement agencies can possess a large set of tools in achieving their goals. Governments of Southern states resorted to police assistance to introduce laws limiting property use by slaves, for example, prohibiting slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they introduced unusual restrictions on private property rights, for example, introducing a policy of strict racial segregation that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activity in certain professions. The application of laws ensuring unequal protection of property and labor rights of African Americans once again demonstrates what understanding of property was at the time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichterstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955).

The iron and steel industries, which had preferential tariffs on their products, were the only ones that could provide a developing network of railroads. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzell 1984, 1990). The angry workers and farmers saw the real goals of bankers and owners of iron and steel companies, whose property was just sponsored by state authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand public powers in matters of property regulation. They achieved a remarkable success in obtaining legislation on an eight-hour working day and law setting railroad tariffs. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, as a result of which these bills were sent to the Supreme Court for consideration.

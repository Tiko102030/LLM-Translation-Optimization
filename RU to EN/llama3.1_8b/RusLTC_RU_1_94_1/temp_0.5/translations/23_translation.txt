In this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary for achieving public goals, were immediately abolished as soon as they impeded the achievement of these goals. At the beginning of the 19th century, state authorities, seeking to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads and railroads, canals. During the construction of state infrastructure, indirect costs had to be borne, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved assets. In accordance with the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would have been responsible for indirect costs, significantly increasing the costs of financing inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding independent entrepreneurs' responsibility for the consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property, the costs of building state infrastructure were sharply reduced. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case of "Callender v. Marsh", establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Callender filed a lawsuit to recover damages caused by repairs to the street, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

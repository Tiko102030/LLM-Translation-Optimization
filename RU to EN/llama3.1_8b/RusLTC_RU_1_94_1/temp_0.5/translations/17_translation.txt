The Supreme Court of the United States also indicated that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property is based on public interest and instrumental considerations, and in the case of 1875 "Kohl v. The United States" ruled that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property is "a result of political necessity, and it cannot be separated from sovereign power". The right to compulsory expropriation of private property was one example of the use of government authority to promote the development of a "well-organized society", increase the quality of "public welfare" or "well-being" through regulations affecting interference with property rights. These regulations reflected common law principles, such as: "the good of the people shall be the supreme law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Frequently cited in court decisions these main principles serve as "common law management programs for a well-regulated society", providing legal grounds for comprehensive state regulation of property in order to reflect the mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

For the first time, it became a precedent in the case of "Palmer v. Mulligan" (1805) from the state of New York, where the owner of the property located downstream filed a lawsuit for damages. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. As an advocate on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: 

The defendants had the same right to their territory... [the principle "use your own so as not to harm another"] must be limited in reasonable bounds so that man may enjoy the pleasure of using his property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for such inconveniences, so that a person who could first build a dam or factory on any public or navigable river would eventually acquire exclusive rights at least up to a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of part of the profit it could have obtained as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents who were prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. But at the same time, by removing existing monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits over indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" in no way depended on the ideas about sacred private property advanced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of them.

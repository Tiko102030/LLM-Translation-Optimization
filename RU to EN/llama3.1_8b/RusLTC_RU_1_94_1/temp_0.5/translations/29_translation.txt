Civil War served as the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers wanted more. For example, the defender of workers' rights, George McNeill, warned that America must "inoculate our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F. Breese: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that trust companies are trying to impose on us today. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the liberation of black slaves, but now workers are nothing more than slaves of combined trust companies" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to the fact that workers united and spoke out against these new demands. Large sums of money were needed for military operations, and closely related to this was the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus, which once again confirmed that a significant financial and industrial capital was required to support government activity. The iron and steel industries, on whose products preferential tariffs were set, were the only ones who could provide the developing network of railways.

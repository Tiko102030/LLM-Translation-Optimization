In the Nollan case, the Supreme Court declared the committee's decision to be unconstitutional and established a new standard for judicial review of land-use regulations. Recording his remarks for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that presenting "a condition regarding the grant of easement" was not an "exaction" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general public access "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting people's visual access to the beach" and is therefore "unconstitutional, and compensation must be made." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard:

Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying cause of the building restriction, the new goal is transformed into something else entirely different from what it was before. Now, the primary concern for government is obtaining general public access without paying any compensation... if the condition in the land-use permit is not considered a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a valid land-use regulation but simply "shakedown". This time Justices Brennan and Marshall dissented. They rejected Scalia's call to reexamine the legislative body's opinion. They argued that "the proper standard for review... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure taken can achieve a public purpose." However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

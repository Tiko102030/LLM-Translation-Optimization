As the principle is applied, it provides access to consideration of social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognition of limitations on ownership because, in his words, actions taken by one owner affect others even when they occur within their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests are at stake and both parties complain about neighborly interference (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions related to property were reevaluated in the 19th century to promote entrepreneurial activity even at the expense of damaging others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" was preserved but now used to protect entrepreneurs from acting according to passive neighbors' requirements that their property remain unscathed. Now, the concept of damage is considered not as a series of wrongful actions against someone else's property, but rather as owners' inability to use their own property. A new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by using their property. It was first set as a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of New York state (1805).

According to the court ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe on the rights of other people and which could not be achieved at its original composition." The court acknowledged that zoning regulations for environmental protection "should be reasonable", but "we believe that preventing a violation of public rights by introducing restrictions on private property use in its initial purposes is justified". There was no "exemption" of property, as there was in the case of Pennsylvania Coal. This was because "the use of property without changing the natural composition of its soil is allowed by law." Property defenders achieved greater success in a court battle over "exemptions" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already made several decisions as early as 1987, such as in the case of "First Episcopal Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), where it required the county administration to pay compensation after a regional flood control organization prohibited the restoration of a flooded church tent camp. The court notified the county administration that "a ruling depriving the owner of using his property is necessary in order for it to give him the right to compensation".

All of the above-mentioned applies only to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions. For them, indeed, nothing can be guaranteed and it is impossible to fully automate the training of neural networks. However, alongside with iterative training algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms that have very high stability and allow for fully automating the learning process. Checking the adequacy of training

Even in the case of successful, at first glance, training, a network does not always learn exactly what its creator wanted from it. There is a known case where the network was trained to recognize tanks by photographs, but later it turned out that all the tanks were photographed on the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of learning to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what was required from it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be done on examples that did not participate in its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be as large as possible, depending on the quality of training. If the errors of a neural network have a probability close to one billionth, then a billion test examples are needed to confirm this probability.

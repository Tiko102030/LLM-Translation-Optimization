Everything mentioned above applies only to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions. For them, it is impossible to guarantee anything and completely automate the training of neural networks. However, alongside with iterative learning algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms possessing very high stability and allowing for complete automation of the learning process. Checking the adequacy of training

Even in case of successful training at first glance, a network does not always learn exactly what its creator wanted it to learn. There is a known case where a network was trained to recognize tank images from photos, but later it turned out that all tanks were photographed on the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of learning to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what was required of it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be performed on examples that did not participate in its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be as large as possible depending on the quality of the training. If a neural network's errors have a probability close to one billionth, then it takes a billion test examples to confirm this probability.

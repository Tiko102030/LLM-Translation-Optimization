All of the above applies only to iterative algorithms for finding neural network solutions. For them, nothing can be guaranteed and training neural networks cannot be fully automated. However, alongside iterative learning algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms with extremely high stability that enable full automation of the training process. Checking the adequacy of training 

Even in the case of successful initial training, the network does not always learn exactly what the creator wanted it to. A known example is a network that learned to recognize tank images from photographs, but later it turned out that all the tanks were photographed against the same background. As a result, the network 'learned' to recognize this type of landscape, instead of 'learning' to recognize tanks. In this way, the network understands not what was required of it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training should be conducted using examples that did not participate in its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be as high as possible, the higher the quality of training. If a neural network's errors have a probability close to one billionth, then a billion test examples are needed to confirm this probability.

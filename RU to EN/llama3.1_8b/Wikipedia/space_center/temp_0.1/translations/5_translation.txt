All of the above applies only to iterative algorithms for finding neural network solutions. For them, it is impossible to guarantee anything and it is not possible to fully automate the training of neural networks. However, alongside iterative learning algorithms, there are non-iterative algorithms that have very high stability and allow for full automation of the training process. Checking the adequacy of training

Even in the case of successful training at first glance, the network does not always learn exactly what its creator wanted it to. There is a known case where the network was trained to recognize tank images from photos, but later it turned out that all the tanks were photographed on the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of "learning" to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what is required of it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be done using examples that did not participate in its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be as large as possible, depending on the quality of training. If a neural network's errors have a probability close to one billionth, then a billion test examples are needed to confirm this probability.

 However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote of Judge Stone, according to which the majority of the Court members separated the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of presumption of constitutionality of the law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, напротив, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom to choose a religion. Environmental protection and the counterattack of the "takings" procedure. After making a decision in the Carolyn Products case, the Supreme Court allowed democratically elected members of the legislative body wide rights in choosing social policy objectives, even though they limited the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued in this context. The Just v. Marinneth County court case of 1972 in Wisconsin наглядно illustrates how the judiciary easily applied that provision in the Carolyn Products case. Ronald and Catherine L. Just filed a lawsuit to cancel regulations preventing them from occupying designated wetland areas for building houses. Rejecting the demands of the Just family, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate use of police powers. Later it wanted to approve an even wider list of restrictions on property rights.

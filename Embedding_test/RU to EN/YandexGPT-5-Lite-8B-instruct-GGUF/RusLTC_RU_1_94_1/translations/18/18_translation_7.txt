 In 1826, Chancellor Kent of New York declared that "every person possesses such a quantity of freedom to acquire, use and dispose of his property as may be commensurate with an acceptable state of property and the mutual rights of others... Although thus protected... the legislature has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as far as may be necessary in order to prevent abuse of the right which might lead to violation of another person's or society's rights in general." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in Illinois, the legislative body lacked three pages to catalog all the powers that the Chicago city administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and restricting "the spinning of hoops, playing ball or flying kites... capable of annoying people", the new city acquired the power that "will force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, tallow candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, latrine, clothing factory or any other dilapidated, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). The authorities of other cities also assumed broad powers over property issues. Thus, in New York, certain norms were established for the transportation of goods across the East River, for example, the price for cargo transportation varied from twenty-five cents for fattened buffaloes or bulls to fifty cents per cord of walnut wood, one cent for empty milk bottles, twenty-five cents for children's corpses and fifty cents for adult corpses (see Novak 1996).

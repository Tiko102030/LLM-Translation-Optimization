 This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, later other authors joined these arguments, including the famous American radical thinker, Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret Locke's so popular conclusions in society. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, admits that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for protecting property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the standpoint of relations between people and objects, it can act as a socially neutral party: the accumulation of the results of one's own labor activity does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. But recognition of the social parameters of property also admits a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person can harm others, for example, in the case of monopolization of resources previously accessible to everyone.

In relation to this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public goals, were immediately abolished as soon as they hindered the fulfillment of these goals. At the beginning of the 19th century, state authorities, seeking to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads, railways, and canals. Indirect costs had to be incurred during the construction of the state's infrastructure, expenses associated with damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved property. Under the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would be held responsible for indirect costs that significantly increased the cost of financing inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding responsibility for independent entrepreneurs' actions regarding neighboring property, the costs of building state infrastructure would have sharply decreased. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Callender v. Marsh", setting a precedent for Massachusetts. Callender filed a claim for compensation for damage caused to him during street repairs, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston.

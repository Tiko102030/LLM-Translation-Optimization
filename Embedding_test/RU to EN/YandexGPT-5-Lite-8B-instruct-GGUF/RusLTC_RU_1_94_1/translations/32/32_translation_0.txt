 Considering in more detail the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Wait recognized several limitations on government regulation. "For the use of regulated property, you can receive compensation," and Wait argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "It is assumed that any law," he stated, "is constitutional. Judges cannot declare what laws are unconstitutional, unless, of course, it is clear and so. If there is doubt, then the expressed will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." Recognizing that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to go for compensation "to the polling stations rather than to court." Property rights advocates were shaken by the open reading of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field thought differently and said that "the principle on which the opinion of the majority is based... undermines private property rights." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of the majority of its legislative body." The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, were another tool for achieving public goals, and no constitutional guarantee was required anymore.

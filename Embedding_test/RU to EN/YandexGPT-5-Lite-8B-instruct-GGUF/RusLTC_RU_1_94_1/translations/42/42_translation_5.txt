 After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the opinion of the majority of lawmakers about natural rights and John Locke's beliefs, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that rather signifies the end of productivity and prosperity, not the protection of the sacred notion of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of a different social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "The Common Law" (1881), he elaborated that the concept of absolute rights contradicts the prerequisites for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even seek to compensate an individual for all the harm done to him... Anyone can found any firm, and in the future, in order to survive in competition, they will poach their competitor's clients, and perhaps thereby even ruin them." In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm brought by competition is permissible according to the principles "of a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881).

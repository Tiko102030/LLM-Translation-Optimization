Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the Supreme Court of the United States, where they were represented by Field's predecessor, John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant secretary of war (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the basic provisions of labor legislation to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property rights. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the ordinances of New Orleans had taken us back to a time "when the prying eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated among the justices, and lawyers therefore feared increasing pressure that regulatory frameworks would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed.

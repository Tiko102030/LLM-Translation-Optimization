Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as it was in the case of property owned or used by European Americans. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from the southern states received the right to own property, however, their rights could change depending on what the political situation in the state was and what the participation of African Americans in it was. The same instrumental approach that the governments of the northern states used to limit property rights with respect to clean air and water was also used in the southern states in order to preserve the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public goals, only the goals differed. Redistribution of property rights in order to stimulate economic growth.

By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach that he used in the Court to nullify the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge Company. He did this in order to independently operate the only bridge across the Charles River, which connects Boston and Cambridge (see the case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge Company had some difficulties. The firm could lose "millions of dollars... that were invested in the construction of railroads and canals on expressway lines... We will be thrown back a century to the inventions of that age and we will have to remain stationary".

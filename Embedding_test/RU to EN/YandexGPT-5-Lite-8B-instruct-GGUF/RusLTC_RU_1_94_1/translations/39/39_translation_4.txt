 A similar situation occurred in the Jacobs case trial, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn a living by any legal means; to seek his favorite profession and, for this purpose, to enter into all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals." After the Allgeyer court decision, a peak in judicial activity in stating motives was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared New York law unconstitutional because, according to this law, bakers' working hours were reduced. Speaking in court, Justice Peckham stated emphatically that "the law necessarily affects the right to enter into a contract between employer and employee," which violates "the general right to conclude a transaction that relates to the activities of his firm," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution." Recognizing the fact that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary reason to interfere with human freedom or his right to enter into a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham said that it was just a matter of which evil was less: the power of the state to legislate or an individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to contract. We believe that the limit of police powers has been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and Lochner's New Deal could be logically defined based on other court decisions that asserted property rights.

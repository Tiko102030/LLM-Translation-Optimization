 Representative Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court supported the legislative power dealing with property regulation issues in Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this pier, and spoke about police rights as follows:
We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, whether he has exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility for it so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their property use, or society as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules can be applied to it that are necessary to achieve the common good and universal welfare. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, fall under appropriate restrictions necessary to confirm that others have not been harmed from the use of this property, and under established legal rules, since the legislative power... can assume that this is a necessary and expedient solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits to its manifestation." Indeed, when using universal, typical phrases like "public interest", "internal structure of the state", "implied right and duty of higher authority to protect everyone" and "common good and universal welfare", Shaw established that police authorities could have a wide range of tools to achieve their goals. Southern state governments resorted to the help of the police in order to introduce laws limiting the use of slaves' property, for example, they prohibited slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after abolition, they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, for instance, they introduced a policy of strict racial segregation that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of the property and labor of African Americans once again demonstrates what the understanding of property was at that time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955).

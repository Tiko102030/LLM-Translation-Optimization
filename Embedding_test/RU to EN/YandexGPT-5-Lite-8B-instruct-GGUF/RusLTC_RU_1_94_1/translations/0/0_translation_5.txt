 INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and social practice
Society has property. Economic systems differ in the definition they give to the concept of property, and often the boundaries are difficult to delineate. Some believe that property rights extend to objects such as land and trees, including the most perishable objects such as wildlife and running water. Others supplement this notion with the claim that property rights also apply to objects such as ideas (patents), some also include humans themselves as slaves. Societies also differ depending on the rights that property owners have; a set of privileges relating to responsibility for taking any actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "more" or "less" protection for property, because each use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions because it affects human relationships; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when the environment is polluted, the neighbors' right to clean air and water is significantly reduced; rights that prohibit environmental pollution limit the rights of this property but protect the property of the neighbors. Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources, even though it may cause damage to the neighbors' property.

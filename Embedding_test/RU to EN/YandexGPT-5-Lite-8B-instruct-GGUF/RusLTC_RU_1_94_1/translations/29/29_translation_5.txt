 The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in America's political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which liberated the country from slavery. Reformers wanted more. For example, George McNeil, a defender of workers' rights, warned that America must "instill republican principles into our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to C. F. Bracey, a train conductor: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that trust companies are trying to impose on us today. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the liberation of black slaves, but now workers are nothing more than slaves of united trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War contributed to workers uniting and protesting against these new demands. Large sums of money were required for military operations, and the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus associated with it confirmed once again that significant financial and industrial capital was needed to support the government's activities. Only iron and steel industries, whose products had preferential tariffs, could provide for the developing network of railroads.

Again expressing its distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as in the Nollan case, that judges should carefully study the regulations in order to insure themselves against "the risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in the case "Dolan v. City of Tigard" (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from owners to the government. The case was initiated when the administration of Tigard, Oregon, stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only on the condition that she give up part of her land for a public park, pedestrian and bicycle paths in order to reduce traffic congestion. Writing an opinion for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration "did not dispute the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle paths at Dolan's expense was a consequence of the need for the city administration to improve the transportation interchange."

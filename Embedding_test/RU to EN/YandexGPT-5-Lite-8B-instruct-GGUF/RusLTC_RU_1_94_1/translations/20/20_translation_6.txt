McCarter" (1908), referred to as "restrictions applicable to property in the interest of the whole society." The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that "By public police and economy I mean the due regulation and domestic order of the state, whereby the people in the country, like members of a well-regulated family, are bound to follow rules of decency and good breeding, to assist their neighbors, and to be honest, industrious, and compliant in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, pp. 4, 162). In the case "Vanderbilt v. Adams" in 1827, Judge Woodworth of New York State described police powers in more detail, saying: "The supreme power in society can and must prescribe how private property rights can be used.... Police powers are based on implied rights and the duty of the higher authority to protect everyone through established law regulations so that the welfare of the whole society increases.... Such state power should exist in every well-regulated society." In the case "Commonwealth v. Algar" (1851), Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court supported the legislative authority dealing with property regulation in Boston Harbor and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this pier, and spoke about police powers as follows: We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each owner of property, whether or not he has exclusive right to it, manages it with all the implied responsibility for it so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their own property or society as a whole.

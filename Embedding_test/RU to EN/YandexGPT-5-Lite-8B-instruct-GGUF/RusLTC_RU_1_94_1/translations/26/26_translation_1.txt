 The use your own so as not to injure another's... Application of this principle provides access to consideration of the social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his words, actions taken by one owner affect others even though they take place on their own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to injure another" is only a norm, not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where the property interests of different people collide and where both parties complain about a neighbor's interference with their territory (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Issues related to the subject of property were revised in the XIX century in order to promote the development of entrepreneurship, even at the expense of causing damage to the property of others. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to injure another" was preserved, but now it was used to protect the right of entrepreneurs to act in accordance with the demands of passive neighbors who want their property left untouched. Now the concept of "damage" was considered not as a series of unlawful actions against someone else's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by using their property. For the first time it set a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of New York State (1805).

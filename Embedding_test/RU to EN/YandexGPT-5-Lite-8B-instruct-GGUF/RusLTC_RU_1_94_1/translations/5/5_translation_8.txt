 The new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of ownership, however, it still views "property rights" as relationships between people and objects (see Barzel 1997; Leibkep 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relationship between people and objects, ownership exists without human society; after all, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Like the relationship between people and the objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it is a reward for labor that shapes the non-human material world.

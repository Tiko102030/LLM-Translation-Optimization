 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "large army of недовольных", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Citing Tideman frequently, they demanded to limit the full power of the state by establishing the boundaries of police powers. The state courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the outbreak of the Civil War, in the case of "Weinhamer v. People" (1856), the New York State Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who sold this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for seizing the property did not take place in accordance with the law. For the first time discovering that the concept of "due process of law" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance." The Weinhamer decision echoed only about thirty years later, after which the case began to be considered with redoubled interest. In the precedent case of Jacobs (1885).

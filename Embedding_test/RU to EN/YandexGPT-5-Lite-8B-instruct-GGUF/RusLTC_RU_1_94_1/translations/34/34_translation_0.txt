 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "large army of недовольных", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Citing Tideman frequently, they demanded to limit the full power of the state by establishing police powers' boundaries. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred rather than instrumental approach to determining private property. Even before the outbreak of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhamer v. People" (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who sold this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for seizing the property did not take place in accordance with the law. For the first time discovering that the concept of "due process of law" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance."

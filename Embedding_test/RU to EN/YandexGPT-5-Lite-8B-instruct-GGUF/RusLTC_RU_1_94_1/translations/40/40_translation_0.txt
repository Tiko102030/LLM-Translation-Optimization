 The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and Lochner's New Deal could be logically defined based on other court decisions that asserted property rights. In the case "Adkins v. Children's Hospital" (1923), the Court overturned a minimum wage law for the District of Columbia, ruling that this law unconstitutionally confiscated the property of workers and firms by violating their "freedom of contract," which should be "the general rule and cannot be taken away." Oliver Wendell Holmes and Pennsylvania Coal.

Shortly before considering the Adkins case, the Supreme Court made another historic ruling that seemed to fully address the protection of property rights. In the case "Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Mahon" (1922), the Court declared a Pennsylvania law unconstitutional because this law limited the activities of coal mining companies in order to preserve the subsoil. The Court ruled that it would be generally accepted that regulation of property could only be carried out to a certain extent, and if it went too far, then the taking of property would already take place.... It remains unclear why human failures or needs will justify shifting responsibility for damage onto someone else.... and society's strong desire to improve its situation will not be enough to guarantee that this goal can be achieved in a shorter way than the constitutional way to buy off charges.... it is only a matter of degree. The mentioned Pennsylvania Coal case has been cited more than a hundred thousand times over the past seventy-five years and is still in force.

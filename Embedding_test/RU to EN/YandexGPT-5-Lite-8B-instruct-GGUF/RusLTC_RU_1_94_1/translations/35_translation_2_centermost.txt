 In the precedent case of Jacobs (1885), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in rented premises because it "prevented the owner from making a profit and freely using his property," and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in the broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual slavery... but also a person's right to use their abilities in all legal ways in order to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or restrain these rights... violate his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislature argued that the ruling in this case was based on the state police powers, and the measures taken were necessary in order to "improve the country's healthcare system." For defenders of government regulation, it did not bode well that the court began to consider this requirement, subsequently concluding that it lacked the required amount of evidence. It was finally established that the law did not actually "deal with health issues at all," and Judge Earl notified the legislature that "under the guise of police instructions, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily infringed." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of a time of unprecedented judicial activity in protecting property rights. For example, in Godcharles v. Wajgman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal tender (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law violated the equal rights of employer and employee, moreover, it is an offensive attempt to subject an unskilled worker to legislative custody" because it prevents workers from selling their labor under any terms they were willing to accept.

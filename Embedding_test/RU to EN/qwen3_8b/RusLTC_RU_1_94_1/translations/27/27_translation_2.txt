For the first time, he became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream initiated a suit for damages. The dam situated upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same rights regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your own property so as not to cause harm to others'] should be limited within reasonable limits to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely due to some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law that provided for some inconvenience of this nature, so that a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of the portion of profit that it could have obtained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and as a result, both the long-standing and new residents possessed the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents whose actions were prohibited from restricting entrepreneurs' activities. At the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases only weakened existing property rights. Therefore, the transformation of the principle 'use your own property so as not to cause harm to others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property introduced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

For the first time, it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream initiated a claim for damages. The dam situated upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same rights regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others'] should be limited to reasonable limits so as not to deprive a person of the enjoyment of using their property merely because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law that provided for some inconveniences of this nature, so that a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose a portion of the profits it could have gained as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both the old and new residents possessed the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents whose actions were prohibited from restricting entrepreneurs' activities. At the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases only weakened existing property rights. It turned out that the transformation of the principle 'use your property in such a way as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property introduced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

For the first time, he became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property downstream filed a claim for damages. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Justice Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same right regarding their territory... [the principle 'use your property so as not to harm others'] should be limited to reasonable bounds to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely due to some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law that provided for some inconvenience of this nature, so that a person who could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose the portion of profit that could have been obtained as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both the old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents being prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. At the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from court proceedings regarding indirect damages, these cases only weakened existing property rights. Therefore, the transformation of the principle 'use your property so as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas about sacred private property introduced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

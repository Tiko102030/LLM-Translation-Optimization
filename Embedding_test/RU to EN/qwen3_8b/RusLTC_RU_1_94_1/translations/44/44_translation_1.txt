In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Holmes, Holmes more resembled a Trojan Horse than a winner—defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "expropriation principle" of property rights based on establishing a balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and will be a non-mandatory procedure. According to Holmes, property should be constantly "reasonably" used, but the notion of what constitutes "reasonably" remains vague. If actions go "too far," in such cases, compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it will be the judges, taking into account the presence of public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what constitutes "too far." Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully evaluate and balance the competing interests of both sides. Property owners, in turn, should not incite everyone against themselves. Holmes argued that "government will scarcely be able to proceed further if, to some extent, the issue of property rights is not resolved."

The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed some form of property in comparison with another form of property. According to one legal historian, Willard Hurst, this approach is one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America—"the freedom of the creative energy of man." Despite the fact that the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on the entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. In theory, if the cost of the project were higher than the amount of the damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the damage caused. However, in practice, all the burden is shifted to the injured party, which only to a greater extent contributes to the increase in the number of projects undertaken by entrepreneurs, while all the risk and operational costs are placed on the shoulders of the neighbors. If property rights were enforced to a greater legal extent, high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by saving on the costs of risk and operational expenses for entrepreneurs, the new principle has even more significantly contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

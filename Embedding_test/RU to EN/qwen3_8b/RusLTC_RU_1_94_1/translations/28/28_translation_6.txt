The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed certain property in comparison with another form of property. According to one legal historian, Willard Hurst, this approach is one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America - "the liberation of the entire creative energy of man." Despite the fact that the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on the shoulders of entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. In theory, if the cost of the project were higher than the amount of the caused damage, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the caused damage. However, in practice, all the burden is shifted to the injured party, which only to a greater extent contributes to the increase in the number of projects by entrepreneurs, while all the risk and operational costs fall on the neighbors' shoulders. If property rights were observed to a greater legal extent, high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by saving on the costs of risk and operational costs for entrepreneurs, the new principle has only further contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

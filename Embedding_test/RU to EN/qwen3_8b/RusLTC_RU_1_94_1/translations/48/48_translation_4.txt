However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote of Judge Stone, according to which the majority of the Court shared the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone's assertions, in cases concerning economic legislation, the Court intends to follow the principles of the presumption of constitutionality of laws, and selected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and limitations on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative actions. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there will be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religious choice. Environmental protection and the counter-measure of the "taking" procedure. After issuing the decision in the Caroline Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body broad rights in choosing objects of social policy, even though they had limited the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy conducted in this context. The court case "Just v. Marinette County" of 1972 in Wisconsin clearly illustrates how the judicial power easily applied the position from the Caroline Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just filed a lawsuit regarding the repeal of regulations preventing them from occupying the designated swampy areas for building homes. The Wisconsin Supreme Court rejected the Just family's claims and ruled that legislative activity is a lawful use of police powers. Later, it wanted to approve a more extensive list of restrictions on property rights.

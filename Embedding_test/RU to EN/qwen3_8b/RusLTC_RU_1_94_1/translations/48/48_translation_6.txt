However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, which stated that the majority of the Court equated property rights with the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases concerning economic legislation, the Court intends to follow the presumption of constitutionality of the law, and selected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative actions. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there will be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religious choice. Environmental protection and the counter-procedure of "expropriation" After the decision in the Carlin Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body broad rights in choosing objects of social policy, even though they restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy implemented in this context. The case "Just v. Marinette County" from 1972 in Wisconsin clearly illustrates how the judicial power easily applied the position from the Carlin Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just filed a lawsuit regarding the repeal of regulations preventing them from occupying the designated swampy areas for building homes. The Wisconsin Supreme Court rejected the Just family's claims and ruled that legislative activity is a lawful use of police powers. Later, it intended to approve a broader list of restrictions on property rights.

Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to the property of neighbors. On the other hand, it may support the affected neighbors and limit the activities of this owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, this reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of loss of clean air and water or the cost of payment that would need to be made to the company to stop it from discharging waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not have privileges, because they would have to reduce pollution levels, or they would have to buy permission from neighbors to pollute. Some question the fact that property rights have a significant impact on income distribution, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social outcome, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those polluting the environment buy the right to do so from their neighbors, or should neighbors pay those polluting the environment to stop doing so? Pollution will occur where the costs of reducing its negative consequences are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will cease when the costs of reducing the negative effect are less. A market of free trade will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

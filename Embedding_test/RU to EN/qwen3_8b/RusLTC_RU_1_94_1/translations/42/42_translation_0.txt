After losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law. Instead of relying on the majority opinion of the legislative assembly members regarding natural rights and the beliefs of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics." Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that more likely signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than the protection of a sacred notion of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "General Law" (1881), he thoroughly discussed how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "Law does not even attempt to compensate a person for all the harm caused to him... Anyone may start any business, and later, to survive in competitive struggle, he will attract his competitor's clients, and possibly even ruin him in the process."

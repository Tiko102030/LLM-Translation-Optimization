After losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case under an alternative legal framework. Instead of relying on the majority opinion of the legislative assembly's members regarding natural rights or on the convictions of John Locke, he accused them of copying Spencer's "Social Statics." Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that more likely signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than the protection of a sacred notion of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "The Common Law" (1881), he thoroughly discussed how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "Law does not even attempt to compensate a person for all the harm inflicted upon him... Anyone may establish any business, and later, to survive in competitive struggle, he will attract his competitor's clients, and possibly even drive him out of business."

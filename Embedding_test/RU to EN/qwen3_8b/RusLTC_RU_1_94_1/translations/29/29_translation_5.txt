The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. Reformers, however, wanted even more. For example, labor rights advocate George McNeil warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to the words of railroad conductor K.F. Breyse: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that today the trust companies try to impose on us. The result of the 1861 Civil War was the emancipation of black slaves, yet today workers are nothing but slaves of consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124–125). Moreover, the Civil War contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. The conduct of military operations required substantial financial resources, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed the need for significant financial and industrial capital capable of supporting government activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones capable of providing the expanding network of railroads.

The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. Reformers, however, wanted even more. For example, labor rights advocate George McNeil warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to the words of the railroad conductor, K.F. Breyse: "The Declaration of Independence was not created with the purposes that today trust companies try to impose upon us. The result of the 1861 Civil War was the emancipation of black slaves, yet today, workers are nothing else but slaves of consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. Conducting military operations required large sums of money, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed that significant financial and industrial capital was needed to sustain government activities. The ironworking and steel manufacturing industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones capable of providing the developing network of railways.

There exists an "inalienable" right of property of the laborer, because: Though the earth and all lower creatures belong jointly to all mankind, yet ... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, according to the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever a person may take from the state in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and adds to it something that belongs to him personally, thereby making it his own property... For since this labor is the inalienable property of the laborer, no one else can have a right to what he has once joined to it, at least in those cases when a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it means to use someone else's labor, i.e., to enslave a person. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, property owners form society and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

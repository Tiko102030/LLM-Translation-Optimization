In the 1875 case "Kohl v. United States," it was stated that the right of eminent domain over private property is "the result of political necessity, and it cannot be separated from sovereign power." The right of eminent domain over private property was one example of the use of governmental power to promote the development of "a well-organized society," to increase the level of "public welfare" or "well-being" through regulations concerning interference with property rights. These regulations reflected principles of common law such as: "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). These fundamental principles, often cited in court decisions, serve as "principles of common law governance in a well-regulated society," providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property in order to reflect mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

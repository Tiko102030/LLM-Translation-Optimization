"Use your own so as not to harm another." Application of this principle ensures access to the consideration of a social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his view, actions of one owner affect others even if they occur on their own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm, not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where property interests of different people clash and both parties claim interference by a neighbor on their territory (see Holmes, 1894, p. 11). Issues concerning the subject of property were reexamined in the 19th century to promote entrepreneurial activity, even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained, but now it was used to protect entrepreneurs' right to act according to the requirements of passive neighbors who want their property to remain undisturbed. Now, the concept of "damage" was considered not as a series of wrongful acts against another's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by the use of their property. This approach was first established in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" in the state of New York (1805).

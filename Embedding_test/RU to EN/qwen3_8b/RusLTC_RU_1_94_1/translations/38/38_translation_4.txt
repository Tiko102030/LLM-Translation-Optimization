The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's particular opinion left a stronger impression in memory than Justice Miller's, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that in the state of Louisiana, fundamental rights to free labor had been violated. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's teachings that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the main part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." Field required twenty years to fully understand the court's decision in the case of "Slotter-House and Mann" before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case of "Allgeyer v. Louisiana," the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not hold a license to conduct commercial activities in Louisiana, which in essence constituted a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was present in the case of Jacobs, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham demonstrated that freedom of a person included not only the absence of "his physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn a living in any lawful manner; to pursue his preferred profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned objectives."

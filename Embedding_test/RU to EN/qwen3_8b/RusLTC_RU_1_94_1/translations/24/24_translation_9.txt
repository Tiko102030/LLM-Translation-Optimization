Calhoun filed a claim for damages caused during street repairs, which resulted in damage to the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited English laws, common law precedents, and this principle in an attempt to secure compensation. However, the defense rejected the use of English precedents with an instrumental presentation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim," which Calhoun had filed, "were to be granted, then it would be necessary to review the correctness of repairs of all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Calhoun's claim for compensation. Then, the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's proposal for a market-based interpretation of the case to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was an extremely unfavorable development for people whose property was directly adjacent to public structures. "Those who purchase property adjacent to a roadway," the court stated, "must take into account the future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." For, in such a case, the amount of compensation would have doubled compared to the original cost of repairs. Similar conclusions were reached in New York in 1828, in the case "Lansing v. Smith Walter Quakenbush," where a claim was brought against the state of New York for damages due to the fact that dock prices fell after the construction of a new harbor in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Calhoun v. Marsh" to reject Quakenbush's claims.

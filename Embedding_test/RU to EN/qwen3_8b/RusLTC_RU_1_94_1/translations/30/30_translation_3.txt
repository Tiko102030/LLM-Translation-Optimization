The ironworking and steel industry, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, could only ensure the developing railway network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system established in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Incensed workers and farmers saw the true aims of bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose ownership was indeed sponsored by government agencies. They sought new laws that would expand public authority in matters of regulating property. They achieved remarkable success in securing the passage of the eight-hour workday law and the railroad rate law. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, leading these bills to be sent for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The 1877 Supreme Court decision in Mann v. State of Illinois greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of the popular radicalism of the time. The plaintiffs argued that the law, by voiding existing contracts, violated the constitutional clause on contracts, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from state interference.

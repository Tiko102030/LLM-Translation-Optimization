The iron and steel industry, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, could only ensure the developing railway network. At the apex of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system, established in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Discontented workers and farmers saw the true aims of bankers and owners of iron and railway companies, whose ownership was directly sponsored by government agencies. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority in regulating property rights. They achieved remarkable success in securing the enactment of a law establishing an eight-hour workday and a law setting railroad rates. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were sent for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case of "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of popular radicalism at that time. The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts, thereby also violating the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from state interference.

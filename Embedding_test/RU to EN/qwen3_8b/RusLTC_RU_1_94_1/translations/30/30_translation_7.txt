The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could provide the expanding railway network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently established centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Discontented workers and farmers saw the true aims of the bankers and owners of the iron and railway companies, whose ownership was directly sponsored by government agencies. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority in regulating property rights. They achieved remarkable success in enacting the law establishing an eight-hour workday and the law setting railroad rates. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were sent to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of popular radicalism at that time. The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from state interference.

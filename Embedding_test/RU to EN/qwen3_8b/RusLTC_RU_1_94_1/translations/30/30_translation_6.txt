The iron and steel industry, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, could only ensure the expanding railway network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently established centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Discontented workers and farmers saw the true aims of the bankers and owners of the iron and railway companies, whose ownership was precisely sponsored by government agencies. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority in regulating property ownership. They achieved remarkable success in securing the law establishing an eight-hour workday and the law setting railroad rates. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were sent for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of popular radicalism at that time. The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the Constitution's contract clause by voiding existing agreements, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from state interference.

According to the court's ruling: "A landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of other people and that could not have been achieved with its original composition." The court recognized that environmental protection zoning rules "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights by introducing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "eminent domain" taking of property, as in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was because "the use of property without altering the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding "eminent domain" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Under the leadership of Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already issued several decisions in 1987. For example, in the case "First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the county's flood control regulatory agency prohibited the restoration of a flood-damaged church campsite. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a regulation that deprives a property owner of the use of their property must be necessary in order to 'give them a right to compensation for damages.'"

This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation. Subsequently, other authors joined these considerations, including the well-known American radical Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reasons to regret such popular societal conclusions of Locke's. For example, Richard Epstein, who accused Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, acknowledges that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it can also serve as a socially neutral factor: the accumulation of results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is accessible to others. However, recognizing social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person may harm others, for example, in the case of monopolizing resources previously accessible to all. Just as social relationships, the theory of property becomes the subject of various disputes (see Fried 1998, p. /think)

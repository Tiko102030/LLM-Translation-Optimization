In the late 19th century, when opposing the opinion of the "numerous army of the discontented," advocates and judges aimed to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tidemann, they demanded limiting the full extent of state power by establishing boundaries for the authority of the police. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to the determination of private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. Pipple" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared a law prohibiting the sale of liquor unconstitutional. After the law took effect, all those who had sold this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for the confiscation of property did not follow the established legal order. For the first time, recognizing that the concept of "established legal order" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative power cannot entirely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thus subjecting property to complete disappearance."

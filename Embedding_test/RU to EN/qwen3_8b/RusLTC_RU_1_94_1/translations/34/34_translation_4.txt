In the late 19th century, when opposing the opinion of "the numerous discontented army," advocates and judges aimed to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tidemann, they demanded limiting the full extent of state power by establishing boundaries for the police's authority. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to determining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. Pipple" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared a law prohibiting the sale of liquor unconstitutional. After the law took effect, all those who had sold this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for seizing property did not follow the established legal order. For the first time, the court recognized that the concept of "established legal order" protected property, ruling that "legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thereby subjecting property to complete disappearance." The decision in the Wainhamer case found resonance only approximately thirty years later, after which the case was examined with renewed interest. This precedent was later reflected in the Jacobs case (1885).

In the late 19th century, expressing opposition to the opinion of "the numerous discontented army," advocates and judges aimed to sanctify private property. Often citing Tidemann, they demanded to limit the full extent of state power by establishing boundaries for police authority. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. Pipple" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared a law prohibiting the sale of liquor unconstitutional. After the law took effect, all those who sold this liquor lost their property. At the same time, the procedure for seizing property did not follow the established legal order. For the first time, recognizing that the concept of "established legal order" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative power cannot entirely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thereby subjecting property to complete disappearance."

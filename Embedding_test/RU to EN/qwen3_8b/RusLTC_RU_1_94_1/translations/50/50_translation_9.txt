The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to seek compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely confirmed the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nolans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to replace their aging bungalow with a larger three-bedroom home similar to other homes in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on the condition that the Nolans agreed to grant the public a right of way across the land between the high tide line and the seawall behind their home. This would allow people to easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply strolling leisurely along the beach. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling the area," which "people would subconsciously not realize that they are near the coast and that they have a complete right to spend time there" (see Igl 1996, p. 255).

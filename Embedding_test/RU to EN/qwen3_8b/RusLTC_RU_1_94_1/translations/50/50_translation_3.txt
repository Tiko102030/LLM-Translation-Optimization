The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to seek damages." The court's decision in the First Anglican Church case, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the core holdings of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nollans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to build a new three-bedroom home in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other homes in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on the condition that the Nollans agreed to grant a common right of way over the territory between the line of high tide and the dike behind their home. This would allow people to easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply strolling leisurely along the sand. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling the area," which "people would subconsciously not realize is near the coast and that they have a complete right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

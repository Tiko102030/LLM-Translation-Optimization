The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nollans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to build a new three-bedroom house in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other houses in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only under the condition that the Nollans agreed to grant a common right of use of the territory between the line of high tide and the dike behind their house. Thus, people could easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply leisurely walking along the sand. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger house would contribute to "filling of the territory," which "people would no longer subconsciously realize that they are near the coast and that they have a complete right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary in order "to give them the right to seek compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles, merely confirming the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case of the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nolans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to build a new three-bedroom home in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other homes in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on the condition that the Nolans agreed to grant a common right of use over the territory between the high tide line and the dike behind their home. Thus, people could easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply leisurely walking along the sand. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling the territory," which "people would subconsciously not realize that they are near the coast and that they have a complete right to spend time there" (see Iglesias 1996, p. 255).

The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary in order "to give them the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles, merely confirming the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case of the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nollans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to build a new three-bedroom house in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other houses in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on the condition that the Nollans agreed to grant a common right of way over the territory between the high tide line and the seawall behind their home. Thus, people could easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply leisurely walking along the sand. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger house would contribute to "filling up the area," which "people would subconsciously not realize that they are near the coast and that they have the full right to spend time there" (see Iglesias 1996, p. 255).

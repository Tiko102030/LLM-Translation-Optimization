The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the right to use their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to seek compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old regulations. Thus, the Nolans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to build a new three-bedroom home in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other homes in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on the condition that the Nolans agreed to grant a common right of use over the territory between the tidal line and the dike behind their home. This would allow people to easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply strolling leisurely along the sand. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling up the area," which "people would subconsciously not realize is near the coast and that they fully have the right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

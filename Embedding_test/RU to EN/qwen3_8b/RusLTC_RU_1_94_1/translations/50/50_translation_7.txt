The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to seek damages." The court's decision in the First Anglican Church case, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the core positions of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nolans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to build a new three-bedroom home in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other homes in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only upon the condition that the Nolans agree to grant a shared right of use over the land between the high tide line and the dike behind their home. Thus, people could easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply leisurely walking along the beach. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling up the area," which "people would subconsciously not realize that they are near the coast and that they have the full right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

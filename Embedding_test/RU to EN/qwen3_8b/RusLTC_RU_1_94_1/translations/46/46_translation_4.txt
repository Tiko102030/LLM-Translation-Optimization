"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938), and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of judicial decisions from the 1930s, which, in turn, contributed to the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, approved the state's actions regarding the regulation of property rights. The Court upheld the law of the state of Minnesota; this law imposed a moratorium on the forced sale of mortgaged property under an adverse economic situation established by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the Constitution's article limiting state regulation should not be "taken literally as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, whose interpretation is possible only with additional information." He added that the Court should interpret this article "only in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its residents." "The effectiveness of the legislative body's actions should not be evaluated by whether its influence on the contract terms is direct or indirect, but rather by whether the decision was lawful and whether it was achieved through reasonable and appropriate methods." This reflects a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:
The country's economic situation may result from the temporary limitation on the enforcement of contract terms being consistent with the true meaning and purpose of the article, and thus not exceeding the state's reserved right to protect vital societal interests. Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful restriction of the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority. At the same time, whether the legislative body conducts a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not interfere.

"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938) and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of judicial decisions from the 1930s, which directly contributed to the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, approved the state's actions regarding property regulation. The Court upheld the Minnesota law, which imposed a moratorium on the forced sale of mortgaged property during an established adverse economic situation. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, yet it remains one of the Constitution's main articles, whose interpretation is possible only with additional context. He added that the Court should interpret this article "in accordance with the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of its residents." "The effectiveness of legislative action should not be evaluated by whether it directly or indirectly affects the terms of the contract, but rather by whether the decision was lawful and whether it was achieved through reasonable and appropriate methods." This reflects a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:
The country's economic situation may result from temporary limitations on the enforcement of contract terms being consistent with the true meaning and purpose of the contract's article, thus not exceeding the state's reserved right to protect society's vital interests. Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful restriction on the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in a manner that does not violate legislative authority. At the same time, whether the legislative body conducts a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not interfere.

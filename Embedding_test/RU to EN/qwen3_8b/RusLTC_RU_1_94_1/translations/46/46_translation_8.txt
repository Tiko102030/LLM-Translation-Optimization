"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938), and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of judicial decisions from the 1930s, which directly contributed to the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in response to the country's economic instability, approved state actions regarding the regulation of property rights. The Court upheld the Minnesota statute, which imposed a moratorium on the forced sale of mortgaged property under established adverse economic conditions. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal precision as a mathematical formula, but it remains one of the Constitution's main articles, whose interpretation requires additional context. He added that the Court should interpret this article "in accordance with the reserved state right to protect the vital interests of its residents." "The effectiveness of legislative action should not be evaluated by whether it directly or indirectly affects the terms of the contract, but rather by whether the decision was lawful and whether it was achieved through reasonable and appropriate methods." This reflects a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status for private property, Hughes concluded that: "The economic situation in the country may result from the temporary limitation on the enforcement of contractual terms being consistent with the true meaning and purpose of the article, thus not exceeding the reserved state right to protect society's vital interests... Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful restriction on the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in a manner that does not violate legislative authority... At the same time, whether the legislative body conducts a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not interfere."

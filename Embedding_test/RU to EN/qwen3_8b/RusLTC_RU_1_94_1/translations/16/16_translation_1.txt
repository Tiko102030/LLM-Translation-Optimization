Local authorities used the right of eminent domain over private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners. The term "right of eminent domain" was first introduced by the Dutch scholar Hugo Grotius in 1625 and was later incorporated into English law by John Locke, who established it as the foundation of "natural law," as the consent of the entire population, which granted all powers to the state's legislative bodies. (see Igel 1996, p. 8). American judges and legal interpreters fully expanded the concept, merely based on instrumental considerations. Denying any inviolability of private property rights, the 1791 Vermont Constitution stated: "Private property should be subject to public use when necessity requires it" (see Eli 1998, p. 33; author's notes).

In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment codified in the United States Constitution the principle of property confiscation. According to this amendment, confiscation without compensation of slave property redistributed nearly $3 billion, half of the total wealth of Southern states. Thus, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Governmental intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widespread belief that government has a moral duty to promote a thriving economy and care for the poor. For example, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and limited the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Hendlin and Hendlin 1947; Berger 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right of compulsory private property expropriation to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

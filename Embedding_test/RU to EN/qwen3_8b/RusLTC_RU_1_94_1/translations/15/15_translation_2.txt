In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment codifies in the United States Constitution the principle of property confiscation. According to this amendment, confiscation without compensation of slave property redistributed nearly three billion dollars, half of the total wealth of the Southern states. As a result, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Governmental intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widely held belief that government has a moral obligation to promote a thriving economy and care for the poor. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and limited the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Henderson and Henderson 1947; Virgin 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right of compulsory private property expropriation to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

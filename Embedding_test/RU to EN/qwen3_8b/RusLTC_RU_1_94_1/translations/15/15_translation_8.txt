As a result, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Government intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widely held belief regarding the moral obligation of government to promote a thriving economy and care for the poor. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and limited the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Hendlin and Hendlin 1947; Berjin 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right to compulsory taking of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

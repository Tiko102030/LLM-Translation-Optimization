In reality, the Thirteenth Amendment codifies in the Constitution of the United States the principle of property confiscation. According to this amendment, the confiscation without compensation of slave property redistributed nearly 3 billion dollars, half of the total wealth of the Southern states. As a result, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Government intervention in matters of private property did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in matters of private property, thus once again confirming the widely held opinion about the moral obligation of the government to promote the development of a thriving economy and support the poor citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited the trade of food products, regulated the level of wages and the possibility of employment, and limited the activities of sellers of alcoholic products, bars, and prostitutes (see Henderson and Henderson 1947; Berger 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right to compulsory expropriation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the Constitution's commerce clause, thereby also violating the recently enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Originally enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the Southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights that protected private property from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded past regulatory powers. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens toward one another and how people must use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice Hale of England's statement that when property "touches the interests of society, it is by law no longer subject to private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when a property owner uses their property in a way that creates an interest for the public, in reality, they themselves desire this, and until that interest disappears, they must accept that now society controls their property, since it is necessary for the purpose of increasing public welfare. If the owner does not wish for society to control their property, they must simply cease its use. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional provisions on contracts, thereby also violating the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment, which protects citizens' rights from governmental interference. Recently enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment is now an inherent civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights that protects private property from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property rights, the Court upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Stating that "the powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "the government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens toward one another and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice Hale of England, stating that when property "touches the interests of society, it is already by law no longer subject to private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when an owner uses his property in such a way that the public develops an interest in it, he himself desires this, and until that interest disappears, he must accept that society now controls his property, since it is necessary for the increase of public welfare. If he does not wish for society to control his property, he must simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the Constitution's commerce clause, thereby also violating the newly enacted 14th Amendment protecting citizens' rights from state interference. Recently enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the 14th Amendment had now become an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights that protected private property from state interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the power of past regulation. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens toward one another and how people must use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice of England Hale's statement that when property "touches the interests of society, it has already, by law, ceased to be a subject of private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when a property owner uses his property in such a way that society develops an interest in it, in fact, he himself desires this, and until that interest disappears, he must simply accept that now society controls his property, since it is necessary for the increase of public welfare. If he does not wish society to control his property, he must simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on state regulation.

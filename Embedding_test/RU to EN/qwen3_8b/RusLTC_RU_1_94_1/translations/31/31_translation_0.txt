The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the Constitution's provisions on contracts, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an inherent civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights that protected private property from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court members. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the scope of past regulation. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and govern the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people must use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Wight cited Lord Chief Justice of England Hale's statement that "when property 'touches the interests of society, it is by law no longer subject to private regulation alone.'" Remembering the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Wight added: "when the owner uses their property in a way that creates a public interest in it, they actually want this, and until that interest disappears, they must accept that society now manages their property, as it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If they do not want society to manage their property, they must simply cease its use. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Wight acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation."

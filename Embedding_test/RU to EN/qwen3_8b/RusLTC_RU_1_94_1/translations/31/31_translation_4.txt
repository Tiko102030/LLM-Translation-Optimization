The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional provisions on contracts, thereby also violating the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Recently enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment is now an essential civil right and constitutional guarantee of property, protecting private property rights from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court members. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property rights, the Court upheld and even expanded past regulatory powers. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens toward one another and how people must use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice Hale of England's statement that when property "touches the interests of society, it is already subject to legal regulation beyond private control." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when an owner uses his property in a way that creates public interest in it, he himself desires this, and until that interest disappears, he must accept that society now manages his property, as it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he must simply cease its use. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

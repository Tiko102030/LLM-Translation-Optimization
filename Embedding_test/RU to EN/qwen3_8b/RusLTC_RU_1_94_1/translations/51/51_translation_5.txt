In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of "a condition regarding the grant of land use permit" does not constitute "expropriation" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general land use "does not serve the purported purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "thus is unconstitutional, and compensation must be provided." In conclusion, Scalia spoke of the new standard: "Because there is no connection between the condition and the cause of the construction restriction, the new purpose transforms into something else, unlike what it was before. Now, the government's main task is to obtain general land use rights without paying any compensation... if the condition specified in the land use permit is not considered a construction ban and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the construction restriction will not be a valid land use rule, but merely a 'present demand.' This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's requirement to review the opinion of the legislative body's members. They argued that 'the proper criterion for consideration... is whether the state can rationally determine whether the adopted measure can achieve the public goal.' However, after these liberal icons retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on state regulation."

In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of a "condition regarding the grant of a land use permit" does not constitute "expropriation" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general land use "does not serve the purported purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "thus is unlawful, and compensation must be provided." In conclusion, Scalia spoke of the new standard: "Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying reason for the construction restriction, the new purpose transforms into something different, unlike what it was before. Now, the government's primary task is to obtain general land use rights without paying any compensation... if the condition specified in the land use permit is not considered a prohibition on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the construction restriction will not be a lawful land use regulation but merely 'a present imposition.' This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall filed a separate opinion. They rejected Scalia's argument to reconsider the position of the legislative body members. They argued that 'the appropriate criterion for review... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the adopted measure can achieve the public purpose.' However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation."

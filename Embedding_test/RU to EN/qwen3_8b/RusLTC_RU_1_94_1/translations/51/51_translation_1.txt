In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of a "condition regarding the grant of land use permit" does not constitute "expropriation" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general land use "does not serve the purported purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "thus is unlawful, and compensation must be provided." In conclusion, Scalia spoke of the new standard: "Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying cause of the construction restriction, the new purpose transforms into something else, unlike what it was before. Now, the government's primary task is to obtain the general right to land use without paying any compensation... if the condition specified in the land use permit is not considered a prohibition on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction is not a lawful regulation of land use, but merely 'a present imposition.' This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's argument to reconsider the position of the legislative body members. They argued this because 'the appropriate standard for consideration... is whether the state can rationally determine whether the adopted measure can achieve the public purpose.' However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation."

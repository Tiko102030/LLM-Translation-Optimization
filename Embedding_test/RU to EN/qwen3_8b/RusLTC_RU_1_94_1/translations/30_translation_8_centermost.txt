The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could provide the developing railway network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system, established in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the real aims of the bankers and owners of the iron and railway companies, whose ownership was precisely sponsored by government agencies. They wanted new laws that would expand the public's authority in regulating property. They achieved remarkable success in securing the law on an eight-hour workday and the law establishing railroad rates. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were sent to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of popular radicalism at that time. The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional clause on contracts by voiding existing agreements, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from government interference.

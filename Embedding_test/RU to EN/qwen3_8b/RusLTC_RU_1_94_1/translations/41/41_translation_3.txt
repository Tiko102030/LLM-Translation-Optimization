It appears that the basis of the aforementioned case lies in John Locke's views on absolute property rights. The case also mentioned the final decision in the Mann case, which was issued by Chief Justice Wait. However, attention must be paid to the last statement previously considered: "this is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously re-established the instrumental vision of property rights, which declared the previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. Holmes had already expressed his opinion in the Lochner case. Beginning his speech with the assertion that "the decision in the case was issued according to the principles of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy most of the country's population," he added: "The right of each person to act as they wish for as long as they desire, provided, of course, that they do not infringe upon the right of others to do the same, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, the postal service, the laws of any of the states, or other municipal institutions that take their money to achieve goals that seem necessary, regardless of whether they want it or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike how Lochner despotically protects intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require achieving a balance. After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law."

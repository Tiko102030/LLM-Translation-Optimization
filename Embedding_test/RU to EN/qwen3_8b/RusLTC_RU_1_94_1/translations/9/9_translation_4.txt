Citing the Bible, Locke argues that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property also limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose has he given us this? For our enjoyment. A person has the right to convert as much of a thing into his own property as he can use for the necessities of his life before it is spoiled. Anything beyond these limits exceeds his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," gaining power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee access to the wealth of society for all. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these considerations, including the well-known radical American thinker, Thomas Paine.

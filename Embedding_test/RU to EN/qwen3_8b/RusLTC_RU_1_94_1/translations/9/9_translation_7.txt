Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property also limits the extent of this property. "God gave us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason, confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did He give us this? For our enjoyment. A man has a right to convert into his own property as much as he can use for the necessities of his life before the thing is spoiled. Whatever goes beyond these limits exceeds his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for destruction or ruin. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," gaining power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee access to the wealth of society for all. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these considerations, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

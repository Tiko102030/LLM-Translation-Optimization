Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature that grants us property in this manner also limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason, confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did he give us this? For enjoyment. A person has the right to convert, through their labor, as much of a thing into their property as they can use for the necessities of their life before it is spoiled. What exceeds these limits surpasses their share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for destruction or ruin. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," gaining power over others. Therefore, limitations on property accumulation are necessary to guarantee everyone access to society's wealth. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, later joined by other authors, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature that grants us property in this manner also limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason, confirmed by inspiration. But why did He give us this? For our enjoyment. A person has the right to convert a thing into their property through their labor as much as they can use it for their own needs before it is spoiled. And anything beyond these limits exceeds their share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for destruction or ruin. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," and to wield power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to ensure everyone's access to the wealth of society. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, later joined by other authors, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

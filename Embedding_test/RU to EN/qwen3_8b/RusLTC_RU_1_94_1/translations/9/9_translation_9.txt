Citing the Bible, Locke argues that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property also limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did he give us this? For enjoyment. A person has the right to convert what they labor into their property as much as they can use for their life's necessities before the object is spoiled. What goes beyond these limits exceeds their share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," gaining power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee everyone access to the wealth of society. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these considerations, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature that grants us property in this manner also limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did he give us this? For enjoyment. A person has the right to appropriate as much property as they can use for their own needs before the object is spoiled. What exceeds these limits surpasses his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for destruction or ruin. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportionate and unequal ownership of land," and to wield power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee everyone access to the wealth of society. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these arguments, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

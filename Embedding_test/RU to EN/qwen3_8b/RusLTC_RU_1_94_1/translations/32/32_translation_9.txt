Examining more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Wait acknowledged several limitations on state regulation. "For the use of regulated property, you may receive compensation," and Wait argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "It is assumed that any law," he stated, "is constitutional. Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it is clearly evident. If there is doubt, the expressed will of the legislative body should be supported by a majority." Acknowledging that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to seek redress "at the polling stations, not in court." Property rights advocates were shocked by the open reading of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field thought otherwise and stated that "the principle on which the majority's opinion relies... undermines private property rights." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be under the control of the majority members of its legislative body." The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights are now also one of society's interests, another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer require any constitutional guarantee.

Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for unlawful activities, dumped factory wastewater into sewers, prohibited the production of hazardous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and storage facilities without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as pharmaceutical production and livestock processing, as well as other economically significant areas not directly related to health issues, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84–94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials resorted to more drastic measures. For example, in 1872, the street commissioner of Jersey City and twenty-five armed police officers entered the premises of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company, a fertilizer plant, and began "mitigating" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary components with them (see Novak 1996, pp. 226).

Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory sewage into sewers, prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and storage facilities without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, as well as other economically significant areas not related to health, such as the right to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials began to resort to force. For example, in 1872, the city commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the territory of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and began to "reduce" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

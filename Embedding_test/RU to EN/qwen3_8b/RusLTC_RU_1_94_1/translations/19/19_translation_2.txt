Local authorities regularly seized homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory wastewater into sewers, banned the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private residences and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance not related to health issues, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sale, and production (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials resorted to more drastic measures. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the fertilizer plant owned by the Manhattan Fertilizing Company and began "mitigating" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying the machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, pp. 226).

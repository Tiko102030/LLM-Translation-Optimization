Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory wastewater into sewers, prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and storage facilities without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as pharmaceutical production and livestock slaughter, as well as other economically significant areas not directly related to healthcare, such as the rights to trade and purchase, to sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84–94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials began to strike. For example, in 1872 in Jersey City, the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the premises of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and initiated the "reduction" of harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, pp. 226).

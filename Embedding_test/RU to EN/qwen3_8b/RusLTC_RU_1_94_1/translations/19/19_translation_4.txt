Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory wastewater, prohibited the production of hazardous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance not related to health, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials resorted to striking. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the city commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the territory of the Manhattan Fertilizing fertilizer plant and began "reducing" the harm caused by the plant's activities, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

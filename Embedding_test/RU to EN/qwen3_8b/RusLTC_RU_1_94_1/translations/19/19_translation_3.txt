Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory sewage into waterways that "endangered people's health," prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private residences and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as pharmaceutical production and livestock slaughter, as well as other economically significant areas not directly related to healthcare, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, p. 84–94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials resorted to strikes. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a city commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the premises of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and began "reducing" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary components with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

The Mann case decision showed that property rights had become one of society's interests, another instrument for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantees. Amid growing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked stronger reactions from those fearing that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events at Haymarket Square in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the "right of the anointed of God" had not allowed limitations on police powers. The spread of non-intervention policies reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum swings once again in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unrestrained throughout all civilized worlds. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to determine what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of the discontented and their evident power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, conservatives increasingly feared that a more despotic and less reasonable despotism might emerge than any that had existed before. In the late nineteenth century, when opposing the views of the "numerous army of the discontented," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

The Mann case decision showed that property rights were now one of society's interests, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantees. Against the backdrop of increasingly growing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked stronger emotional reactions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events at Haymarket Square in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings had not allowed limitations on police powers. The spread of the policy of non-interference reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled throughout the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to determine what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their evident power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the conditions of universal suffrage, conservatives increasingly feared that a more despotic and less reasonable form of autocracy might emerge than any that had existed before. In the late nineteenth century, when expressing opposition to the views of "the numerous army of dissatisfied people," advocates and judges intended to sanctify private property.

The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights had now become one of society's main concerns, another instrument for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantees. In the context of increasing social unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked stronger reactions from those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events at Haymarket Square in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of the anointed of God had not allowed the police's powers to be limited. The spread of the policy of non-intervention reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum has swung back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared illegal, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled throughout the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to determine what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the development of universal suffrage, conservatives increasingly feared that a more despotic and less reasonable despotism might emerge than any that had existed before. In the late nineteenth century, when opposing the views of "the numerous army of dissatisfied people," advocates and judges intended to sanctify private property.

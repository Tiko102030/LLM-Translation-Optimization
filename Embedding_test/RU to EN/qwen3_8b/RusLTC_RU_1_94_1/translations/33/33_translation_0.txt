The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now also one of society's interests, becoming another tool for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantees. Against the backdrop of increasingly growing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked a stronger outburst of feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings did not allow limiting police powers. The spread of the non-intervention policy reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unrestrained in all civilized worlds. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to determine what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power in enforcing their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the development of universal suffrage, conservatives feared even more that a despotism more oppressive and less reasonable than any that had existed before might emerge. In the late 19th century, when opposing the views of "the numerous army of dissatisfied people," advocates and judges intended to sanctify private property.

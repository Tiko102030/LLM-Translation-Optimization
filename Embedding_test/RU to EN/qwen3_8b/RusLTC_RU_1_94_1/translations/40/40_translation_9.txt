The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner era could logically have been defined based on other judicial decisions that affirmed property rights. In the case of "Adkins v. Children's Hospital" (1923), the Court invalidated a minimum wage law for the District of Columbia, ruling that this law constitutionally confiscated the property of workers and firms, violating their "freedom of contract," which should be "a general rule that cannot be taken away." Shortly before considering the Adkins case, the Supreme Court made another historic ruling that seemingly entirely concerned the protection of property rights. In the case of "Pennsylvania Coal Co. v. Mahon" (1922), the Court declared Pennsylvania's law unconstitutional because this law, in order to preserve the subsurface, restricted the operations of coal companies. The Court held that the general rule would be that regulation of property may be carried out only to a certain extent, but if it goes too far, it would constitute taking property.

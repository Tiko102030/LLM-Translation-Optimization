Representative Lemuel Shaw of the Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts supported the legislative authority dealing with the regulation of property within Boston Harbor and prohibiting construction beyond this wharf, and he spoke about the rights of the police as follows: "We consider this to be an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every property owner, regardless of whether they possess an exclusive right to it or not, manages it with the full responsibility implied, so that its use should not cause harm to other individuals who also have the right to derive enjoyment from their own property, nor to society as a whole. All property in this community... is either fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules applicable to it may be necessary for achieving the public good and general welfare. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to the action of appropriate restrictions necessary to ensure that others are not harmed by the use of this property, and to the action of legal rules, since legislative authority... may assume that this is a necessary and reasonable solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: 'It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits of its manifestation.' Indeed, by using universal, typical phrases such as 'public interest,' 'internal structure of the state,' 'implied right,' and 'obligation of the supreme authority to protect all,' Shaw established that the police authorities may possess a broader set of tools to achieve their objectives. Southern state governments resorted to the help of the police to introduce laws limiting the use of property, for example, prohibiting slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they established unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation, which limited the sale of property to African Americans or their employment in certain types of professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of property and labor for African Americans once again demonstrates the understanding of property at that time. (See Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955.)

Lemuel Shaw, a representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Court, supported the legislative authority dealing with the regulation of property within Boston Harbor and prohibiting construction beyond this wharf, and spoke about the rights of the police as follows: "We consider this to be an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every property owner, regardless of whether they possess an exclusive right to it or not, manages it with the full responsibility implied, so that its use should not cause harm to other people who also have the right to enjoy the use of their own property, nor to society as a whole. All property in this society... is either fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules applicable to it may be necessary for achieving the common good and general welfare. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to the action of appropriate restrictions necessary to ensure that no one else is harmed by the use of this property, and to the action of legal rules, since the legislative authority... may assume that this is a necessary and reasonable solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: 'It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits of its manifestation.' Indeed, by using universal, typical phrases such as 'public interest,' 'internal structure of the state,' 'implied right,' and 'the obligation of the supreme authority to protect all,' and 'common good and general welfare,' Shaw established that the police authorities may have a broader set of tools to achieve their goals. Southern state governments resorted to the help of the police to introduce laws limiting the use of property by slaves, for example, prohibiting slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation, which limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activities in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of property and labor rights for African Americans once again demonstrates the understanding of property at that time. (See Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955.)

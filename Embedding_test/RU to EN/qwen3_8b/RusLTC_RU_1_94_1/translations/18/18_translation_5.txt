Chancellor Kent of New York declared in 1826 that "each person possesses such an amount of freedom for acquiring, using, and disposing of their property that it may be compared with a tolerable state of property and mutual rights of others... Although, thus, property is protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as much as may be necessary to prevent abuse of rights that may lead to violation of the rights of another person or of society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the entire 19th century. For example, in 1837, the legislative body in the state of Illinois did not have enough three pages to list all the powers that the city of Chicago administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and restricting "spinning a hoop, playing ball, or launching paper kites... capable of annoying people," the new city acquired the power to "compel the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle wax shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, outhouse, sewing factory, or any other dilapidated, disgusting building or house to clean, remove, or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, p. 3-6).

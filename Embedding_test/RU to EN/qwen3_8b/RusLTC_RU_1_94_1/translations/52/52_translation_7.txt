But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on state regulation. For example, in the case Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required that the property owner in South Carolina be compensated for the damage to his property after a new "Coastal Line Organization" law halted construction on his land. Again expressing his distrust of elected legislators, Scalia warned, as in the Nolan case, that judges must carefully examine the regulations to protect themselves from the "risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in the case Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were tasked with shifting part of the burden of proof from the property owners to the government. The case was brought when the City of Tigard, Oregon administration stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only if she donated part of her land for a public park, pedestrian and bicycle paths to reduce traffic congestion on the roads. Writing for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist asserted that the city administration "did not dispute the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle paths at Dolan's expense was a result of the city administration's need to improve transportation infrastructure." The rulings in the Nolan and Dolan cases signal the arrival of a new era of intensified property regulation. This could lead to property rights returning to the level they were at before the New Deal.

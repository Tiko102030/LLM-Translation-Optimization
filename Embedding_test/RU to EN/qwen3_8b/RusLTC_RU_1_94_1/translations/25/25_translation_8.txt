The New York court used an instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Calender v. Marsh" to dismiss Quicken's claims. The court asserted: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily more or less affect the comfort and property of people; and in the case when the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "this case is based on the principle that the public good must prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise  
The precedent, as seen in the case "Lansing v. Calender," does not prohibit the government from improving the state's infrastructure at the expense of adjacent property owners, shifting the obligation to some individuals to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case "Torp v. Rutland and Ballston Railroad" (1855), Chief Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to protect their tracks, because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own property in such a way as not to harm others' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas)." The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property.

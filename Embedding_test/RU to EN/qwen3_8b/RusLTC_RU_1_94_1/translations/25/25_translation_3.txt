The New York court employed an instrumental approach used in the Massachusetts case "Calender v. Marsh" to dismiss Quicken's claims. The court asserted: "Any significant public improvement necessarily and to some extent affects the comfort and property of people; and in the case when the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "this case is based on the principle that the public good should prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise

Not prohibiting the government from improving the state's infrastructure at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedent law, as in the case of "Lansing v. Calender," shifted the obligation to some people to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case "Torp v. Rutland and Ballston Railroad" (1855), Chief Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to protect their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas)." The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property rights.

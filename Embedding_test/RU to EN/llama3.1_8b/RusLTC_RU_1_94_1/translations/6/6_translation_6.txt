There exists an "undeniable" right to property of the worker because: Although land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, yet... the labor of his body and the work of his hands by strict account belong to him. Whatever man then withdraws from that state in which nature created and preserved this subject, he combines it with his labor and joins to it something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... For since this labor is undeniable property of the worker, no one but himself can have a right to what he once joined to it, at least in those cases when there remains sufficient quantity and quality for common use by others. Thus Locke sanctifies property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone's labor, i.e., turning man into a slave. From this follows that the rights of property should owe nothing to society; yet owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

There exists an "undeniable" right to property of the working person because: Although the land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, yet... the labor of his body and the work of his hands are strictly speaking his own. Whatever a man may derive from that state which nature has provided and preserved, he combines it with his labor and joins to it something belonging to him personally, and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is undeniable property of the working person, no one but himself can have rights to what he once joined to it, at least in those cases when there is a sufficient quantity of the same quality remaining for others' use. Thus Locke sanctifies property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone else's labor, i.e., turning a man into a slave. From this follows that property rights should owe nothing to society; nonetheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

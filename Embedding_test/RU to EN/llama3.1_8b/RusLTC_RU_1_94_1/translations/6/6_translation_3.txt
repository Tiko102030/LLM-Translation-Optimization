There exists an "undeniable" right of property for the worker because: Although land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, yet... the labor of his body and the work of his hands are strictly speaking his own. Whatever man may extract from that state in which nature created and preserved this subject, he combines it with his labor and joins to it something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is undeniable property of the worker, no one but him can have a right to what he once joined to it, at least in those cases when there is sufficient quantity and quality remaining for others' common use. Thus Locke sanctifies property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone's labor, i.e., turning man into a slave. From this follows that the rights of property owe nothing to society; yet owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

There exists an "indisputable" right of property for the worker because: Although land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, nonetheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands are strictly speaking his own. Whatever he then extracts from that state in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and adds something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is indisputable property of the worker, no one other than he can have rights to what he once combined with it, at least in those cases when a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for others' common use. Thus Locke sanctifies property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone else's labor, i.e., turning a person into a slave. From this follows that property rights should owe nothing to society; nonetheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

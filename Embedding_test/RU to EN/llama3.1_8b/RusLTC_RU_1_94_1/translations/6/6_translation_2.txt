There exists an "undeniable" right of property for the worker because: Although the land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, yet... the labor of his body and the work of his hands are strictly speaking his own. Whatever man may derive from that condition in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and adds something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is undeniable property of the worker, no one but him can have rights to what he once combined with it, at least in cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for others' common use. Thus Locke sanctifies property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone's labor, i.e., turning man into a slave. From this follows that property rights should owe nothing to society; nonetheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

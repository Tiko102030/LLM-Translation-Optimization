There exists an "undeniable" right to property of the worker because: Although the land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, yet... the labor of his body and the work of his hands by strict account belong to him. Whatever man then withdraws from that state in which nature created and preserved this subject, he combines it with his labor and joins to it something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is undeniable property of the worker, no one but him can have rights to what he once joined to it, at least in those cases when a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for common use by others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone's labor, i.e., turning man into a slave. From this follows that property rights should owe nothing to society; nonetheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

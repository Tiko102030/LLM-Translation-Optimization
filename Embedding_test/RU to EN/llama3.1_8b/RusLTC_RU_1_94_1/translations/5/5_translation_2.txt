The new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of property, but still considers "property rights" as relations between people and objects (see Barzel 1997; Liebke 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relation between people and objects, property exists without human society; for Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Like the relations between people and objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic value because it is a reward for labor that shapes the non-human material world.

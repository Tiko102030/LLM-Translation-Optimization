According to the court ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe on the rights of other people and which could not be achieved with its original composition." The court recognized that zoning regulations for environmental protection "must be reasonable", but "we believe that preventing violations of public rights by introducing restrictions on private property use in its original purposes is justified". There was no "exemption" from property, as there was in the case of Pennsylvania Coal. This was because "using property without changing the natural composition of its soil is allowed by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in a court battle over "exemptions" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's counsel Antonin Scalia, the Court had already made several decisions as early as 1987. For example, in the case of "First Evangelical Lutheran Church v. County of Los Angeles" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after a regional flood control agency prohibited the restoration of a flooded church tent camp. The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using their property" was necessary in order for it to "give them the right to compensation".

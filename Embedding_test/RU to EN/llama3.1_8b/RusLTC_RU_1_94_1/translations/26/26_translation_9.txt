As the principle is applied, access to consideration of social interpretation of property is provided. Judge Redfield urged that the limitations on property rights be recognized because, in his words, actions taken by one owner can affect others even if they occur on their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where the interests of different people's property are at stake and both sides complain about the neighbor's interference with their territory (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions related to the subject of property were re-examined in the 19th century to promote entrepreneurial activity even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained but was now used to protect entrepreneurs from acting according to the requirements of passive neighbors who want their property left untouched. Now, the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of wrongful actions against someone else's property but rather as an inability of owners to use their own property. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for harm caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case of Palmer v Mulligan (New York State, 1805).

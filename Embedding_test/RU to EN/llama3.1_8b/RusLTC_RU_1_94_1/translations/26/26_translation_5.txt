As the principle is applied, access to consideration of social interpretation of property is provided. Judge Redfield urged that the limitations on property rights be recognized because, in his words, actions taken by one owner affect others even when they occur on their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting interests of different people are at stake and both sides complain about interference from their neighbor (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions related to property were re-examined in the 19th century to promote entrepreneurial activity even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" was preserved but now used to protect entrepreneurs from acting according to the requirements of passive neighbors who want their property left untouched. Now, the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of wrongful acts against someone else's property, but rather as an inability of owners to use their own property. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for harm caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case of Palmer v. Mulligan (1805) in New York state.

As the principle is applied, access to consideration of social interpretation of property is provided. Judge Redfield urged recognition of limitations on property rights because, in his words, actions taken by one owner can affect others even if they occur on their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests are at stake and both sides complain of interference from neighbors (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions related to property were re-examined in the nineteenth century to promote entrepreneurial activity even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" survived but was now used to protect entrepreneurs from acting according to passive neighbors' requirements that their property remain untouched. Now, the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of wrongful acts against someone else's property, but rather as an inability of owners to use their own property. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for harm caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case of Palmer v Mulligan (1805) in New York state.

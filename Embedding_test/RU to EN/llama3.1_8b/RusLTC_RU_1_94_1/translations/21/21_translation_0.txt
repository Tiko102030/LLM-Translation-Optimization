The representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Court, Lemuel Shaw, supported legislative authority dealing with issues of property regulation in Boston Harbor and prohibiting building construction outside this harbor area, and spoke about police rights as follows: We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every owner of property, regardless of whether they have exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all implied responsibility so that its use should not cause harm to other people who also have the right to enjoy their own property, nor to the community as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is either fully or partially owned by the government, and may be subject to those general rules necessary for achieving the common good and general welfare. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to corresponding limitations necessary to confirm that others have not been harmed by its use, and to established law rules, since legislative authority... can assume it is a necessary and reasonable decision. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits on its manifestations." Indeed, when using universal, typical phrases such as "public interest", "internal arrangement of the state", "implied right" and "the duty of higher authority to protect all" and "common good and general welfare", Shaw established that police agencies can have a wide range of tools at their disposal in achieving their goals. Governments of southern states resorted to police help to introduce laws limiting the use of property by slaves, for example, prohibiting slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they introduced unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activity in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of property and labor for African Americans once again demonstrates what was understood about property at the time. (See Cohen 1991; Lichtstein 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955).

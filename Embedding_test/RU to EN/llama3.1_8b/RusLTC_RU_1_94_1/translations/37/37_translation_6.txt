The Louisiana Supreme Court had ruled that "the loss of private rights in this case is without significance due to the general profit and trade turnover of the commercial organization" (see Novak 1996, p. 230-31). Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the US Supreme Court, where they were represented by Field's predecessor, John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant secretary of war (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell had ruled in Dred Scott that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later he used the main provisions of labor legislation to convince the court to protect a citizen's right to property. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans ordinances "brought us back to the time when the inquisitive eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery". The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and lawyers therefore feared growing pressure for an expanded regulatory complex and a redistribution of income, property, and power.

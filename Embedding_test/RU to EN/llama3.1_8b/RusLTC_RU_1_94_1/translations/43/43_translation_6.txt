In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy as has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894, "Privilege, Criminal Intent and Design", Holmes based himself on the aforementioned representation of the harm caused by competition, thereby showing the general necessity for an instrumental approach and the principle of balancing interests, especially in cases where judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At a Supreme Court hearing in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glucose Co. v. United States Glucose Co." "In modern society," he declared, "each of its parts is closely connected with another, and if something affects one of them, then the others will feel it to some extent. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the end of legislative power comes, it is not enough simply to show that there has been some progress in the development of law, i.e., when it will already be possible to intervene in trade or existing transactions."

The Mann decision showed that property rights were now also one of the interests of society, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of increasingly growing unrest in society, this approach was increasingly alarming to conservatives and caused a greater explosion of emotions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that occurred on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent lawyer Christopher Tiedeman (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of God's anointed did not allow for limiting police powers. The spread of non-interference policy reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms". But now: the political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unconstitutional, and now it is needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled in all civilized countries. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to establish what wage workers should receive for their labor, and how many hours they should work daily...

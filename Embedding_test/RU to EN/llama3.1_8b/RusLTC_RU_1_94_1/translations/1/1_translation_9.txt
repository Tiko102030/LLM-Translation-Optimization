Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it can support affected neighbors and limit the activity of the given owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, this reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of air and water loss or the cost of payment that must be paid to the enterprise so that it no longer discharges waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not have privileges because they would have to reduce pollution levels, or they would have to buy permission from neighbors to pollute. Some question whether property rights have a significant impact on income distribution, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social result, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment buy permission from their neighbors or should neighbors pay those who pollute the environment to stop doing so. Pollution of the environment will occur where costs of reducing its negative consequences are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and it will be stopped when costs of reducing the negative effect are less. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient result regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the right to air and water.

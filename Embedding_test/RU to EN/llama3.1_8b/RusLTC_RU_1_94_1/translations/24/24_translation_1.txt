Calender filed a claim for damages caused to him during the repair of the street, as a result of which his house's foundation in Boston was damaged. His attorney cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle to achieve compensation. But the defense side rejected the use of an English precedent with an instrumental explanation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances that had developed in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim" filed by Calender "is satisfied, then we will have to check the correctness of repairs for all highways in our country". This argument found resonance with judges who rejected Calender's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's proposal for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was undoubtedly bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public facilities. "Those who buy property next to a road - the court said - should take into account future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." Because in that case the compensation amount would double compared to the initial cost. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case "Lansing v. Smith Quackenbush" a claim was filed against the state of New York for damage due to the fact that docks fell in price after building a new bay in Albany. The New York court used an instrumental approach, used in the case "Calender v. Marsh" of Massachusetts State to reject Quackenbush's demands.

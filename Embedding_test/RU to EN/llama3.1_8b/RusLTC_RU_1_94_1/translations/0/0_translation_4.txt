INTRODUCTION: The concept of property in economic theory and public practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ in how they define the concept of property, and it is often difficult to draw boundaries. Some believe that the right to property extends to objects such as land and trees, including the most ephemeral ones, such as living nature and running water. Others supplement this definition by stating that the right to property also applies to objects such as ideas (patents), and some also include humans in this category as slaves. Societies also differ depending on the rights held by owners of property; a set of privileges related to responsibility for actions taken towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection of property, because each use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, as it involves human relationships; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when polluting the environment, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; laws prohibiting pollution limit the rights of this property but protect the property of neighbors. Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to neighboring property.

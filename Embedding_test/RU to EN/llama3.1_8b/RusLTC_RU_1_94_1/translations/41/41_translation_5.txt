It appears that the underlying views of J. Locke regarding absolute property rights are at the basis of this case. The final decision in the Mann case was also mentioned, which was made by Chief Justice Waite. However, attention should be drawn to the last statement considered earlier: "it is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., once again consciously reinstated an instrumental view of property rights that declared unconstitutional fifty years of judicial practice. In the Lochner case, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Beginning his speech with the fact that "the decision was made according to the principles of economic theory, which however does not please a large part of the population," he added: The right of each person to act as they see fit for as long as they wish, provided that the rights of others are not violated, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal management, or laws of any state or municipal institution that takes their money to achieve goals that seem necessary regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner who despotically defends intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require a balance. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to start a new case for an alternative law.

It appears that the case in question is based on John Locke's views regarding absolute property rights. The Mann decision was also mentioned in this case, which was rendered by Chief Justice Waite. However, attention should be drawn to the last statement considered earlier: "it is merely a matter of degree." This limitation, written by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously reinstated an instrumental view of property rights that declared unconstitutional fifty years of judicial practice. In the Lochner case, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Beginning his speech with the fact that "the decision was made according to the principles of economic theory, which however does not please a large part of the population," he added: The right of each person to act as they wish for as long as it is desired, provided that the rights of others are not violated, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal management, or laws of any state or municipal institution that takes their money to achieve goals that seem necessary regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner who despotically defends intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require a balance. After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

A similar situation was in the case of Jacobson and Judge Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right to "work where he wants; earn a living by any lawful means; pursue his chosen profession and for this purpose conclude all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals." After the Allgeyer decision, the peak of judicial activity in explaining motives was established eight years later in the case of Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. In court, Judge Peckham resolutely stated that "the law necessarily affects the right to contract between employer and employee," which is a violation of "common law on making a contract related to his business" which in turn is "protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution." Recognizing that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior", Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary reason for interfering with human freedom or his right to make a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker". In conclusion, Peckham spoke about what was only a question of which evil was less: state power to issue laws or human personal freedom and freedom to make contracts. We believe that the limit of police powers has been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner can be logically identified from other court decisions that asserted property rights.

A similar situation was in the case of Jacobson v. Massachusetts, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right to "work where he pleases; earn a living by any lawful means; pursue his chosen profession and for this purpose enter into all contracts which may be proper, necessary, and essential to the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives." After the Allgeyer decision, the peak of judicial activism in explaining motives was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. In court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law necessarily affects the right to contract between employer and employee," which is a violation of "the general right to make contracts relating to his business" which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Recognizing that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary occasion for interference with human liberty or his right to make a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham said it was simply a question of which evil was less: state power to issue laws or human freedom and the right to make contracts. We believe that the limits of police authority were reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner can be logically identified from other court decisions that asserted property rights.

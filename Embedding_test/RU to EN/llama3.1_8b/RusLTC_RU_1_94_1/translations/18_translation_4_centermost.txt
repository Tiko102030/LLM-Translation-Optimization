The Chancellor of New York, Kent, in 1826 declared that "every man has such an amount of liberty to acquire, use and dispose of his property as may be comparable with the acceptable state of the property and mutual rights of others... Although thus protected by law... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use to the extent necessary to prevent abuse of the right which may lead to a violation of another person's or the entire society's rights." To protect public welfare and consider the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in Illinois in 1837, the legislative body did not have enough space on three pages to list all the powers that would be held by the administration of Chicago regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and limiting "the spinning top, playing ball or launching paper snakes... capable of annoying people", a new power appeared in the city to "force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, privy, sewing factory or any other old, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6).

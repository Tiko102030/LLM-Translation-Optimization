Examining the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society more closely, Wait acknowledged several limitations on state regulation. "You can get compensation for using regulated property," and Wait argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "It is assumed that any law - he said - is constitutional. Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it is clear and so. If there is doubt, then the expressed will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." Acknowledging that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to seek compensation "at polling stations, not in court". Property rights defenders were shocked by the open reading of police powers in Mann's case. Judge Stephen Field thought otherwise and said that "the principle on which the majority opinion relies... undermines private property rights." "If it becomes law - he warned - then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of the majority members of its legislative body".

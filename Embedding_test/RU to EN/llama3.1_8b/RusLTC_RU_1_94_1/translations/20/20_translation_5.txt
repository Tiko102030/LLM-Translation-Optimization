Such actions were justified by the concept of "police powers" of the state or what Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., writing for the Supreme Court in the case of Hudson County Water Company v. McCarter (1908), called "restrictions applicable to property, in the interest of all society." The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that "by public police and economy I mean due regulation and internal arrangement of the state, whereby persons within the country, as well as members of a well-ordered family, are bound to conform to rules of decorum and good breeding, assist their neighbours, and be orderly, industrious, and peaceable in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case of Vanderbilt v. Adams (1827), Judge Woodworth of New York State more fully described police power, saying: "The supreme authority in society may, and must, prescribe how private rights to property shall be exercised.... Police powers are based on implied rights and duties of the highest authority to protect all by means of laws established for that purpose, so as to increase the welfare of society as a whole.... Such governmental power should exist in every well-regulated community." In the case of Commonwealth v. Alger (1851), Justice Lemuel Shaw of Massachusetts Supreme Court supported legislative authority dealing with issues of property regulation on Boston Harbor and prohibiting building structures outside this harbor, and spoke of police powers as follows: "We think it an established rule, arising from the very nature of a well-organized civil society, where every owner of property, whether he has or has not exclusive right to it, exercises it with all the implied responsibility that its use should not injure other persons having like right to enjoy their own property, nor the community at large."

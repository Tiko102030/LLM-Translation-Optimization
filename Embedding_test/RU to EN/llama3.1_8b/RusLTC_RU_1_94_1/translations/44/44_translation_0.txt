When it will be possible to intervene in existing or already concluded trade agreements is unclear. The scope of activity must be defined and quantitative differences must be determined. In the case of Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Mahon, Holmes was more like a Trojan Horse than a champion of sacred property rights - instead of considering property rights as natural rights, he based "the principle of eminent domain" on establishing balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must constantly be used in a "reasonable" manner, but the notion of what is "reasonable" remains vague at this point. If actions go "too far", then compensation will be necessary; however, it is up to judges to determine what constitutes "going too far," taking into account public needs and special circumstances. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully weigh and balance competing interests of both parties. Property owners should not, in turn, provoke everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "the government can hardly continue its activities if some degree of property question is not resolved".

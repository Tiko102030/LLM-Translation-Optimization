When it will be possible to intervene in trade or existing transactions is unclear. The scope of activity must be defined and quantitative differences determined. In the case of Pennsylvania Coal Company v Holmes, Holmes was more of a Trojan Horse than a champion of sacred property rights - instead of considering property rights as natural rights, he based "the principle of expropriation" on establishing balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must be constantly used in a "reasonable" manner, but the notion of what is "reasonable" remains vague. If actions go "too far", then compensation will be necessary; however, it is up to judges, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, to determine what constitutes "going too far". Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully weigh and balance competing interests of both parties. Property owners should not, in turn, provoke everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "the government can hardly continue its activities if some degree of property question is not resolved".

In the case of Pennsylvania Coal Holmes was more like a Trojan Horse than a winner - defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "principle of taking" property based on establishing balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must be constantly used "reasonably", but the idea of what is "reasonable" remains vague at this point. If actions go "too far", then in this case compensation for damage will be necessary; however, it is judges who, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, will determine what means "too far". Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case judges must carefully weigh and balance competing interests of both parties. Owners should not, in turn, provoke everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "the government is hardly likely to be able to continue its activities if the question of property is not resolved to some extent".

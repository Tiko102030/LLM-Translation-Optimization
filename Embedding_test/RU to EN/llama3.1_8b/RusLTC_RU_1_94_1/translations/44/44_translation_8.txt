When it will be possible to intervene in trade or existing transactions is unclear. The scope of activity must be defined and quantitative differences must be determined. In the case of Pennsylvania Coal Company v Holmes, Holmes was more like a Trojan Horse than a champion of sacred property rights - instead of considering property rights as natural rights, he based "the principle of expropriation" on establishing balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must be constantly used "reasonably", but the idea of what is "reasonable" remains vague at this point. If actions go "too far", then in this case compensation would be necessary; however, it's up to judges to determine what "too far" means, taking into account public needs and special circumstances. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case judges must carefully weigh and balance competing interests of both parties. Property owners should not, in turn, provoke everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "the government is unlikely to be able to continue its activities if the question of property is not resolved to some extent".

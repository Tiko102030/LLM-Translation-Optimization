Despite constitutional guarantees regarding property, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the 13th Amendment enshrines in the United States Constitution the principle of confiscation of property based on wealth. Pursuant to this amendment, uncompensated confiscation of slave owners' property redistributed nearly $3 billion, half of all southern state wealth. As a result, emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of confiscation of property. Government intervention into private property did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby only once again confirming the widely held opinion that it is a moral duty of government to promote the development of a thriving economy and support low-income citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, as well as restricted the activities of liquor vendors, bars, and prostitutes (see Handlin and Handlin 1947; Bergen 1989; Novak 1996; Lorie 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used their right to compulsory expropriation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

Conclusion: The process of developing property rights Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Note remains an existing law. As soon as it was put up for discussion, the Supreme Court approved almost all bills regulating property and recognized that the powers of elected members of the legislative body should be "as vast as the country's economic needs." At the same time, the Supreme Court has established itself as a watchdog over private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. To them it equated the right to pollute one's territory or engage in discrimination based on gender, racial, or religious characteristics, which are now above all property owners' claims. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that the right to property stands on two pillars: the essence of harm and property as an instrument, not a sacred right. Recognizing the dual nature of losses, courts consider any claimed "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," all of which must be balanced. After the principle of balancing interests was approved by Holmes, the Court had to consider each claimed "right" to property in instrumental implementation, since alternating interests would lead to achieving more global public goals. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute and should never become an end in itself.

In the landmark case of Jacobs (1885), the New York State Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on leased premises because it "interfered with the owner's right to receive profit and freely use his property," and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal liberty." Comparing the concepts of individual freedom and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that in its broadest sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only the freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of man to employ his faculties by all lawful means for living and working... Therefore, any laws which weaken or fetter these rights are violations of his fundamental right to liberty. The legislature argued that the decision was based on state police powers and that the measures taken were necessary to "improve public health in the country." For advocates of government regulation, it boded ill that the court was considering this requirement, and subsequently concluded that it lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was found that the law actually "did not concern itself with questions of public health at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the guise of police regulations, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily invaded." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of a time of unprecedented judicial activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the case of Godcharles v. Wigeman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment by lawful money (not company stock).

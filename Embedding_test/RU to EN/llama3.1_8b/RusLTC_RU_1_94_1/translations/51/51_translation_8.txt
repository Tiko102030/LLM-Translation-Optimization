Recording his speech for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that presenting "conditions regarding the granting of a land-use permit" was not an "exaction" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general public access to the beach "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting people's visual access to the beach" and "is therefore improper, and compensation must be made." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard:

Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying reason for limiting construction, the new goal is transformed into something else entirely different from what it was before. Now the main task of the government is to obtain a general land-use permit without paying any compensation.... If the condition specified in the land-use permit is not considered a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a legitimate land-use rule but rather "a genuine extortion." This time Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's call to reconsider the legislative body's view. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the adopted measure can achieve a public purpose."

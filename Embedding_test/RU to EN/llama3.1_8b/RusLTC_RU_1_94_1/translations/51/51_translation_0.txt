Recording his speech for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that presenting "conditions regarding the grant of easement" was not an "exaction" according to the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general public access "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting the ability of people to visually access the beach" and "is thus improper, and compensation must be made." In conclusion Scalia spoke about the new standard: Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying reason for limiting construction, the new goal is transformed into something else entirely unlike what it was before. Now the main task of the government is to obtain general public access without paying any compensation.... If the condition provided in the land-use permit is not considered a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the limitation on construction will not be a valid land-use rule but will only be "a genuine extortion." This time Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's call to reconsider the legislative body's view. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure taken can achieve a public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

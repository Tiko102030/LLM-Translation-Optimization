Recording his speech for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that presenting "conditions regarding the grant of easement" was not an "exaction" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general public access to the beach did not serve the intended purpose of protecting people's visual access to the beach and thus was "unconstitutional, and compensation must be made." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard:

Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying reason for restricting construction, the new goal is transformed into something else entirely different from what it was before. Now the primary concern of government is to obtain general public access without paying any compensation.... If the condition specified in the land-use permit is not considered a ban on building and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on building will not be a valid land-use rule but rather "a genuine extortion". This time Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's call to reconsider the legislative body's view. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration.... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure taken can achieve a public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

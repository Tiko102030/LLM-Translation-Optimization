In the case of Nollan v California Coastal Commission, the Supreme Court declared the commission's decision unconstitutional and established a new criterion for judicial review of land-use regulations. Recording his speech for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the presentation of "conditions regarding the granting of a coastal development permit" was not an "exaction" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general public access to the beach "does not serve the purported purpose of protecting the ability of people to visually access the beach" and "is thus improper, and compensation must be made." In conclusion Scalia spoke about the new standard:

Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying reason for limiting construction, the new goal is transformed into something else entirely different from what it was before. Now the primary concern of government is to obtain a general public access easement without paying any compensation.... if the condition specified in the land-use permit is not considered a prohibition on building and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the limitation on construction will not be a valid land-use regulation but rather "a real extortion". This time Justices Brennan and Marshall dissented. They rejected Scalia's call to reconsider the legislative body's opinion. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration.... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure taken can achieve a public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

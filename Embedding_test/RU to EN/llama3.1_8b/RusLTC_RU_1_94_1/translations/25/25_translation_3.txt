The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the case of "Calder v. Jones" from Massachusetts to dismiss Quackenbush's demands. The court argued: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect people's comfort and property; and when the preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of the damage should be considered as part of the price paid for future benefits from this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "this case is based on the principle that the general good must prevail over private interests." State support for private entrepreneurship

Not prohibiting the government from carrying out state beautification at the expense of adjacent property owners, the precedent law, as in the situation of "Lansing v. Calder", shifted the responsibility to some people to cover expenses related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). In later cases, such instrumental norms became private instances. For example, in the case of "Torpey v. The Rutland and Burlington Railroad" (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of all people's lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace, as well as the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle provides access to a social interpretation of property.

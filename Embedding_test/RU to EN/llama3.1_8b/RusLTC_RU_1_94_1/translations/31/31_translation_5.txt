Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision regarding contracts, which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from state interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had become an inalienable civil right and constitutional guarantee of property, safeguarding private property rights from government interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the Court's 1877 members, who rejected the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Speaking about how "powers are inherent to any supreme authority," the Court ruled that "the government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other, as well as how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England in a statement that, when property "affects public interests, it ceases by law to be subject solely to private regulation."

Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision regarding contracts and also infringed on the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment's protection of citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an inalienable civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property from government interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of members of the 1877 Court who rejected the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee of property and even expanded the powers of previous regulation. Speaking about what "powers are inherent to any supreme authority," the Court ruled that "the government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England in a statement that when property "affects the interests of society, it by law ceases to be subject to private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the highest law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: When the owner of property uses it in such a way that there arises an interest in it from society, he actually wants this and until the interest disappears, he must only submit to the fact that now society manages his property because it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

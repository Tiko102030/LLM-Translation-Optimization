Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision regarding contracts, which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment was now an inalienable civil right and constitutional guarantee of property, which protected private property from government interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the Court's 1877 members. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court affirmed and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Speaking about what "powers are inherent in any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "the government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England's statement that, when property "affects public interests, it ceases by law to be a subject of private regulation." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the highest law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: When a property owner uses their property in such a way that there arises an interest in it from the public, he actually wants this and must only submit to the fact that society now manages his property, as necessary for increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

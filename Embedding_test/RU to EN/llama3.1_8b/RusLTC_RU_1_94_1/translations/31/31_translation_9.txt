Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision regarding contracts and also breached the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an inalienable civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property from government interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the Court's 1877 members. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Speaking about what "powers are inherent in any sovereign power", the Court ruled that "the government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England's statement that, when property "affects public interests, it by law ceases to be a subject of private regulation".

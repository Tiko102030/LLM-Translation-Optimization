Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision regarding contracts which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an inalienable civil right and constitutional guarantee of property, which protected private property rights from government interference. However this view was supported by a minority of the Court's 1877 members who rejected the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Speaking about what "powers are inherent to any supreme authority", the Court ruled that "the government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people must use their property but only when it is necessary for the common good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England in a statement that when property "affects public interests, it by law ceases to be subject to private regulation alone". Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the highest law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: 

when an owner uses their property so as to create interest in it among the public, he actually wants this and until that interest disappears, he must only submit to society managing his property because it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply stop using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on government regulation.

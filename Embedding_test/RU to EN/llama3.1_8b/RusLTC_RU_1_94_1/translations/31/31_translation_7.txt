Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision regarding contracts, which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens from state interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an inalienable civil right and constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property from state interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of members of the 1877 Court, which rejected the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property and even expanded the powers of previous regulation. Speaking about the fact that "powers are inherent in any supreme authority," the Court ruled that "the government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England's statement that, when property "affects public interests, it ceases by law to be a subject of private regulation."

The Supreme Court of the United States also indicated that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property is based on public benefit and instrumental considerations, and in the case of 1875 "Kohl v. United States" ruled that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property - "is a result of political necessity, and it cannot be separated from supreme authority". The right to compulsory expropriation of private property was one example of the use of government power to promote the development of a "well-organized society", to raise the level of quality of "public benefit" or "welfare" through regulations concerning interference with property rights. These regulations reflected common law principles such as: "the welfare of the people shall be the supreme law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Frequently cited in court decisions, these main principles serve as "common law programs for managing a well-regulated society", providing the legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property in order to reflect the mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

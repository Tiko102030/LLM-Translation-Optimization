For the first time, it became a precedent in the case of "Palmer v. Mulligan" (1805) in New York State, where the owner of property downstream brought an action for damages against the dam upstream that had collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: The defendants had the same right to their territory... [the principle of "use your own so as not to harm another"] must be limited in reasonable bounds so as not to deprive a person of the enjoyment of his property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law that provided for certain inconveniences of this character, such that a person who could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river would eventually acquire an exclusive right, at least over a certain distance, regardless of whether he owned the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of that part of the profit it might have obtained as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and it turned out that both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents who were prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. But, at the same time, by eliminating the existing monopoly power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits over indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" in no way depended on J. Locke's ideas about sacred private property. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of them.

For the first time, this became a precedent in the case of Palmer v Mulligan (1805) of New York State, where the owner of property downstream brought an action for damages against the owner of a dam upstream that had collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same right to their territory... The principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' must be limited in reasonable bounds so as not to deprive a person of the enjoyment of his property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for certain inconveniences of this character, such that a man who could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river would eventually acquire an exclusive right, at least over a certain distance, regardless of whether he owned the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, will be deprived of that part of the profit it might have obtained as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, and it so happened that both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents who were forbidden from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. But at the same time, by removing existing monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits over indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" in no way depended on J Locke's ideas about sacred private property. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of them.

For the first time, this principle was established in the case of Palmer v. Mulligan (1805) in New York State, where a property owner downstream brought an action for damages against a dam upstream that had flooded his home. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same right to their territory... The principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' must be limited within reasonable bounds so as not to deprive a person of the enjoyment of their property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law that allowed for certain inconveniences of this character, such that a person who could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river would eventually acquire exclusive rights, at least over a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose part of the profit it could have gained from competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, so that both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents who were prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. However, at the same time, by eliminating existing monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits over indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" in no way depended on J. Locke's ideas about sacred private property. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general affirmation of them.

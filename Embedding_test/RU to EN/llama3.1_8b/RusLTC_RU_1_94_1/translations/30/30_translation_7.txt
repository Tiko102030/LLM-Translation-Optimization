The iron and steel industries, which had preferential tariffs on their products, were the only ones that could provide a developing network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was recently centralized banking system, founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzell 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the true goals of bankers and owners of iron and railway companies, whose property was just sponsored by state authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority over issues of property regulation. They achieved a remarkable success in obtaining legislation establishing an eight-hour working day and a law setting railroad tariffs. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, as a result of which these bills were sent to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's decision in the case "Munn v. Illinois" (1877) greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about growing interest in radical ideas that were popular at the time. The plaintiffs argued that by abolishing existing contracts, the law violated the clause on contracts in the constitution and also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference.

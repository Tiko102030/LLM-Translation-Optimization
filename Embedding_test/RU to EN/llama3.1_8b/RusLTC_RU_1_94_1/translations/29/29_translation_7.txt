The Civil War served as the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers wanted more. For example, labor rights defender George McNeill warned that America must "infuse our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F. Breese: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that trust companies are trying to impose on us today. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, but now workers are nothing more than slaves of combined trust companies" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to the fact that workers united and opposed these new demands. Large sums of money were needed for military operations, and closely related to this was the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus, which once again confirmed that a significant financial and industrial capital was needed to support government activities. The iron and steel industries, on which preferential tariffs were established, were the only ones that could provide the developing network of railways.

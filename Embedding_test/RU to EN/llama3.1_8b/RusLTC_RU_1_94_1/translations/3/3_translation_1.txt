The Horwitz (1992), Demsetz (1964); and Dillon (1895) views are that property rights can be seen as a manifestation of the individuality of the owner on one hand, while on the other hand, they can also be viewed as the right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect owners from the consequences of external impacts caused by polluters. However, such an overall comparison of property rights concepts can be misunderstood because these rights primarily restrict the rights of other people. Property rights give individuals state-approved privileges for monopolistic management of resources, as well as the opportunity to appeal to authorized law enforcement agencies in order to limit the activities of other people. Thus, property significantly limits some benefits due to the fact that government authorities generally support owners more than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a discussion about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather an examination of property regulation that forces one to choose whose property needs to be protected and whose property rights have an advantage in fighting for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so obscure because legal issues are repeatedly discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the US legal system.

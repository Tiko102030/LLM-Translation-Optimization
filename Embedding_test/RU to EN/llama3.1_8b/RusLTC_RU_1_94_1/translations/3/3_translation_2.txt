The Horwitz (1992), Demsetz (1964); and Dillon (1895) views on property rights are based on the idea that protection of property is an expression of individuality of the owner. On the other hand, there is a right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect owners from the consequences of external impacts caused by polluters. However, such a general comparison of concepts of property rights can be understood incorrectly because these rights, for the most part, limit the rights of other people. Property rights give people state-approved privileges for monopolistic management of resources, as well as the opportunity to appeal to authorized law enforcement agencies in order to restrict the activities of other people. Thus, property significantly limits some benefits due to the fact that government bodies primarily support owners of property rather than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a discussion about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather an examination of property regulation that forces one to choose whose property needs to be protected and whose property rights have an advantage in fighting for them. The discussion on democratic rights and their regulation has become so unclear because legal issues are repeatedly discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the US legal system. Thus, according to the federalism system, a single Federal Government is formed, while each state has its own legal system established. Also, at each level of management there is a clear division of executive, legislative and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure compliance with the norms of the Constitution in which they are interpreted.

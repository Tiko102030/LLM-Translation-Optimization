The Horwitz (1992), Demsetz (1964); and Dillon (1895) views are thus considered from one side as the protection of property rights as an expression of individuality of the owner. From another side, the right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect owners of property from the consequences of external impact of polluters is considered. However, such a general comparison of concepts of property rights can be understood incorrectly, since these rights, for the most part, limit the rights of other people. Property rights give people state-approved privileges to monopolize resources, the opportunity to appeal to authorized law enforcement agencies in order to restrict the activities of other people. Thus, property significantly limits some benefits because government authorities support owners of property more than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a discussion about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather an examination of property regulation that forces us to choose whose property needs to be protected and whose property rights have an advantage in fighting for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so unclear because legal questions are repeatedly discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the US legal system. Thus, according to the federalism system, one Federal Government is formed, and a separate legal system is established for each state. Also, on each level of management there is a clear division of executive, legislative, and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure compliance with the norms of the Constitution in which they are interpreted.

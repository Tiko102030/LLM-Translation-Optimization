The Horwitz (1992), Demsetz (1964); and Dillon (1895) views on property rights are based on the idea that protection of these rights is an expression of individuality of the owner. On the other hand, there is a right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect owners from external impacts caused by polluters. However, such a general comparison of concepts of property rights can be understood incorrectly because these rights, for the most part, limit the rights of other people. Property rights give people state-approved privileges to monopolize resources, as well as the opportunity to appeal to authorized law enforcement agencies in order to restrict the activities of other people. Thus, property significantly limits some benefits due to the fact that government bodies primarily support owners rather than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a discussion about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather an examination of property regulation that forces one to choose whose property needs to be protected and whose property rights have an advantage in fighting for them. The discussion on democratic rights and their regulation has become so obscure because legal issues are repeatedly discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the US legal system.

It appears that the underlying basis of this case is John Locke's views on absolute property rights. The Mann decision was also mentioned in this case, and it was rendered by Chief Justice Waite. However, attention should be drawn to the last statement considered earlier: "it is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously re-established an instrumental view of property rights that declared unconstitutional fifty years of judicial practice. In the Lochner case, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Beginning his speech with the fact that "the decision was made according to the principles of economic theory, which however does not satisfy a large part of the population," he added: The right of each person to act as they see fit for as long as they wish, provided that the rights of others are not violated, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal management, or laws of any state or municipal institution that takes their money to achieve goals that seem necessary regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner who despotically defends intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require a balance. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to start a new case for an alternative law.

The United States v. Carolene Products (1938) and the United States v. Darby (1941), were prototypes of 1930s court decisions that served as a cause for the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. The most notable was the 1934 court decision in Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell, where the Supreme Court, in light of the economic instability in the country, approved state actions regarding property regulation. The Court upheld a Minnesota statute; this law imposed a moratorium on the deprivation of the right to redeem pledged property under conditions established by legislative order due to an unfavorable economic situation. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the article of the Constitution limiting government regulation should not be "considered with literal accuracy as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, whose interpretation is possible only with additional information." He added that the Court should interpret this article only "in accordance with the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of its residents". "The effectiveness of legislative activity must be evaluated not by direct or indirect influence on contract conditions, but whether a lawful decision was made and reasonable and appropriate methods were used to achieve it." This speaks to a social and instrumental approach to determining property rights, which in turn was transformed into a constitutional principle. Denying any privileged status for private property, Hughes concluded that: The economic situation in the country can be the result of how temporary restrictions on the enforcement of contract conditions will be coordinated with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus not exceed the reserved right of the state to protect vital interests of society.... Since this article is not an unconditional and completely unauthorized restriction on the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body should make decisions so as not to violate legislative authority.... In any case, whether a reasonable or unreasonable policy will be conducted by the legislative body, the Court will no longer concern itself with it.

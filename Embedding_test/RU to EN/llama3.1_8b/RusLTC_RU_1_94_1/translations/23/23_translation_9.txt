Regarding this case, as with all American law, property rights initially necessary for achieving public goals were immediately abolished as soon as they hindered the achievement of these goals. At the beginning of the 19th century, state authorities, striving to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads and railroads, canals. When building infrastructure in the state had to bear indirect costs, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved property. In accordance with the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would have borne responsibility for indirect costs, significantly increasing the cost of financing inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding independent entrepreneurs' liability for the consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property, indirect costs on building state infrastructure were sharply reduced. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calder v. Marsh", establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calder filed a lawsuit to recover damages caused to him during street repairs, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

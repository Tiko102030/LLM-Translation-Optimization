Local authorities regularly confiscated homes where immoral activities took place, filled in the sewers of factories that "posed a threat to public health", banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, without paying any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of industries, including those related to healthcare, such as drug manufacturing and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance but unrelated to healthcare issues, like trade and commerce (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve its public goal, officials would go on strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of a fertilizer factory owned by Manhattan Fertilizing Company and began "reducing" the harm caused by the factory's actions, destroying machinery and taking necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

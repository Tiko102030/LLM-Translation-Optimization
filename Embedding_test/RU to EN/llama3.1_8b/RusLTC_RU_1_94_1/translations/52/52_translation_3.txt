However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed additional restrictions on government regulation. For example, in Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required a property owner in South Carolina to be compensated for damage to his property after a new "Beachfront Management Act" halted construction on his land. Again expressing distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as he had in Nollan, that judges must carefully scrutinize takings for insurance against the risk that private property will be used under the guise of reducing serious harm to society. The Court refined its new standards in Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from owners to government.

Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance with the judges, and lawyers feared growing pressure for expanded regulation, redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has lingered in memory more than Judge Miller's majority opinion, which was later bolstered by arguments supporting police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that Louisiana had violated the fundamental right to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's prescriptions, stating that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, "property is 'the main branch of freedom... in the American sense.'" It took Field twenty years to grasp the court's decision in Slotterhouse v. Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company without a license to conduct business in Louisiana, effectively violating the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was present in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only "the absence of his physical restraint," but also encompassed the right "to work where he pleases; to earn a living by any lawful calling; to pursue his chosen profession and for this purpose to enter into all contracts which may be proper, necessary, or essential to the successful attainment of these ends."

Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges and lawyers feared growing pressure for expanded regulatory measures and redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has lingered in memory more than Judge Miller's majority opinion, which was later bolstered by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that Louisiana had violated the fundamental right to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's prescriptions that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "the primary part of liberty... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to grasp the court's decision in the case of Slotterhouse v. Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in Louisiana, which essentially violated the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was present in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical restraint," but also encompassed the right "to work where he pleases; to earn his living by any lawful calling; to pursue a vocation and to make all contracts which may be necessary and proper for the successful pursuit thereof."

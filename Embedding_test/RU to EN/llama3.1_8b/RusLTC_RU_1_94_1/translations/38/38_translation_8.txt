Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance with the judges and lawyers feared growing pressure for expanded regulation and redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has stuck in memory more than Judge Miller's majority opinion which was later bolstered by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that Louisiana had violated fundamental rights to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property on John Locke's prescriptions that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "the primary part of liberty... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to grasp the court's decision in the case of Slotterhouse v. Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in Louisiana, which essentially was a violation of freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the case of Jacobson and Justice Supreme Court Rufus W. Peckham proved that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical confinement," but also encompassed the right "to work where he pleases; to earn his living by any lawful means; to pursue a desired profession and for this purpose to enter into all contracts which may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned goals."

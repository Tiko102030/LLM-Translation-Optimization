Butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance with the judges, and lawyers feared growing pressure for expanded regulatory measures and redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion has lingered in memory more than Judge Miller's majority opinion, which was later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that Louisiana had violated the fundamental right to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on Locke's prescriptions that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, "property is 'the main branch of freedom... in the American sense of the word'". It took Field twenty years to grasp the court's decision in the case of Slotterhouse v. Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in the state of Louisiana, which essentially violated the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was present in the case of Jacobson, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical restraint", but also encompassed the right "to work where he pleases; to earn his living by any lawful calling; to seek his livelihood in any lawful pursuit and for this purpose to make all contracts which may be proper, necessary and essential to the successful attainment of these ends".

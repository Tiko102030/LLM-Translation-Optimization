In the landmark case of Jacobs (1885), the New York State Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on rented premises because it "interfered with the owner's right to receive profit and freely use his property," and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal liberty." Comparing the concepts of individual freedom and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that in its broadest sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only the freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of man to employ his faculties by all lawful means for living and working... Therefore, any laws which weaken or fetter these rights... are violations of his fundamental right to liberty. The legislature argued that the decision was based on police powers of the state, and the measures taken were necessary to "improve public health in the country." For advocates of government regulation, it boded ill that the court would consider this requirement, which it ultimately concluded lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was found that the law actually "did not concern itself with any questions of public health," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the guise of police regulations, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily invaded." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of an unprecedented era of judicial activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the case of Godcharles v. Wigeman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment by lawful money (and not company stock). The court argued that "the law is a denial of equal rights to employer and employee, more than that, it is an insulting attempt to subject unskilled labor to legislative guardianship," because it prevents workers from selling their labor on any terms they were willing to accept.

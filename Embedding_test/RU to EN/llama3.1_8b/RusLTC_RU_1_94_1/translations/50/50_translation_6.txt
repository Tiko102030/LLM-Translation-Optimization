The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that a "resolution depriving an owner of using their property" was necessary in order to give them "the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rely on constitutional principles but merely confirmed the basic facts of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the 1987 case of Nollan v. California Coastal Commission was a completely different case because it established a new standard for old rules. The Nollans, who owned a coastal plot of land situated between public parks to the north and south of each other, wanted to build a new three-bedroom house in place of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in this district.

The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that a "resolution depriving an owner of using their property" was necessary in order to give them "the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rely on constitutional principles but simply confirmed the basic facts of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the 1987 case of Nollan v. California Coastal Commission was a completely different case because it established a new standard for old rules. As owners of a coastal plot of land located between public parks to the north and south of each other, the Nollans wanted to build a new three-bedroom house instead of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in this district. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on condition that the Nollans agree to grant an easement for common use of the territory between the high tide line and the dike located behind their home.

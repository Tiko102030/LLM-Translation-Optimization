At the end of the 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of "a multitude of dissatisfied people", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Thielemann, they demanded that all the fullness of state power be limited by establishing a boundary on police authority. State courts took the initiative in determining new grounds for the sacred, rather than instrumental approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhammer v. People" (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into effect, all those who were selling this liquor at the time lost their properties. However, the procedure for seizing property did not go through the established legal process. For the first time, discovering that the concept "established by law" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance".

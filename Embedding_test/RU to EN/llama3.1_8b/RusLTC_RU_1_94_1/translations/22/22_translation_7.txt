The property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as that owned or used by Americans of European descent. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from southern states gained the right to own property, but their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and the extent of African American participation in it. The same instrumental approach used by northern state governments to limit property rights regarding clean air and water was also used in southern states to maintain the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined for achieving public goals, but with different objectives. Redistribution of property rights to stimulate economic growth By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach used by him in court to annul the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge Company. He did this in order to personally manage the only bridge over the Charles River, which connects Boston and Cambridge (see the case of "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge company experienced some difficulties.

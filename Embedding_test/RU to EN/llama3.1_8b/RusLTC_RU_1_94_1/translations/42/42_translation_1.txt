Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to start a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the opinion of most legislative assembly participants about natural rights and J. Locke's beliefs, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that would rather mean the end of productivity and prosperity than protection of the sacred notion of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental view or a choice of another public goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used for defending property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he elaborated on how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even attempt to compensate a person for all the harm caused to him... Anyone can found any company, and later, in order to survive in competition, he will poach his competitor's clients, and possibly even bankrupt them."

This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, which were later joined by other authors, including Thomas Paine, a well-known American radical thinker. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret such popular social inferences from Locke's reasoning. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke created "no slightest difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). Considering property from the perspective of human relationships and objects may also present it as a socially neutral aspect: accumulation of results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. However, recognizing social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person can harm others, such as in cases of monopolization of resources previously accessible to all.

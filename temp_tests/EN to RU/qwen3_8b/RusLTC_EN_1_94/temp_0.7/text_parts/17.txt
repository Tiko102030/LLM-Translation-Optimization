We shall be thrown back to the improvements of the last century, and obliged to stand still." Here, as throughout American law, property rights established to advance social goals were overturned when they impeded these objectives. Eager to promote industry and commerce, early nineteenth-century governments embarked on a campaign of road, canal, and railroad building. Infrastructure construction involved indirect costs, damages to adjacent property, and a loss in value to property bypassed by improvements. Under the legal doctrine of sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas, or "use your own so as not to injure another," investors would have been responsible for the indirect costs, significantly increasing the financial cost of improvements. Changing the meaning of the sic utere doctrine, to free entrepreneurs from responsibility for the effects of their actions on neighboring property, would dramatically reduce the cost of infrastructure building.

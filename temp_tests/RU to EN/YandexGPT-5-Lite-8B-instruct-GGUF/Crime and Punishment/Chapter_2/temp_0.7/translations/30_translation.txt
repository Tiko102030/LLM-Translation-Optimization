 — Ilya Petrovich! — the clerk began solicitously, but stopped to wait for a moment, because an agitated lieutenant could not be restrained any other way than by the arms, as he knew from his own experience. As for the stately lady, at first she trembled so much from thunder and lightning; but strangely enough: the more numerous and stronger the curses became, the more gracious her appearance became, the more charming her smile directed at the formidable lieutenant grew. She shuffled in place and squatted endlessly, impatiently waiting for them to finally let her have her say, and she waited. “I didn’t make any noise or fights, Mr. Captain,” she suddenly started chattering like spilled peas, with a strong German accent, albeit fluently in Russian, “and there was no scandal at all, but they came drunk, and I will tell you everything, Mr. Captain, it’s not my fault… I have a noble house, Mr. Captain, and noble treatment, Mr. Captain, and I always, always didn’t want any scandal. But they came completely drunk and then asked for another drink again, and then one lifted his legs and started playing the piano with them, and that’s not good at all in a noble house, and he broke the whole piano, and there is absolutely no manners here at all, and I said so. And he took a stick and started pushing everyone from behind with it. And then I quickly called the janitor and Karl came, he took Karl and punched him in the eye, and Henriette also got an eye poked, and they hit me five times on the cheek. And that’s so indelicate in a noble house, Mr. Captain, and I shouted. And he opened the window to the canal and stood at the window, like a little pig, squealing; and that’s shameful.”

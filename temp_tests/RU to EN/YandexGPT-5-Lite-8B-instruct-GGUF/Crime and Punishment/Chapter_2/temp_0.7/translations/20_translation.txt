 It was some particularly disheveled man with a fixed idea in his look. "You won't find out anything from this because he doesn't care," Raskolnikov thought. — Go there, to the clerk, said the scribe and jerked his finger forward, pointing to the very last room. He entered that room (the fourth in order), cramped and packed with people — a crowd, somewhat more neatly dressed than in those rooms. There were two ladies among the visitors. One was in mourning, poorly dressed, sitting at the table opposite the clerk and writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and ruddy-red, spotted, prominent woman, and dressed far too ostentatiously, with a brooch on her chest the size of a tea saucer, was standing off to one side and waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed his summons to the clerk. The latter glanced at it briefly, said "wait" and continued to deal with the lady in mourning. He breathed more freely.

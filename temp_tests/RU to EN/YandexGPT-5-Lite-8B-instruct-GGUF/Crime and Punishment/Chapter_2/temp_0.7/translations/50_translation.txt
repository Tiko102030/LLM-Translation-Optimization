 Further, in the recess of the courtyard, the corner of a low, sooty, stone shed peeked out from behind the fence, apparently part of some kind of workshop. There must have been some kind of establishment here, coachmaking or locksmithing, or something like that; coal dust was visible everywhere, almost from the gate. “That’s where I would throw it and leave!” — he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the yard, he stepped through the gate and immediately saw a trough fastened near the gate right there on the fence (as is often done in such houses where there are many factory workers, artels, cab drivers, etc.), and above the trough, also on the fence, was written in chalk the usual joke in such cases: “Stopping a wagon is prohibited here.” Therefore, it’s already good that there’s no suspicion that he came and stopped. “Here I could throw everything all at once into a pile and leave!”

After looking around again, he had already stuck his hand in his pocket when suddenly he noticed a large unhewn stone near the outer wall, between the gate and the trough, where the distance was about an arshin wide (perhaps about a pound and a half), adjoining directly to the stone street wall. Beyond this wall there was a street, a sidewalk, you could hear passers-by scurrying around, of which there were always many; but no one could see him behind the gate, except if someone came from the street, which, however, could very well happen, so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grasped its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength and turned the stone over. A small depression formed under the stone; immediately he began to throw everything from his pocket into it.

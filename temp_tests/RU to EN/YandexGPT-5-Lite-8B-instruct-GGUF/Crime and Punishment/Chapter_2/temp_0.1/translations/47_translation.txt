 Then he left the room, this time even leaving it wide open. He walked quickly and firmly, and although he felt all broken up, his consciousness was with him. He was afraid of being chased, afraid that in half an hour, in a quarter of an hour, perhaps instructions to follow him would already be issued; therefore, at any cost, it was necessary to hide the evidence for the time being. It was necessary to sort things out while there was still at least some strength and some reasoning left... Where to go? This had been decided long ago: “Throw everything into the ditch, and make an end of it.” He had resolved this even in the night, in his delirium, in those moments when, he remembered, he had tried several times to get up and go: “quickly, quickly, and throw everything away.” But throwing things away turned out to be very difficult. He had been wandering along the embankment of the Catherine Canal for half an hour or more, and several times glanced at the accesses to the ditch where he met them. But it was impossible to even think about carrying out his intention: either rafts were standing right by the accesses and laundresses were washing clothes on them, or boats were moored, and people were swarming everywhere, and from all sides of the embankments, from all directions, one could see, notice: it was suspicious that a person had deliberately gone down, stopped, and was throwing something into the water. What if the cases didn't sink but floated?

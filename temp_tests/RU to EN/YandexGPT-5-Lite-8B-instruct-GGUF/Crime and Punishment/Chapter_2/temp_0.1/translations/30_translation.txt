 — Ilya Petrovich! — the clerk began solicitously, but stopped to wait for a while, because an enraged lieutenant could not be restrained any other way than by the arms, as he knew from his own experience. As for the stout lady, at first she trembled so much from thunder and lightning; but strangely enough: the more numerous and stronger the curses became, the more gracious her appearance became, the more charming her smile turned towards the formidable lieutenant. She minced on the spot and squatted continuously, impatiently waiting for the moment when she would finally be allowed to have her say, and waited. “I didn’t make any noise or fighting, Mr. Captain,” she suddenly chattered away like spilled peas, with a strong German accent, although smartly in Russian, “and no, no scandal, but they came drunk, and I will tell you everything, Mr. Captain, it’s not my fault… I have a noble house, Mr. Captain, and noble treatment, Mr. Captain, and I always, always did not want any scandal. And they came completely drunk and then asked for three drinks again, and then one lifted his feet and started playing the piano with them, and that’s not good at all in a noble house, and he broke the piano completely, and there’s no manners here at all, and I said so. And he took a stick and started pushing everyone from behind with it. And then I quickly called the janitor and Karl came, he took Karl and gave him a black eye, and Henriette also got a black eye, and they hit me in the cheek five times. And that’s so indelicate in a noble house, Mr. Captain, and I screamed. And he opened the window onto the canal and stood at the window, like a little pig, squealing; and that’s shameful.”

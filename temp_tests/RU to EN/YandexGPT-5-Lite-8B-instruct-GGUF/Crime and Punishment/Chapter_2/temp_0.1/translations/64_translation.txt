 But Raskolnikov was already going out into the street. On Nikolaevsky Bridge he had to come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. He was hit hard on the back with a whip by the coachman of one carriage because he almost got run over by the horses, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The lash so angered him that he, jumping to the railings (for some unknown reason he was walking in the very middle of the bridge where they drive and not walk), ground his teeth angrily and snapped them. Laughter could be heard all around, of course. 
“And rightly so!”
“Some kind of swindler.”
“It’s known that someone will pretend to be drunk and deliberately run into the wheels; and you have to answer for him.”
“That’s how they make a living, dear sir, that’s how they make a living…” [21 - That's how they make a living — Newspapers of that time often told about Petersburg бедняках who made a living by deliberately throwing themselves under the wheels of carriages in order to receive assistance for injuries (see, for example, "Golos", 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he was standing at the railings and still looking senselessly and angrily after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt someone putting money in his hands.

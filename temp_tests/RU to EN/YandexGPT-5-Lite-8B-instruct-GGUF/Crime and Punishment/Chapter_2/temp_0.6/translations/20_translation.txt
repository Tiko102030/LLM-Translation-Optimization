 It was some particularly disheveled man with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't find out anything from this because he doesn't care," Raskolnikov thought. — Go there, to the clerk, said the scribe and pointed forward with his finger, pointing to the very last room. He entered that room (the fourth in order), cramped and packed with people—folk a little cleaner dressed than those in the other rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One was in mourning, poorly dressed, sitting at the table opposite the clerk and writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and ruddy-red, spotted, a prominent woman, and dressed much too richly, with a brooch on her chest as big as a tea saucer, was standing aside and waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed the summons to the clerk. He glanced at it briefly, said "wait" and continued working with the lady in mourning. He breathed more freely.

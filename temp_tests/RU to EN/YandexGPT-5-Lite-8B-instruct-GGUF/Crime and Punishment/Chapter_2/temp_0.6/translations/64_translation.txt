 But Raskolnikov was already going out into the street. On Nikolaevsky Bridge he had to come to his senses once again due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. He was hit hard on the back with a whip by the coachman of one carriage because he almost got run over by the horses, even though the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The whiplash made him so angry that he, jumping to the railings (for some unknown reason he was walking in the very middle of the bridge where vehicles travel rather than people walk), grinded his teeth angrily and snapped them. Laughter could be heard all around, of course. 
“And rightfully so!”
“Some kind of cheat.”
“Obviously, he pretends to be drunk and deliberately gets in the way of the wheels; and you have to take responsibility for him.”
“That's how they make a living, уважаемый, that's how they make a living…” [21 — That's how they make a living…— Newspapers of that time often told about Petersburg бедняки who made a living by deliberately throwing themselves under the wheels of carriages in order to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, “Golos,” 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood at the railings and was still бессмысленно and angrily looking after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt someone putting money in his hands.

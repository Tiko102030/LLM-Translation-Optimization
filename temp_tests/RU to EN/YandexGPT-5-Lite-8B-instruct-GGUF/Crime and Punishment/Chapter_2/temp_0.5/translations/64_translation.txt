 But Raskolnikov was already going out into the street. On Nikolaevsky Bridge he had to come to his senses again completely due to one very unpleasant incident for him. He was hit hard on the back with a whip by the coachman of a carriage because he almost fell under the horses, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The blow of the whip angered him so much that he, jumping to the railings (for some reason unknown to him he was walking in the very middle of the bridge where they drive, not walk), ground his teeth злобно and snapped them. Laughter was heard all around, naturally.  
“And rightly so!”  
“Some kind of sharper.”  
“It’s known that someone will pretend to be drunk and deliberately get in the way of the wheels; and you have to take responsibility for him.”  
“That’s how they make a living, dear sir, that’s how they make a living…” [21 - That's how they make a living — Newspapers of that time often told about Petersburg бедняки who made a living by deliberately throwing themselves under the wheels of carriages to receive assistance for injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).]

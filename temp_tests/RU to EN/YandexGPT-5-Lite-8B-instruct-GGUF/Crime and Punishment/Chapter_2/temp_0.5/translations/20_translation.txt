 It was some particularly disheveled man with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't find out anything from this because he doesn't care," Raskolnikov thought. — Go there, to the clerk, — said the scribe and pointed forward with his finger, indicating the very last room. He entered that room (the fourth in order), cramped and crammed full of people — a crowd, somewhat more neatly dressed than those in the other rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One in mourning, poorly dressed, was sitting at the table opposite the clerk and writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and purple-red, spotted, a noticeable woman, and dressed much too luxuriously, with a brooch on her chest the size of a tea saucer, was standing off to one side waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed his summons to the clerk. He glanced at it briefly, said "wait" and continued working with the lady in mourning. He breathed more freely.

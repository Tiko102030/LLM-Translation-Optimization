 But Raskolnikov was already going out into the street. On Nikolaevsky Bridge he had to come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. He was sharply lashed on the back with a whip by the coachman of a one-horse carriage because he nearly got run over by the horses, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The whip stroke made him so angry that he, jumping to the railings (for some reason unknown to him, he was walking in the very middle of the bridge where they drive, not walk), ground his teeth and clicked them злобно. Laughter could be heard all around, of course. 
“And for a good reason!” 
“Some kind of cheat.” 
“You see, they pretend to be drunk and deliberately go under the wheels; and you have to take responsibility for them.” 
“That’s how they make their living, уважаемый, that’s how they make their living…” [21 - That's how they make their living… — Newspapers of that time often told about Petersburg бедняки who made a living by deliberately throwing themselves under the wheels of carriages in order to receive assistance for injuries (see, for example, «Голос», 1865, № 65).] But at that moment, as he stood at the railings and was still looking senselessly and angrily after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt someone shoving money into his hands.

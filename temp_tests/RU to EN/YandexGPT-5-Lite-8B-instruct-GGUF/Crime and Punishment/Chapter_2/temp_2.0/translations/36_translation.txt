 — Pardon me, Captain, — he began rather familiarly, addressing Nikodim Fomichev all of a sudden, — understand my situation... I'm even ready to ask them for forgiveness if I have shirked anything on my part. I am a poor and sick student, crushed (he said that very word: "crushed") by poverty. I'm an ex-student because now I can't support myself, but I will get the money... My mother and sister are in the -th province. They will send it to me, and I... will pay. My landlady is a kind woman, but she got so angry that I lost my lessons and haven't paid for four months that she doesn't even send me lunch... And I don't understand at all what this bill of exchange is! Now she demands payment from me on this loan letter, what can I pay her, judge for yourselves!.. — "But this isn't our business..." the clerk was about to remark again...
—  Let me, let me, I completely agree with you, but please also let me clarify, — Raskolnikov resumed, addressing not the clerk but Nikodim Fomichev again, trying with all his might to address Ilya Petrovich too, even though the latter stubbornly pretended to be rummaging through papers and contemptuously ignored him —  let me also explain that I have lived with her for about three years, since my arrival from the province and before... Before... in any case, why shouldn't I admit it, I promised right from the beginning that I would marry her daughter, a verbal promise, completely free... She was a girl... in fact, she even liked me... although I wasn't in love... in short, youth, that is, I mean to say, my landlady used to give me a lot of credit then and I led a somewhat such life... I was very frivolous...
—  We don't need such intimacies from you, sir, and there's no time, — Ilya Petrovich interrupted грубо and triumphantly, but Raskolnikov passionately stopped him, although he suddenly found it extremely difficult to speak. —  But let me partly tell everything... what happened and... in turn... though this is unnecessary, I agree with you, to tell —  a year ago this girl died of typhus, while I remained a tenant as I was, and the landlady, when she moved to her current apartment, told me... and said amicably... that she had complete faith in me and everything... but would I like to give her this loan letter for one hundred and fifteen rubles, all that she considered my debt. Please sir: she specifically said that as soon as I gave this paper, she would lend me credit again as much as she liked and that never, ever, —  those were her own words —  would she use this paper until I paid myself... And now, when I have lost my lessons and I have nothing to eat, she serves the claim for payment... What can I say now? — These sensitive details, sir, do not concern us, — Ilya Petrovich impudently cut in, —  you must give feedback and an obligation, but that you happened to be in love there and all these tragic passages, we don't care about it at all.

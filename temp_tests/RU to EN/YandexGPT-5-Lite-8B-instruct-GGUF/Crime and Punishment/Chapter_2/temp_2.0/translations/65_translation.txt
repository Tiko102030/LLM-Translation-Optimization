He looked: an elderly купчиха in a hat and goat shoes, and with her a girl in a шляпке and with a green зонтик, probably a daughter. “Take this, батюшка, for Christ's sake.” He took it, and they walked past. The amount of money was two copecks. By their dress and appearance, they could easily mistake him for a beggar, a real street grosh collector, and the giving of a whole two-copeck piece he probably owed to the stroke of the whip, which had moved them to pity. He clutched the two copeck in his hand, walked about ten steps and turned his face towards the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was without a slightest cloud, and the water was almost blue, which is so rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which from no point is outlined better than when looking at it from here, from the bridge, not reaching twenty steps to the chapel, was shining so brightly, and through the clear air even each of its decorations could be clearly made out. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the stroke; one restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

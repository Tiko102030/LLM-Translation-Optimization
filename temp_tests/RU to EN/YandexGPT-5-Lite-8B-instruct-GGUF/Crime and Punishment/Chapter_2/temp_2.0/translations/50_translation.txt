 Further, in the recess of the courtyard, the corner of a low, sooty, stone barn peeped out from behind the fence, evidently part of some kind of workshop. There must have been some sort of establishment here, carriage or locksmith, or something like that; everywhere, almost from the gates, there was a lot of coal dust darkened. “That’s where I would drop it and leave!” — he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the yard, he stepped through the gate and immediately saw, right by the gate, a trough attached to the fence (as often happens in such houses, where there are many factory workers, artel members, taxi drivers, etc.), and above the trough, right there on the fence, an ever-present joke in such cases was written in chalk: “Stopping a carriage is forbidden here.” Therefore, it’s already good that there is no suspicion that he entered and stopped. “I could drop everything all at once in a heap somewhere and leave!”

Looking around once more, he had already put his hand in his pocket when suddenly, near the outer wall, between the gate and the trough, where the entire distance was an arshin wide, he noticed a large unhewn stone, perhaps about a pound and a half in weight, adjacent directly to the stone street wall. Beyond this wall was the street, the sidewalk, one could hear passers-by scurrying around, of whom there were always many; but no one could see him beyond the gate, unless someone came from the street, which, however, could very well happen, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top tightly with both hands, gathered all his strength and turned the stone over. A small depression formed under the stone; immediately he began to throw everything out of his pocket into it.

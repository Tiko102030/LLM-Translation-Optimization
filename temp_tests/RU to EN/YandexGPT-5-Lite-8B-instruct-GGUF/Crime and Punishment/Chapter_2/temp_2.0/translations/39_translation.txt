 The clerk began to dictate the form of a usual reply in such cases to him, that is, “I cannot pay, I promise then (someday), I will not leave the city, I will not sell or give away my property, and so forth.” — “You can't write, the pen is falling out of your hand,” noted the clerk, peering curiously at Raskolnikov. — “Are you ill?” — “Yes... my head is spinning... go on!” — “That’s all; please sign.” The clerk took the paper away and turned to others. Raskolnikov handed back the pen, but instead of getting up and leaving, he rested both elbows on the table and gripped his head with his hands. It was as if a nail were being driven into his crown. An odd thought suddenly occurred to him: to stand up right away, go to Nikodim Fomich and tell him everything from yesterday, every last detail, then go to the apartment together and show them the things in the corner, in the hole. The urge was so strong that he had already risen from his seat to carry it out.

 It was some particularly disheveled man with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't find out anything from this because he doesn't care," Raskolnikov thought. — Go there, to the clerk, said the scribe and pointed ahead with his finger, pointing to the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), cramped and packed with people — a crowd that was somewhat better dressed than those in the other rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One in mourning, poorly dressed, was sitting at the table opposite the clerk and writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very stout and purple-red, with spots, a prominent woman, and wearing something already overly elaborately dressed, with a brooch on her chest, as big as a teacup saucer, stood off to one side and was waiting for something. Raskolnikov thrust his summons at the clerk. The latter glanced at it briefly, said: "Wait" and continued to deal with the lady in mourning. He let out a sigh of relief.

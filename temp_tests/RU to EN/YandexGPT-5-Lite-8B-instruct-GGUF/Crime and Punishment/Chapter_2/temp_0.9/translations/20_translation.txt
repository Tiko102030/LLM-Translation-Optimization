 It was some particularly disheveled man with a fixed idea in his gaze. “You won’t find out anything from this because he doesn’t care,” Raskolnikov thought. “Go there, to the clerk,” said the scribe and pointed forward with his finger, indicating the very last room. He entered that room (the fourth in order), cramped and packed full of people — folk a bit cleaner dressed than those in the other rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One in mourning, poorly dressed, was sitting at the table facing the clerk and writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and ruddy-complexioned, with spots, a noticeable woman, and overdressed in some way, with a brooch on her chest as big as a tea saucer, was standing aside and waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed the clerk his summons. The latter glanced at it briefly, said “wait” and continued to deal with the lady in mourning. He breathed more freely.

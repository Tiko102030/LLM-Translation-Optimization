 But Raskolnikov was already going out into the street. On Nikolaevsky Bridge he had to come to his senses again once due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. He was whipped on the back with a whip by a coachman of one carriage because he almost fell under the horses, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The lash so angered him that he, having отскочил to the railings (it is unknown why he was walking in the very middle of the bridge where they drive and not walk), angrily ground his teeth and snapped them. Laughter раздавался around, of course. “And for a good reason!” “Some kind of rascal.” “It’s known, pretending to be drunk and deliberately getting under the wheels; and you have to answer for him.” “That’s how they make their living, sir, that’s how they make their living…” [21 — That’s how they make their living… — In newspapers of that time, it was often told about Petersburg poor people who made a living by deliberately throwing themselves under the wheels of a carriage in order to receive assistance for injuries (see, for example, “Golos”, 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood at the railings and still бессмысленно and angrily stared after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt someone putting money in his hands.

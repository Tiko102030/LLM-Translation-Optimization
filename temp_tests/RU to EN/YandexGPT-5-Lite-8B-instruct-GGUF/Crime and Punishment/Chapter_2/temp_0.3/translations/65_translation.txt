 But at that moment, as he was standing by the railings and still looking senselessly and angrily after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt someone putting money in his hands. He looked: an elderly купчиха, in a kokoshnik and goat shoes, and with her a girl, in a hat and with a green зонтик, probably her daughter. “Take it, батюшка, for Christ's sake.” He took it, and they passed by. The money was two silver rubles. By their dress and appearance, they could very well mistake him for a beggar, a real street grosh collector, and the giving of a whole two-ruble coin he probably owed to the lash of the whip, which had touched their hearts. He clutched the two-ruble coin in his hand, walked ten steps and turned his face towards the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was without a single cloud, and the water was almost blue, which is so rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which from no point is outlined better than when looking at it from here, from the bridge, not reaching twenty steps to the chapel, shone so brightly, and through the clear air even each of its decorations could be clearly made out. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; one restless and not quite clear thought occupied him now exclusively.

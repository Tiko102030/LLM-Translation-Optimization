 Then he left the room, this time even leaving it wide open. He walked quickly and firmly, and although he felt all broken up, his consciousness was with him. He was afraid of being chased, afraid that in half an hour, in a quarter of an hour, perhaps instructions to follow him would already be issued; therefore, at any cost, it was necessary to hide the evidence for now. It was necessary to sort things out while there was still at least some strength and some reasoning left… Where to go? This had been decided long ago: “Throw everything into the ditch, and make an end of it.” He had resolved this even in the night, in his delirium, in those moments when, he remembered, he tried several times to get up and go: “quickly, quickly, and throw everything away.” But throwing things away proved to be very difficult. He had been wandering along the embankment of the Catherine Canal for half an hour or more, and looked several times at the accesses to the ditch where they met. But it was impossible to even think about carrying out his intention: either barges were standing right by the accesses and laundresses were washing clothes on them, or boats were moored, and people swarmed everywhere, and from all sides of the embankments, from all directions, one could see, notice: it would be suspicious that a person deliberately went down, stopped, and threw something into the water. What if the cases don't drown but float?

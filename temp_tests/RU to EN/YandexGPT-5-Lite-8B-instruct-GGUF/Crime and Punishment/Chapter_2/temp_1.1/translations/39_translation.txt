 The clerk began to dictate to him the form of a response common in such cases, that is, “I can't pay, I promise then (sometime), I won't leave the city, I will not sell or give away my property, etc.” — “You can’t write, the pen is falling out of your hand,” observed the clerk, peering curiously at Raskolnikov. “Are you sick?” — “Yes… dizzy… go on!” — “That's all; sign your name.” The clerk took back the paper and turned to others. Raskolnikov gave up the pen, but instead of getting up and leaving, he rested both elbows on the table and squeezed his head with his hands. It was as if a nail were being driven into his crown. A strange thought suddenly occurred to him: to get up now, go to Nikodim Fomich and tell him everything from yesterday, every detail, then go to the apartment together and show them the things in the corner, in the hole. The impulse was so strong that he had already got up to fulfill it.

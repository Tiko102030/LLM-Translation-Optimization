As for the curvaceous lady, at first she trembled so much from thunder and lightning; but strange thing: the more numerous and stronger the swear words became, the friendlier her appearance became, the more charming her smile turned to the formidable lieutenant. She scurried in place and squatted endlessly, impatiently waiting for them to finally let her have her say, and she got it. “I didn’t make any noise or fight, Mr. Captain,” she suddenly chattered like spilled peas with a strong German accent, albeit fluently in Russian, “and no scandal at all, but they came drunk, and I’ll tell you everything, Mr. Captain, but I’m not to blame…I have a noble house, Mr. Captain, and noble treatment, Mr. Captain, and I never, ever wanted any scandal. And they came completely drunk and then again asked for three drinks, and then one raised his feet and started playing the piano with them, which is not good at all in a noble house, and he broke the whole piano, and there’s absolutely no manners here at all, and I said so. And he took a stick and started pushing everyone from behind with it. And then how I soon became a caretaker called for, and Karl came, he took Karl and hit him in the eye, and Henriette also got her eye hit, and they slapped my cheek five times. And this is so indelicate in a noble house, Mr. Captain, and I cried out. And he opened the window on the canal and stood at the window, like a little pig, squealing; and that’s shameful.”

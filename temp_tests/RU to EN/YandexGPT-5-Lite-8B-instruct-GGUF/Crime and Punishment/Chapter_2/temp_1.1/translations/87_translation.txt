 — Shall we have some tea?
— Some tea, I suppose so.
— Pour it out. Wait, I'll pour for you myself; sit down at the table. He immediately gave orders, poured, then poured another cup, abandoned his breakfast and moved back to the sofa. As before, he took the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him up and began to feed him tea with a teaspoon, again continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if in this process of blowing was the most important and salutary point of recovery. Raskolnikov kept silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite strong enough to lift himself and sit on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to have control over his hands enough to hold a spoon or cup, but perhaps even to walk. But for some strange, almost animal cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength until the time came, to lie low, to pretend, if necessary, that he did not even understand completely, and at the same time to listen and find out what was going on here? However, he could not control his disgust: after gulping down about ten spoons of tea, he suddenly freed his head, pushed the spoon away capriciously and fell back onto the pillow. There were real pillows under his heads now — feather ones with clean cases; he also noticed that and took it into consideration.

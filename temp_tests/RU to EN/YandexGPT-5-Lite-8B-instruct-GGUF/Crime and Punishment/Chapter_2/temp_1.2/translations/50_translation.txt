 Further, in a recess of the yard, an angle of a low, sooty, stone shed peeked over the fence, obviously part of some workshop. There must have been some kind of establishment here, coach-building or mechanic's shop, or something like that; coal dust could be seen blackened almost from the gate itself. "That's where I would throw it all and leave!" — it suddenly occurred to him. Not noticing anyone in the yard, he stepped through the gates and immediately saw a trough attached near the gate, just on the fence (as often happens in such houses with many factory workers, artel members, cabbies, etc.), and above the trough, right there on the fence, was written in chalk an everlasting witticism in such cases: "Stopping a wagon is forbidden here." Consequently, it was already good that there was no suspicion that he had come and stopped. "Here I can throw everything into a pile somewhere and leave!"

Looking around once more, he had already stuck his hand into his pocket when suddenly he noticed a large rough stone near the outer wall, between the gate and the trough, where the entire distance was about an arshin wide (about 70 centimeters), perhaps weighing about a and a half pood (about 24 kg), adjacent directly to the stone street wall. Beyond this wall there was a street, a pavement, one could hear passers-by scurrying around, as there were always quite a few of them; but no one could see him beyond the gate, unless someone came from the street, which, however, was very likely to happen, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed hold of its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength and turned the stone over. Underneath the stone there was a small depression; immediately he began to throw everything out of his pocket into it.

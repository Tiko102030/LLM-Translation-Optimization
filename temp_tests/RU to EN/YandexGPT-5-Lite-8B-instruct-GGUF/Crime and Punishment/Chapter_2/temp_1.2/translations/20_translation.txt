 It was some particularly disheveled man with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't find out anything from this because he doesn't care," Raskolnikov thought. "Go there, to the clerk," said the scribe and pointed forward with his finger, indicating the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), cramped and crammed with people — folk, somewhat cleaner dressed than those in the other rooms. There were two ladies among the visitors. One in mourning, poorly dressed, was sitting at the table opposite the clerk and writing down something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and ruddy-red, spotted, a prominent woman, and overly elaborately dressed, with a brooch on her chest as big as a tea saucer, was standing off to one side waiting for something. Raskolnikov shoved his summons to the clerk. The latter glanced at it briefly, said "wait" and continued dealing with the lady in mourning. He breathed more freely.

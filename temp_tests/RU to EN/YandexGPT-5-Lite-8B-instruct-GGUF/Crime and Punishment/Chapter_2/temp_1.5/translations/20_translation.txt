 It was a particularly disheveled man with an unwavering idea in his eyes. "You won't find out anything from this because he doesn't care," Raskolnikov thought. — Go there, to the clerk, — said the scribe and jerked a finger forward, pointing to the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), cramped and packed with people — people somewhat cleaner-dressed than those in other rooms. There were two ladies among the visitors. One in mourning clothes, poorly dressed, was sitting at the table opposite the clerk and writing down something under his dictation. The other lady, very stout and ruddy-complexioned, with spots, a noticeable woman, dressed rather too luxuriously with a brooch on her chest as big as a teacup, was standing in the corner and waiting for something. Raskolnikov thrust his notice to the clerk. The latter glanced at it briefly, said: "wait" and continued working with the lady in mourning. He let out a sigh of relief.

Local authorities regularly confiscated houses in which immoral activities took place, засыпали wastewater from factories that «endangered people's health», prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals and put out fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, and did not pay any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of sectors of the economy, including those related to healthcare, such as the production of medicines and livestock slaughter, and others that were economically significant and not related to health issues, such as the right to trade and buy, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation failed to achieve a social goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed policemen entered the territory of a fertilizer plant owned by «Manhattan Fertilizing» and proceeded to «reduce» harm caused by the plant's activities by destroying the machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

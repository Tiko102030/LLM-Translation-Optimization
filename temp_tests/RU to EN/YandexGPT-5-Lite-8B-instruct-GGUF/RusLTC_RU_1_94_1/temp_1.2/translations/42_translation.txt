 Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the opinion of the majority of lawmakers about natural rights and on the beliefs of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice, which rather means the end of productivity and prosperity, not the protection of the sacred idea of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or a different social goal selection. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "The Common Law" (1881), he spoke in detail about how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the prerequisites for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even seek to compensate a person for all the harm caused to him... Anyone can found any firm, and furthermore, in order to survive in the competition, he will poach his competitor's customers, and perhaps thereby even ruin him."

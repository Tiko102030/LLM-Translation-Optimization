 This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these discussions, including the well-known American with radical views, Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret such popular conclusions by Locke in society. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, admits that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for protecting property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the position of relations between people and objects, it can also act as a socially neutral side: accumulating the results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. But recognition of the social parameters of ownership also admits a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person can harm others, for example, in the case of monopolization of resources previously accessible to all.

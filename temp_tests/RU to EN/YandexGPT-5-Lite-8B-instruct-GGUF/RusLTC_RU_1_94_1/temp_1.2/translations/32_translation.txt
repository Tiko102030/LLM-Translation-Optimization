 Considering in more detail the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on state regulation. "For the use of regulated property you can receive compensation," and Waite argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "It is assumed that any law," he declared, "is constitutional. Judges cannot declare what laws are unconstitutional, unless, of course, it is clear and so. If there is doubt, then the expressed will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." Recognizing that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to go for compensation "to the polling stations and not to court." Property rights advocates were shaken by the open reading of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field thought differently and said that "the principle on which the majority opinion rests... undermines the rights of private property." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of the majority of its legislative body." The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee.

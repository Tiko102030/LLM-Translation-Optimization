 INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and social practice
Society has property. Economic systems differ in terms of the definition they give to the concept of property, and often it is difficult to determine its boundaries. Some believe that property rights extend to objects such as land and trees, including the most perishable ones like wildlife and running water. Others add to this notion by claiming that property rights also apply to objects such as ideas (patents), and some even include humans themselves as part of property. Societies also differ in terms of the rights that property owners have; a set of privileges related to responsibility for actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection of property, because each use affects others' property. Property has dimensions because it affects relationships between people; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when the environment is polluted, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; laws prohibiting environmental pollution limit the rights of this property but protect the neighbors' property. Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources, even though it may cause damage to the neighbors' property.

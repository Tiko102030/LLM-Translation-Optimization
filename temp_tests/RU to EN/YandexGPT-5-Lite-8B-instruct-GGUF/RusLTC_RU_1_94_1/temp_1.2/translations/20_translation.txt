McCarter" (1908), referred to as "restrictions applicable to property in the interest of the whole society." The concept of police powers has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that "By police and economy I mean the proper regulation and domestic order of a state, by which the people in the country, like members of a well-organized family, are bound to follow rules of decency and good breeding, to help their neighbors, and to be honest, industrious, and docile in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, pp. 4, 162). In the case "Vanderbilt v. Adams" (1827), Judge Woodworth of New York State described police powers in more detail, saying: "The supreme power in society can and must prescribe how private property rights can be used.... Police powers are based on implied rights and the duty of the highest authority to protect everyone through legally established regulations so that the well-being of the entire society increases.... Such governmental power should exist in every well-regulated society." In "Commonwealth v. Algar" (1851), Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court Justice Lemuel Shaw supported the legislative power dealing with property regulation in Boston Harbor and prohibiting building outside this pier, and spoke about police powers as follows: We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, whether or not they have exclusive right to it, manages it with all the implied responsibility for it so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their property, nor to society as a whole.

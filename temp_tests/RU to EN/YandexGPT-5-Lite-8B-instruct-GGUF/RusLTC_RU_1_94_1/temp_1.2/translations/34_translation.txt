 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "numerous army of недовольных," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Often quoting Tideman, they demanded to limit the full power of the state by establishing a boundary for police powers. The state courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhamer v. People" (1856), the New York State Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that banned the sale of liquor. After the law came into effect, all those who had been selling the liquor lost their property, and the procedure for seizing the property did not follow the legal process. For the first time discovering that the concept of "legal process" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance." The decision in the Weinhamer case was echoed only about thirty years later, after which the case began to be considered with redoubled interest. In the precedent-setting case of Jacobs (1885).

 Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court supported the legislative power dealing with property regulation issues in Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this pier, and spoke about police powers as follows:
We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each owner of property, whether he has exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all implied responsibility so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their own property or society as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules can be applied to it that are necessary to achieve the common good and public welfare. The rights to property, like all other social and ordinary rights, fall under appropriate restrictions necessary to ensure that others have not been harmed by the use of this property, and under established legal rules, since the legislative power... may assume that this is a necessary and expedient decision. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to set limits or bounds to its manifestation." Indeed, using universal, typical phrases like "public interest", "internal structure of the state", "implied right and the duty of higher authority to protect everyone" and "common good and public welfare", Shaw established that police authorities could have a wide range of tools to achieve their goals. The governments of the southern states resorted to the help of the police in order to introduce laws limiting the use of slaves' property, for example, they banned slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after abolition, they imposed unusual restrictions on the rights of private property, for instance, they introduced a policy of strict racial segregation that restricted the sale of property to African Americans or their employment in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of the property and labor of African Americans once again demonstrates what kind of understanding of property there was at the time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955).

 However, the most important thing in this matter was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote of Judge Stone, according to which the majority of the members of the Court separated the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of the presumption of constitutionality of the law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of choice of religion. Environmental protection and counteroffensive procedure "takings" After making a decision in the case of Carolyn Products, the Supreme Court allowed democratically elected members of the legislative body broad rights to choose social policy options, even though they restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued in this context. The court case "Just v. Marinette County" 1972 of Wisconsin clearly illustrates how the judiciary easily applied that provision in the Carolyn Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just sued to cancel regulations preventing them from occupying designated wetland areas for building houses. Rejecting the demands of the Just family, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a lawful use of police powers. Later it wanted to approve an even broader list of restrictions on property rights.

According to the court ruling: "The owner of the land does not have any absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of other people and that could not be achieved with its original composition." The court recognized that zonal environmental protection rules "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights by introducing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "expropriation" of property, as in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was due to the fact that "the use of property without changing the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates were more successful in a legal battle regarding "expropriations" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court made a number of decisions уже in 1987. For example, in the case of "First Angelic Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after a district flood control organization banned the restoration of a flooded church campground. The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving the owner of using his property" is necessary in order "to entitle him to compensation for damages."

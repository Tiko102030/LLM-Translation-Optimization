 The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated among the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increased pressure that the regulatory framework would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion is more memorable than Justice Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority of votes and later supplemented with arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that basic rights to free labor had been violated in Louisiana. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on J. Locke's precepts that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "an essential part of freedom... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to understand the court's decision in the Slaughter-House and Mann case before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not receive an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in Louisiana, which was essentially a violation of freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacob's case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn his living in any legal way; to seek a favorite profession and for this purpose to conclude all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals."

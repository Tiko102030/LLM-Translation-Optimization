In this case, the final decision in the Mann case was also mentioned, and it was made by Chief Justice Waite. However, attention should be paid to the last statement considered earlier: "it's just a matter of severity." This limitation, written by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes, consciously re-established an instrumental vision of property rights, which declared previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. In the Lochner case, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Beginning his speech with the fact that "the decision in the case was made according to the basics of economic theory, which, however, does not suit most of the country's population," he added:
The right of every person to act as they please for as long as possible, provided, of course, that the rights of others to do the same are not violated, does not correspond to the basic tenets of law schools, postal services, laws of any of the states or other municipal institutions that take his money to achieve goals that seem necessary, regardless of whether he wants it or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner's despotic defense of intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests required achieving a balance. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to start a new case for alternative law.

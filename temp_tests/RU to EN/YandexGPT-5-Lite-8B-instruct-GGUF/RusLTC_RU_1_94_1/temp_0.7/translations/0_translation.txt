 INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and social practice
Society owns property. Economic systems differ in their definition of what property is, and often it can be difficult to define the boundaries. Some believe that property rights extend to such objects as land and trees, including the most perishable objects such as wildlife and running water. Others complement this concept with the statement that property rights also apply to objects such as ideas (patents), some also include humans themselves as slaves. Societies also differ depending on the rights that property owners have; a set of privileges concerning responsibility for taking certain actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "more" or "less" protection of property, because each use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions because it affects human relationships; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when the environment is polluted, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; rights that prohibit environmental pollution limit the rights of this property but protect the property of the neighbors. Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources, even though it may cause damage to the property of neighbors.

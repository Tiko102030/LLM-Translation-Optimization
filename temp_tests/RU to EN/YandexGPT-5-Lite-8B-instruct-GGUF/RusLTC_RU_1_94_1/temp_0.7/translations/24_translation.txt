 Callender filed a claim for compensation for damage caused to him during the street repair, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle in order to achieve compensation. But the defence side rejected the use of the English precedent with an instrumental statement of the case. She explained that due to the circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim" which Callender presented "is satisfied, then it will be necessary to check the correctness of the repairs of all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Callender's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts court accepted the state's proposal for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damage compensation, which was incomparably bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public structures. "Those who buy property near a roadway," the court выдвинул, "should take into account the future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." After all, in this case, the amount of compensation would double compared to the original cost. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case of «Lansing v Smith Walter Quackenbush», a claim was filed against the state of New York for damage due to the fact that docks fell in price after a new bay was built in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach used in the case of "Callender v Marsh" of Massachusetts in order to dismiss Kuakenbusch's claims.

 Lemuel Shaw, a representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Judiciary, supported the legislative power dealing with property regulation issues in Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting building outside this pier, and spoke about police rights as follows:
We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, whether he possesses exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their property use, or society as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules necessary for achieving the common good and universal welfare can be applied to it. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are also subject to appropriate restrictions necessary to confirm that the use of this property has not harmed others, and under legally established rules, since the legislative power... may assume that this is a necessary and expedient solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits to its manifestation." Indeed, using universal, typical phrases such as "public interest", "internal structure of the state", "implied right" and "duty of higher authority to protect everyone" and "common good and universal welfare", Shaw established that police authorities could have a wide range of tools to achieve their goals. Southern states' governments used police to enforce laws limiting the use of slaves' property, for example, they prohibited slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after abolition, they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing strict racial segregation policies that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of the property and labor of African Americans once again demonstrates what the understanding of property was at that time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955).

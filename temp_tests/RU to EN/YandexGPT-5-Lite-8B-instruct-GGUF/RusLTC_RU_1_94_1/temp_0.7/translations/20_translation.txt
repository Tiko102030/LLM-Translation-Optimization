McCarter" (1908), referred to as "restrictions applicable to property in the interest of the whole society." The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that "By publick Police and Economy, I understand the due Regulation and domestic Government of a State, whereby the People in a Country, like the Members of a well-regulated Family, are obliged to conform themselves to Rules of Decency and Good Manners, to assist their Neighbours, and to be honest, industrious, and compliant in their Designs" (see Blackstone 1979, pp. 4, 162). In the case "Vanderbilt v. Adams" in 1827, Judge Woodworth of New York State elaborated on police powers by saying: "The supreme authority in society can and must prescribe how private property rights can be used.... Police powers are based on the implied rights and the duty of the higher power to protect everyone through legally established regulations so that the welfare of the whole society increases.... Such state power should exist in every well-regulated society." In the case "Commonwealth v. Algar" (1851), Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Court supported legislative authority dealing with property regulation in Boston Harbor and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this pier, and spoke about police rights as follows: We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each owner of property, whether he has exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their property, or society as a whole.

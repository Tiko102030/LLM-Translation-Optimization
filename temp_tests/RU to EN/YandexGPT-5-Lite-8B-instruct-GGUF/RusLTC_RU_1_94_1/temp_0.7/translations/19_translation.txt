Local authorities regularly confiscated houses in which immoral activities took place, засыпали factory wastewater that "endangered people's health," prohibited the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, and did not pay any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of sectors of the economy, including those related to healthcare, such as the production of medicines and slaughterhouses, and others that were of economic importance and did not relate to health issues, such as the right to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation failed to achieve a public goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the "Manhattan Fertilizing" fertilizer plant and proceeded to "reduce" the harm caused by the plant's activities by destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

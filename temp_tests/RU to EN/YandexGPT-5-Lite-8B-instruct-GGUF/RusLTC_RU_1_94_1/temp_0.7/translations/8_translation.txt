A certain form of property as a tool may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke defends property rights not as resolutely as one might understand from the famous passage quoted above (see Horn 1990; Ketzelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he addresses the issue of the right of Stuart English monarchs to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament. But besides this issue, Locke recognizes a social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that everyone should have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be limited in order to ensure that everyone can share the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and as good" for others. Referring to the Bible, Locke argues that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property also limits the extent of that property.

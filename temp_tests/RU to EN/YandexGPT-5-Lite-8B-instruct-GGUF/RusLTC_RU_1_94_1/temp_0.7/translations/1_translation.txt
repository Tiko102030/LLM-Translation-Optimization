 Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it might harm the property of neighbors. On the other hand, it can support affected neighbors and limit the activities of that owner. This makes ownership rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of loss of clean air and water or the cost of a payment that would need to be made to the enterprise so that it no longer discharges waste into the environment. Factories that actively pollute the environment would be much poorer if they did not have privileges, because they would have to reduce pollution levels, or they would have to buy permission to pollute from their neighbors. Some doubt the fact that property rights have a significant impact on the distribution of incomes, while others argue in favor of its effectiveness. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of ownership rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same effective social result, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment buy these rights from their neighbors, or should neighbors pay those who pollute so they stop doing it? Environmental pollution will take place where the costs of reducing its negative consequences are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will be stopped when the costs of reducing the negative effect are less. A free trade market will lead to the same effective result regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

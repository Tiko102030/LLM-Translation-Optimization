 In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm competition brings is permissible in accordance with the principles of "a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, pp. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Criminal Intent, and Design," Holmes based himself on the aforementioned idea of the harm competition brings. Thus, he wanted to demonstrate the general need for an instrumental approach and a "principle of balance of interests," especially in conditions where judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At the Supreme Court session in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glue Company v. United States Glue Company." "In modern society," he stated, "every part is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, the others will feel it more or less. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the end of legislative power comes, it will not be enough just to show that there has been some progress in law-making, i.e., when it will already be possible to interfere with trade or existing transactions."

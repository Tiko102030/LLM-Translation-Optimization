 However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but Judge Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the Court members separated the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of presuming the constitutionality of the law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom to choose a religion. Environmental protection and the counterattack of the "takings" procedure After the decision in the Carolyn Products case, the Supreme Court allowed democratically elected members of the legislative body broad rights in choosing social policy objects, even though they limited the actions of property owners.

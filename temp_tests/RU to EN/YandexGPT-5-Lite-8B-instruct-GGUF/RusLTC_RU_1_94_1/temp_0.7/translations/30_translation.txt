 The iron and steel industries, for which preferential tariffs were established, were the only ones that could provide for the developing railway network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was the recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the real goals of bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose property was sponsored by state authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand the powers of the public in regulating ownership. They achieved поразительного success in obtaining a law on an eight-hour working day and a law establishing railway tariffs. There were objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result these bills were referred to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court decision of 1877 in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed the business circles of the state, who were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of radicalism that was popular at the time. Plaintiffs argued that by canceling existing deals, the law violated the constitutional clause on transactions, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference.

 In the precedent case of Jacobus (1885), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in rented premises because it "prevented the owner from making a profit and freely using his property", and thus "he was deprived of his property and personal freedom". Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and property rights protection, Justice Earl stated that liberty, in its broad sense and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual slavery... but also a person's right to use their abilities in all legal ways in order to live and work... Therefore, any laws that weaken or constrain these rights... are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The Legislative Assembly argued that the decision in this case was based on state police powers, and the measures taken were necessary in order to "improve the country's healthcare system". It did not bode well for defenders of government regulation that the court began to consider this demand, subsequently concluding that it did not have the required amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was determined that the law actually "did not touch on health issues at all", and Justice Earl notified the legislative body that "under the guise of police directives, one cannot arbitrarily intrude into personal rights and private property". The Jacobus case marked the beginning of an unprecedented period of judicial activity protecting property rights. For example, in Godcharles v. Wainjman (1866), the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal tender money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law is a violation of equal employer and employee rights, moreover, it is an offensive attempt to subject unskilled workers to legislative guardianship" because it prevents workers from selling their labor under any terms they are willing to accept.

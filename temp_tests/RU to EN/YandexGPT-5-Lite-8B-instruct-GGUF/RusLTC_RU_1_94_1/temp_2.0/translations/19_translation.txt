Local authorities regularly confiscated houses where immoral activities took place, filled in the сточных вод from factories that "endangered people's health", banned the production of hazardous chemicals and put out fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, and without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of sectors of the economy, including those related to healthcare, such as the production of medicines and slaughtering livestock, and others that were economically important and did not involve health issues, such as the right to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation failed to achieve its public goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed policemen entered the territory of a fertilizer plant owned by "Manhattan Fertilizing" and proceeded to "reduce" the harm caused by the factory's activities, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, pp. 226).

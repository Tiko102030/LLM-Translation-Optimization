 The New York court used the instrumental approach used in the case of «Callender v. Marsh» in Massachusetts to dismiss Kuekenbusch's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily more or less affect people's comfort and property; and in cases where the preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In the end, the court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the common good must prevail over private interests." Government support for private entrepreneurship.

Without prohibiting the government from improving the state at the expense of the owners of adjacent property, case law, as was the situation in «Lancing v. Callender», shifted the responsibility for covering the costs associated with achieving public goals to some people (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental norms became special cases. For example, in the case of «Thorpe v. Rutland and Burlinton Railroad Company» (1855), Chief Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court upheld a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of state order in the state implies protecting the lives, limbs, health, comfort and peace of all people, and protecting all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (Latin sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Applying this principle provides access to consideration of the social interpretation of property.

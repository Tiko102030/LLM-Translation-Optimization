 The plaintiffs argued that by overturning existing transactions, the law had violated a clause in the constitution regarding deals, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference. Issued recently to protect the civil rights of slaves freed in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment now became an inherent civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property rights from government interference. However, this opinion was supported by only a minority of members of the Court in 1877. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Stating that "powers are inherent in any supreme power", the Court ruled that "the government can regulate both the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England in saying that when property "affects the interests of society, it no longer legally ceases to be subject to private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the highest right" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when the owner of property uses it in such a way that the public has an interest in it, in fact he wants this himself, and until this interest disappears, he should simply come to terms with the fact that society now controls his property because it is necessary to increase social welfare. If he does not want society to control his property, he should simply stop using it. Considering in more detail the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

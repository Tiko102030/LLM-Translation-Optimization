 However, the most important thing in this matter was not the decision itself, but Judge Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the Court separated the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of the presumption of constitutionality of the law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic rulings and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom to choose a religion. Environmental protection and the backlash of the "takings" procedure.
After the decision in the Caroline Products case, the Supreme Court allowed democratically elected members of the legislature wide powers in choosing social policy objectives, even though they limited the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued in this context. The Just v. Marionett County case of 1972 in Wisconsin наглядно illustrates how the judiciary easily applied that provision in the Caroline Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just filed a lawsuit to cancel ordinances preventing them from occupying the marshlands intended for them to build houses. Rejecting the claims of the Just family, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is lawful use of police powers. Later it wanted to approve an even wider list of restrictions on property rights.

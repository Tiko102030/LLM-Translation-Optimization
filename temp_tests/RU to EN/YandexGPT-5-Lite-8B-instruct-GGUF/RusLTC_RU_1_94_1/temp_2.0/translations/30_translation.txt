 The iron and steel industries, for whose products preferential tariffs were established, could only ensure the developing railway network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was the newly centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the real goals of bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose ownership was sponsored by state authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand the powers of the public in regulating ownership. They achieved striking success in that they got a resolution on the law on an eight-hour workday and a law establishing railway tariffs. There were objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were referred to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The decision of the Supreme Court in 1877 in the case «Mann v. Illinois» greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of radicalism that was popular at the time. Plaintiffs argued that by canceling existing transactions, the law violated a clause in the Constitution on transactions, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from governmental interference.

Chancellor Kent of New York in 1826 announced that "every person possesses such an amount of freedom to acquire, use and dispose of their property as may be comparable to an acceptable state of property and the mutual rights of others... Although thus protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as much as it may be necessary in order to prevent abuse of law that might lead to violation of another person's rights or those of society at large." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in 1837 in Illinois, the legislative body lacked three pages to catalog all the powers that the Chicago City Administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and limiting "hula-hooping, playing ball or flying kites... capable of annoying people", the new city gained the power that "will force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, tallow candle shop, soap factory, leather tannery, stable, barn, outhouse, clothing factory or any other shabby, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). Authorities in other cities also assumed broad powers over property issues. Thus, in New York, certain norms were established for the transportation of goods along the East River, for example, the price for transporting cargo varied from twenty-five cents for fattened buffaloes or bulls to fifty cents per cord of walnut wood, one cent for empty milk bottles, twenty-five cents for child corpses and fifty cents for adult corpses (see Novak 1996).

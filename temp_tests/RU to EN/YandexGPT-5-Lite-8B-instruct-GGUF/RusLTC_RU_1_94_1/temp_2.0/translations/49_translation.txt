According to the court's ruling: "The owner of the land has no absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of other people and that could not be achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that zonal environmental protection rules "should be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights through the introduction of restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "expropriation" of property, as there was in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was due to the fact that "the use of property without changing its natural soil composition is allowed by law." Property rights defenders achieved greater success in court litigation regarding "expropriations" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court adopted a number of decisions as early as 1987. For example, in the case "First Evangelical Lutheran Church of the Augsburg Confession v. County of Los Angeles" (1987), the court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the district flood control organization banned the restoration of a flooded church tent camp. The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using his property" was necessary in order "to give him the right to compensation."

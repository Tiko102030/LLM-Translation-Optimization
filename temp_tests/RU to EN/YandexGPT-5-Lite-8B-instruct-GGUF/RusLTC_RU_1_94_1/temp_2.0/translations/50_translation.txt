 The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that a «regulation depriving an owner of using his property» was necessary in order to «entitle him to compensation». The court decision on the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by judges Brennon and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles; it merely confirmed the main provisions of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case of the same year, "Nollan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a совершенно different case because it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, owning a coastal plot of land located between public parks to the north and south of each other, the Nollans wanted to build a new three-bedroom house in place of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in that district. The California Coastal Commission granted the necessary permission only on condition that the Nollans would consent to granting general use rights to the territory between the tide line and the dam behind their house. Thus, people could easily reach the parks located both to the north and south by leisurely walking along the sand. The commission explained its requirement by saying that a larger house would contribute to «development of the area», that «people would no longer subconsciously realize that there was a coast nearby and that they had every right to spend time there» (see Eagle 1996, p. 255).

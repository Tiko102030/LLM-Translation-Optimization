 A similar situation occurred in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom not only included the absence of "his physical limitations," but also covered the right "to work where he wants; to earn a living by any legal means; to pursue his favorite profession and, for this purpose, to enter into all transactions that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the above goals." After the Allgeyer decision, a peak in judicial activity in presenting motives was set eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it сократило working hours for bakers. Speaking in court, Justice Peckham stated emphatically that "the law necessarily affects the right to enter into contracts between an employer and an employee," which violated "the general right to make deals relevant to his firm's activities," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution." Recognizing the fact that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary basis for interfering with human freedom or his right to make a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham spoke about how it was just a matter of which evil was less: the state's power to legislate or an individual's right to personal freedom and freedom to contract. We believe that the limit of police powers has been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, инструментализм, and Lochner's New Deal can be logically defined based on other court decisions that asserted property rights.

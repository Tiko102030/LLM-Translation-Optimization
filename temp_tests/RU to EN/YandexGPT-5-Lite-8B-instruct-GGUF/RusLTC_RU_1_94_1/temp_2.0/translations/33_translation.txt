 The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now also a public interest, another tool for achieving social goals, and did not require any constitutional guarantee. Against the background of increasing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused great feelings among those who feared that the expanded powers of the police would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, a prominent jurist, Christopher Tiedeman (1971 [1886]), warned that the divine right of kings did not allow to limit the powers of the police. The spread of laissez-faire policy reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by the government". But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been outlawed, and it is now needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant throughout the civilized world. The state is intended to protect the weak from the хитрости of the stronger, to establish what wage a worker should receive for his work and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the large army of discontented and their obvious power in that they, especially with the development of universal suffrage, can forcibly implement their ideas about civil statehood in the civilized world, conservatives were even more afraid that there might appear a sort of autocracy that is more oppressive and unreasonable than anything before. In the late XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of the "large army of discontented", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

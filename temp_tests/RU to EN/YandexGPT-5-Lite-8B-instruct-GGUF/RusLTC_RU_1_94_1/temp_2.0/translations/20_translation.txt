McCarter" (1908), called "restrictions applicable to property, in the interest of the whole society." The concept of police powers has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that "By public police and economy, I mean the due regulation and domestic order of the state, whereby the people of the country, like the members of a well-regulated family, are bound to observe rules of decency and good manners, to help their neighbours, and be honest, industrious, and submissive in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case "Vanderbilt v. Adams" (1827), Justice Woodworth of New York State elaborated on police powers by saying: "The supreme power in society can and must therefore prescribe how private property rights may be used.... Police powers are based on implied rights and the duty of the supreme authority to protect everyone through statutorily established regulations so that, ultimately, the welfare of the whole society is increased.... Such a government should exist in every well-regulated society." In "Commonwealth v. Algar" (1851), Representative Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Court upheld legislative power dealing with property regulation in Boston Harbor and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside that pier, and spoke about police powers as follows: We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, whether he possesses exclusive rights to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility so that its use does not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their property use, nor society as a whole.

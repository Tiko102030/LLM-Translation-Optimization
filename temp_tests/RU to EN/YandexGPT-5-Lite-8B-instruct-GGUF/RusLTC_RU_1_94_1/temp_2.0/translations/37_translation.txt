The butchers of New Orleans took their case to the Supreme Court of the United States, where they were represented by Field's predecessor, John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant secretary of war (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the basic provisions of labor legislation to persuade the Court to protect the citizen's right to property ownership. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the decisions of the city of New Orleans took us back to a time "when the nosy eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated among the justices, and lawyers therefore feared increasing pressure that regulatory frameworks would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed.

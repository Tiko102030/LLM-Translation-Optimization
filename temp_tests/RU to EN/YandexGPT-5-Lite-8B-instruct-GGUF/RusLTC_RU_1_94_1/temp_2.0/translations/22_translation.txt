Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as it was in the case of property owned or used by people of European descent in America. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from the southern states received the right to own property, however, their rights could change depending on what the political situation in the state was and what role African Americans played in it. The same instrumental approach that the governments of the northern states used to limit property rights with regard to clean air and water was also used in the southern states to keep African Americans dependent on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve social goals, but the goals differed. Redistribution of property rights to stimulate economic growth.
By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, United States Supreme Court Chief Justice Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach he used before the court to annul the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge company. He did this in order to independently operate the only bridge across the Charles River that connects Boston and Cambridge (see the case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge firm was having some difficulties. The firm could lose «millions of dollars ... that were invested in the construction of railroads and canals on expressway lines... We will be thrown back a century to the inventions of that age and forced to remain motionless».

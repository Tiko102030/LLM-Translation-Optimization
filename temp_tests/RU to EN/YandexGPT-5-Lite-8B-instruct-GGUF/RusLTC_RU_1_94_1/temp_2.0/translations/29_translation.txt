 The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in America's political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. Reformers wanted more. For example, George McNeill, a defender of workers' rights, warned that America must "inoculate our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to C. F. Bracey, a train conductor: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that trust companies are trying to impose on us today." The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the liberation of black slaves, however, now workers are nothing more than slaves of united trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War contributed to the fact that workers united and opposed these new demands. Large amounts of money were needed for warfare, and the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus associated with it further confirmed that significant financial and industrial capital was required to support the activities of the government.

 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "numerous army of недовольных", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Citing Tideman frequently, they demanded to limit the full scope of state power by establishing police powers' boundaries. The State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental approach to determining private property. Even before the Civil War began, in the case of Weinstein v. People (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who had been selling this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for confiscating the property did not take place in accordance with the legal procedures. For the first time discovering that the concept of "the legal order" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislature cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance". The Weinstein case decision resonated only after about thirty years, after which the case began to be viewed with renewed interest. In the Jacobs' precedent case (1885).

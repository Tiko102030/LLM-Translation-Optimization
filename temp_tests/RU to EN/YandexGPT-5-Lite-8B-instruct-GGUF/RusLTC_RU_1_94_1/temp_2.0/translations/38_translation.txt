 The butchers of New Orleans lost their case by a vote of five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increased pressure over the fact that the regulatory framework would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion is more memorable than Judge Miller's, which was supported by a majority of votes and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that fundamental rights to free labor were violated in the state of Louisiana. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on J. Locke's prescription that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, then property is "a basic part of freedom...in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to make sense of the court's decision in the case of Slaughter-House and Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not receive an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in the state of Louisiana, which essentially violated the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacob case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that a person's liberty included not only the absence of "his physical limitation," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn his living in any lawful way; to seek his favorite profession and for this purpose to enter into all transactions that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals."

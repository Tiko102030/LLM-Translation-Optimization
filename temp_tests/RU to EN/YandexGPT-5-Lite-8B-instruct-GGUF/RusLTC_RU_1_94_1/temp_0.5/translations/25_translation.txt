 The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the case of Callender v. Marsh of Massachusetts to reject Queckenbush's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect, more or less, the comfort and property of people; and where the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In the end, the court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the common good must prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise.

Without prohibiting the government from carrying out improvements in the state at the expense of the owners of adjacent property, case law, as was the case in Lansing v. Callender, imposed on some people the obligation to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became particular cases. For example, in Thorpe v. Rutland & Burlington Railroad Company (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state order of the state implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (Latin sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle ensures access to consideration of the social interpretation of property.

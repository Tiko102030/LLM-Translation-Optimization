South Carolina Coastal Council" (1992), the Court required that a real estate owner in South Carolina be compensated for damage to his property after the new law "On the Organization of the Coastline" suspended construction on his land. Again expressing its distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as in the Nollan case, that judges must carefully study the regulations in order to insure themselves against "the risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in the case "Dolan v. City of Tigard" (1994), where the courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from the owners to the government. The case was filed when the administration of Tigard, Oregon, stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only on condition that she give up part of her land for a public park, pedestrian and bicycle paths in order to reduce traffic congestion. Recording a speech for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration "did not dispute the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle paths at Dolan's expense is a consequence of the need for the city administration to improve the transportation interchange." Decisions in the Nollan and Dolan cases indicate the onset of a new era of enhanced property regulation research. This could lead to the fact that the right of ownership will again be at the level it was before the New Deal.

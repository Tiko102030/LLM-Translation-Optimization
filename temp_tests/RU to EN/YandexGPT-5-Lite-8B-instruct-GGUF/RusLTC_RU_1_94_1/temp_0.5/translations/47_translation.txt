 Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:
The economic situation in the country can be the result of how the temporary limitation on the forced fulfillment of contractual conditions will be согласованный with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unauthorized restriction on the protectionist policy of the state, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority.... At the same time, whether the legislative body will pursue a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will no longer be concerned. Back in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon the assessment of economic legislation and разбирательства in court cases on property rights issues, the legislature had already declared the need to "protect the vital interests of society". After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court almost completely agreed with his reform program. No other decision has so clearly illustrated the new approach in the activities of the Supreme Court as was done in the case "United States v. Carolyn Products" (1938). In this particular case, the company was sued based on the "Skimmed Milk Act" of 1923, which prohibited the interstate sale of milk with the addition of fat and oil, considering it as the sale of a "deliberately spoiled food product harmful to health". The attorneys for Carolyn Products claimed that the law had unlawfully restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court considered the law valid because, in its opinion, it was a fair instrument of state regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would be an ordinary, simple confirmation of the established police powers. However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the Court had divided the concepts of property rights and a bunch of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

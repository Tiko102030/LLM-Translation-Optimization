 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of the «numerous army of dissatisfied», lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Citing Tideman frequently, they demanded to limit the full scope of state power by establishing a boundary for police powers. State courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for a sacred rather than instrumental approach to defining private property. Even before the outbreak of the Civil War, in the case «Weinhamer v. People» (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who sold this liquor lost their property, and the procedure for seizing the property was not carried out in accordance with the law. For the first time discovering that the concept of «due process of law» protected property, the court ruled that «the legislative branch cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance». The decision in the Weinhamer case found an echo only about thirty years later, after which the case began to be considered with redoubled interest. In the precedent case of Jacobs (1885).

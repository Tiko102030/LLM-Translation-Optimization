 The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now also a public interest, another instrument for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the background of growing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused greater emotion among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent jurist Christopher Tiedemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings did not allow limiting the police powers. The spread of laissez-faire policy reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by the government." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government interference in human rights is outlawed, and now it is needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant throughout the civilized world. The state is призвано to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to establish what wages a worker should receive for their labor, and how many hours they should work each day...
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power in that they, especially under the development of universal suffrage, can forcefully implement their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, conservatives feared even more that there might emerge an autocracy that is more despotic and unwise than any that has existed before. In the late XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of the "numerous army of dissatisfied people," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

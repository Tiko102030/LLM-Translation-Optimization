The most notable was the court decision of 1934. In the case "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," the Supreme Court, due to the prevailing economic instability in the country, approved the actions of the state regarding the regulation of property. The court upheld a Minnesota law; this law imposed a moratorium on foreclosure in conditions of an unfavorable economic situation established by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the article of the Constitution restricting government regulation should "not be considered with literal accuracy as any mathematical formula, but it still remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information." He added that the court should interpret this article only "in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its inhabitants." "To assess the effectiveness of a legislative body, one must not look at whether it has a direct or indirect impact on contractual conditions, but rather at whether the decision made is legal and achieved by reasonable and appropriate methods." This speaks to a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which in turn was transformed into a constitutional principle. Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that: The economic situation in the country may be the result of how the temporary restriction on the enforcement of contractual terms will be reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the treaty is not an unconditional and completely unlawful limitation of the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative legality.... At the same time, whether the legislative body pursues reasonable or unreasonable policies, it will no longer be the court's concern.

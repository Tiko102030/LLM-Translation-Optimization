 The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using their property" is necessary in order "to grant them the right to compensation for damages." The judicial decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles; it merely reaffirmed the main provisions of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the same year's case "Nollan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987) was a completely different case because it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, owning a coastal plot of land located between public parks to the north and south of each other, the Nollans wanted to build a new three-bedroom house instead of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in the district. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permission only on the condition that the Nollans agree to grant general access rights to the territory between the high tide line and the dam located behind their house. Thus, people will be able to easily reach the parks located both in the northern and southern directions by simply taking a leisurely stroll along the sand. The committee explained its requirement by saying that a larger house would contribute to "development of the area," that "people would no longer subconsciously realize that there is a coast nearby and that they have every right to spend time there" (see Eagle 1996, p. 255).

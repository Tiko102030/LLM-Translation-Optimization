 The plaintiffs argued that by canceling existing transactions, the law violated the constitution's clause on transactions, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference. Recently issued in order to protect the civil rights of slaves freed in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment was now an integral civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property that protected the rights of private property from government interference. However, this opinion was supported by a minority of members of the Court in 1877. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Saying that "powers are inherent in any sovereign power", the Court ruled that "the government can regulate both the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England in saying that when property "affects the interests of society, it no longer legally ceases to be subject only to private regulation." Recalling the principle "the welfare of people is the highest right" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when the owner of property uses it in such a way that the public has an interest in it, he actually wants it himself, and until the interest disappears, he must simply come to terms with the fact that society now manages his property because it is necessary to increase social welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply stop using it. Considering in more detail the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

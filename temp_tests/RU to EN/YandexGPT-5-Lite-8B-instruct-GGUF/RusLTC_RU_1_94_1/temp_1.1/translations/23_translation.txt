In relation to this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public goals, were immediately abolished as soon as they hindered the fulfillment of these goals. In the early XIX century, state authorities, seeking to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads, railways, and canals. The construction of state infrastructure had to bear indirect costs, expenses associated with damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved property. According to the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would be held responsible for indirect costs, significantly increasing the cost of financing inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding responsibility by independent entrepreneurs for the consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property, the costs of constructing state infrastructure would have резко уменьшились. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Callender v. Marsh," setting a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Callender filed a lawsuit to seek compensation for damage caused to him during street repair, which resulted in damage to the foundation of his house in Boston.

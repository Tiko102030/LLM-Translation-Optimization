Although the land and all inferior creatures belong jointly to all people, still...the labor of his body and the work of his hands most strictly belong to him. Whatever then he removes out of that state which nature hath provided and left in it, he has mixed his labour with it, and joined something to it that is his own, and thereby makes it his property...For this labour being the property of the labourer, no man but himself can have a right to what another hath thus mixed his labour with, at least where there is enough, and as good left in common for others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it means to use someone's labor, i.e., to turn a person into a slave. It follows that the rights of ownership owe nothing to society; nevertheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

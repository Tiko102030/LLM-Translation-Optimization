 The butchers of New Orleans lost their case by a vote of five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increased pressure over the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion is more memorable than that of Justice Miller, who was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented with arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that basic rights to free labor were violated in the state of Louisiana. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on J. Locke's precepts that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "a fundamental part of freedom...in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to understand the decision of the court in the Slaughter-House &amp; Mann case before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not receive an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in the state of Louisiana, which was essentially a violation of freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacob's case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical constraints," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn his living in any lawful way; to pursue a beloved profession and, for this purpose, to make all deals that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for successful achievement of the aforementioned goals."

 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "large army of discontented", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Frequently quoting Tideman, they demanded that the full extent of state power be limited by setting boundaries for police authority. The state courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhamer v. People" (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who sold this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for confiscating property did not follow the legal procedure. For the first time discovering that the concept of "due process of law" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislature cannot completely abolish commercial activity in its entirety and thereby subject property to total disappearance."

 The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also a public interest, another tool for achieving social goals, and no constitutional guarantee was required anymore. Against the backdrop of increasingly growing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused greater emotion among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that occurred on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent jurist Christopher Tiedemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings did not allow limiting the powers of the police. The spread of laissez-faire policy reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by the government." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government interference with human rights is outlawed, and now it is needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant throughout the civilized world. The state is called to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to establish what wages a worker should receive for their labor and how many hours they should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous army of discontented and their obvious power in that they, especially under the development of universal suffrage, can forcibly implement their ideas about a civic state in the civilized world, conservatives feared even more that there could emerge an autocracy that is more despotic and unwise than any that existed before. In the late 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of the "numerous army of discontented," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

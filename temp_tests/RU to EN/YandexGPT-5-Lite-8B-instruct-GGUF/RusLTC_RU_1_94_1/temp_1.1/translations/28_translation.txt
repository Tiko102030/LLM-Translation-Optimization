 The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, by redistributing rights, it only asserted some property in comparison to other property. According to one of the legal historians, Willard Hurst, this approach is one of the working principles of the rule of law in the United States of America — "the release of all human creative energy." Despite the fact that the burden of compensating neighbors for indirect losses remains on the shoulders of entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to start profit-generating projects. In theory, if a project were more expensive than the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of damage caused. However, in practice, the entire burden is shifted to the injured party, which further increases the number of entrepreneurs' projects, and all the risk and operating costs fall on the neighbors. If property rights were more legally respected, then high operating costs would impede the implementation of the entrepreneur's plans.

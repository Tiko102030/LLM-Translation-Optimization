 A similar situation occurred in the Jacobs case trial, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom not only included the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn a living by any legal means; to seek his favorite profession and, for this purpose, to conclude all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals." After the decision in the Allgeyer case, a peak in judicial activity in stating motives was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared New York law unconstitutional because, according to this law, bakers' working hours were reduced. Speaking in court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law necessarily affects the right to conclude a contract between an employer and an employee," which violates "the general right to conclude a transaction related to his firm's activities," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution." Recognizing the fact that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary basis for interfering with human freedom or his right to form a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham spoke about how it was just a matter of which evil was less: the state's power to make laws or an individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to make contracts. We believe that the limit of police powers has been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and Lochner's New Deal could be logically defined based on other court decisions that upheld property rights.

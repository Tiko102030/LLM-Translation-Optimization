 The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the case «Callender v. Marsh» of Massachusetts to reject Kuakenbush's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily more or less affect the comfort and property of people; and in a case where preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In the end, the court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the common good must prevail over private interests." State support for private entrepreneurship.

Without prohibiting the government from carrying out improvements in the state at the expense of the owners of adjacent property, case law, as it was in the situation «Lansing v. Callender», shifted to some people the responsibility for covering the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became particular cases. For example, in the case «Thorpe v. Rutland & Burlington Railroad Company» (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "protecting state order implies protecting the lives, limbs, health, comfort and peace of all people, and protecting all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (Latin sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Applying this principle provides access to consideration of social interpretation of property.

Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as was the case with property owned or used by people of European descent in America. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from the southern states received the right to own property, however, their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and the participation of African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach that was used by the governments of the northern states to limit ownership rights with regard to clean air and water was also used in the southern states in order to preserve the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were determined to achieve public purposes, only the purposes differed. Redistribution of property rights in order to stimulate economic growth. By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach he used in the Court to nullify the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge firm. He did this in order to independently manage the only bridge across the Charles River that connects Boston and Cambridge (see the case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge firm experienced some difficulties. The firm could lose "millions of dollars... which were invested in the construction of railways and canals on highways... We will be thrown back a century to the inventions of that age and forced to remain immobile".

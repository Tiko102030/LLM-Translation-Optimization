 Callender filed a lawsuit for compensation for damage caused to him during the street repair, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited quotations from English laws, common law precedents, and this principle in order to obtain compensation. But the defense dismissed the use of the English precedent with an instrumental presentation of the case. She explained that due to circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim" filed by Callender "is satisfied, then it will be necessary to check the correctness of repairing all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Callender's lawsuit for compensation. Then the Massachusetts court adopted the state's proposal for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damage compensation, which was unequivocally bad news for people whose property was directly adjacent to public structures. "Those who buy property next to a thoroughfare," the court выдвинул, "should take into account the future expansion or contraction of the street or road."

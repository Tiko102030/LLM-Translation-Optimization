 However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but Judge Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the Court divided the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases concerning economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of presumption of constitutionality of the law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, наоборот, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of choice of religion. Environmental protection and counterattack of the "takings" procedure. After making a decision in the case of Carolyn Products, the Supreme Court allowed democratically elected members of the legislative body broad rights in choosing social policy objects, even despite the fact that they limited the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued in this context. The Just v. Marinneth County court case of 1972 in Wisconsin наглядно illustrates how the judicial power easily applied that provision in the Carolyn Products case. Ronald and Catherine L. Just sued to cancel resolutions preventing them from occupying designated wetlands for building houses. Rejecting the claims of the Just family, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate use of police powers. Later, it wanted to approve an even broader list of restrictions on property rights.

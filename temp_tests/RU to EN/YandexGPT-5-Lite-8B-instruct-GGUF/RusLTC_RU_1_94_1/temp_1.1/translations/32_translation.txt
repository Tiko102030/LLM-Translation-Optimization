 Considering in more detail the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Wait recognized several limitations on state regulation. "You can receive compensation for the use of regulated property," and Wait argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislature. "It is assumed that any law," he stated, "is constitutional. Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional, unless, of course, it is clear as such. If there is doubt, then the expressed will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." Recognizing that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to go for redress of harm "to polling places rather than to court." Property rights advocates were shaken by the open reading of police powers in the Mann case. Justice Stephen Field thought differently and said that "the principle on which the majority opinion rests... undermines private property rights". "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of a majority of its legislative body." The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no constitutional guarantee was required anymore.

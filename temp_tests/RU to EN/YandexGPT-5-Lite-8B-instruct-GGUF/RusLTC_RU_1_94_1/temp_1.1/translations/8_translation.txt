A certain form of ownership as an instrument may be convenient, but will never be necessary. However, Locke himself defends property rights not so decisively as one might understand from the famous passage quoted above (see Horn 1990; Katsnelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he addresses the issue of the right of Stuart monarchs in England to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament. But besides this question, Locke recognizes a social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that everyone should have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be limited in order to ensure that everyone can share the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and as good" for others. Referring to the Bible, Locke argues that the same law of nature which gives us property also limits the extent of this property.

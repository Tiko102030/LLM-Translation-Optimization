South Carolina Coastal Council» (1992), the Court required that a real estate owner in South Carolina be compensated for damage to his property after a new law «On the Organization of the Coastline» suspended construction on his land. Again expressing his distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as in the case of Nollan, that judges must carefully examine the regulations in order to insure themselves against «the risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes». The Court refined its new standards in the case «Dolan v. City of Tigard» (1994), where courts were tasked with shifting part of the burden of proof from owners to the government. The case was opened when the administration of Tigard, Oregon, stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only on the condition that she give up part of her land plot for a public park, pedestrian and bicycle paths in order to reduce traffic jams on the road. Recording a speech for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration «did not dispute the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle paths at Dolan's expense was a consequence of the need for the city administration to improve the transport interchanges».

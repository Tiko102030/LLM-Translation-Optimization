Chancellor Kent of New York in 1826 declared that "every person possesses such an amount of freedom to acquire, use and dispose of his property as may be compared with an acceptable state of property and the reciprocal rights of others... Although, thus, property is protected... the legislature has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as much as it may be necessary in order to prevent abuse of the right that could lead to violation of the rights of another person or of society as a whole." To protect public welfare and consider the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the nineteenth century. For example, in 1837 in Illinois, the legislature lacked three pages to catalog all the powers that the administration of Chicago would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transport, and restricting "spinning hoops, playing ball or flying kites... capable of annoying people", the new city gained the power that "will force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, tallow candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, latrine, clothing factory or any other dilapidated, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). The authorities of other cities also assumed broad powers over property issues. Thus, in New York, certain norms were established for the transportation of goods across the East River, for example, the price for transporting a load varied from twenty-five cents for well-fed buffaloes or bulls to fifty cents for a cord of nutwood, one cent for empty milk bottles, twenty-five cents for child corpses and fifty cents for adult corpses (see Novak 1996).

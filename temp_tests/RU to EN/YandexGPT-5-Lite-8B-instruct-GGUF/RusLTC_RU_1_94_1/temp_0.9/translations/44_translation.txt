In this regard, in the Pennsylvania Coal case, Holmes acted more like a Trojan Horse rather than a winner-protector of sacred property rights. Instead of treating property rights as a natural right, Holmes justified the "takings principle" based on establishing a balance between competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on the circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property must be constantly used "reasonably," but the idea of what is "reasonable" remains vague. If actions go "too far," then compensation for damage will be necessary in this case; however, it will be up to the judges, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, to determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully assess and balance the competing interests of both parties. Owners should not, in turn, alienate everyone. Holmes argued that "the government will hardly be able to continue its activities any longer if the issue of property is not resolved to some extent."

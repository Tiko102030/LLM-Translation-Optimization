 INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and social practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ in their definition of what property is, and often it can be difficult to delineate the boundaries. Some believe that property rights extend to objects such as land and trees, including the most perishable objects like wildlife and running water. Others supplement this concept by stating that property rights also apply to objects such as ideas (patents), and some even include humans themselves as slaves.
Societies also differ depending on the rights that property owners have; a set of privileges concerning responsibility for taking any action towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "more" or "less" protection for property, because each use affects others' property. Property has dimensions because it affects human relationships; I have the right to распоряжаться (manage) my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when polluting the environment, the neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; rights prohibiting environmental pollution limit the rights of this property but protect the property of the neighbors. Society, on the one hand, does not prevent the owner from using his land and water resources, even though it may cause damage to the neighbors' property.

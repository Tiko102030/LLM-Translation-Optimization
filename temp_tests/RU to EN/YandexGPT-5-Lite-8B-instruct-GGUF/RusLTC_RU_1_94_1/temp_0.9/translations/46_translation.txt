The most notable was the court decision of 1934. In the case "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," the Supreme Court, in connection with the prevailing economic instability in the country, approved the actions of the state regarding the regulation of property. The court upheld a Minnesota state law; this law imposed a moratorium on foreclosing mortgaged property in conditions of an adverse economic situation established by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the article of the Constitution limiting government regulation should "not be considered with literal accuracy as any mathematical formula, but still it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information." He added that the court must interpret this article only "in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect the vital interests of its residents." "To assess the effectiveness of the legislative body, we need not be concerned with whether it has a direct or indirect impact on contractual terms, but rather whether the decision made is lawful and whether it was achieved through reasonable and appropriate methods." This speaks to a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which in turn was transformed into a constitutional principle. Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes came to the conclusion that: The economic situation in the country may be the result of how temporary restriction on enforcement of contractual terms will be reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the state's reserved right to protect vital interests of society.... Since this article of the treaty is not an unconditional and completely incompetent limitation of the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative legitimacy.... At the same time, whether the legislative body will pursue reasonable or unreasonable policies, it will no longer be the court's concern.

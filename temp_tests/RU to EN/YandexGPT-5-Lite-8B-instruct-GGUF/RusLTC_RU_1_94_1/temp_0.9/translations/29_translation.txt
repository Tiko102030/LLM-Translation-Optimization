 The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in America's political life, demonstrated the power of the national government that liberated the country from slavery. Reformers wanted more. For example, labor rights advocate George McNeil warned that America must "instill republican principles into our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F. Bracy, "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that trust companies are trying to impose on us today." The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the liberation of black slaves, however, now workers are nothing more than slaves of объединенных трастовых companies (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to workers uniting and speaking out against these new demands. Large sums of money were needed for military operations, and the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus associated with it confirmed once again that significant financial and industrial capital was required to support government activities. Iron and steel industries, whose products had preferential tariffs, could only provide for the developing network of railroads.

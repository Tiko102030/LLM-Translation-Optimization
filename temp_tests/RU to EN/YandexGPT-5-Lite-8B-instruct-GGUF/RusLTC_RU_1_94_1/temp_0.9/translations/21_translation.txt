 Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court supported the legislative power dealing with property regulation issues in Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this pier, and spoke about police rights as follows:
We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, regardless of whether he possesses exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their property use, or society as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules necessary for the sake of achieving the common good and universal welfare can be applied to it. Property rights, like all other social and conventional rights, are also subject to appropriate restrictions necessary to ensure that others are not harmed from the use of this property, and to established legal regulations, since the legislative power... may assume that this is a necessary and expedient solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to set boundaries or limits for its manifestation." Indeed, using universal, typical phrases like "public interest", "internal structure of the state", "implied right" and "duty of higher authority to protect everyone" and "common good and universal welfare", Shaw established that the police authorities could have a wide range of tools in achieving their goals. The governments of the southern states used the help of the police to introduce laws restricting the use of slaves' property, for example, they prohibited slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after abolition of slavery, they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection for the property and labor of African Americans further demonstrates what understanding of property there was at the time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichtenstadt 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955).

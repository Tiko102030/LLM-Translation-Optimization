According to the court ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that violate the rights of other people and that could not be achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that zonal environmental protection rules "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "seizure" of property, as there was in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was due to the fact that "the use of property without changing the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates were more successful in a legal battle regarding "seizures" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court made a number of decisions already in 1987. For example, in the case of "The First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the district flood control organization banned the restoration of a flood-damaged church tent camp. The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving the owner of using his property" is necessary in order "to give him the right to compensation."

 However, the most important thing in this matter was not the decision itself, but Judge Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of the Court divided the concepts of property rights and a bunch of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of the presumption of constitutionality of the law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative ones. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom to choose a religion. Environmental protection and the backlash of the "takings" procedure After the decision in the Carolyn Products case, the Supreme Court allowed democratically elected members of the legislative body wide powers in choosing social policy objectives, even though they restricted the actions of property owners.

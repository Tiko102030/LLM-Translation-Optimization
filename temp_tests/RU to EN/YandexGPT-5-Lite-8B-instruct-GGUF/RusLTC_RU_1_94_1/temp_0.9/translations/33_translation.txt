 The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, were another instrument for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of increasingly intense unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused a greater explosion of feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent jurist Christopher Tiedeman (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of God's anointed one did not allow to limit the powers of the police. The spread of laissez-faire policies reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by the government." But now:
government intervention in human rights is outlawed, and it is now needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil that threatens the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism and anarchism are rampant throughout the civilized world. The state is призвано to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to establish what salary an employee should receive for his work, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of a large army of disgruntled people and their obvious power in that they, especially with the development of universal suffrage, can forcefully implement their ideas about the civil state in the civilized world, conservatives were even more afraid that there might appear such an autocracy that is more despotic and more unreasonable than any that existed before. In the late XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of the "large army of disgruntled", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

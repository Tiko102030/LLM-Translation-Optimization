 The concepts of property, instrumentalism and Lochner's New Deal could be logically defined based on other court decisions that asserted property rights. In the case "Adkins v. Children's Hospital" (1923), the Court overturned the minimum wage law for the District of Columbia, ruling that this law unconstitutionally confiscated the property of workers and firms by violating their "freedom of contract", which should be "the general rule and cannot be taken away." Oliver Wendell Holmes and Pennsylvania Coal.
Shortly before considering the Adkins case, the Supreme Court made another historic decision that seemingly fully concerned itself with protecting property rights. In the case "Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Mahon" (1922), the Court declared a Pennsylvania law unconstitutional because this law limited the activities of coal companies in order to preserve the subsoil. The Court ruled that it would be generally true that regulation of property could only be carried out to a certain extent, but if it went too far, then there would already be an expropriation of property.... It remains unclear why human failures or needs will justify shifting responsibility for damage onto another... and the strong desire of society to improve its situation will not be enough to guarantee that this goal can be achieved in a shorter way than through constitutional means of evading charges.... it is only a matter of degree. The mentioned "Pennsylvania Coal" case has been cited more than a hundred thousand times over the subsequent seventy-five years and is still valid law.

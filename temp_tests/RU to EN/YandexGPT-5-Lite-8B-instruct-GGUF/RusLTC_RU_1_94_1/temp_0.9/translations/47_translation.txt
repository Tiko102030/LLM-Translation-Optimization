 By denying any privileged status to private property, Hughes came to the conclusion that:
The economic situation in the country can be a result of how the temporary limitation on the forced fulfillment of contractual conditions will be aligned with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the reserved right of the state to protect vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the treaty is not an unconditional and completely unauthorized restriction of the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority.... At the same time, whether the legislative body will pursue a reasonable or unreasonable policy, it will no longer be the Court's concern. Back in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon its assessment of economic legislation and its proceedings in legal cases on property rights issues, the legislative body had already declared the need to "protect the vital interests of society." After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court practically fully agreed with his reform program. No other decision has so clearly illustrated the new approach in the activities of the Supreme Court as was done in the case "United States v. Carolyn Products" (1938). In this particular case, the company was sued on the basis of the "Skim Milk Act" of 1923, according to which the interstate sale of milk with added fat and butter was prohibited, it was considered as the sale of a "deliberately spoiled food product harmful to health." The representatives of Carolyn Products argued that the law had unlawfully restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court deemed the law valid because, in its opinion, it was a fair instrument of state regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would have been an ordinary, simple confirmation of the established police powers. However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of Court members had separated the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

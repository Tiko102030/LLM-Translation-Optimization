Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the Supreme Court of the United States, where their interests were represented by Field's legal predecessor, John A. Campbell, a Democrat and Assistant Secretary of War (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used basic labor legislation to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the rulings by New Orleans brought us back to a time "when the prying eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The New Orleans butchers lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated among the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increased pressure that the regulatory complex would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed.

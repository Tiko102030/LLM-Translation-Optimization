 The new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of ownership, however, it still views "property rights" as relationships between people and objects (see Barzel 1997; Leibkopf 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relationship between people and the objects they own, property exists without human society; after all, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Like relationships between people and the objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it is a reward for labor that shapes the inanimate material world. According to John Locke, "labor, at first, gave the right to property" (see J. Locke 1952, p. 27 [par. 45]).

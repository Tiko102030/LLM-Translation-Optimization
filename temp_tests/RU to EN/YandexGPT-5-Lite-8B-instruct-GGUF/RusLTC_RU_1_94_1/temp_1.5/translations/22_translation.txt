Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as it was in the case of property owned or used by people of European descent in America. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from the southern states received the right to own property, however, their rights could change depending on what the political situation in the state was and what the involvement of African Americans in it was. The same instrumental approach that was used by the governments of the northern states to limit ownership rights with respect to clean air and water was also used in the southern states in order to preserve the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve social goals, only the goals were different. Redistribution of property rights in order to stimulate economic growth. By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach that he used in the Court to invalidate the treaty rights of the firm "Charles River Bridge". He did this in order to independently operate the only bridge across the Charles River that connects Boston and Cambridge (see the case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the "Charles River Bridge" company experienced some difficulties. The firm could lose "millions of dollars... that were invested in the construction of railways and canals on expressway lines... We will be thrown back a century to the inventions of that age and will be forced to remain stationary."

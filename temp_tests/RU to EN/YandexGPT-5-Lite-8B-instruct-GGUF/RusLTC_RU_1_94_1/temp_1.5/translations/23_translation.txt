In this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public goals, were immediately abolished once they hindered these goals. In the early 19th century, state authorities seeking to stimulate industry and trade launched a campaign to build roads, railroads, and canals. Indirect costs were incurred during the construction of state infrastructure, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved property. Under the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would be held accountable for indirect costs that significantly increased the cost of financing inventions. By modifying this principle, i.e., by avoiding independent entrepreneurs' responsibility for the consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property, the costs of building state infrastructure would резко уменьшиться. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Callender v. Marsh," which set a precedent for Massachusetts. Callender filed a claim for damages caused to him during street repairs, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston.

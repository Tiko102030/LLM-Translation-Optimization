 A similar situation occurred in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "physical restrictions," but also encompassed the right "to work where one wants; to earn a living through any legal means; to seek a beloved profession and, for this purpose, to conclude all deals that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for achieving the aforementioned goals successfully." After the Allgeyer court decision, a peak in judicial activity in stating motivations was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. Speaking in Court, Justice Peckham stated emphatically that "the law necessarily affects the right to conclude a contract between employer and employee," which violated "the general right to conclude a deal relevant to the activities of his firm," which was "protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution." Recognizing the fact that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary reason to intervene in human freedom or in his right to form a free contract through reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham said it was just a question of which evil is lesser: the state's power to legislate or an individual's right to personal liberty and freedom to contract. We believe that the limit of police powers has been reached and fully addressed in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and Lochner's New Deal could be logically defined based on other court decisions that asserted property rights.

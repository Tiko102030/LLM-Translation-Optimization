Local authorities regularly confiscated houses in which immoral activities were carried out, filled up the sewage from factories that «endangered people's health», banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, and did not pay any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, and others that were economically significant and not related to healthcare issues, such as the right to trade and buy, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve a social goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the fertilizer plant belonging to Manhattan Fertilizing and proceeded to «reduce» the harm caused by the actions of the plant, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

South Carolina Coastal Council" (1992), the Court required that a real estate owner in South Carolina be compensated for damage to his property after a new law "On Shoreline Management" suspended construction on his land. Again expressing its distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as in the case of Nollan, that judges must carefully study regulations in order to insure themselves against "the risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in the case "Dolan v. City of Tigard" (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from the owners to the government. The case was initiated when the administration of the city of Tigard, Oregon, stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only on the condition that she donate part of her land for the creation of a public park, pedestrian and bicycle paths in order to reduce traffic congestion on the road. Recording the speech for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration "did not challenge the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bicycle paths at Dolan's expense is a consequence of the need for the city administration to improve the transportation interchange."

 Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes came to the conclusion that:
The economic situation in the country may be the result of how the temporary restriction on the compulsory fulfillment of contractual conditions will be reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the reserved right of the state to protect vital interests of society.... Since this article of the treaty is not an unconditional and completely unauthorized restriction on the protectionist policy of the state, the legislative body should make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative competence.... At the same time, whether the legislative body will pursue a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will no longer be concerned about it. Back in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon its assessment of economic legislation and adjudication in court cases on property rights issues, the legislature had already declared the need to "protect vital interests of society". After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court almost completely agreed with his reform program. No other decision has so clearly illustrated the new approach in the activities of the Supreme Court as was done in the case "United States v. Carolyn Products" (1938). In this particular case, the company was sued based on the "Low-Fat Milk Act" of 1923, which prohibited the interstate sale of milk with added fat and butter, considering it the sale of "intentionally adulterated food product harmful to health". Representatives of Carolyn Products argued that the law had improperly restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court found the law valid, because, in its opinion, it was a fair instrument of state regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would have been an ordinary, simple confirmation of established police powers. However, the most important thing in this case was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth footnote, according to which the majority of Court members separated the concepts of property rights and a bundle of sacred civil liberties that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

 The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American politics, demonstrating the power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. Reformers wanted more. For example, labor rights advocate George McNeil warned that America must "instill Republican principles into our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F. Brice: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that trust companies are trying to impose on us today. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the liberation of black slaves, but now workers are nothing more than slaves to the united trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the civil war contributed to workers uniting and speaking out against these new demands. Large sums of money were required for military operations, and the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus associated with this once again confirmed that significant financial and industrial capital was needed to support government activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products had preferential tariffs, could only ensure the developing network of railroads.

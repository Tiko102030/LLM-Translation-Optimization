 INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and social practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ in their definition of property, and boundaries are often difficult to define. Some believe that property rights extend to objects such as land and trees, including the most perishable ones like wildlife and running water. Others add to this concept by arguing that property rights also apply to objects such as ideas (patents), and some even include human beings themselves as part of this category. Societies also differ depending on the rights that property owners have; a set of privileges relating to responsibility for taking any actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "more" or "less" protection for property, because every use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions because it affects human relationships; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when polluting the environment, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; laws prohibiting environmental pollution limit the rights of this property but protect the property of neighbors. Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to the property of neighbors.

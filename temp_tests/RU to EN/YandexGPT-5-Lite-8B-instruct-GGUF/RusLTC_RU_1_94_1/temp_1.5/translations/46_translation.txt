The most notable was the 1934 judicial decision. In the case "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," the Supreme Court, due to the country's economic instability, approved government actions regarding property regulation. The court upheld a Minnesota state law; this law imposed a moratorium on foreclosure in conditions of an unfavorable economic situation established by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the Constitution article limiting state regulation should "not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, but it still remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information." He added that the Court should interpret this article only "in accordance with the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of its residents." "To assess the effectiveness of the legislative body's activities, it is necessary not by whether it has a direct or indirect impact on contractual terms, but rather by whether the decision made is lawful, and whether it was achieved by reasonable and appropriate methods." This speaks to the social and instrumental approach to defining property, which in turn has been transformed into a constitutional principle. Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that: The economic situation in the country can result from how temporary limitation on enforcement of contractual terms will be aligned with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus will not go beyond the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unauthorized restriction of the state's protectionist policy, the legislature should make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative legality.... At the same time, what reasonable or unreasonable policy the legislative body will pursue, it will no longer be the Court's concern.

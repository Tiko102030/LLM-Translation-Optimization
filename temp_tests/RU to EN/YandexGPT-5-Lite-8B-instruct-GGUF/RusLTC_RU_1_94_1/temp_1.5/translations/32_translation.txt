 Considering in more detail the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation. "You can receive compensation for using regulated property," and Waite argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislature. "It is assumed that any law," he stated, "is constitutional. Judges cannot declare what laws are unconstitutional, unless of course it is clear and so. If there is a doubt, then the expressed will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." Recognizing that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to go for compensation "to the polling stations rather than to court." Property rights advocates were потрясены открытым чтением полномочий полиции in the Mann case. Justice Stephen Field thought differently and said that "the principle on which the majority opinion rests... undermines the rights of private property." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of the majority of its legislative body." The decision in the Mann case showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, another tool for achieving public goals, and no constitutional guarantee was longer required.

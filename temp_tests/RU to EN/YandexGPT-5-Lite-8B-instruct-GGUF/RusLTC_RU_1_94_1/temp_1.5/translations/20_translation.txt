McCarter" (1908), referred to as "the restraints applicable to property for the interest of the community." The concept of police powers has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that "By the state's police and economy, I mean the proper regulation and internal arrangement of the state, whereby the people in the country, like members of a well-organized family, are bound to follow rules of decency and good breeding, to assist their neighbors, and to be honest, industrious, and compliant in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case "Vanderbilt v. Adams" in 1827, Judge Woodworth of New York State described police powers in more detail, saying: "The supreme power in society can and must prescribe how private property rights can be used.... Police powers are based on implied rights and the duty of higher authority to protect everyone through established law so that ultimately the well-being of the whole society is increased.... Such governmental power should exist in every well-regulated society." In the case "Commonwealth v. Algar" (1851), Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court supported the legislative authority dealing with property regulation in Boston Harbor and prohibiting the construction of buildings outside this pier, and spoke about police powers as follows: We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each owner of property, regardless of whether he or she has exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility for it so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy their own property, or the whole society as a whole.

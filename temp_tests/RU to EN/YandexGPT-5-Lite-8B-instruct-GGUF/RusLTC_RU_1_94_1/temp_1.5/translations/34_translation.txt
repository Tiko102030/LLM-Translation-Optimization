 At the end of the 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of the "numerous army of discontented", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Frequently quoting Tideman, they demanded to limit the full power of the state by setting a boundary for police powers. The state courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhamer v. People" (1856), the New York State Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that banned the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who had been selling this liquor lost their property, and the procedure for изъятие собственности did not take place in accordance with the law. For the first time discovering that the concept of "legal order" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance." The Weinhamer case found an echo only about thirty years later, after which it began to be viewed with renewed interest. In the landmark case of Jacobs (1885).

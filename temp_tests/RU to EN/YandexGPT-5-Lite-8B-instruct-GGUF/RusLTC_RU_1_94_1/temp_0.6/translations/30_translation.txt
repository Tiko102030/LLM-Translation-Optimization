 The ironworking and steel industries, for whose products preferential tariffs were established, could only provide the developing network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was the recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the real goals of the bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose property was sponsored by government authorities. They wanted new laws that would expand the powers of the public in regulating ownership. They achieved поразительного success in that they secured a provision in law on an eight-hour workday and a law establishing railroad tariffs. There were objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result these bills were referred to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court decision of 1877 in the case "Mann v. Illinois" greatly alarmed the business circles of the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of radicalism popular at that time. Plaintiffs argued that by canceling existing transactions, the law violated a clause in the Constitution on transactions, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference.

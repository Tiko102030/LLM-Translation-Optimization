The judicial decision in the case of the First Episcopal Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles but merely confirmed the main provisions of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nollan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different matter because it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, owning a coastal plot of land located between public parks to the north and south of each other, the Nollans wanted to build a new three-bedroom house in place of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permission only on the condition that the Nollans agreed to grant a general right to use the territory between the high tide line and the dam located behind their house. This way, people could easily reach the parks located both to the north and south by simply walking неспеша along the sand. The commission explained its requirement by saying that a larger house would contribute to "development of the area," that "people would no longer subconsciously realize that there is a coast nearby and that they have every right to spend time there" (see Eagle 1996, p. 255).

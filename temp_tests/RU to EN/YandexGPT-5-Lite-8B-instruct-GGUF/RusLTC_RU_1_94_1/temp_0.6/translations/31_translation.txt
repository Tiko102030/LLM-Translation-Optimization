 The plaintiffs claimed that by abolishing existing transactions, the law violated a clause in the Constitution regarding transactions, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference. Recently issued to protect the civil rights of slaves liberated in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment now became an integral civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property rights from government intervention. However, this opinion was supported by a minority of members of the Court in 1877. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Stating that "powers are inherent to any sovereign power," the Court ruled that "the government can regulate both the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England in stating that when property "affects the interests of society, it legally ceases to be subject only to private regulation." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when an owner uses his property in such a way that the public has an interest in it, he actually wants this himself, and until that interest disappears, he should simply resign himself to the fact that society now manages his property because it is necessary to increase social welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply stop using it. Considering in more detail the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

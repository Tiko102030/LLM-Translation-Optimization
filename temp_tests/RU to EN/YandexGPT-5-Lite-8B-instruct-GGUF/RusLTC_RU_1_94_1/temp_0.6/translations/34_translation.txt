 At the end of the XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of a "numerous army of недовольных", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Citing Tideman frequently, they demanded to limit the entirety of state power by setting boundaries for police powers. The state courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhamer v. People" (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that banned the sale of liquor. After the law came into force, all those who had been selling this liquor lost their property, while the procedure for изъятие собственности did not take place in accordance with the established legal procedure. For the first time discovering that the concept of "established legal procedure" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance."

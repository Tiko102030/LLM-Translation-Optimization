Local authorities regularly confiscated houses where immoral activities took place, засыпали factory wastewater that "endangered people's health," banned the production of dangerous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, and did not pay any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as medicine production and livestock slaughtering, and others that were economically significant but unrelated to healthcare issues, such as the right to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation failed to achieve its public goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed policemen entered the territory of the "Manhattan Fertilizing" fertilizer plant and proceeded to "reduce" the harm caused by the factory's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

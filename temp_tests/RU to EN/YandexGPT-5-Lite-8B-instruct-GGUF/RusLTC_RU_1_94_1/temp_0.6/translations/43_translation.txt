 In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm competition brings is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy that has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, pp. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Criminal Intent and Design," Holmes relied on the aforementioned idea about the harm competition brings. Thus, he wanted to show the general necessity of having an instrumental approach and a "balance of interests principle," especially in conditions when judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At a Supreme Court session in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glue Company v. United States Glue Company." "In modern society," he declared, "each part is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, then the rest will feel it more or less. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the end of legislative power comes, it will not be enough just to show that there has been some progress in the development of the law, i.e., when it will already be possible to interfere with trade or existing transactions."

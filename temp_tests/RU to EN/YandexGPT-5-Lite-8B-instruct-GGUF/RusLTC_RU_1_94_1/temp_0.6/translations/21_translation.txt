 Representative Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court supported the legislative power dealing with property regulation issues in Boston Harbor territory and prohibiting building construction outside this pier, and spoke about police rights as follows:
We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, whether or not he has exclusive right to it, manages it with all the implied responsibility for this purpose so that its use should not harm either other people who have the same right to enjoy using their property or society as a whole. All property in this commonwealth... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules can be applied to it that are necessary to achieve the common good and universal welfare. Property rights, like all other social and conventional rights, also fall under the relevant restrictions necessary to ensure that others have not been harmed from using this property, and under established law rules, since the legislative power... can assume that this is a necessary and appropriate solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits to its manifestation." Indeed, when using universal, typical phrases like "public interest", "internal structure of the state", "implied right" and "duty of higher power to protect everyone" and "common good and universal welfare", Shaw established that the police could have a wide range of tools to achieve their goals. Southern states' governments used the police to enforce laws restricting the use of slaves' property, for example, they prohibited slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, for instance, they introduced a policy of strict racial segregation that limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor in certain professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of the property and labor of African Americans once again demonstrates what understanding of property there was at the time. (see Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Wiener 1978; Woodward 1955).

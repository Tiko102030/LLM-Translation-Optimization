Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as that of property owned or used by people of European descent in America. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from the southern states gained the right to own property, but their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and African American participation in it. The same instrumental approach that was used by the governments of the northern states to limit property rights regarding clean air and water was also used in the southern states to preserve the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public purposes, but the goals differed. Redistribution of property rights with a view to stimulating economic growth. By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach he used in the Court to void the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge Company. He did this in order to independently operate the only bridge across the Charles River that connects Boston and Cambridge (see "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge company had some difficulties. The firm could lose "millions of dollars... that were invested in the construction of railroads and canals on highway lines... We will be thrown back a century to the inventions of that age and forced to remain stagnant".

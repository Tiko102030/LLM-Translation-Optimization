 The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and lawyers therefore feared increased pressure that the regulatory framework would be expanded and income, property, and power redistributed. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion is more remembered than Judge Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority of votes and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that basic rights to free labor had been violated in Louisiana. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on J. Locke's precepts that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "an essential part of freedom... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to understand the court decision in the Slaughter-House and Mann case before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to conduct business in Louisiana, which was essentially a violation of freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in the Jacob case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wants; to earn his living in any lawful way; to seek a favorite profession and for this purpose to conclude all transactions that may be appropriate, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals."

 Callender filed a claim for compensation for damage caused to him during the street repair, which damaged the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle in order to achieve compensation. But the defense side rejected the use of English precedent with an instrumental presentation of the case. She explained that due to circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim" that Callender presented "is satisfied, then it will be necessary to check the correctness of repairing all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Callender's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts court accepted the state's offer for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damage compensation, which was undoubtedly bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public structures. "Those who buy property near the roadway," the court выдвинул, "must take into account the future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." After all, in this case, the amount of compensation would double compared to the original amount of expenses. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case of "Lansing v. Smith Walter Quackenbush," a claim was brought against the state of New York for damage due to docks falling in price after a new harbor was built in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach used in the case of "Callender v. Marsh" of Massachusetts in order to reject Queckenbush's demands.

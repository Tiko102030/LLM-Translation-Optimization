 The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the case of Callender v. Marsh in Massachusetts to reject Queckenbush's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect more or less the comfort and property of people; and in a case where the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In the end, the court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the common good must prevail over private interests." Government support for private enterprise.

Without prohibiting the government from carrying out state improvements at the expense of adjacent property owners, case law, as in the situation of Lansing v. Callender, shifted the responsibility to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals onto some people (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became particular cases. For example, in the case of Thorpe v. Rutland & Burlington Railroad Company (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of state order in the state implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (Latin sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle ensures access to consideration of the social interpretation of property.

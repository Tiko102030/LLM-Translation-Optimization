Chancellor Kent of New York in 1826 declared that "every person has so much freedom to acquire, use and dispose of his property as can be compared with an acceptable state of property and the mutual rights of others... Although thus protected, property... the legislature has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as far as may be necessary to prevent abuse of the right that might lead to violation of the rights of another person or society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in Illinois, the legislature did not have enough three pages to catalog all the powers that the Chicago city administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and restricting "the spinning of hoops, playing ball or flying kites... capable of annoying people", the new city had the power that "will force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, tallow candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, latrine, clothing factory or any other shabby, disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). The authorities of other cities also assumed broad powers over property issues. Thus, in New York, certain norms were established for the transportation of goods on the East River, for example, the price for transporting cargo varied from twenty-five cents for well-fed buffaloes or bulls to fifty cents per cord of walnut wood, one cent for empty milk bottles, twenty-five cents for child corpses and fifty cents for adult corpses (see Novak 1996).

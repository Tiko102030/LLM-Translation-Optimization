 The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in America's political life, demonstrated the power of the national government, which liberated the country from slavery. Reformers wanted more. For example, the defender of workers' rights, George McNeil, warned that America must "instill republican principles into our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to the train conductor, C.F. Bracy: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that trust companies are trying to impose on us today. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the liberation of black slaves, however, now workers are nothing more than slaves of united trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War contributed to workers uniting and speaking out against these new demands. Large sums of money were required for military operations, and the expansion and centralization of the government apparatus associated with this once again confirmed that significant financial and industrial capital was needed to support the government's activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products had preferential tariffs, were the only ones who could provide for the developing network of railroads.

Writing an opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that imposing "a condition regarding the granting of land use permits" is not a "taking" under the Fifth Amendment. The general public use requirement "does not serve the intended purpose to protect people's visual access to the beach" and "thus it is unlawful, and compensation must take place." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard: Because there is no connection between the condition and the root cause of the construction restriction, the new goal transforms into something different, dissimilar from what it was before. Now the main task for the government is to obtain a general right of land use without paying any compensation.... if the condition provided in the nature management permit is not considered as a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a legitimate land use rule, but rather "a real extortion." This time Justices Brennan and Marshall stated their dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's demand to revise the view of members of the legislative body. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration.... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the adopted measure can achieve a public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

Local authorities regularly confiscated homes where immoral activities were taking place, filled in the sewers of factories that "posed a danger to human health", banned the production of hazardous chemicals and put out fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, without paying any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as pharmaceuticals production and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance but unrelated to healthcare issues, like trade rights and purchase/sale/production (see Novak 1996, p.84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve its public goal, officials would go on strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the fertilizer factory owned by "Manhattan Fertilizing" company and began to "reduce" the harm caused by the factory's actions, destroying machinery and taking with them all necessary parts (see Novak 1996, p.226).

At the end of the 19th century, while expressing opposition to the opinion of a "numerous army of dissatisfied", lawyers and judges aimed to consecrate private property. Frequently citing Thiedemann, they demanded that state authority be limited by establishing a boundary on police powers. State courts took the initiative in defining new grounds for the sacred, rather than instrumental approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Weinhammer v. People" (1856), the New York Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law took effect, all those who were selling this liquor at the time lost their properties. The property seizure procedure did not follow the legal order. Firstly discovering that the concept "established by law" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any form and thereby subject property to complete disappearance".

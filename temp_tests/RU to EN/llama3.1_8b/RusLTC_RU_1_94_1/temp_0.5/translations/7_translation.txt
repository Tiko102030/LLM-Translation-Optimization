The purpose of a public organization is that property becomes an end in itself. Such views were spread throughout the American republic, and Locke's reasoning continued to be cited by judges of the late 19th century Supreme Court arguing against public regulation of property rights (see Eli 1998). Others reason from the position of instrumentalism. For them, property rights must be protected, i.e., these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an undisputable right exceeding all others. Instead, the problem of property has now become paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines protection for all property rights.

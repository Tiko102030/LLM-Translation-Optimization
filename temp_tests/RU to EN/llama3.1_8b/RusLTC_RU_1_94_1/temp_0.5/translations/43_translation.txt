In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible according to the principles of "such a policy as has nothing in common with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894, "Privilege, Criminal Intent and Design," Holmes based his presentation on the aforementioned notion of the harm caused by competition. He thereby wanted to show the general necessity for an instrumental approach and the principle of balancing interests especially in cases where judges weigh competing rights instead of relying on syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At a Supreme Court hearing in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glucose Co. v United States Glucose Co." "In modern society," he declared, "each of its parts is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, then the others will feel it more or less. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and legislative power comes to an end, it will not be enough simply to show that there has been some progress in the development of law, i.e., when intervention can already be made in trade or existing transactions."

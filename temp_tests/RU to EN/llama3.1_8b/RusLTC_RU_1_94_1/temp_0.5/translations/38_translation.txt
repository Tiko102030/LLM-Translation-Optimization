Butchers of New Orleans lost their case by five to four votes, but Campbell's arguments found resonance with the judges and lawyers feared growing pressure on expanding regulatory measures and redistributing income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's concurring opinion has left a stronger impression than Miller's dissenting opinion, which was supported by the majority vote and later buttressed by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that Louisiana had violated basic rights to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property on Locke's prescriptions that because free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the primary part of liberty... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to grasp the court's decision in Slotemaker-Hoekstra v. Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain insurance from a company that did not have a license to conduct commercial activities in Louisiana, which effectively violated freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation arose in Jacobson's case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he pleases; to earn his living by any lawful calling; to pursue a vocation in which he is interested and for this purpose to make all contracts which may be proper, necessary, and essential to the successful attainment of these ends."

A similar situation existed in the case of Jacobson v. Massachusetts, and Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right to "work where he pleases; earn a living by any lawful means; pursue his chosen profession and for this purpose enter into all contracts which may be proper, necessary, and essential to the successful attainment of the foregoing objectives." Eight years after the Allgeyer decision set the standard for judicial activity in articulating motivations, it was established again in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared a New York law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers under that law. In court, Justice Peckham resolutely stated that "the statute necessarily involves the right to contract between employer and employee," which is a violation of "the general right to make contracts relating to his business" which in turn "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the Federal Constitution." Acknowledging that police actions regarding property regulation are justified because they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case, there was no necessary reason for interference with human freedom or their right to make a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker. In conclusion, Peckham said it was simply a question of which evil is less: state authority to issue laws or human rights to personal freedom and freedom of contract. We believe that the limit of police power has been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner can be logically determined from other court decisions that asserted property rights.

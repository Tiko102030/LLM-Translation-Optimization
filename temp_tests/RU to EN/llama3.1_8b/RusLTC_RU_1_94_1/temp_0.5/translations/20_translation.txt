Similar actions were justified by what is called the "police powers" of the state, or as Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. wrote to the Supreme Court in the case of Hudson County Water Company v. McCarter (1908), referred to as "restrictions applicable to property for the benefit of all society." The concept of police powers has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s William Blackstone wrote that "by public police and economy I mean due regulation and internal arrangement of the state, whereby persons within it, as well as members of a well-ordered family, are bound to obey the rules of propriety and good breeding, assist their neighbors, and be orderly, industrious, and peaceable in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case of Vanderbilt v. Adams (1827) Judge Woodworth of New York State more fully described police powers, saying: "The supreme power in society may, and must, prescribe how private rights to property may be exercised.... Police powers are based on implied rights and duties of the highest authority to protect all by means of laws established so as to increase the welfare of all society.... Such state authority should exist in every well-regulated community." In the case of Commonwealth v. Alger (1851) Associate Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Court supported legislative power dealing with issues of property regulation on Boston Harbor and prohibiting buildings from being constructed beyond this dock, and spoke about police powers as follows: "We think it an established rule, arising out of the very nature of a well-organized civil society, that every owner of property, whether he has or has not the sole right to it, manages it with all the implied responsibility attaching thereto, so that its use shall not injure other people having an equal right to the enjoyment of their own property, nor the community at large."

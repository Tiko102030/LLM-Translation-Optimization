Despite constitutional guarantees regarding property, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment enshrines in the United States Constitution the principle of confiscation of property. Pursuant to this amendment, uncompensated confiscation of slave property redistributed nearly $3 billion, half of the total wealth of southern states. As a result, emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of confiscation of property. Government intervention in private property did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities frequently intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widely held opinion that it is a moral duty of government to promote economic development and support low-income citizens. For example, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited food trade, regulated wages and employment opportunities, and restricted the activities of liquor vendors, bars, and prostitutes (see Handlin and Handlin 1947; Bergin 1989; Novak 1996; Lorie 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used their right to confiscate private property by force in order to achieve public goals at the expense of property owners.

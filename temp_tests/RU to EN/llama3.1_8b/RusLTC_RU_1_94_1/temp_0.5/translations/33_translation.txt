The Mann decision showed that property rights were now also one of the interests of society, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of growing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused a greater explosion of emotions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent jurist Christopher Tiedeman, (1971 [1886] year) warned that the right of God's anointed did not allow limiting police powers. The spread of non-interference policy reduced the number of "human rights and freedoms violations by government". But now: 
the political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government intervention in human rights is declared unconstitutional, and now it is needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil threatening the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant all over civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, establish what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily... 
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power in that they can, especially under the conditions of universal electoral rights development, forcibly impose their ideas about civil state on civilized world, conservatives were even more afraid that such an autocracy could appear which would be more despotic and less reasonable than any existing before. At the end of XIX century, speaking out against the opinion of "the numerous army of dissatisfied people", lawyers and judges intended to consecrate private property.

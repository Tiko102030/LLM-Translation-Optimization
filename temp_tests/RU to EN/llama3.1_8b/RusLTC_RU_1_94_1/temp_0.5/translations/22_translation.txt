Property owned or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as that held or used by Americans of European descent. After the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, black people from southern states gained the right to own property, but their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and the level of participation of African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach used by governments in northern states to restrict property rights with respect to clean air and water was also used in southern states to maintain the dependence of African Americans on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined for achieving public goals, but the objectives differed. Redistribution of property rights to stimulate economic growth By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through redistributing property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach that he used in court to nullify the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge Company. He did this in order to manage alone the only bridge across the Charles River, which connects Boston and Cambridge (see case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge company experienced some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... invested in building railroads and canals on high-speed mainlines... We will be pushed back a century to the inventions of that era and we will have to remain stationary".

The Supreme Court of Louisiana passed a law stating that "the loss of private rights in this case has no significance due to the general profit and commercial turnover of the given commercial organization" (see Novak 1996, p. 230-31). The butchers of New Orleans took their case to the US Supreme Court, where it was represented by John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant secretary of war (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell had ruled in Dred Scott that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later he used the basic principles of labor legislation to convince the court to protect a citizen's right to property rights. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans ordinances returned us "to the time when the nagging eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery". The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance with the judges, and lawyers therefore feared growing pressure regarding what would be extended regulation complex and redistributed income, property, and power.

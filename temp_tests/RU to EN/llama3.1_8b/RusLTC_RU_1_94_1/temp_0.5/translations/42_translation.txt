Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the opinion of the majority of lawmakers about natural rights and the beliefs of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "The Social Statics of Spencer". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that means the end of productivity and prosperity rather than protection of the sacred idea of free labor. According to the provisions of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the principles of another economic theory, another instrumental vision or the choice of a different public goal. This was not the first time that Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he elaborated on how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even aim to compensate a person for all losses caused by harm inflicted upon him... Anyone can found any company, and subsequently, in order to survive in competition, they will entice customers away from their competitor, and possibly even bankrupt them."

Conclusion: The process of developing property rights Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Note remains an active law. As soon as it was brought up for discussion, the Supreme Court approved almost all bills regulating property and recognized that the powers of elected legislative members should be "as vast as the country's economic needs." At the same time, the Supreme Court has established itself as a watchdog over private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. To them it equated the right to pollute its territory or practice discrimination based on gender, racial, or religious criteria, which are now above all property owners' claims. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that property rights stand on two pillars: the substance of harm and property as an instrument, not a sacred right. Recognizing the dual nature of harm, courts consider any defended "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," with all of them needing to be balanced. After Holmes' balance-of-interests principle was approved, the Court must consider each defended "right" to property in instrumental execution, since the alternation of interests would lead to achieving more global public goals. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute, should never become an end in itself.

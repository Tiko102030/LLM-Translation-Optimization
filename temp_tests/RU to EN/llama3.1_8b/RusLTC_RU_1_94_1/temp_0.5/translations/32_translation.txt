Examining the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society more closely, Wait acknowledged several limitations on state regulation. "You can get compensation for using regulated property," and Wait claimed that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "Any law is considered constitutional," he said. "Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it is clear and obvious. If there is any doubt, then the will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." 

Acknowledging that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to seek compensation for harm "at the polling stations, not in court." Property rights defenders were shocked by the open reading of police powers in Mann's case. Judge Stephen Field thought otherwise and said that "the principle on which the majority opinion relies... undermines private property rights." 

"If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all business activity in the state will be at the mercy of the majority members of its legislative body." The decision in Mann's case showed that property rights were now also among the interests of society, another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee.

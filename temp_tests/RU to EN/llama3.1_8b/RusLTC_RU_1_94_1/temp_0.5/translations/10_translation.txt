This part of Locke's improperty theory contributed to the emergence of discussions on natural rights as a limitation on individual accumulation, which were later joined by other authors, including Thomas Paine, an American with radical views. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret such popular conclusions in society. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke created "no little difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). Considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it can serve as a socially neutral aspect: accumulation of results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. However, recognizing social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person may harm others, for example, in the case of monopolization of resources previously accessible to all.

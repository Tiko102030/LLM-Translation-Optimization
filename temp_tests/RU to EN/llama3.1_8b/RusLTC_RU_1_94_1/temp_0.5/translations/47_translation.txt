Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:

The economic climate in the country could be a result of how a temporary restriction on the enforcement of contract conditions would be reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus not exceed the reserved right of the state to protect vital public interests.... Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely invalid limitation on protectionist state policy, the legislative body should make decisions so as not to violate legislative legitimacy.... In this case, the reasonable or unreasonable policy will be conducted by the legislative body, and the Court will no longer have anything to do with it. Even in 1934, when the Court was ready to refuse the review of economic legislation and the examination of cases on property rights, the legislative body had already declared the need to "protect vital public interests." After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court almost entirely agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach of the Supreme Court as did the case "United States v. Carolene Products Company" (1938). In this particular case, a company was sued under the 1923 law on skim milk, which prohibited interstate sales of milk with added fat and oil, considering them to be the sale of "intentionally spoiled food products harmful to health." The representatives of the Carolene Products Company argued that the law unjustifiably restricted contract freedom. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court found the law valid because it was a fair tool for state regulation necessary for protecting public health.

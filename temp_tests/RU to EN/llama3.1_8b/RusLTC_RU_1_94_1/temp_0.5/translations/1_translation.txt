Society on one hand does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it can support damaged neighbors and limit the activity of this owner. This makes the right of ownership distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of pure air and water loss or the cost that the enterprise must pay to stop dumping waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not possess privileges, because they would have to reduce pollution levels or buy a neighbor's permission to pollute. Some question whether property rights have a significant impact on income distribution, while others argue in favor of their efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social result, the same combination of production and goods services. Should those who pollute the environment buy the right from their neighbors or should neighbors pay those who pollute the environment to stop doing it? Pollution of the environment will occur where costs of reducing its negative consequences are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will cease when costs of reducing the negative effect are less. The free trade market will lead to the same efficient result regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the right to air and water.

California Coastal Commission, the Supreme Court declared the committee's decision unconstitutional and established a new criterion for judicial review of land-use regulations. Recording his speech for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that presenting "conditions regarding the grant of a coastal development permit" did not constitute an "taking" under the Fifth Amendment". The requirement of general public access to the beach "does not serve the intended purpose of protecting the ability of people to visually access the beach" and "is thus unlawful, and compensation must be made." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard:

Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying reason for limiting construction, the new goal is transformed into something entirely different from what it was before. Now the primary concern of the government is to obtain general public access to the beach without paying any compensation.... If the condition specified in the coastal development permit is not considered a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a lawful land-use regulation but merely "a real extortion". This time Justices Brennan and Marshall dissented. They rejected Scalia's call to reconsider the legislative body's opinion. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration.... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure taken can achieve a public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

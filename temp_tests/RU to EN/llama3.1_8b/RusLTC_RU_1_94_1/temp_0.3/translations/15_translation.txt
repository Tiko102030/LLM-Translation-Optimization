Despite constitutional guarantees regarding property, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment enshrines in the United States Constitution the principle of confiscation of property based on wealth. Pursuant to this amendment, uncompensated confiscation of slave owners' property redistributed nearly $3 billion, half of all Southern states' wealth. As a result, emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples of property confiscation in history. Government intervention in private property did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widely held opinion that it is a moral duty of government to promote the development of a thriving economy and support low-income citizens. For example, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, as well as limited the activities of liquor vendors, bars, and prostitutes (see Handlin and Handlin 1947; Bergen 1989; Novak 1996; Lorini 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right to compulsory expropriation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

However, the most important aspect of this matter was not the decision itself, but rather the fourth footnote of Judge Stone's opinion, according to which the majority of the Court shared the concepts of property rights and sacred civil liberties, which were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of constitutional presumptions of law, while elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. Stone, on the other hand, warned that there would be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and the countermovement of "takings"

After the Carolene Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body to have broad powers in choosing social policy objects, even though they limited the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policies conducted in this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case of Justice v. Marinette County vividly illustrates how judicial power easily applied the Carolene Products provision. Ronald and Catherine L. Just filed a lawsuit regarding the revocation of ordinances that prevented them from occupying designated wetlands for building homes. Rejecting the Just family's demands, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate exercise of police powers.

There exists an "undeniable" right of ownership for the worker, because: Although the land and all lower beings belong to people in common, nonetheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands are his by strict account. Whatever man may extract from that state in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and adds something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... For since this labor is undeniable ownership of the worker, no one other than he can have rights to what he once combined with it, at least in cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for others' common use. Thus Locke sanctifies property as physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone else's labor, i.e., turning a person into a slave. From this it follows that property rights should owe nothing to society; nevertheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

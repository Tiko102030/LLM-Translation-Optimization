American property was valued even less: debts to English creditors were, without much deliberation, annulled; some states, including Virginia, closed cases for hearings on suits filed by British subjects (see Eli 1998, p. 35). The widespread confiscation of the property of opponents of independence from England did not bode well for the safety of property in the new republic. The main rebels fought to curb what they saw as "excess democracy" (see Tomlins 1993, p. 60). By ensuring property security, "the protector of any right" became a concern for the creators of the first constitutions of all states.

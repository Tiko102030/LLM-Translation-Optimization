Denying any privileged status to private property, Hughes came to the conclusion that:

The economic climate in the country can be a result of how temporary restrictions on the enforcement of contract conditions will be coordinated with the true meaning and purpose of its articles, and thus not exceed the reserved right of the state to protect vital public interests.... Since this article is not an unconditional and completely invalid restriction on the protectionist policy of the state, the legislative body should make decisions so as not to violate the legislative authority.... In doing so, the legislative body will conduct a reasonable or unreasonable policy, which the Court will no longer concern itself with. As early as 1934, when the Court was ready to refuse to evaluate economic legislation and to adjudicate cases on property rights, the legislative body had already stated the need to "protect vital public interests". After Roosevelt's re-election in 1936 and his attempt to "remove" the Court in 1937, the Court had almost completely agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach of the Supreme Court as was done in the case of "United States v. Carolene Products Co." (1938). In this specific case, a company was sued under the 1923 "Non-Fat Milk Act", which prohibited interstate sales of milk with added fat and oil, considering them to be the sale of "intentionally spoiled food products harmful to health". The representatives of the Carolene Products Company argued that the law unreasonably restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting arguments in favor of property rights, the Court considered the law valid because it was a fair instrument of state regulation necessary for protecting public health. The decision itself would have been an ordinary and simple confirmation of established police powers. However, more importantly in this case was not the decision itself, but Justice Stone's fourth footnote, according to which most members of the Court shared the concepts of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

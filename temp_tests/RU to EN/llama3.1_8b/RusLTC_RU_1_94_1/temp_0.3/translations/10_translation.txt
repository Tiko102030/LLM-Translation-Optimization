This part of Locke's immanent theory contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, which were later joined by other authors, including the well-known American radical Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret such popular social inferences from Locke. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke created "no little difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). Considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it may also appear as a socially neutral aspect: accumulation of results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. However, recognizing social parameters of ownership allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person can harm others, for example, in cases of monopolization of resources previously accessible to all.

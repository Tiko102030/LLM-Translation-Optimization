Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision on contracts which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had become an unalienable civil right and constitutional guarantee of property, protecting private property from government interference. However this view was supported by a minority of members of the Court in 1877. Dismissing the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee of property, the Court affirmed and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Saying that "powers are inherent to any supreme authority", the Court ruled that "the government may regulate both the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chancellor Hale of England in a statement that when property "impacts public interests, it by law ceases to be subject to private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of people is the highest law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: 

when a property owner uses their property so as to give rise to an interest in it from the public, they actually want this and until that interest disappears, they should only submit to society managing their property because it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he simply must cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

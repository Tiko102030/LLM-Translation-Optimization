The iron-processing and steel industries, which had favorable tariffs imposed on their products, were the only ones that could provide a developing network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was recently centralized banking system, founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzell 1984, 1990). Angry workers and farmers saw the true goals of bankers and owners of iron-processing and railway companies, whose property was just as supported by state organs. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority in matters of regulating property. They achieved remarkable success in achieving a law on an eight-hour working day and a law setting railroad tariffs. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, and consequently these bills were sent to the Supreme Court for consideration. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive state regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's decision in 1877 in the case "Munn v. Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about growing interest in radical ideas that were popular at the time. The plaintiffs claimed that by invalidating existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional clause on contracts and also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference.

Examining more closely the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limits on government regulation. "You can be compensated for use of regulated property," and Waite argued that the ultimate decision to award compensation must be made by a legislative body. "It's assumed any statute is constitutional," he declared. Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional, unless they clearly are; if there is doubt, the will of the legislative majority must prevail." Admitting that this power could be abused, he encouraged citizens to pursue redress through electoral offices rather than the courts. Advocates for property rights were dismayed by the reading-in of police powers in Mann v. Oklahoma. Judge Stephen Field differed and said that "the principle on which a majority holds . . . subverts private property rights." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and commercial activity in the state will be at the disposal of the majority members of its legislative bodies".

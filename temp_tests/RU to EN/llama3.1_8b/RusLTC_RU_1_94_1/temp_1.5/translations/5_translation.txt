A new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of property, yet still considers “property rights” as relations between humans and objects (see Barzel 1997; Liebhart 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As a relation between humans and objects, property exists without human society; for instance, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Like the relations between humans and objects that they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it is a reward for the labor that shapes the inanimate material world.

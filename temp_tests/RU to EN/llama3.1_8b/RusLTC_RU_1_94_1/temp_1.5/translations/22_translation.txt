Property owned or used by African Americans did not possess the same degree of inviolability as property owned or used by Americans of European origin. Following the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, blacks from southern states gained the right to own property, however their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and the level of involvement of African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach that was used by governments of northern states for restricting property rights regarding pure air and water were also applied in the south to keep African Americans dependent on whites. In both cases, property rights were determined in order to achieve public goals, but the objectives differed. Redistribution of property ownership with the aim of stimulating economic growth By sponsoring construction of public infrastructure through redistribution of property rights, the Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 relied on the instrumental approach that he used in court to invalidate the contractual rights of Charles River-Bridge company. He did this in order to manage individually a single bridge over the Charles river, which connects Boston and Cambridge (see case of "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense of Charles River-Bridge company encountered some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... invested in building iron roads and canals on high-speed highways... We will be pushed back a century to the inventions of that time and we will have to remain motionless".

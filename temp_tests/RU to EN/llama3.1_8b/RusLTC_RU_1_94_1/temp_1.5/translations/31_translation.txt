Plaintiffs argued that by nullifying existing contracts, the law violated the clause of the Constitution regarding contracts, which also violated the newly issued Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil liberties of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an unalienable civil right and constitutional guarantee of property ownership that protected individual rights from government intervention. However this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 court members who, rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee on property, upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Speaking about what "powers are inherent to any supreme authority," the court decided that "the government can regulate the behavior of its citizens towards one another and how people should use their property but only when necessary for the common good." The Chief Justice of the Supreme Court, Waite, quoted Lord Chancellor Hale's statement that, when a person's property affects society, it in fact stops being a subject of merely private regulation.

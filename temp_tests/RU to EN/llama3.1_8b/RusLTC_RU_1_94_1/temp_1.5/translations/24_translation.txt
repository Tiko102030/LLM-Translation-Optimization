Calender filed a claim for compensation in connection with damage caused to his house in Boston during the street repair work. His attorney cited references from English laws, common law precedents, and this principle to get him compensated. However, the opposing side dismissed using an English precedent case citing that due to the American circumstances, the road had to be repaired. They added: "If such a claim by Calender were granted, it would be necessary to examine the accuracy of every highway repair in our country." This argument convinced the judges who denied Calender's compensation claim. Then Massachusetts state court accepted the state proposal to interpret the case with market value, aiming to avoid subsequent damage claims, which was bad news for those whose property directly bordered public structures. "Those buying a home near roadways should be aware of future lane expansions or reductions," ruled the court.

Representative of the Supreme Court of Massachusetts Lemuel Shaw supported legislative authority governing property regulation issues at Boston Harbor and prohibiting building structures outside that harbor, and on police rights stated:

We believe it is an established norm arising from the very nature of a well-organized civil society where every owner of property, regardless of whether he holds exclusive or non-exclusive rights to it, manages it with the implied responsibility of using it in such a way that its use should not cause harm to others who have a similar right to enjoy their own property, nor to society as a whole.

All property in this community is either completely or partially owned by the government and may be subject to common rules necessary for achieving general welfare and overall prosperity. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to corresponding limitations required to ensure that others have not been harmed by the use of the given property, as well as established laws, because legislative authority can assume that such is a necessary and reasonable decision.

In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is far easier to feel and understand life and its sources than it is to establish limits or boundaries to their manifestations." Indeed, when using universal and typical phrases like "public interest," "the internal structure of the state," "implied rights and duties" and "high authority protecting all," Shaw established that law enforcement can possess a large number of tools in achieving their objectives.

Southern states governments resorted to police assistance for enforcing laws limiting slave property use, such as banning slave owners from teaching their slaves how to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they implemented unusual restrictions on private property rights, for example by introducing harsh racial segregation policies, which prohibited African Americans from buying real estate or practicing certain occupations.

Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources even if this may cause harm to neighboring property. On the other hand, it can support affected neighbors and limit the activities of that owner. This makes ownership a distributive right. When owners have rights to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the magnitude of the loss of clean air and water or the cost of payment that will have to be paid to the enterprise so that it does not discharge waste into the environment any longer. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not possess privileges because they would have to reduce pollution, or they would have to buy a neighbor's permission to pollute. Some question whether property rights have significant impact on income distribution, while others in turn present arguments in favor of their effectiveness. In his famous article of 1960, Ronald Coase claimed that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries would lead to the same effective social result, the same combination of goods and services production. Should those who pollute the environment buy rights from their neighbors or should neighbors pay those who pollute to stop it? Pollution of the environment will occur where costs of reducing its negative consequences are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will be stopped when costs of reducing the negative effect are lower. A free market trade will lead to the same effective result regardless of distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns rights to air and water.

The actions were justified by the concept of police powers, or as Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. put it in Hudson County Water Company v. McCarter (1908), "restrictions upon property in the interest of all". The concept of police powers has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s William Blackstone wrote that by public police and economy he meant such proper regulation and internal arrangement as is necessary for the well-ordering and maintenance of the state, whereby persons in the country, as members of a well-regulated family, are bound to conform themselves to rules of decorum and good manners, assist their neighbours, and be orderly, industrious, and harmless in their designs (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In Vanderbilt v. Adams (1827) New York State Supreme Court Justice Woodworth more specifically described police powers saying: "The supreme power in society...may and ought to prescribe how the private rights of property shall be exercised... Police powers are based on implied rights and duties of the highest authority to protect all, by means of statutes established by law, so that the general welfare may thus be promoted... Such a public power is indispensable in every well-regulated society." In Commonwealth v. Alger (1851), the representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Court Lemuel Shaw upheld legislative authority over the regulation of property rights within Boston harbor and prohibiting buildings from being erected outside this harbor area, and stated on police powers: "We consider it an established maxim, arising out of the very essence of a well-ordered civil society, where every owner of property, whether he has or has not exclusive dominion, is regarded as administering it with all the implied responsibilities, so that its use shall cause no inconvenience to others enjoying like right to the pleasure of using their own, nor injury to society in general."

A similar situation was in the Jacobson case court and Judge of the Supreme Court Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also covered the right to "work wherever he wants; earn a living by any lawful means; engage in search for his desired profession, and for this purpose conclude all deals that may be proper, necessary and important for successful achievement of the above mentioned goals." After the Allgeyer case ruling, there was a peak of judicial activity explaining motives eight years later in the Lochner v. New York (1905) case, where The Court declared the New York law unconstitutional because it contained reductions in working hours for bakers. Speaking at the Court, Judge Peckham resolutely stated that "the law unambiguously infringes upon the right to enter into a contract between employer and employee," which is a violation of "common law right to make contracts related to their company activities" protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution. Recognizing the fact that police actions in regulating property were justified, as they protect "health or moral conduct", Peckham claimed that in this case "there is no necessary ground for interfering with human freedom or his right to make a free contract by reducing working hours for baker's position." In conclusion, Peckham spoke of what it was just a question of which of the two evils is less: State power to issue laws or the human right to personal freedom and contract-making. We believe that the limits of police authority have been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism and New Deal Lochner could be logically identified from other court decisions that asserted rights of property.

For the first time, it set a precedent in the case of "Palmer v Mullan" in the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream filed an action for damages after a dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: The defendants had the same right with regard to their territory... [the principle of "use your own so as not to harm others"] should be limited in reasonable bounds so that a person may enjoy the use of their property simply due to some silly inconvenience or damage to others... There was a law allowing for some such inconveniences, as when one could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river and would acquire exclusive right, at least over a certain distance, whether his neighboring banks belonged to him or not.... the community whose interests should not be forgotten would be deprived of that portion of its profit which it might obtain by way of competitive business. This approach democratized property rights, so that old as well as new residents enjoyed the same privileges. Thus there passed an redistribution of wealth from established residents in favor of entrepreneurs because settled inhabitants were prevented from limiting entrepreneurial activity. At the same time, by removing established monopolistic authority and liberating entrepreneurs from lawsuits regarding indirect losses, these cases weakened existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the principle "use your own so as not to harm others" had nothing to do with ideas about sacred private property put forth by J Locke.

When can it be possible to intervene in trade or already existing deals? The scope of activity should be delimited and the quantitative differences defined. In the Pennsylvania Coal case Holmes was more in the role of a Trojan Horse than a victor-defender of sacred property rights. Instead of treating the right to property as an inherent right, Holmes established "the taking principle" for property based on establishing balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on circumstances and is a discretionary procedure. According to Holmes, property should be reasonably used constantly; however, the idea of what reasonable means is currently vague. If actions go too far, in that case necessary compensation would follow; yet exactly which judges, taking into account public needs and special circumstances, decide that it has gone too far. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case judges must carefully evaluate and balance competing interests on both sides. Property owners should not, in turn, put everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "government can hardly continue its activities at all if some aspect of property will not be resolved."

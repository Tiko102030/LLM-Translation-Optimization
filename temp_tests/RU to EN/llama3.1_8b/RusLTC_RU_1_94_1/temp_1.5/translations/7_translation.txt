The goal of the public organization is that property becomes an end in itself (Smith 1999). Such views were spread in American republics, and Locke's arguments continued to be cited by late 19th-century Supreme Court judges who opposed public regulation of property rights (see Elie 1998). Others reason about property from a standpoint of instrumentalism. For them, property rights must be protected, i.e., these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it the end in itself, unchallengeable right, which prevails over everything else. Instead, the problem of property has become paramount, and this very instrumental approach undermines protection of all property rights.

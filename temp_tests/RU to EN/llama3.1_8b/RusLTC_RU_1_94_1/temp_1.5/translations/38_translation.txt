The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and attorneys feared increasing pressure regarding expanded regulations and a reallocation of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissent has stuck in memory more strongly than Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority of votes and later reinforced with arguments favoring police powers. Like Campbell, Field claimed that Louisiana had violated the fundamental rights to free labor. Referencing Adam Smith, he grounded his defense of property on Locke's prescriptions that, since free labor is the ultimate source of property, "property is 'the principal part of liberty... in an American sense.'" It took Judge Field twenty years to unravel the court's decision in Sloughter-House v Mann before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, Allgeyer v Louisiana announced that a state law was unconstitutional. According to this law, no one could obtain insurance with companies not licensed to operate commercially in Louisiana, which in essence was an infringement on freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar case appeared at Jacobson, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham proved that human freedom encompassed more than simply "absence of his physical restriction" but also included the right "to work where he pleased; to earn his living in any lawful way; to pursue a vocation and thereby enter into all proper, necessary and pertinent contracts for the successful attainment of these objectives."

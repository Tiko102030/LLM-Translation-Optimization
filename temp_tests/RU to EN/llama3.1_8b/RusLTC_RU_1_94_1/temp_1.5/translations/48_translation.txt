However, the most important aspect of this matter was not the decision itself, but rather the fourth footnote by Judge Stone, according to which the majority of the Court divided the concepts of property rights and sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases related to economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by principles of constitutional law presumptions, while elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for assessing economic provisions and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established on instrumental grounds, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislations. Stone, instead, warned that there would be a thorough investigation of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or religious freedom protection.

Environmental protection and "taking" procedure reversal
After ruling in Carolene Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected legislative members chosen by democratic means broad discretion in choosing social policy objects, even though they restricted actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main objectives of policies conducted within this context. The Wisconsin state court's 1972 Justice v Marinette County case aptly illustrates how judicial power easily applied to Carolene Products' condition. Ronald and Catherine L. Justice filed an action regarding a voidance of decrees hindering them from occupying parcels intended for them, but designated as wetlands, in order to build houses.

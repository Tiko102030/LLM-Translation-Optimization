The Mann decision revealed that property rights were now also one of the interests of society, and another tool to achieve public goals, no longer requiring any constitutional guarantee. In light of growing public unrest, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and triggered even greater emotional explosions in those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After events at Haymarket Square in 1886, prominent legal scholar Christopher Tiedemann warned that the "divine right" did not allow for limits on police power. The spread of non-intervention policies reduced the number of government human rights violations. Now, however: the political pendulum swings back in the opposite direction... Government intervention into human rights is declared unconstitutional, and now it is needed as a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unruly across all civilized nations. The state is tasked with protecting the weak from cunning of the stronger, establishing the wage that workers deserve for their labor, and the hours they must work daily...

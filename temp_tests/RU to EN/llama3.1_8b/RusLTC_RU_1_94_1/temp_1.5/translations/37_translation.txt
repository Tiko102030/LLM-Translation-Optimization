The Supreme Court of Louisiana enacted a law which established that "the losses in private property in this case are entirely without consequence due to the overall profit and commercial traffic of said mercantile firm" (see Novak 1996, p.230-31). Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the United States Supreme Court, where they were represented by John A. Campbell, a Democrat and assistant secretary of war, predecessor to Field (see Foner 1988, p.530). Having served on the High Court before the War, Campbell ruled in Dred Scott that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States; fifteen years later he used core provisions of labor legislation to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property rights. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans city ordinances returned us to "the time when the annoying eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to his bakery." The butchers from New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found an echo among the judges, and lawyers thus feared growing pressure regarding what measures would be added to regulatory complex, how income, property, and power would be redistributed.

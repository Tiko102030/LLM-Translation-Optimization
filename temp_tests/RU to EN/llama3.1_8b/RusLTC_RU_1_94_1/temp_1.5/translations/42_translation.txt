After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to start a new case based on alternative law. Instead of relying in his speech on the opinion of most legislative assembly participants on natural rights and the beliefs of John Locke, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". In this way, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that means the end of productivity and prosperity rather than a protection of the sacred concept of free labor. According to the terms of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the bases of another economic theory, another instrumental view or the choice of a different social goal. It was not for the first time that Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to defend property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881) he elaborated in detail that the concept of absolute rights is contrary to the preconditions of creating a competitive market economy.

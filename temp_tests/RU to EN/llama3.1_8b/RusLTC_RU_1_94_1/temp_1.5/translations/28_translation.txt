A new approach did not represent a general criticism of property rights or a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of these rights it merely established certain property in relation to other property. According to one historian of law, Villard Hall, this approach is one of the working principles of the American rule of order - "the freeing up of all human creative energy." Although the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect losses remains on the shoulders of entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to start projects that generate profits. In theory, if a project was worth more than the cost of damage, its profitability would still rise even if an entrepreneur had to pay the amount of damages incurred. However, in practice, all costs are transferred to the injured party, which only increases the number of entrepreneurs' projects, while all risks and operational expenses fall on the shoulders of neighbors. If property rights were observed more legally, high operational costs would have prevented the execution of an entrepreneur's plans.

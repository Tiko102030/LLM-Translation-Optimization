As applied to this principle, it provides access to the social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield urged that limitations be recognized on the right of ownership because, as he said, the actions performed by one owner affect others even if they take place within their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is only a norm, not a rule; it should not be applied in most cases where conflicting property interests are at stake and both parties complain of each other's interference on their territories (see Holmes 1894 p. 11). Questions concerning matters of ownership were reviewed in the nineteenth century to foster business activity even if this came at the expense of causing harm to others' property rights. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" was preserved but is now used to protect entrepreneurs from acting in accordance with passive neighbors who wish their property to remain unspoiled. At that time, the concept of "damage" was viewed not as a series of unjustifiable actions towards another's property but as an inability for owners to use theirs.

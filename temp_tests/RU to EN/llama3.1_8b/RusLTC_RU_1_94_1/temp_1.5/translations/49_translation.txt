According to the court ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe on the rights of other people and could not be achieved at its initial composition." The Court recognized that zoning rules for environmental protection "should be reasonable", but "we believe that preventing the violation of public rights by introducing restrictions on private property usage in its original purpose is justified." There was no "taking" of property, as had occurred in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was due to the fact that "using property without changing the natural composition of its soil is allowed by law". Property right advocates achieved greater success in court proceedings regarding "takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's counsel Antonin Scalia, the Court made a series of decisions as early as 1987. For example, in the case of First English Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after regional flood control district banned repair of a flooded church tent camp after it had been damaged by a flood. The Court informed the county administration that "a decree depriving an owner of the use of his property" is necessary in order for "it to grant him the right to compensation for damages".

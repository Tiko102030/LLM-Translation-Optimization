In his opinion for the court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that "the condition concerning the grant of a permit to use property" did not constitute a "taking" under the Fifth Amendment." The requirement for general zoning "does not serve the assumed purpose of protecting the people's visual access to the beach" and is thus "unconstitutional, and compensation must be had". In conclusion Scalia stated:

Because there is no nexus between the condition imposed and the primary purpose of restricting construction, a new purpose has been transformed into something quite different from what it was before. The main problem now facing government is obtaining general zoning rights without paying any compensation... if the condition specified in a land-use permit is not considered to be a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose as such, then a limitation on construction will no longer be a lawful rule of land use but only "extortion". On this occasion, justices Brennan and Marshall filed a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's proposal to re-examine the legislature's intent. They argued that "the proper standard for consideration... is whether government can rationally decide if a measure taken by it may reach public purpose."

At the end of the 19th century, in response to the opinion "of the numerous army of discontented", lawyers and judges sought to consecrate private property. Frequently citing Tiedemann, they demanded that government authority be limited by establishing a boundary of police powers. The state courts took the initiative to establish new grounds for a sacred, rather than an instrumental approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinheimer vs People" (1856), the Appellate Court of New York State declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law took effect, all those who were selling it at exactly this moment lost their properties. However, the procedure for expropriation did not go through in accordance with established law. For the first time, upon discovering the concept of "established by law order" protected property, the court decided that "the legislative power cannot completely eliminate commercial activity in any of its manifestations, and thereby expose property to complete extinction."

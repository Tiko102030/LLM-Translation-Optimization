Iron processing and steel industries with favorable tariffs were the only ones capable of supporting a developing rail network. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was the recently centralized banking system, founded in New York during the war (see Benzell 1984, 1990). Angered workers and farmers saw the true intentions of bankers and owners of iron and steel companies, whose property was sponsored by the state authorities themselves. They wanted new laws that would broaden public control over property regulation issues. They achieved a remarkable success in securing legislation establishing an eight-hour workday and railroad tariffs. There were also objections from many major businessmen as a result of which these bills were sent for review to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still endorsed comprehensive state control over property rights regulation. The Supreme Court's decision in Mann v. Illinois (1877) strongly alarmed business circles in the state, concerned with increasing interest in popular radicalism ideas of that time. The plaintiffs claimed that by canceling existing contracts, the law violated the clause of the Constitution concerning contracts and thus breached a newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government intervention.

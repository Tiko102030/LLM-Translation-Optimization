There exists an "indisputable" right of property to the laborer because: 
Although the land and all lower beings belong in common to all people, nonetheless... The labor of his body and the work of his hands, strictly speaking, belong to him. Whatever he then derives from this condition, which nature created and preserved, he combines it with his labor and joins to it something belonging exclusively to him, thus making it his property... Since this labor is the laborer's undisputed property, no one but him may have a right to what he once joined to it, at least in those cases when there is sufficient of the same kind remaining for common use by others. Thus Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone else's labor, i.e., turning people into slaves. From this follows that rights of property should give nothing to society; nonetheless, owners form societies and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

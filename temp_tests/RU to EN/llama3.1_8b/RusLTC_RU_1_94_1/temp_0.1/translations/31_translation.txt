Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated a constitutional provision on contracts and also infringed upon the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had become an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property from government interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the Court's 1877 members. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee of property, the Court affirmed and even expanded the authority of previous regulation. Speaking about what "powers are inherent in any supreme authority", the Court ruled that "the government may regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people should use their property but only when it is necessary for the common good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale of England's statement that, when property "affects public interests, it ceases by law to be a subject of private regulation." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the highest law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: 

when a property owner uses their property in such a way that there is an interest in it from the public, he actually wants this and until the interest disappears, he must only submit to society managing his property because it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply stop using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

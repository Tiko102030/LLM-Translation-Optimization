The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner could have been logically determined from other court decisions that upheld property rights. In the case of "Adkins v. Children's Hospital" (1923), the Court struck down a minimum wage law for the District of Columbia, ruling that this law unconstitutionally confiscated workers' and firms' property, violating their "freedom of contract," which must be "a common rule and cannot be taken away." Oliver Wendell Holmes and Pennsylvania coal

Just before considering Adkins' case, the Supreme Court made another historic decision that seemed to fully concern the protection of property rights. In the case of "Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Mahon" (1922), the Court declared a Pennsylvania law unconstitutional because this law, in order to preserve subsoil, limited the activities of coal mining companies. The Court ruled that a common rule for all would be that regulation of property can only be carried out up to a certain degree; if it goes too far, then it will pass into confiscation.... It remains unclear why human failures or needs would justify shifting responsibility for damage onto another's shoulders.... and the strong desire of society to improve its position would not be enough to guarantee achieving this goal by a shorter, constitutional means than paying off an accusation... this is only a question of degree. The Pennsylvania Coal case, mentioned over 100,000 times in the following seventy-five years, is still in effect today. It contributed to the emergence of a new field of law on confiscation.

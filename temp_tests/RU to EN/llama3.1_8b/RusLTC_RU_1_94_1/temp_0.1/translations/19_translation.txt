Local authorities regularly confiscated homes where immoral activities took place, dumped factory sewage that "posed a threat to people's health", banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, without paying any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as pharmaceuticals production and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance but unrelated to public health issues like trade rights and sales (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve the public goal, officials began to strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, a street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and proceeded to "reduce" the harm caused by the factory's actions, destroying machinery and taking necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

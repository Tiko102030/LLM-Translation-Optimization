American property was valued even less: English debts to creditors were, without much deliberation, annulled; some states, including Virginia, closed cases scheduled for hearing on claims filed by British subjects (see Eli 1998, p. 35). The widespread confiscation of property from opponents of independence from England did not bode well for the security of property in the new republic. The main rebels fought to curb what they saw as "excess democracy" (see Tomlins 1993, p. 60). By securing property rights, "the protector of any right" became a concern for the creators of the first constitutions of all states.

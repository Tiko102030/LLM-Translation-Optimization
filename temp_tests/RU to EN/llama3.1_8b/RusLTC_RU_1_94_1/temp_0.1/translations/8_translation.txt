Instead, the issue of property rights is now paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines protection for all property rights. A certain form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke defends property rights not so resolutely as might be understood from the well-known excerpt quoted above (see Horn 1990; Ketznelson 1998, p. 77). In this excerpt he addresses the question of the right of English Stuart monarchs to levy taxes without parliamentary consent. But in addition to this particular issue, Locke recognizes a social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that each person should have as much as they can use. Accumulation should be limited so that everyone can share the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and in the same state" for others.

The purpose of a public organization is that property becomes an end in itself. Such views were spread in the American republic, and Locke's reasoning continued to be cited by judges of the late nineteenth-century Supreme Court opposing public regulation of property rights (see Eli 1998). Others reason about property from the position of instrumentalism. For them, property rights must be protected, i.e., these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an unassailable right that surpasses all others. Instead, the problem of property has now become paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines protection of all property rights.

A certain form of ownership as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke does not defend property rights as resolutely as can be understood from the well-known excerpt quoted above (see Horn 1990; Ketznelson 1998, p. 77). In this excerpt, he addresses the question of the right of the Stuart monarchs of England to levy taxes without parliamentary consent. But in addition to this issue, Locke recognizes a social definition of ownership, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke said that each person should have as much as they can use. Accumulation should be limited so that everyone can share the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and still enough" for others.

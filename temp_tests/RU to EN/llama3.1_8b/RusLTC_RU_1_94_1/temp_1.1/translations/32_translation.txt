Examining the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society more closely, Wade recognized several limitations on state regulation. "You can get compensation for using regulated property," and Wade maintained that the final decision on compensation should be made by a legislative body. 

"It is assumed that any law - he said - is constitutional. Judges cannot declare what laws are unconstitutional unless it is clear. If there is doubt, then the expressed will of the legislature must be supported by a majority." 

Admitting that this authority could be abused, he urged citizens to seek redress in "the voting booth, not in court". The defenders of private property were shocked by the open reading of police powers in the case of Mann. Judge Stephen Field thought otherwise and said that the principle on which the majority opinion relied would undermine property rights. 

"If it becomes law - he warned - then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of a simple majority of its legislative body." The decision in Mann's case showed that now private property rights were also considered one of the interests of society, another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee.

Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words in order to start a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the opinions of most legislative body members regarding natural rights and J. Locke's beliefs, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that would rather indicate the end of productivity and prosperity than protection of a sacred notion of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental view or choice of another public goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged existing models of legal justification used to defend property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he detailed that the concept of absolute rights contradicts the preconditions for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even try to compensate a person for all damages caused to him... Anyone can found any firm, and later on, in order to survive in competitive struggle, he will entice away his competitor's clients, and perhaps thus even ruin them."

The society does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources even if it may cause harm to neighboring property. On the other hand, it can support the neighbors affected by this and limit the activity of the aforementioned owner. This makes property right distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces their neighbor's real income by the amount of loss of pure air and water or the cost that they would have to pay a company to stop dumping waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if not for privileges because they would have had to reduce pollution levels, or buy permission from neighbors to pollute. Some question whether property rights significantly affect income distribution, while others argue in favor of their efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase claimed that regardless of property rights allocation, any clear establishment of boundaries would lead to the same effective social outcome, the same production combination of goods and services. Should those polluting the environment buy permits from their neighbors or should neighbors pay those polluting the environment to stop it? Pollution of the environment will occur where costs to reduce its negative effects are more than the value of clean air for neighbors and will be stopped when costs to reduce a negative effect are less. A free trade market will lead to the same effective result regardless of property rights allocation and regardless of who owns the right to air and water.

Butchers of New Orleans lost their case by five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance with the judges and therefore his lawyers were worried about increasing pressure over what would be extended regulatory complex measures, redistributed income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's special opinion lingered in memory more than Judge Miller's majority opinion, which was later reinforced by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the state of Louisiana had violated basic rights to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his property defense on Locke's prescriptions, noting that since free labor is a final source of property, "property itself - it is the primary part of freedom ... in the American sense of the word." It took Field twenty years to grasp the court's decision in the Sloughter House case before his views were accepted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the Allgeyer v. Louisiana case, the Court declared a state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain insurance from an enterprise that did not have permission for business activities in the state of Louisiana, thus essentially violating freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation occurred in Jacobs' court and Chief Justice Rufus W. Peckham demonstrated that human freedom included not only "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right to "work wherever he wants; earn a living in any lawful way; pursue a desired profession, and with this goal in mind conclude all appropriate, necessary, and important contracts for achieving the above goals."

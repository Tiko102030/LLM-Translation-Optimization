Cohen 1991; Liechtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955). Property belonging to or used by African Americans did not have the same degree of inviolability as property owned or used by Americans of European descent. Following the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, black people in southern states obtained the right to own property, but their rights could change depending on the political situation in the state and the level of participation by African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach was used in southern states as in northern states to limit the rights of property owners over pure air and water for preserving African American dependence on whites. In both cases, the rights of property were determined for achieving public goals, only with different objectives. Redistribution of property ownership for economic growth 

By redistributing property rights to stimulate economic growth by supporting public infrastructure construction through redistributive measures, U.S. Supreme Court Chief Justice Roger Taney in 1837 used the instrumental approach he employed at the court to nullify the company "Charles River Bridge" contract rights. He did it to manage personally a single bridge over the Charles River connecting Boston and Cambridge (see Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge case). The defense side for the company "Charles River Bridge" experienced some difficulties. The company might lose "millions of dollars... invested in building railroad lines and canals along high-speed highways... We will be thrown back a century to the inventions of this century and will have to remain immobile forever".

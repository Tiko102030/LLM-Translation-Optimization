In the landmark case of Jacobson (1885), the New York State Court of Appeals declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on rented premises because it "interfered with the owner's right to profit and freely use his property" and thereby "deprived him of his property and personal liberty." Comparing concepts of individual freedom and property rights, Judge Earl stated that freedom, in its broadest sense, means not only "the right to be free from actual slavery... but also the right of a man to use his faculties by all lawful means necessary to earn an honest living..." Therefore, any laws that weaken or fetter these rights are considered violations of one's fundamental rights to liberty. The Legislature claimed that the Jacobson ruling was based on state police powers and that the measures taken were necessary to "improve public health in the country." For defenders of government regulation, nothing boded well for their position when the court took up the case; it concluded, however, that they lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was found that the law did not, in fact, pertain to any aspect of public health, and Judge Earl notified the Legislature that "in the name of police power no private rights or individual property could be freely invaded." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an unprecedented period of judicial activism defending individual liberties. For example, in the Godcharles case (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment by legal tender (not company stocks).

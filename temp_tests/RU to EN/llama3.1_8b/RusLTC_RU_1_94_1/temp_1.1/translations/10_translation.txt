This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, with other authors subsequently engaging in similar reasoning, including the noted radical American Thomas Paine. Contemporary defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret so popular a notion among the public as Locke’s inference. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for instance, admits that Locke created "no trouble at all" in defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). Considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and things, it can also serve as a socially neutral side: accumulating results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others.

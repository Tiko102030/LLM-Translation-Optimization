The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that a "declaration depriving an owner of using his property" is necessary in order to give him "the right to seek compensation". The court decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brannon and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles, but only confirmed the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal Case. However, a similar case from the same year, Nollan v California Coastal Commission (1987), was an entirely different matter because it established a new standard regarding old rules. Owning a coastal land plot located between public parks to the north and south of each other, Nollans wanted to build a three-bedroom house instead of their dilapidated bungalow, similar to other houses in this district. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only on condition that Nollans agree to provide an easement for access to the area between the high tide line and the dike behind their home. In this way, people could easily reach parks located in both the northern and southern directions by simply strolling along the sand leisurely.

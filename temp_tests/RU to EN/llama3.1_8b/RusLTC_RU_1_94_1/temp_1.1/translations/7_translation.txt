The purpose of a public organization is to make property an end in itself. Such views were spread throughout the American republic, and Locke's reasoning continued to be cited by 19th-century judges who argued against public regulation of property rights (see Eli 1998). Others argue about ownership from the perspective of instrumentalism. For them, property rights should be protected, i.e., these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; they do not make it an end in itself, an undeniable right that surpasses all else. Instead, the issue of property has become primary, and the instrumental approach undermines the protection of all property rights.

The Mann case decision showed that property rights were also considered a public interest and another tool to achieve public goals, no longer requiring any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of increasingly heightened social unrest, this approach was more and more alarming for conservatives and sparked even greater outrage among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). Following events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, a well-known jurist, Christopher Tiedeman, (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of the Messiah did not permit limitations on police powers. The spread of the policy of non-interference had reduced the number of "violations of human rights and freedoms by governments". But now:

The political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government intervention into human rights has been declared unconstitutional, and it's needed as though it's a panacea for any social evil that threatens societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled across all civilized countries. The state must protect the weak from cunning of the stronger, set how much workers should receive for their labor, and how many hours they should work every day...

Considering these unusual demands made by the numerous army of dissatisfied people and their evident power to impose their ideas on a civil society in a civilized world, particularly given the development of universal suffrage, conservatives became even more fearful of the emergence of such an autocracy that would be more despotic and less judicious than any that had previously existed. At the end of the 19th century, while speaking out against the "numerous army of dissatisfied people," lawyers and judges sought to consecrate private property.

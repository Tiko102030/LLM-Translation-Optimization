In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is tolerable in accordance with the principles of "a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his essay of 1894, "Privilege, Criminal Intent and Design", Holmes based his view on the aforementioned presentation of harm caused by competition. By doing so, he aimed to show the general necessity of an instrumental approach and the "principle of balancing interests" especially in situations where judges weigh competing rights instead of resorting to syllogistic arguments (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At a Supreme Court hearing in 1903, Holmes applied this approach in the case of "The Diamond Glucose Company v. United States Glucose Company". "In modern society", he said, "each of its parts is closely linked to another, and if something affects one of them, others will feel it too, to a greater or lesser degree. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and legislative power ceases to exist, it will not be enough simply to show that there has been some progress in the drafting of laws, i.e. when we can already intervene in commercial or existing agreements."

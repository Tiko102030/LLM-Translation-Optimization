Such actions were justified by what is called "police power" of the state, or what Oliver Wendell Holmes, writing for the Supreme Court in Hudson County Water Co. v. McCarter (1908), called "restrictions applicable to property, in the interests of all society". The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s William Blackstone wrote that "under the name of public police and economy I mean the due regulation and internal order of the state, by means whereof a people shall be governed according to the rules of propriety and good breeding; assisting their neighbours, and being orderly, industrious and inoffensive" (see Blackstone 1979, pp. 4, 162). In Vanderbilt v. Adams (1827) Judge Woodworth of New York state more fully described police power, saying: "The highest authority in the society, therefore, may and must prescribe how private rights to property may be exercised.... Police powers are based on the implied rights and duties of supreme authority to protect all by means of laws established so as to promote the welfare of all the community.... Such public power ought to exist in every well-regulated society". In Commonwealth v. Alger (1851), Justice Lemuel Shaw of the Supreme Court of Massachusetts upheld legislative authority concerning regulation of property rights in Boston harbor and prohibiting construction outside this dock, saying about police powers: "We think it a settled rule, arising from the very nature of a well-organized civil community, where every owner of property, whatever exclusive right he may have to it, uses it with all implied responsibility so that its use must not injure either other persons having equal right to enjoy their own property or the community at large."

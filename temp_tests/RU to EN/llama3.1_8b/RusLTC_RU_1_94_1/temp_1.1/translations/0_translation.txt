Introduction: The concept of property in economic theory and public practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ on how they define the concept of property, and often it is difficult to establish boundaries. Some consider property rights to include such objects as land and trees, including the most ephemeral ones such as living nature and running water. Others add to this notion that property rights also cover objects such as ideas (patents), with some extending this concept to people themselves. Societies differ depending on the rights held by property owners; sets of privileges related to responsibility for actions taken in relation to other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing either "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because each use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, as it concerns interpersonal relationships; I have a right to manage my property, which reduces your right to complete freedom. For example, when polluting the environment, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; rights prohibiting pollution limit those of that specific property, but protect the neighbors' property.

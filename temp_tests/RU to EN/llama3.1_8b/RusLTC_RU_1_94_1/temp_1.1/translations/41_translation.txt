It appears that the basis for this case is based on J Locke's views regarding absolute property rights. The Mann case was also cited in this instance, with Justice Wait presiding over its final resolution. However, attention must be drawn to the preceding statement: "it is merely a question of degree". This restriction, penned by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes, purposefully revived an instrumental view of property, which rendered unconstitutional fifty years of judicial precedent on this issue. Holmes already expressed his views in the Lochner case. Starting his speech with the observation that "the decision was based on the principles of economic theory, however, which fails to satisfy a large part of the population", he added: The right of every man to do as he pleases so long as he does not interfere with others' right to do the same is in conflict with the basic tenets of law schools, postal administration, laws of any state or other municipality taking money for the achievement of goals deemed necessary regardless of whether they want it or not. The Fourteenth Amendment takes no account of Mr Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner who dictatorially defends intensive property rights, Holmes thought competing interests required balance to be achieved. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words in order to launch a new suit for alternative law.

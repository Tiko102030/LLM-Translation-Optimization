At the end of the 19th century, expressing themselves against the opinion of a "numerous army of dissatisfied", lawyers and judges intended to consecrate private property. Often citing Tiedemann, they demanded limiting the entire plenitude of state power by establishing a boundary of police powers. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for the sacred rather than an instrumental approach to determining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Wainwright v People" (1856), New York's Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law went into effect, all those who were selling this very liquor lost their properties.

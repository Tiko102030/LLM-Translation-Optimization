The Louisiana Supreme Court upheld the law, stating that "the loss of private rights in this case is immaterial in view of the general profits and trade of the commercial organization" (see Novak 1996, p. 230-31). The butchers of New Orleans took their case to the U.S. Supreme Court, represented by predecessor Field's predecessor, John A. Campbell, a Democrat and Assistant Secretary of War (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court prior to the war, Campbell had ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later he used key labor legislation principles to persuade the Court to protect a citizen's property rights. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that New Orleans' ordinances brought us back to "the time when the government's inquisitive eye followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery". The New Orleans butchers lost their case by five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the justices, and attorneys therefore feared growing pressure regarding what would be an expanded regulatory complex, redistributing income, property and power.

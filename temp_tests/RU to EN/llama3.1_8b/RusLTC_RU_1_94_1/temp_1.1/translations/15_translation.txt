Despite constitutional guarantees regarding property rights, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the 13th Amendment codified in the US Constitution the principle of eminent domain. Pursuant to this amendment, the involuntary confiscation of slave property redistributed nearly $3 billion, half of the southern states' total wealth. In connection with this, the emancipation of slaves in America is one of the most significant examples in history of confiscated property. Government intervention in private property affairs did not cease with these confiscations. During the colonial and interwar periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming a widely-held opinion that it is the moral duty of government to foster a thriving economy and provide for low-income citizens. For example, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and limited the activities of liquor vendors, bars, and prostitutes (see Handlin and Handlin 1947; Bergen 1989; Novak 1996; Lora 1999; Levy 2000). And local authorities used their right to compulsory expropriation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of property owners.

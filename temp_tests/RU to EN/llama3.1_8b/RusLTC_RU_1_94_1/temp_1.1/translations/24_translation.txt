Calender filed a claim for compensation for the damage caused to him during the repair of the street, as a result of which his foundation was damaged in Boston. His attorney cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle in order to obtain compensation. But the defense side rejected the use of the English precedent with an instrumental explanation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances that have developed in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim, which Calender filed, 'is satisfied, then we will need to check the correctness of the repairs on all highways in our country.'" This argument resonated with the judges who rejected Calender's request for compensation. Then, the Massachusetts court adopted the state's proposal for a market interpretation of the case to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was undoubtedly bad news for people whose property directly adjoins public facilities. "Those who buy property near the road - put forward by the court - must take into account future widening or narrowing of the street or road." Since in that case, the amount of compensation would double compared to the initial expenditure costs. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case of "Lansing v. Smith Quackenbush", a claim was filed against the state of New York for damages due to the fact that docks fell in price after building a new bay in Albany. The New York court used an instrumental approach, the one used in the case of "Calender v. Marsh" from the Massachusetts State, in order to reject Quackenbush's demands.

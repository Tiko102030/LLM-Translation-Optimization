Plaintiffs argued that by invalidating existing contracts, the statute violated a clause in the Constitution regarding contracts, which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment's protection of citizens' rights from government interference. Issued recently to protect the civil liberties of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had become an inalienable right, constitutional property guaranteeing ownership, protecting private property from state interference. However this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 court members. Declining the plaintiff's constitutional protection for ownership, the Court confirmed and even expanded the power of previous regulation. Stating that "powers are inherent in any supreme authority", the Court decreed that "the government may regulate citizens' conduct towards each other and how people should use their property but only when it is necessary for the general good". Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Hale's saying about how, when property "affects public interests, it by law ceases to be subject only to private regulation". Recalling the principle of "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: 

When a property owner uses their property in such a way that public interest becomes apparent, actually he himself desires it, and up until interest ceases, he must only submit to society managing his property because it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If not, then he should simply cease using his property. Considering the boundaries of public authority more thoroughly in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on government regulation.

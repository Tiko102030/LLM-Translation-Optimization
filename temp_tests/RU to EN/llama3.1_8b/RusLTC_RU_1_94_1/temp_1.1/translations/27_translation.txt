For the first time, he set a precedent in the case "Palmer v Mulligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream filed an action for damages. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded:

The defendants had the same right as regards their territory... [the principle "use your own so as not to harm another"] should be limited within reasonable bounds, lest a person be deprived of the pleasure of using his property simply due to some petty inconvenience or damage to others. There existed a law providing for certain inconveniences of this nature, such that a man who could initially build a dam or factory on any public or navigable river would eventually acquire exclusive right, at least over a specified distance, regardless of whether he owned the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of part of the profit it might have gained from competitive rivalry. This approach democratized property rights, and as a result both old and new residents enjoyed the same privileges. Thus, a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs occurred at the expense of long-time residents, who were forbidden from restricting entrepreneurial actions. However, by eliminating established monopoly power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation over indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It follows that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" depended in no way on the ideas about sacred private property advanced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of them.

The New York Court used an instrumental approach, one that was used in the case of Cullen v Marsh in the state of Massachusetts to dismiss Quackenbush's claims. The court held: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect, to a greater or lesser extent, people's comfort and property; and when the damage preserved has a distant causal connection and is predictable, no compensation for damage will be recognized, and the amount of damage itself must be considered as part of the cost paid for future benefits given by the said public improvement". The court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the general good should prevail over private interests." State support of private entrepreneurship

Not prohibiting government from beautifying a state at the expense of property owners, a pre-existing law such as was in the case of Lansing v Cullen shifted some people's obligation to cover expenses related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). In later cases, such instrumental norms became individualized examples. For example, in the case of Torrey vs The Rutland and Burlington Railroad Co. (1855), Chief Justice of the Vermont Supreme Court Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their paths because "the preservation of public order within a state implies the protection of all lives, limbs, health, comfort, and quiet of all persons, and protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to injure another'" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas).

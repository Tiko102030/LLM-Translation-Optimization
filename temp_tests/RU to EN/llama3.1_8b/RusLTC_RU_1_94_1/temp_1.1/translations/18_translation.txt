The Chancellor of New York Kent in 1826 declared that "every person has such a quantity of freedom to acquire, use and dispose of their property as may be comparable with the acceptable state of other people's property and mutual rights... Thus, while property is thus protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the way and model of its use as far as necessary to prevent an abuse of the right that can lead to the infringement of another person or the entire community". In order to protect public welfare and consider the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in Illinois in 1837, the legislative body did not have enough pages to list all the powers that would be granted to the Chicago city administration regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and limiting "ringing a bell, playing ball or launching paper snakes... capable of annoying people", the new city had power which "would compel the owner or renter of any grocer's shop, wine cellar, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, outhouse, sewing factory or other old, dilapidated building or house to clean out, remove or destroy it" (see Nowak 1996, pp. 3-6).

There is an "undeniable" right of ownership to the worker, because: 
Although the earth and all lower beings belong to all people in common, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands belong to him by the strictest account. Whatever he then draws out of that condition, which nature has created and preserved, he combines it with his labor and adds something belonging personally to him, and thus makes it his property... Since this labor is the undeniably owned by the worker, no one but him can have rights to what he once joined to it, at least in those cases when sufficient quantity and quality of the same remains for common use by others. Thus, Locke sanctifies ownership as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property - an important natural right; using it means using someone's labor, i.e., turning a man into a slave. From this follows that property rights should owe nothing to society; nonetheless, owners form society and governments in order to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

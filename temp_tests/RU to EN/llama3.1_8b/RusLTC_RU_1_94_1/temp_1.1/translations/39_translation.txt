A similar situation occurred in the case of Jacobson v. Massachusetts, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right to "work where he wishes; to earn a living by any lawful means; to pursue his chosen occupation and for this purpose to make all such contracts as may be proper, necessary, or essential for the successful pursuit of the aforementioned purposes." Following the Allgeyer decision, the level of judicial scrutiny on motivation was established eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), where the Court declared a New York statute unconstitutional because it restricted baker workers' hours. In the case, Justice Peckham asserted that "the law arbitrarily interferes with the right to make contracts by imposing upon both employee and employer a limitation as to the number of hours in which latter may work," violating the "common right to make a contract which may lawfully be entered into for the benefit, not only of his employer, but of the community." He also acknowledged that actions taken by police regarding property regulation were justified because they protected "health or moral welfare," yet argued that there was no "reasonable necessity" for intervening in individual freedom or their right to make a free contract by limiting hours for bakers. Concluding, Peckham noted that this was merely a question of which evil is the lesser: state power to issue laws or human personal liberty and freedom to make contracts. We believe that the scope of police power was reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner can be logically deduced from other court decisions asserting property rights.

The Supreme Court of Louisiana upheld the law stating that "losses to private right in this case are of no moment in view of the general profit and commercial operation of said mercantile organization" (see Novak 1996 p. 230-31). The butchers of New Orleans took their case to the Supreme Court of the United States, where their interests were represented by predecessor Field's counsel John A. Campbell, a Democrat and Assistant Secretary of War (see Foner 1988 p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell had ruled in Dred Scott v. Sandford that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later he used the underlying principles of labor legislation to convince the court to protect a citizen's property rights. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the New Orleans city ordinances sent us back "to a time when the inquisitive eye of government followed the butcher into the slaughterhouse and the baker into the bakery." The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments struck a chord with the judges, and attorneys thus feared growing pressure for expanded regulatory complexes and redistributed income, property, and power.

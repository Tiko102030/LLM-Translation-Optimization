In the landmark case of Jacobson (1885), the Appellate Court of New York State ruled a law unconstitutional that prohibited the manufacture of cigars in rented premises because it "prevented the owner from earning profits and freely using his property", thus "depriving him of his property and personal liberty". Comparing concepts of individual freedom and protection of property, Judge Earl declared that, broadly speaking, and as understood in this country, freedom means not only a right to be free from actual slavery... but also the right of an individual to use their abilities by all lawful means to live and work... Therefore, any laws weakening or limiting these rights are violations of his basic right to freedom. The legislative body claimed that the decision was based on police powers of the state and that the measures taken were necessary "to improve public health in the country". For advocates of government regulation, nothing good was augured by the court's consideration of this request, which ultimately concluded it lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law actually "did not concern itself with issues of public health at all" and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police instructions, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily intruded upon". The case of Jacobson marked the beginning of a period of unprecedented judicial activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the case of Godcharles v. Wiggle (1866), the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania ruled unconstitutional a law requiring payment in lawful money (not company stock).

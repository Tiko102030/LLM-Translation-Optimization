This part of Locke's possessive theory contributed to the emergence of discussions on natural rights as constraints on individual accumulation, which were later joined by other authors, including Thomas Paine, a notable American with radical views. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reason to regret Locke's popular deductions in society. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" in defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). Considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it can also function as a socially neutral side: the accumulation of results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. However, acknowledging social parameters of property allows for the distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person may harm others, such as in cases of resource monopolization previously accessible to all.

In the case of Nollan v California Coastal Commission, the US Supreme Court ruled that a requirement by the commission was unconstitutional and established a new standard for judicial review of land-use regulations. In recording his opinion for the court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the presentation of "conditions relating to the grant of a coastal development permit" did not constitute "taking" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general coastal access "does not serve the purported goal of protecting the ability of people to visually access the beach" and "is therefore invalid and compensation must be provided". In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard: 

Because there is no nexus between the condition and the primary purpose of limiting development, a new end has been created that is not only different from what it was before but also incompatible with that prior state. Now the main task for government is to obtain a general coastal development permit without paying any compensation.... If the condition in the coastal development permit will not be considered as a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a lawful land-use regulation but merely "a bald taking". This time Justices Brennan and Marshall dissented. They rejected Scalia's call for re-examining the legislative body's view that government regulation should take into account what is rationally capable of accomplishing its intended purpose.

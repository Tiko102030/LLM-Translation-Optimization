Property rights are considered as an expression of the individuality of the property owner on one hand, and on the other hand, as a right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect property owners from the consequences of external impacts of polluters. However, such a general comparison of property rights can be understood incorrectly because these rights, for the most part, limit the rights of other people. Property rights give individuals government-approved privileges to monopoly control over resources and the ability to address authorized law enforcement agencies in order to limit the activities of others. Therefore, property significantly restricts some advantages because state authorities, on the whole, support property owners more than other citizens. Hence, the discussion of issues related to natural freedom and justice is not a debate about principles but rather a consideration of property regulation that requires a choice as to whose property should be protected and whose property rights have an advantage in their defense. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so obscure because legal questions are frequently discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the United States' legal system. According to the federalist system, one Federal Government is formed, and for each state its own legal system is established. Also, on each level of management there is a clear division of executive, legislative, and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure compliance with the norms of the Constitution in which they are also interpreted.

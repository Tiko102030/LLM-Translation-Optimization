As the principle is applied, it grants access to consideration of social interpretation of property. Justice Redfield advocated recognizing limitations on property rights because, as he said, actions taken by one owner can affect others even if they occur within their own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where the interests of different people's property intersect and both sides complain about neighboring interference (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Property questions were re-examined in the 19th century to promote business development, even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" has remained, but now it was used to protect entrepreneurs from acting according to passive neighbors' demands for their property to remain untouched. Now the concept of "harm" was considered not a series of wrongful acts against other people's property, but rather an inability of owners to use their own. A new approach allowed businessmen not to pay compensation for harm caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case of Palmer v. Mulligan (1805) in New York state.

Local authorities regularly confiscated homes where immoral activities were taking place, dumped factory waste, which "endangered people's health," banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires that destroyed private housing and warehouses, without paying any compensation in the process. Officials gained access to dozens of industries including those related to healthcare such as drug manufacture and livestock slaughter and other economically significant ones unrelated to healthcare such as trade rights and purchases (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). When regulation could not achieve its public goal, officials began striking. For example in Jersey City in 1872 the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the fertilizer factory owned by Manhattan Fertilizing Company and started "reducing" harm caused by the factory's actions, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

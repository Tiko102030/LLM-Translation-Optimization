The Supreme Court of the United States in its rulings "United States v. Carolene Products" (1938) and "United States v. Darby" (1941), was a prototype for the judicial opinions of the 1930s that led to the expansion of judicial pragmatism and instrumental approach towards property rights. Notable was the court ruling in 1934, in the case of "Home Building & Loan Ass'n v. Blaisdell". In this case, the Supreme Court, taking into account the economic instability in the country, approved state actions to regulate property rights. The court upheld a Minnesota law that placed a moratorium on the foreclosure of mortgaged property during an economic downturn as specified by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the article of the Constitution limiting government regulation should not be "interpreted with literal accuracy as some mathematical formula," but rather remains one of the main articles of the Constitution whose interpretation requires additional information.

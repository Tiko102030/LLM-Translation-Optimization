For the first time in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of New York state (1805), where the owner of the property located downstream brought a suit for damages against the dam owner upstream who caused flooding to his house by breaking down. Appearing on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded that:

The defendants had the same right with regard to their territory... [the principle "use your own in such a way as not to harm another"] must be limited within reasonable bounds so that a person is not deprived of the enjoyment of his property simply due to some unnecessary inconvenience or damage to others. There was a law providing for certain types of inconveniences of this nature, whereby a person who could initially build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river would eventually acquire exclusive rights, at least within a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not.... The public interest, whose interests should not be forgotten, will be deprived of that part of the profit it could have obtained as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights and resulted in both old and new residents enjoying the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of long-time residents being prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. But, at the same time, by eliminating established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from court proceedings regarding indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It resulted that the transformation of the "use your own in such a way as not to harm another" rule depended on no ideas about sacred private property proposed by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of them.

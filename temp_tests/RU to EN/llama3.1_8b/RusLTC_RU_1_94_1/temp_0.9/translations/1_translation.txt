Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it can support affected neighbors and limit the activity of this particular owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of air and water loss or the payment cost that would need to be paid to a company not to dispose of waste in the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not have privileges, as they would have to reduce pollution levels, or they would have had to buy permission from neighbors to pollute. Some question the significant impact of property rights on income distribution, while others argue for its effectiveness. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social result, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment buy these rights from their neighbors or should neighbors pay those who pollute to stop it? Pollution of the environment will occur where costs of reducing its negative consequences are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will be stopped when the costs of reducing the negative effect are less. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient result regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the right to air and water.

Calender filed a lawsuit to compensate for damage caused to him during the street repair, as a result of which his foundation in Boston was damaged. His lawyer cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents, and given principle to achieve compensation. However, the defense side rejected the use of an English precedent with instrumental case description. They explained that due to the circumstances existing in America, the road needed to be repaired, and "if such a lawsuit" filed by Calender "is satisfied, then all highways in our country would have to be reviewed". This argument resonated with the judges who dismissed Calender's lawsuit for compensation payment. Then the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's proposal for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent damage claims, which was decidedly bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public facilities. "Those buying property near a thoroughfare - submitted the court - must take into account future road widening or narrowing." For this reason, compensation would have doubled compared to initial expenses. Such conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case of "Lansing v Smith W Keckhout," an action was brought against the state of New York for damage caused by the fact that docks fell in value after a new bay was built in Albany.

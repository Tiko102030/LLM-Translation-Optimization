However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but rather the fourth footnote of Judge Stone's opinion, in which he stated that the majority of the Court divided the concepts of property rights and sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases involving economic legislation, the Court intends to be guided by the principles of constitutional presumptions of law, and elected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Because such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. Stone, on the other hand, warned that there would be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion. Environmental protection and the procedure of "takings"

Following the Carolene Products decision, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body to have broad powers in selecting social policy objects, even though they limited the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policies pursued in this context. The 1972 Wisconsin case of "Just v. Marinette County" clearly illustrates how judicial authority easily applied the Carolene Products provision. Ronald and Catherine L. Just brought a suit regarding the overturning of regulations that prevented them from occupying designated wetlands for building homes. Rejecting the Just family's claims, the Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled that legislative activity is a legitimate exercise of police power.

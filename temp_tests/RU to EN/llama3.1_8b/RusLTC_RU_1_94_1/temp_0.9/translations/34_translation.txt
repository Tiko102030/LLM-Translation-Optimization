At the end of the 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of "the numerous army of dissatisfied," lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Often citing Tiedemann, they demanded that all the fullness of state authority be limited by establishing a boundary on the powers of the police. State courts took the initiative in defining new bases for the sacred, rather than instrumental approach to determining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War in the case "Weinhammer v. People" (1856), the Appellate Court of New York State declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law went into effect, all those who were just selling this liquor lost their properties. In this case, however, the procedure for expropriation did not pass through the legally established order. For the first time discovering that the concept "the legally established order" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative power cannot completely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations and thereby subject property to complete disappearance." The decision in the Weinhammer case found an echo only approximately thirty years later, after which the case began to be considered with doubled interest.

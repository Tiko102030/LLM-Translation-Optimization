Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts the law violated Article 1, Section 10 of the Constitution regarding contracts and also violated the recently adopted Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens' rights from government interference. Recently enacted to protect the civil liberties of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had become an essential civil right and constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property rights from state intervention. However this view was supported by a minority of members of the 1877 Court. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee of property, the Court affirmed and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Speaking of what "powers are inherent in any sovereign authority", the Court ruled that "the government may regulate not only the conduct of its citizens towards one another but also how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good". The Chief Justice of the Supreme Court, Waite, quoted Lord Hailburton, chief justice of England, in his statement that, when a "property interests society's, it already ceases to be subject to private regulation." Recalling the principle "salus populi suprema lex est" (the welfare of the people is the highest law), Waite added: 

When a property owner uses their property in such a way as to interest the public in it, they actually want that and up to the point when this interest does not disappear, they should only endure that society now manages their property because it is necessary for the increase of the common good. If they do not want society to manage their property, then he must simply cease its use. Considering more closely the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Waite recognized several limitations on state regulation.

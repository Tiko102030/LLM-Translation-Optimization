The US Supreme Court also indicated that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property is based on public interest and instrumental considerations, and in the case of 1875 "Cooley v. United States" it ruled that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property - "is a result of political necessity, and it cannot be separated from sovereign authority". The right to compulsory expropriation of private property was one example of government power used to promote the development of a "well-organized society," raise the quality of "public benefit" or "welfare" through enactments related to intervention in property rights. These enactments reflected common law principles such as: "the welfare of the people shall be the highest law" (Latin salus populi suprema lex est) and "use yours so that you do not harm another" (Latin sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Frequently cited in court decisions, these main principles serve as "common law programs for managing a well-regulated society", providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property to reflect the interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

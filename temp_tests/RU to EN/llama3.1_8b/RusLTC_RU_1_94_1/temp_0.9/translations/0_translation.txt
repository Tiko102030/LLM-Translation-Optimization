INTRODUCTION: the concept of ownership in economic theory and public practice

Society has property. Economic systems differ in how they define the concept of ownership, and often it is difficult to draw boundaries. Some believe that property rights extend to such items as land and trees, including even short-lived objects like living nature and running water. Others supplement this definition by asserting that property rights also apply to such objects as ideas (patents), and some include human beings themselves in the list of property. Societies also differ depending on the rights that owners have; sets of privileges related to responsibility for actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering law as providing either "greater" or "lesser" protection of ownership, because each use affects the properties of others. Ownership has dimensions since it involves relationships between people; I have a right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when pollution is involved, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; prohibitive laws that protect neighboring property rights limit but also protect neighboring property rights.

The iron-processing and steel industries, for whose products preferential tariffs had been established, were alone able to supply the developing network of railways. At the pinnacle of the entire financial pyramid was a recently centralized banking system, founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzell 1984, 1990). The enraged workers and farmers saw the true aims of bankers and owners of iron-processing and railway companies, whose property was precisely subsidized by state authorities. They sought new laws that would expand public authority over matters of property regulation. They achieved a remarkable success in achieving passage of legislation on an eight-hour workday and on railroad tariffs.

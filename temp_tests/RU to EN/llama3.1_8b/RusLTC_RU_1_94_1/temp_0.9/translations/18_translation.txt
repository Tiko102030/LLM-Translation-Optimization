The Chancellor of New York, Kent, in 1826 declared that "every man has such an amount of liberty to acquire, use and dispose of his property as may be equal to a state of good condition of the estate and mutual rights of others... Although thus protected by property... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the mode and manner of its use so far as may be necessary for preventing the abuse of the right which may lead to the violation of the right of another person or of society in general." In order to protect public well-being and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in the state of Illinois, the legislative body was unable to fit three pages in the catalog with all the powers that would be possessed by the administration of Chicago in relation to property questions. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation, and limiting "the revolving hoop, ball play or launching paper snakes... capable of irritating people," a new power emerged in the city which "would force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, toilet, sewing factory or any other old, disgusting building or house to clean up, move or destroy it" (see Novak 1996 p. 3-6).

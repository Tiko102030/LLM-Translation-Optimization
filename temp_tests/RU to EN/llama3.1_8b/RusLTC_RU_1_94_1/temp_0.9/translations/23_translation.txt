Regarding this case, as well as all American law, property rights initially necessary for achieving public goals were immediately repealed as soon as they hindered their execution. At the beginning of the XIX century state authorities, aiming to stimulate industry and trade, launched a campaign to build roads and railroads, canals. When constructing infrastructure costs of the state had to bear indirect expenses, expenditures related to damage adjacent property and losses in value of unimproved property. Pursuant to the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would have borne responsibility for indirect costs significantly increasing the cost of funding inventions. Changing the meaning of this principle, i.e. avoiding independent entrepreneurs' accountability for consequences of their actions relative to adjacent property, sharply reduced the cost of building state infrastructure. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calder v. Massachusetts", establishing a precedent for the State of Massachusetts. Calder brought suit for compensation for damage caused him during street repairs, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

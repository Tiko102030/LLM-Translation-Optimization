Despite constitutional guarantees regarding property, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment enshrines in the United States Constitution the principle of property confiscation. Pursuant to this amendment, unpaid confiscation of slaves' property redistributed almost 3 billion US dollars, half of the total wealth of southern states. As a result, emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Governmental interference with private property did not end with these confiscations. During colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly interfered with private property matters, thus only further confirming the widely held opinion that it is a moral duty for government to promote economic development and support low-income citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, as well as limited the activities of liquor sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Hendlin and Hendlin 1947; Bergen 1989; Novak 1996; Lorimer 1999; Levy 2000). And local authorities used their right to compulsory expropriation of private property in order to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

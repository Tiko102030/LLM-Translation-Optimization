Plaintiffs argued that, by abolishing existing contracts, the law had violated Article 5 of the Constitution on contracts and thereby violated the recently issued Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from state interference, originally designed to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in Southern states. However this view was not upheld by a minority of Supreme Court members in 1877 who declined plaintiff's constitutional guarantee for property rights, and even strengthened past regulation. Referring to what they claimed were inherent powers of any supreme authority, the Court held that "government may regulate the conduct of its citizens towards each other, as well as how people use their property – provided this will be necessary for public benefit".

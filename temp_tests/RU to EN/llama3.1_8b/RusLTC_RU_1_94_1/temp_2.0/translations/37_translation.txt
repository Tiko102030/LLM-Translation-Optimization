The Louisiana Supreme Court passed a law establishing that "loss of private right in the case had no significance whatsoever by reason of the common profit and business turnover of the said commercial enterprise" (see Novak 1996, p. 230-31). Butchers from New Orleans took their case to the U.S. Supreme Court where their interests were represented by Precedent to Field's successor, John A. Campbell, a Democrat and Assistant Secretary of War (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Having worked on the U.S. Supreme Court before the war, Campbell ruled in Dred Scott v. Sandford that African-Americans could not be citizens of the United States fifteen years later, he used the core tenets of labor legislation to convince the court to protect the right to property for a citizen. Quoting battles against feudalism, Campbell claimed that New Orleans' edict "sent us back into that day when 'the prying eye of the government followed the butcher to his slaughter-house and the baker to his oven.' New Orlean butchers lost their case five to four but Campbell's arguments found an echo among judges, thus prompting a growing concern among lawyers over increasing pressure on what measures would be added in regulation, redistributed income, property, and power.

The New York Court used an instrumental approach employed in the case of "Caldwell v. Marsh" from Massachusetts to dismiss Keukinbuch's demands. The court held that: "Any significant public improvement necessarily affects, at least in some degree, the comfort and property of individuals; and when damage is preserved by a remote causal relationship and predictable it is no compensation for damage, and the sum of damage should be regarded as part of the price paid to achieve benefits arising from the said public improvement." The court concluded: "This case is based on the principle that the general good must prevail over private interests". Public support of private entrepreneurship
Although not prohibiting the government from undertaking state improvements at the expense of adjacent property owners, the precedence law, as it was in the situation of "Lansing v. Caldwell", shifted to some people the responsibility for covering costs related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). In later cases such instrumental norms became private examples. For example, in the case "Torrey v. R & B Railway" (1855) the chief justice of the Vermont Supreme Court Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their routes because "upholding public order and security within the state necessarily entails protecting all lives, limbs, health, comfort, and quiet enjoyment of all people and all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to injure another'" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle provides access to a consideration of social interpretation of property.

In the case of Nollan vs California Coastal Commission the Supreme Court ruled that the Commission's decision was unconstitutional and established a new criterion for judicial review of takings clauses. In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia found that a "condition requiring grant of an easement" was not a "taking" within the meaning of the Fifth Amendment. The general condition requirement did not serve to protect people’s access to beaches and thus “is unconstitutional and the payment of compensation is required." Concluding his opinion Scalia said: Due to lack of nexus between condition and underlying purpose of land-use regulation, new objective was converted into something other than what it had been previously. Now the main problem for government was getting general land-use permission without any compensation. ... If the condition in the permit does not serve as prohibition against building construction and serves the same governmental purpose then a ban on construction would be unlawful zoning rule but only a "naked exaction". This time justices Brennan and Marshall took issue with Scalia's decision to overturn the view of the legislative members of Commission. They argued that “the proper standard of review... is whether government can reasonably conclude, by application of reason, whether its measure can attain the public goal it aims to pursue.”

 Cohen 1991; Liechtenstein 1996; Weinner 1978; Woodard 1955. Property owned or used by African Americans did not possess the same degree of inviolability as that of property owned or used by Americans of European descent. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, negroes from Southern states were granted the right to own property, but their rights could be altered depending on the state's political situation and how much participation African-Americans had in it. The same instrumental approach that Northern governments employed for restricting property rights over clean air and water was also used in Southern states to keep African-Americans dependent on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public goals, but with different objectives. Redistribution of property for the purpose of stimulating economic growth Supporting construction of public infrastructure by redistributing property rights, the President of the United States Supreme Court Robert Taney in 1837 resorted to instrumental approach which he used at the Court for voiding a firm's contractual right "Charles River-Bridge." He did this so as to manage personally the single bridge over the Charles river, which connected Boston and Cambridge (see case of "Charles River-Bridge vs. Warren Bridge"). The plaintiff's party had some trouble. The firm could lose "millions of dollars... invested in the construction of railroad lines and canals on main routes... We would be driven back a century to the inventions of that time and remain motionless".

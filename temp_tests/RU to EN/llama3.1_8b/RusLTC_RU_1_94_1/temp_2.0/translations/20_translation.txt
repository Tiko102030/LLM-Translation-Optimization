Such actions were justified by what is called the "police power" of the state, or as Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., writing for the Supreme Court in Hudson County Water Co. v. McCarter (1908), termed it "restraints applicable to property in favor of all society." The concept of police powers has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s William Blackstone wrote that by "the administration of public justice and good government" he meant "a proper regulation and internal order of the state; whereby people in the land, as members of well-ordered families, are bound to behave orderly and temperately, help their neighbors, be just, honest and fair in their dealings." (see Blackstone 1979, at 4, 162). In Vanderbilt v. Adams (1827) Judge Woodward of the New York Supreme Court elaborated further on police powers saying that "the supreme authority in society...may and must prescribe how private rights in property shall be exercised.... Police power is founded on implied rights and duties of supreme authority to protect all by law-established regulations so as at length to promote the general welfare of society." In Commonwealth v. Alger (1851) Chief Justice Shaw of Massachusetts supported legislative authority regulating property use in Boston Harbor and forbidding buildings outside it, saying "We think this is a settled principle growing out of the very essence of a well-ordered civil society where every owner of property, whether he holds the exclusive right to it or not, uses it with all the implied responsibility which goes therewith, that its use must be in no way an injury to others having like rights in enjoying their own property, or to the community at large."

Introduction: The concept of ownership in economic theory and public practice
Society has property. Economic systems differ on how they define the concept of ownership, and often it is difficult to draw boundaries. Some believe that the right of ownership extends to such objects as land and trees, including even short-lived items such as living nature and running water. Others add that the concept of ownership also includes other objects, such as ideas (patents), and some consider human beings as part of property in a sense that is equivalent to a slave. Societies also differ according to the rights of owners of property; the range of privileges relating to responsibility for actions with respect to other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as granting "big" or "small" protection to ownership, since every use of it affects the property of others. Ownership has dimensions, because it concerns relationships between people; I have the right to manage my property, which reduces your freedom.

Civil war served as the beginning of the establishment of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government that freed the country from slavery. The reformers, however, wanted more. For example, the defender of the rights of laborers, George McNair, warned that America had to "inoculate our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to K.F.Bressey, a train conductor: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes they are trying to impose on us by trusts today. The outcome of the Civil War of 1861 was the liberation of black slaves, however, now it is the workers who are nothing other than slaves of the combined trusts" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the civil war also facilitated the fact that workers joined together and came out against these new demands. Large sums of money were needed to finance military actions, as well as related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus, which again proved that a significant financial and industrial capital was needed that could provide for the activities of the government. The iron processing and steel industry, which had preferential tariffs imposed on their products, was the only one that could provide the developing network of railroads.

Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Annotation remains a valid law. As it was brought up for discussion, the Supreme Court has approved almost all legislation governing property, and recognized that the powers of elected legislative officials should be "as vast as the country's economic needs". At the same time, however, the Supreme Court firmly established itself as the guardian of private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. To them it has equated the right to pollute one's territory or to discriminate based on sexual, racial, or religious criteria, now above all property claims. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that property rights stand on two columns: harm in its very essence and property as an instrument rather than sacred law. Recognizing the double-sided nature of harm, courts consider any asserted "property right" as one among several competing "rights", where all must be balanced.

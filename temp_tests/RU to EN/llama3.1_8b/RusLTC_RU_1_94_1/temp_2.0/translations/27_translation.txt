For the first time, he set a precedent in the case of "Palmer v Molligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of the property located downstream filed an action for damages against the dam owner upstream, who had caused flooding to his home. In speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: Respondents had the same right with respect to their territory ... the principle of "use your own so as not to harm others" should be limited within reasonable bounds in order not to deprive a person of the pleasure of using his property simply due to some unnecessary inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law that provided for some inconveniences of this character, whereby one who first constructed a dam or factory on any public or navigable river eventually acquired an exclusive right, at least over a certain distance, regardless of whether he owned the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose out on that part of the profit which it might gain through competitive activity. This approach democratized property rights, and thus old as well as new residents had the same privileges. It was passed a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of settled inhabitants, who were forbidden from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs.

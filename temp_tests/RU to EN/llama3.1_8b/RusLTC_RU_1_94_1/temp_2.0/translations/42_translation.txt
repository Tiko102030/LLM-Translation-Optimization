Losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose words to start a new cause for alternative law. Instead of relying on his speech on the opinion of the majority of participants in the legislative assembly on natural rights and the convictions of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "The Social Statics of Spencer". He thus characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that would rather mean the end of productivity and prosperity than the protection of a sacred concept of free labor. According to the principles of an instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision or the choice of a different public goal. It was not the first time that Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to defend property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he thoroughly explained how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the prerequisites for creating a competitive market economy.

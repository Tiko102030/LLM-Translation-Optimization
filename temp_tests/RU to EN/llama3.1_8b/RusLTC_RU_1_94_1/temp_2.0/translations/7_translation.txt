Such views were spread in the American republic, and Locke's reasoning continued to be cited by late nineteenth-century Supreme Court justices opposed to public regulation of property rights (see Eli 1998). Others reason about property from the standpoint of instrumentalism. For them, property rights must be protected, i.e. these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an undeniable right exceeding all else. Instead, the issue of property has become primary, and the very instrumental approach undermines protection of all property rights.

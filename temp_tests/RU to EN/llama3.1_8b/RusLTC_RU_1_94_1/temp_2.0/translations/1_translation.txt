The society does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources even if it may harm the neighbors' property, but on the other hand it can support the injured neighbors and limit the activities of that particular owner, making ownership a distributive right. When owners have rights to pollute the environment, this reduces their neighbor's real income by the amount of pure air or water loss or payment cost they will have to pay the enterprise so as not to pour waste into the surrounding area. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer without privileges because they would have to reduce pollution levels, or buy neighbors' permission for pollution. Some question the fact that property rights have significant impact on income distribution, while others bring up arguments in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the ownership distribution, any clear definition of boundaries would lead to the same socially efficient outcome and connection between production and provision of goods and services.

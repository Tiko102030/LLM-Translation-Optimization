Calender brought a suit to compensate him for the damage caused during roadwork that damaged the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney quoted English laws, common law precedents, and given principles to gain compensation. However, the defendant rejected using the English precedent with an instrumental explanation of the case. She explained it by saying that due to circumstances that had developed in America, the street needed repair, and "if such a claim" filed by Calender "is upheld, then we would have to review the correctness of repairs on all highways nationwide". This argument resonated with the judges who dismissed Calender's petition for compensation. The Massachusetts state court then adopted the state's proposal to interpret the case from an economic standpoint in order to avoid similar future suits for damages, which was unfortunately disastrous news for those whose property directly bordered public facilities. "Those who buy up real estate near a road, brought forth by the court," – must assume responsibility of any future expansion or contraction of the road. This would lead to double the amount of compensation paid out as compared to the initial cost of expenses. Similar findings were made in New York in 1828.

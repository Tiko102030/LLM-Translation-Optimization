Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes came to the conclusion that:

The economic conjuncture in the country could be the result of how a temporary restriction on enforcing contract conditions is coordinated with the true meaning and purpose of its articles, and thus will not exceed the reserved power of the state to protect vital public interests.... As this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unfettered limitation of state protectionist policy, the legislative body should make decisions in such a way that they do not violate statutory legitimacy. At the same time, whether or not it was reasonable or unreasonable for the legislative authority to take this policy would be outside the purview of the court.

As early as 1934, when the Court was ready to refrain from assessing economic legislation and adjudicating property rights issues in civil suits, the legislative body had already announced its need "to protect vital public interests". After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempt to remove the Court in 1937, the Court itself had almost entirely come into agreement with his reform program. No other decision illustrated the new approach taken by the Supreme Court more clearly than did South Dakota v. Dole (1980).

However, after these icons of liberalism retired to their pension in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed additional restrictions on state regulation. For example, in Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court ordered a property owner in South Carolina to be compensated for damage to his property after the newly enacted Beachfront Management Act suspended construction on his land. Once again expressing its distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as he did in Nollan, that judges must carefully scrutinize regulations to safeguard against "the risk that private property will be put under for the diminution of serious harm to society be used in public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where the courts were ordered to transfer part of the burden of proof from owners to government.

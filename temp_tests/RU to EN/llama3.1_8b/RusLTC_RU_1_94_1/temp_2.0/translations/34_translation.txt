At the end of the 19th century, advocating against the opinion of "a multitude of dissatisfied persons", lawyers and judges aimed to consecrate private property. Often citing Thielemann, they required limiting all-faceted state power by establishing boundaries on police authority. State courts took the initiative in determining new foundations for a sacral rather than an instrumental approach to defining private property. Even before the outbreak of the Civil War, the New York Appellate Court ruled in the case of Weinhamer v. People (1856) that the law which prohibited the sale of liquor was unconstitutional. After the law went into effect, properties were deprived of all those who were selling this liquor at exactly the same time. The expropriation procedure did not pass in accordance with the prescribed legal procedure. For the first time discovering that the concept "prescribed legal procedure" protected property, the court ruled that "the legislative power cannot completely abolish business activity in any form, thereby causing the loss of all property".

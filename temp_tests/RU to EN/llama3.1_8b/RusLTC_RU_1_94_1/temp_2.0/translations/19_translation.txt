Local government officials regularly seized houses where immoral activities were being conducted, poured sewage from factories which "posed a danger to public health," banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires while destroying private housing and warehouses without providing any compensation in the process. Officials obtained access to dozens of industries, including those related to healthcare, such as the manufacture of medicines and livestock slaughter (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve a public goal, officials began picketing. For instance, in Jersey City in 1872 the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the fertilizer plant owned by the Manhattan Fertilizing Company and set about "reducing" the damage caused by the factory's actions, while destroying machinery and appropriating necessary details (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

In the landmark case of Jacobson (1885), the New York State Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on rented premises because it "impeded the owner's right to gain profits and freely use their property" and therefore caused the owner "to lose both his property and personal liberty." Comparing concepts of personal freedom and property rights, Judge Earl stated that freedom in its broadest sense — as understood in our country — means a right not only to be free from actual servitude... but also the human right to use their talents by all legal means to live and work. Therefore, any laws that weaken or curtail these rights... are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislative body contended that the ruling was based on the state's police powers and took measures necessary for "improving public health in the country." Defenders of government regulation predicted that when the Court examined this claim, it would conclude that it did not have sufficient evidence, which is indeed what happened next; it found instead that the law actually had "no bearing at all on questions of public health," and Judge Earl so notified the legislative body that under the pretext of state guidance one could not freely intrude into personal rights and private property. The case of Jacobson marked the beginning of the unprecedented judiciary's activities in protecting individual liberties; for instance, in Gobrecht v. Bartholomew (1822), the U.S. Supreme Court held an unconstitutional law requiring payment by legitimate currency (and not stock) to be unconstitutional. The court argued that this "constituted an unjust infringement upon the equal rights of employers and employees." In short, such an imposition could result in laborers selling their talents only under conditions which they were prepared to accept, effectively undermining a vital liberty interest; thus the state must always tread carefully lest it infringe on the liberties protected by due process.

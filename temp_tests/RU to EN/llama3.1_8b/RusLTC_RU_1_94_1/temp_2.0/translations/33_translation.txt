The Mann decision showed that now property rights were also considered one of the interests of society and another tool to achieve public goals, no longer requiring any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of growing social unrest at the time, this approach was increasingly terrifying for conservatives and caused a bigger explosion of emotions in those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After events on Haymarket Street in 1886, the noted lawyer Christopher Tiedeman, (1971 [1886] ed.), warned that the God anointed right did not allow restrictions to be imposed on police powers. The spread of non-interference policies had reduced the number of "human rights abuses by the government". But now:
The political pendulum swings in the opposite direction again... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now they are needed as if they were a panacea for any social evil that threatens to hinder societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism have become uncontrollable throughout the civilized world. The state is supposed to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, determine what wage workers should receive for their labor, and how many hours daily they must work...
Considering these unusual demands by a large army of dissatisfied people and their evident power, especially in conditions where universal suffrage was developing, conservatives were even more afraid that such an absolute government, more despotically and imprudently than any that had existed before, might appear. In the late 19th century, opposing the "army of discontents", lawyers and judges intended to consecrate private property.

The US Supreme Court also noted that the right to compulsory eminent domain is based on public benefit and instrumental considerations, and in the case of "Cohens v. The United States" (1875) decreed that the right to compulsory eminent domain is "a result of political necessity, and it cannot be divorced from supreme authority". The right to compulsory eminent domain was an example of the use of government power to promote development towards a "well-organized society", raise the quality of "public welfare" or "prosperity" by way of legislation relating to encroachment upon property rights. Such enactments reflected common law principles, such as: "the good of people let it be the highest law" (lat.) and "use your own in such a manner so as not to injure another's" (lat.). Frequently cited in court decisions these main principles served "common law programs for governing in well-regulated society", providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property in order to reflect the mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

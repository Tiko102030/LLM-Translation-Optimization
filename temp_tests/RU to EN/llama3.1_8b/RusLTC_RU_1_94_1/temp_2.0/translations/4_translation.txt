There is a clear separation of powers on each level of management, with executive, legislative and judicial branches, each committed to respecting and enforcing the norms of the Constitution in which they are interpreted. The accommodation of these differences in interpretation was the cause of subsequent division between written constitutions, codifications of law, and "common law" that emerged as legally binding in England, described by several English legal writers. Various sources offer a rich material for judicial interpretation. Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. was not the first to acknowledge that judges in American legislation are independent actors due to various methods of interpretation. In their capacity as interpreters of legal norms, they use their experience to apply law as an instrument for advancing their ideas about an ideal society (see Fisher, Horwitz and Reed 1993). Property rights in America were secured by judges and political leaders through civil processes occasioned by specific institutions of the American government, a distinct legal tradition, and a policy conflict.

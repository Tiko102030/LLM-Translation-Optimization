New York Chancellor Kent declared in 1826 that "every individual enjoys such a quantity of liberty to acquire, use and manage his property as may be comparable to an acceptable condition of estate and reciprocal rights of others… Thus, while thus protecting property... legislative bodies have power to prescribe manner and mode of its employment so far as it shall become necessary to prevent abuse which may cause the infringement upon other individuals or all society in general." To safeguard public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed a broad range of powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 in the state of Illinois legislative bodies lacked three pages to catalogue all the rights possessed by the Chicago City administration regarding property matters. In addition to regulating and licensing waterborne transport and limiting "the revolving hoop, ball play or launch of paper snakes... capable of annoying people", the new city had the power to "cause the owner or lessee of any grocery shop, winery, soap box store, laundry, tannery, stable, warehouse, privy, sewing factory or other worn-out disgusting house or building to clean out, remove, or demolish it" (Novak 1996, p. 3-6). Authorities in other cities also assumed broad powers over property issues.

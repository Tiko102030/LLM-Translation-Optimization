Considering the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society more closely, Wait recognized several limitations on state regulation. "You can get compensation for using regulated property," and Wait argued that the final decision to compensate should be taken by the legislative body. "Any law is assumed to be constitutional," he said, "judges cannot declare what laws are unconstitutional unless it's clearly stated otherwise. If there is any doubt, the will of the legislative body must be supported by a majority." Acknowledging that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to seek compensation "at polling stations, not in court". Property rights advocates were shocked by the open reading of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field thought otherwise and said that "the principle on which the opinion of the majority... undermines private property rights." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be at the mercy of its legislative body's majority members". The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now also one of society's interests, another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee.

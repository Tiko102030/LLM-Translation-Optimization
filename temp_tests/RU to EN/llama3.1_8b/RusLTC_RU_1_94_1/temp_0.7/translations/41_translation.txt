It appears that the case in question is based on the views of J. Locke regarding absolute property rights. The Mann case was also mentioned, and it was decided by Justice Waite. However, one must note the final statement previously considered: "this is merely a matter of degree." This restriction written by Judge Oliver Wendell Holmes again deliberately established an instrumental view of property rights that declared unconstitutional fifty years of prior judicial practice. In the Lochner case, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Starting his speech with the observation that "the decision in the case was based on economic theory principles which however do not please a large part of the population," he added:

The right of each person to act as they see fit for as long as they want provided of course that others' rights are respected is not in accordance with fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal services, laws of any state or municipal institution which takes their money for achieving goals that seem necessary regardless of whether the individual wants them. The 14th Amendment does not take into account Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. In contrast to how Lochner despotically defends intensive property rights, Holmes thought that competing interests required a balance. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to start a new case for an alternative law.

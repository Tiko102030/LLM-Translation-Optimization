The Mann decision showed that now property rights were also one of the interests of society, they were another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of increasingly growing unrest in society, this approach was increasingly alarming to conservatives and caused a greater explosion of feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events that occurred on Haymarket Street in 1886, prominent jurist Christopher Tiedeman (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings did not allow the limitation of police power. The spread of non-interference policy reduced the number of "governmental violations of human rights and freedoms." But now: 
the political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government interference in human rights has been declared unconstitutional, and it is needed now as if it were a panacea for any social evil threatening the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are rampant all over civilized world. The state must protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, determine what wage workers should receive for their labor, and how many hours they should work daily... 
Considering these strange demands of a large army of dissatisfied people and their obvious power to force into life their ideas of civil state in the civilized world, conservatives were even more afraid that such an autocracy would arise, which is more despotic and unreasonable than any previous one. At the end of the 19th century, speaking out against the opinion of "a large army of dissatisfied people", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property.

In the case of Nollan v. California Coastal Commission, the Supreme Court declared the commission's decision unconstitutional and established a new criterion for judicial review of land-use ordinances. In recording his statement to the court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the "condition regarding the granting of coastal access" was not an "exaction" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general coastal access does not serve the intended purpose of protecting people's ability to visually access the beach and is therefore "unconstitutional, and compensation must be paid." In conclusion, Scalia spoke about the new standard:

Because there is no connection between the condition and the primary cause of the building restriction, the new goal is transformed into something else, unlike what it was before. The main task for the government now is to obtain a general coastal permit without paying any compensation.... If the condition in the land-use permit is not considered a ban on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the construction restriction will not be a valid land-use rule but rather "pure extortion". This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's call to reconsider the legislative body's position. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure taken can achieve a public purpose." But after these liberal icons retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

Despite constitutional guarantees regarding property rights, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment enshrines in the Constitution of the United States the principle of confiscation based on property. Pursuant to this amendment, uncompensated seizure of slave property redistributed nearly $3 billion USD, half of all southern states' wealth. Consequently, emancipation of slaves in America was one of the most significant examples of property confiscation in history. Government intervention into private property matters did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thus repeatedly confirming a widely held opinion about the moral duty of government to promote a thriving economy and support low-income citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited food trade, regulated wage levels and job access, as well as restricted the activities of liquor vendors, bars, and prostitutes (see Handlin and Handlin 1947; Berghahn 1989; Novak 1996; Lorentzen 1999; Levy 2000). Local authorities also used their right to compulsory expropriation of private property in order to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

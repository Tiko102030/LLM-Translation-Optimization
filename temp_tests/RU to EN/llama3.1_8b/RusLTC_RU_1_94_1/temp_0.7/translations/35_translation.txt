In the landmark case of Jacobson (1885), the New York State Appellate Court ruled that a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on leased premises was unconstitutional because it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use their property," thus "depriving them of their property and personal liberty." By comparing the concepts of individual freedom and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that, in the broadest sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of a person to use their abilities in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, any laws that weaken or restrict these rights are violations of his fundamental right to freedom. The legislative body claimed that the ruling was based on state police authority, and that the measures taken were necessary to "improve public health in the country." Those who defended government regulation did not expect that the court would consider this claim, subsequently concluding that it lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was determined that the law in fact "did not concern itself with any issues related to public health," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police instructions, personal rights and private property cannot be arbitrarily infringed upon." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an unprecedented era of judicial activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the case of Godcharles v. Wigeman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court ruled unconstitutional a law requiring payment by legal tender (and not company stocks).

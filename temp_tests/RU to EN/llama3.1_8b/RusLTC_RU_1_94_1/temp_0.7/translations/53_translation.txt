Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth Circuit remains an active law. As it has been discussed, the Supreme Court approved nearly all property-related legislation and recognized that the powers granted to elected members of the legislative branch must be "as vast as the economic needs of the country." At the same time, the Supreme Court established itself as a guardian of private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. It equated them with the right to pollute one's territory or engage in discrimination based on sex, race, or religious characteristics, which are now paramount over all property claims. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that property rights rest on two pillars: the essence of harm and property as an instrument, not sacred right. Recognizing the dual nature of losses, courts view any claimed "right" to property as one of several competing "rights," which must be balanced. After the Holmesian principle of balancing interests was approved, the Court had to consider each claimed "right" to property in instrumental execution, as alternating interests would lead to achieving more global public goals. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute, and should never be an end in itself.

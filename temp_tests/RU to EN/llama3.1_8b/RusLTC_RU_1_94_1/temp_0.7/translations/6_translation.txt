There exists an "incontestable" right of property for the worker because: Although land and all lower beings belong to all people in common, nevertheless... The labor of his body and the work of his hands, by the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever then a man draws out from that state which nature hath provided and preserved is properly said to be acquired, and belongs to him who has acquired it. For as much as he was once united to nothing else in the world but to his natural condition, there being nothing in the earth by nature that is anybody's, it belonged equally to all men; so when he either takes in or puts out of that original state any one thing, he thereby acquires a new right and appropriates something to himself which was common. In vain shall you pretend there is a natural community, if nature made nothing to be common among them. For this labour being the unquestionable property of the labourer, nobody else has right to present demand of it in regular terms or personally to seize it from him, without manual force, upon work done, and risk run, and hardship undergone; such is the original title I pretend for private dominion and separate possession enjoyed by individuals. Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone else's labor, i.e., turning a person into a slave. From this it follows that property rights should not benefit society; nonetheless, property owners form societies and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

The U.S. Supreme Court has also indicated that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property is based on public benefit and instrumental considerations, and in the case of 1875's "Cohens v. United States" decided that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property - "is a result of political necessity, and it cannot be separated from supreme power". The right to compulsory expropriation of private property was one example of government authority used to promote development in a "well-organized society," raising the quality level of "public welfare" or "prosperity" through regulations concerning intervention in the rights of property owners. These enactments reflected principles of common law such as: "the good of the people shall be the supreme law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so that you do not harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Frequently cited in court decisions these main principles serve as "common law programs for governance of a well-regulated society", providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property to reflect the mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

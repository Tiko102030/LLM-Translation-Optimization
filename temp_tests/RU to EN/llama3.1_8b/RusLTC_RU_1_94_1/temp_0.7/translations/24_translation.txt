Calendrier filed a lawsuit for damages caused to him during the repair of the street, as a result of which his home's foundation was damaged in Boston. His lawyer cited quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle to seek compensation. However, the defense side rejected the use of an English precedent with an instrumental explanation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances that have arisen in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a lawsuit," filed by Calendrier, "is satisfied, then all highways in our country will need to be checked for correct repairs." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Calendrier's claim for compensation. Then, the Massachusetts State Court adopted the state's proposal to interpret the case in a market-oriented manner in order to avoid similar subsequent damage claims, which was undoubtedly bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public structures. "Those who buy property next to the road," the court stated, "- should take into account future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." Since in such cases the compensation amount would double compared to the original expenses. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828 in the case of "Lansing v. Smith W. Kneeland", where a lawsuit was filed against the state of New York for damage caused by the fact that docks fell in price after building a new harbor in Albany. The New York Court used an instrumental approach, similar to the one used in the case of "Calendrier v. Marsh" of Massachusetts State, to dismiss Kneeland's claims.

Regarding this case, just as with all American law, rights of property, originally necessary for achieving public goals, were immediately abolished as soon as they obstructed their execution. At the beginning of the 19th century, state authorities, seeking to stimulate industry and trade, embarked on a campaign to build roads and railroads, canals. When building infrastructure in the state, indirect costs had to be borne, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in value of unimproved properties. In accordance with the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would have been responsible for indirect costs, significantly increasing funding expenses for inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by avoiding independent entrepreneurs' responsibility for consequences of their actions regarding neighboring property, indirect costs on building infrastructure in the state were sharply reduced. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Callender vs Marsh", establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Callender sued for damages caused to him during street repairs, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

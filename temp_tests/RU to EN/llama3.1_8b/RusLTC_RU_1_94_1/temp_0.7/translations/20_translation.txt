Such actions were justified by the concept of "police powers" of the state or as Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. put it in his opinion for the Supreme Court in "Hudson County Water Company v. McCarter" (1908), describing them as "restrictions applicable to property, in the interest of all members of society." The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s William Blackstone wrote that "under the term State Police and Economy I mean due regulation and internal management of the State, whereby persons within it, as well as members of a well-organized family, are bound to conform themselves to the rules of propriety and good breeding, assist their neighbors, and be orderly, industrious, and submissive in their intentions" (see Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case of Vanderbilt v. Adams (1827), Judge Woodworth of New York State elaborated on police power saying: "The supreme authority in society may, and ought to prescribe how private rights to property shall be exercised.... Police powers are based upon implied rights and duties of superior authority to protect all by means of established law regulations so as ultimately to increase the welfare of the whole community.... Such governmental power should exist in every well-regulated society." In the case of Commonwealth v. Alger (1851), the Massachusetts Supreme Court Justice Lemuel Shaw supported legislative authority dealing with questions of property regulation on Boston Harbor and prohibiting construction of buildings beyond its wharf, and spoke of police powers as follows: We consider this to be a settled rule arising from the very nature of a well-organized civil society where each owner of property, regardless of whether he has or does not have an exclusive right thereto, manages it with all the implied responsibility that it should not cause harm to other persons having a similar right to enjoy their property, nor to the community as a whole.

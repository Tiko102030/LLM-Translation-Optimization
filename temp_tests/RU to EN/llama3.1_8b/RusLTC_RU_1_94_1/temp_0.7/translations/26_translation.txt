Judge Redfield urged that the limitations on proprietary rights be recognized because, in his words, the actions of one owner may affect others even when confined to their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm and not a rule; it should not guide us in most cases where conflicting property interests are at issue and both parties complain of interference from neighbors (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions concerning matters of property were re-examined in the nineteenth century for the purpose of promoting entrepreneurial activity even at the expense of harming other people's property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained but was now used to protect businesspeople from acting according to the requirements of passive neighbors who wanted their property left undisturbed. At that time, the concept of "harm" came to be viewed not as a series of wrongful actions with respect to someone else's property but rather as an inability on the part of owners to use their own property. The new approach allowed businesspeople not to pay compensation for harm caused by the use of their property. It first became a precedent in Palmer v. Mulligan, New York State (1805)

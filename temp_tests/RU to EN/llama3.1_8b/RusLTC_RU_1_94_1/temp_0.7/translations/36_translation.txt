The court maintained that "the law is an infringement of the equal rights of employers and employees, in addition to being a derogatory attempt to subject unqualified workers to legislative tutelage," because it hinders workers from selling their labor at any conditions they were willing to accept. The judges' prejudice towards the policy of state non-interference in the economy was perhaps best illustrated by the West Virginia Court of Appeals in the case "State of West Virginia v Goodwill" (1889). According to the court's decision, a law requiring payment in money was unconstitutional because the government should not do for its people what they can better do for themselves. The Supreme Court of the United States did not hesitate to criticize state regulation, but Justice Stephen Field questioned ideas about police powers as early as 1872 in the case "Beneficial Association of Butchers of New Orleans v Crescent City Livestock Landing & Slaughter-House." This case arose after the legislative body of Louisiana had restricted the slaughter of cattle in New Orleans. Since public health was not threatened, this law was similar to ordinances prescribed before by many other cities.

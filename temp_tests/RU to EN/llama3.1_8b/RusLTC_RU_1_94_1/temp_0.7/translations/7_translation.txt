Such views were spread in the American republic, and Locke's reasoning continued to be cited by judges of the late nineteenth century Supreme Court who argued against public regulation of private property (see Ellick 1998). Others reason about property from an instrumentalist position. For them, property rights must be protected, i.e., these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an unassailable right that transcends all others. Instead, the problem of property has become primary, and the instrumental approach undermines protection for all property rights.

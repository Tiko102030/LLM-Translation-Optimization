INTRODUCTION: the concept of property in economic theory and public practice
Society has ownership. Economic systems differ in how they define the concept of property, and boundaries are often difficult to specify. Some believe that the right of ownership extends to such items as land and trees, including the most ephemeral objects, such as living nature and running water. Others supplement this definition by asserting that the right of ownership also extends to such objects as ideas (patents), some also include in it the human being as a slave. Societies also differ depending on the rights that owners possess; sets of privileges related to responsibility for actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection of property, because each use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, as it affects relationships between people; I have a right to manage my own property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when polluting the environment, the neighbors' right to clean air and water is significantly reduced; rights that prohibit pollution limit the rights of this ownership but protect the property of neighbors.

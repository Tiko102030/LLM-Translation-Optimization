For the first time in the case of Palmer v Mullan (1805) of New York State), where a property owner downstream brought an action for damages against a dam located upstream that had flooded his house. In court, Judge Livingston stated: 

Respondents have the same rights to their territory... [the principle "use your own so as not to harm another"] must be limited in reasonable bounds so as not to deny a person the enjoyment of their property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law providing for some inconvenience of this kind, such that a man who could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river would acquire an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether he owned the adjacent banks or not.... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of that part of the profit it might have obtained through competition. This approach democratized property rights and resulted in both old and new residents having the same privileges. Thus, a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs occurred at the expense of long-established inhabitants who were prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. However, at the same time, by removing established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits regarding indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights.

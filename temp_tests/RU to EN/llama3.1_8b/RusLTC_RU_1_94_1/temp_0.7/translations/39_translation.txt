A similar situation was in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitation," but also covered the right to "work where he wants; earn a living by any lawful means; pursue his chosen profession and conclude all agreements necessary, proper, and essential for achieving these goals." After the Allgeyer court decision, eight years later in Lochner v. New York (1905), the Court declared that the New York law was unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. Appearing before the Court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law necessarily interferes with the right to make a contract between employer and employee," which is a violation of "the general right to enter into a contract related to his business" protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the US Federal Constitution. Recognizing that police actions in relation to property regulation were justified as they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there was no necessary reason for interfering with human freedom or his right to make a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham spoke of what would be less evil: state power to issue laws or human rights to personal freedom and contract freedom. We believe that the limit of police powers had been reached and fully considered in this case.

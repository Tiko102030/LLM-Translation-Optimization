Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit an owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it can support the affected neighbors and limit the activity of this owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, this reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of clean air and water loss or the cost of payment that must be paid to the enterprise so that it no longer discharges waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not have privileges because they would have to reduce pollution levels, or they had to buy a neighbor's permission to pollute. Some question the fact that property rights have significant influence on income distribution, while others provide arguments in its favor. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries would lead to the same efficient social result, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment buy the right from their neighbors or should neighbors pay those who pollute the environment to stop it? Pollution of the environment will occur where costs of reducing its negative consequences are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will be stopped when costs of reducing the negative effect become smaller. A free market economy will lead to the same efficient result regardless of property rights distribution and regardless of who owns the right to air and water.

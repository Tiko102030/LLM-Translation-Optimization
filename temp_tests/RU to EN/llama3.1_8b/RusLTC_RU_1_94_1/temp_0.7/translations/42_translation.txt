Losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected words to launch a new lawsuit for alternative law. Instead of basing his argument on the opinion of the majority of legislative assembly members on natural rights and the beliefs of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics". In doing so, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that meant the end of productivity and prosperity rather than a protection of the sacred concept of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory or another instrumental vision or choice of a different public goal. It was not for the first time when Holmes challenged an existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he elaborated on how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even attempt to compensate a person for all the damage caused by it... Anyone can establish any firm, and in the furtherance of it, to survive in competition, they will lure away customers from their competitor, and perhaps even bankrupt them."

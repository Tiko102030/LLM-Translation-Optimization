The New York Court used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Calder v. Marsh" to dismiss Quackenbush's claims. The court held: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect, more or less, the comfort and property of people; and when the preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself must be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of the given public improvement." In conclusion, the court ruled: "this case is based on the principle that the general good should prevail over private interests". State support of private enterprise

While not prohibiting the government from conducting state beautification at the expense of adjacent property owners, the precedent established in the case of "Lansing v. Calder" shifted the burden to some people of covering expenses related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case of "Torp vs. The Rutland & Burlington Railroad Co." (1855), Vermont Chief Justice Isaac Redfield upheld a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies protecting the lives, limbs, health, comfort and tranquility of all people, and protecting all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm others'" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle provides access to a social interpretation of property.

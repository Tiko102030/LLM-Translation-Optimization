The Chancellor of New York, Kent, declared in 1826 that "each person has such an amount of liberty to acquire, use and dispose of their property as may be comparable to the acceptable state of property and mutual rights of others... Although property is thus protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its usage to the extent necessary to prevent abuse of right which can lead to the violation of another person's or society's rights in general." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in Illinois in 1837, the legislative body lacked three pages to catalog all the powers that would be held by the administration of Chicago city regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transport, and limiting "the rotation of a wheel, playing ball or launching paper snakes... capable of annoying people", the new city was granted power to "force an owner or renter of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, privy, sewing factory or any other old, disgusting building or house to clean it, move it or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). Authorities in other cities also took broad powers regarding property issues.

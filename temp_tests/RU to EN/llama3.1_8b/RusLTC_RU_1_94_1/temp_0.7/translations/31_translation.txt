Plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment to the Constitution protecting citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment was now considered an inalienable civil right and a constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property from state intervention. However, this view was supported by a minority of members of the 1877 Court. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court confirmed and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Saying that "powers are inherent in any supreme power," the Court ruled that "the government can regulate its citizens' behavior towards each other and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." The Chief Justice of the Supreme Court, Waite, quoted Lord Hale, the chief justice of England, in saying that when property "affects public interests, it ceases by law to be subject to private regulation only."

But after these liberal icons retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed additional limits on state regulation. For example, in the case "Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council" (1992), the Court required compensation to be paid to a property owner in South Carolina for damage to their property after the newly enacted "Coastal Zone Management Act" halted construction on their land. Again expressing his distrust of elected legislators, Scalia warned, as he did in Nollan, that judges must carefully review ordinances to protect themselves from "the risk that private property will be used for public purposes under the guise of mitigating serious harm to society." The Court refined its new standards in Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from owners to government. The case was brought when the city administration of Tigard, Oregon, stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only if she donated part of her land for a public park and pedestrian-bicycle path to reduce traffic congestion. Recording Scalia's speech for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration "did not contest the fact that the construction of a pedestrian and bicycle path at Dolan's expense was a consequence of the city's need to improve its transportation interchange." The Nollan and Dolan decisions mark the beginning of a new era of enhanced scrutiny of regulatory takings. This may lead property rights back to the level they were before the New Deal.

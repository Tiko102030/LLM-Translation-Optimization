Civil War served as the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers wanted more. For example, labor rights defender George McNeill warned that America must "inject republican principles into our industrial system" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F.Breasy: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that corporate trusts are trying to impose on us today. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, but now workers are nothing more than slaves of combined corporate trusts" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the civil war also contributed to the fact that workers came together and opposed these new demands. Large sums of money were needed for military operations, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus only further confirmed that a significant financial and industrial capital was required to support government activity.

However, more importantly in this matter was not the decision itself but the fourth footnote of Judge Stone, according to which most members of the Court shared the concepts of property rights and the sacred civic freedoms that were guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone's assertions, the Court will be guided by the principles of presumptive constitutionality of laws in cases related to economic legislation, while elected legislative officials will be responsible for evaluating economic ordinances and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislation. Stone instead warned that a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religion, would take place. Environmental protection and the countermotion "takings"

After the decision in the case of Carolene Products, the Supreme Court allowed elected officials through democratic means to have broad rights in choosing objects of social policy, even though they had restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main objectives of the policies conducted in this context. The court case "Justice v. Marinette County" of 1972 in Wisconsin vividly illustrates how judicial power easily applied the provision in Carolene Products. Ronald and Catherine L. Justice filed a complaint about revoking ordinances that prevented them from occupying designated for them wetlands to build houses. Rejecting the Justice family's demands, the Supreme Court of Wisconsin ruled that legislative activity is a lawful exercise of police powers. Later it wanted to approve an even broader list of restrictions on property rights.

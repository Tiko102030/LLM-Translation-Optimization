According to the court ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unqualified right to alter the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe on the rights of other people, which could not be achieved with its original composition." The court found that zoning regulations for environmental protection "must be reasonable", but "we believe that preventing infringement of public rights by introducing restrictions on the use of private property in its original purpose is justified." There was no "taking" of property like there was in Pennsylvania Coal. This was because "the use of property without changing the natural composition of its soil is allowed by law". Property right defenders achieved greater success in the subsequent court case regarding "takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980, headed by Reagan's attorney Antonin Scalia, who had already made several decisions by 1987. For example, in the case "First English Evangelical Lutheran Church v County of Los Angeles" (1987) the Court required the County of Los Angeles Administration to pay compensation after a regional flood control organization prohibited the restoration of a flooded church tent camp. The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using their property is necessary in order for it to give him the right to compensation."

Despite constitutional guarantees of property rights, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the 13th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution enshrines the principle of confiscation for wealth. Under this amendment, uncompensated confiscation of slave owners' property redistributed almost $3 billion dollars, half of all Southern state wealth. This is one of the most significant examples in history of confiscation of private property. Government interference with private property did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and antebellum periods regional governments and local authorities regularly interfered in private property matters, only further substantiating a widespread opinion about government's moral obligation to promote the development of a thriving economy and provide for indigent citizens. For example, most local governments set up transportation rates and bread prices, limited food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and restricted sales activities of liquor vendors, bars, and prostitutes (see Handlin and Handlin 1947; Bergeron 1989; Novak 1996; Lorini 1999; Levi 2000).

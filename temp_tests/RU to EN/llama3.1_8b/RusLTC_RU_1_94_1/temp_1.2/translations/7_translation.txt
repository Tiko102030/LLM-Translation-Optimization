The purpose of a public organization is that property becomes an end in itself (Smith 1999). Such views were spread throughout the American republic and Locke's arguments continued to be cited by judges of the late nineteenth century Supreme Court opposing public regulation of property rights (see Eli 1998). Others argue from the standpoint of instrumentalism. For them, property rights should be protected, i.e. these rights are a stimulus for entrepreneurs (see North 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; they do not make it an end in itself, an unquestionable right that transcends everything else. Instead, the issue of property has become primary, and the very instrumental approach undermines protection of all property rights.

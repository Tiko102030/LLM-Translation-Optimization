Such actions were justified by the concept of "police powers" of the state or what Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., in the case of Hudson County Water Company v. McCarter (1908), called "restraints upon property in behalf of all." The concept of police power has a long history in Anglo-Saxon law, first mentioned in 1187. In the 1760s, William Blackstone wrote that by the state police and economy I mean due regulation and internal management of the state, whereby people in the country, as members of a well-organized family, are bound to follow rules of decency and good breeding, help their neighbors, and be orderly, industrious and obedient in their intentions. (See Blackstone 1979, p. 4, 162). In the case of Vanderbilt v. Adams (1827), Judge Woodworth of New York State more detailed described police powers by saying: "The highest power in society may and must prescribe how private rights to property are to be exercised.... Police powers are based on implied rights and obligations of higher authority to protect all through established laws, so as to ultimately increase the welfare of the whole society…. Such governmental authority must exist in every well-regulated community". In the case of Commonwealth v. Algar (1851), the representative of the Supreme Court of Massachusetts Lemuel Shaw supported legislative authority dealing with regulation of property rights on Boston Harbor and forbidding construction of buildings beyond that harbor, and spoke about police powers as follows: We believe this is a established norm arising from the very essence of well-organized civil society, where every owner of property, regardless of whether he possesses exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all implied responsibility, so its use should not cause harm to other people possessing the same rights to enjoy their property as they do, nor to society at large.

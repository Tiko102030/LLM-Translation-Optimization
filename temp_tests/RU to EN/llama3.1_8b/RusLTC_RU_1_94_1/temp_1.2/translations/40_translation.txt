The concepts of property, instrumentalism and the New Deal Lochen law can be logically deduced from other court decisions that established the rights to ownership. In the case "Adkins v. Children's Hospital" (1923), the Court invalidated the minimum wage statute for the District of Columbia, holding that this law confiscatorily confiscated the property of workers and firms in a way that violated their "freedom of contract", which was a general rule that cannot be taken away.

 Shortly before the Adkins case came up for consideration, the Supreme Court issued another landmark decision that seemed to fully cover the protection of ownership rights. In the case of "Pennsylvania Coal Company v. Mahon" (1922), the Court declared Pennsylvania's law unconstitutional because this statute in order to preserve the subsoil restricted the activities of coal mining companies.

 The Court held that the general rule for all was that regulation of property could be done only to some extent, but if it went too far, then there would already be a taking of property... It remains unclear why human failures or needs should justify shifting the responsibility of injury to another's shoulders... and a strong desire on the part of society to improve its position will not suffice to guarantee that goal through any means other than a constitutional one... this is merely a question of degree.

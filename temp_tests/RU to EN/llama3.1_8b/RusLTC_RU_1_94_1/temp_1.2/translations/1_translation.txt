Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it can support affected neighbors and limit the activity of that particular owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have rights to pollute the environment, it reduces real income for neighbors by the value of air and water loss or the cost payment they will have to pay to a firm not to throw waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not have privileges because they would have had to reduce pollution levels or buy permission from their neighbors to pollute. Some question that property rights exert significant influence on income distribution, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights any clear establishment of borders will lead to the same efficient social outcome, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those polluting the environment buy rights from their neighbors or should neighbors pay them who are polluting the environment so they would stop? Pollution will occur where costs to reduce its negative effects are greater than the value of clean air for neighbors and will be ceased when costs to decrease the negative effect are smaller. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of property rights distribution and regardless of who owns rights on air and water.

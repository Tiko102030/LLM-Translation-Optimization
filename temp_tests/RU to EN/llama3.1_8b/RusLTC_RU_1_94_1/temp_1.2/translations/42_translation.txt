After losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to launch a new case for alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the majority opinion of lawmakers regarding natural rights and J. Locke's beliefs, he accused them of copying "Spencer's Social Statics." Thus, he characterized their protection of property rights as an instrumental choice that would rather mean the end of productivity and prosperity than the protection of the sacred concept of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the fundamentals of another economic theory or a different instrumentalist view or a choice of a different social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to defend property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he explicitly stated that the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises for creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "The law does not even attempt to compensate a person for all the damage done to him... Anybody may establish any firm, and subsequently, in order to survive the competition, he will entice away clients of his competitor and possibly even ruin them."

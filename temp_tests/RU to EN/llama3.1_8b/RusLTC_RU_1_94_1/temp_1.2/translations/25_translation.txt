The New York court employed an instrumental approach used in the case of "Callender v Marsh" from Massachusetts to reject Quackenbush's demands. The court argued: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily, to a greater or lesser extent, affect people's comfort and property; and when the preserved damage has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation will be recognized for the damage, but rather the amount of damage should be considered as part of the price paid for future benefits of this public improvement." At the end of the trial, it concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the general welfare must prevail over private interests". State support of private entrepreneurship 

Not prohibiting the government from making state improvements at the expense of property owners, the precedent of law, as was the case in the situation of "Lansing v Callender", shifted to some individuals the responsibility for covering costs related to achieving public goals (see Horowitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case of "Torp vs The Railroad Company 'Rutland & Burlington'" (1855), Chief Justice of the Vermont Supreme Court Isaac Redfield upheld a law requiring railway companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of state public order entails the protection of lives, limbs, health, comfort and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas)..

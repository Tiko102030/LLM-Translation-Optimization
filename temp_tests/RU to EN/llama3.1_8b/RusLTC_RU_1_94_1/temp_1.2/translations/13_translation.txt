Property of the English was evaluated at an even lower level: debts owed to British creditors were, without further ado, annulled; some states, including Virginia, closed court proceedings for cases filed by British subjects (see Eli 1998, p. 35). The widely practiced confiscation of property from opponents of independence from England did not bode well for the security of property in the new republic. The main rebels were struggling to curb what they considered "an excess of democracy" (see Thomlinson 1993, p. 60). Ensuring property security made "the guardian of any right" a concern for the creators of the first constitutions of all states.

In this case, as with all American law regarding property rights necessary for achieving public goals, these rights were immediately abolished as soon as they obstructed their execution. At the beginning of the 19th century, state authorities striving to stimulate industry and trade launched a campaign to build roads and railways, canals. During construction of the state's infrastructure it was forced to bear indirect costs, expenditures related to adjacent property damage and losses due to unimproved property values. According to the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (Lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would have been responsible for indirect costs significantly increasing funding costs for inventions. By changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., by excluding independent entrepreneurs from responsibility for consequences related to neighboring property, expenditures on building state infrastructure sharply decreased. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calder v. Massachusetts", setting a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calder filed an action seeking compensation for damage caused to him during street repairs as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

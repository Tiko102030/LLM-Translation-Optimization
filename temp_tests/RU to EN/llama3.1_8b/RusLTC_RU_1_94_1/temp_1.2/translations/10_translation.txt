This part of Locke's property theory facilitated the emergence of discussions on natural rights as constraints on individual accumulation, with other authors subsequently incorporating these arguments, including the well-known radical American Thomas Paine. Modern advocates of sacred private property had reason to regret so widely accepted a popular inference from Locke's views. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in biblical exegesis, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke posed "no slightest difficulty" for protecting property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the perspective of relationships between individuals and objects, it may take on both social neutrality: accumulation of results of one's own labor activity does not belittle what is available to others.

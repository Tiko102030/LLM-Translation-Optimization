At the end of the 19th century, speaking out against the view "of a multitude of dissatisfied army", lawyers and judges intended to sanctify private property. Often citing Thiedemann, they demanded that the full authority of state power be restricted by establishing boundaries on the powers of the police. State courts took the initiative in defining new grounds for the sacred rather than instrumental approach to defining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case "Weinhammer v People" (1856), the Appellate Court of New York declared unconstitutional a law that prohibited the sale of liquor. After the law came into effect, all those who were selling this liquor at that very moment lost their property. In the process of expropriation, no proceedings in accordance with established law took place. For the first time discovering that the concept "the established legal procedure" protected property, the court decided that "legislative power cannot completely abolish any manifestation of business activity and thus subject property to complete annihilation". The decision in the case of Weinhammer had an echo only about 30 years later, after which the case became considered with doubled interest.

Plaintiffs claimed that by nullifying existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional provision regarding contracts which also infringed on the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect the rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment now served as an inherent civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property, which protected private property rights from government interference. However, this view was supported by only a minority of Court members. Rejecting the constitutional guarantee of plaintiff's property, the Court validated and even expanded upon previous regulatory powers. In ruling that "powers are inherent in any sovereignty," the Court decreed that "the government can regulate citizens' behavior towards one another and how they must use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chancellor of England Hale's statement that when a property interests society, it ceases by law to be subject to private regulation.

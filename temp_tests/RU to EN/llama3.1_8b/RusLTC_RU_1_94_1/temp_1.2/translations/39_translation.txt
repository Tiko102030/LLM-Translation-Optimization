A similar situation occurred in the case of Jacobson, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical incapacities," but also encompassed the right to "work where he pleases; earn his own living by any lawful means; pursue a desired vocation and for that purpose make all contracts which may be proper, necessary and essential in order successfully to attain these objects." After the Allgeyer decision set the precedent of explaining the reasons for the ruling eight years later in Lochner vs. New York (1905), the Court ruled that a New York law was unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. In court, Peckham declared firmly that "the law necessarily involved an invasion of the right to contract between the employer and employee" which was a violation of "a general right to make a contract relating to his business or calling" which is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment of the United States Federal Constitution. Noting that police actions in regulating property were justified because they protect "health or morals," Peckham claimed there was no necessary justification for interference with human freedom or the right to form a free contract by reducing working hours for bakers. In conclusion, Peckham said it was simply a question of which evil is greater: the state's power to enact laws or a person's right to personal liberty and the freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limits of police power were reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the New Deal Lochner can be logically inferred from other judicial decisions that have affirmed property rights.

The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found an echo among the judges, and lawyers feared increasing pressure for extended regulatory measures and redistributed income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissent has stuck with people more than Judge Miller's opinion that was supported by a majority vote and later reinforced with police powers' arguments. Like Campbell, Field claimed that Louisiana had infringed upon the fundamental right to free labor. Citing Adam Smith, he based his property defense on Locke's prescriptions stating that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property itself constitutes "the primary part of liberty... in the American sense." It took Field twenty years to understand the Supreme Court's decision in the case of Lochner v. New York before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in Allgeyer v. Louisiana, the court declared a state law unconstitutional.

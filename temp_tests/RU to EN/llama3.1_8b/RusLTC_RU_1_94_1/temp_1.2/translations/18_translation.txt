New York Chancellor Kent declared in 1826 that "every man has so much freedom for acquiring, using and disposing of his property as may be comparable with a fair state of the estate and mutual rights of others... While thus property is protected... The legislative body has the right to prescribe how and by what means it shall be used, as far as may be necessary to prevent an abuse of the right which may lead to an infringement on the rights of another person or the general public". To protect the common welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments took upon themselves wide powers throughout the XIX century. For example, in 1837 the Illinois legislature did not have enough space for three pages to list all the powers that would be exercised by the Chicago city administration regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transport and limiting "the rotation of a wheel, playing ball or launching paper snakes... capable of annoying people", in the new city there emerged authority that "would force the owner or tenant of any general store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, warehouse, privy, sewing factory or any other decrepit and disgusting building or house to clean it up, move it or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6). Authorities in other cities also took upon themselves wide powers regarding property issues.

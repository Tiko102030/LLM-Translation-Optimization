The Mann decision showed that now property rights were also among the interests of society, they had become another tool for achieving public goals and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Against the backdrop of growing unrest in society, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and caused a bigger explosion of emotions in those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After events on Haymarket Street in 1886, renowned jurist Christopher Tiedemann (1971 [1886] ) warned that the "sacred right" of the people had not allowed limiting police authority. The spread of non-intervention policy reduced the number of human rights and freedoms infringements by the government. But now: the political pendulum is swinging in the opposite direction again... Government intervention in human rights is declared unconstitutional, and it is now necessary as a panacea for any social evil threatening the prosperity of society. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled across all civilized countries. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, establish what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work every day...

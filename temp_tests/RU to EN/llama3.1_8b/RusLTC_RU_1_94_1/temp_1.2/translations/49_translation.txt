According to the court ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unqualified right to alter the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe upon the rights of other people and could not be achieved with its original composition." The court recognized that zoning regulations for environmental protection "should be reasonable," but believed "that warning against infringement on public rights by introducing restrictions on the use of private property in its initial purposes is justified." There was no "taking" of property as seen in Penn Central. This was because "the use of the property without altering the natural composition of its soil is allowed by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success with a subsequent court battle over "takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, The Court had already made several rulings on this issue as early as 1987. For example, in the case of First Evangelical Lutheran Church v. County of Los Angeles (1987), the Court required Los Angeles County to pay compensation after a regional flood control district banned restoration of a church's damaged tent camp following flooding. The Court informed Los Angeles County that "a declaration depriving the owner of his use" of the property must give him the right to "compensate for damage."

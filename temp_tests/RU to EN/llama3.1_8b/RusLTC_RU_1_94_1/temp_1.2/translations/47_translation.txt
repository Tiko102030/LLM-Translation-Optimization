Denying any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:

The economic conjuncture in the country may be a result of how temporary restriction on compulsory performance of contract conditions is harmonized with the true meaning and purpose of its clause, and therefore it will not exceed the reserved state right to protect vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the contract is not absolute and utterly invalid restriction on protective policy of the state, the legislative body must take decisions in a way that does not infringe legislative legality.... Meanwhile, as the reasonable or unreasonable policy would be conducted by the legislative body, it will no longer concern the Court. Even in 1934, when the Court was ready to give up reviewing economic legislation and dispute resolution in matters of property rights, the legislative body already stated the need to "protect vital interests of society". After Roosevelt's re-election in 1936 and his attempt to "put the Court out of business" in 1937, the Court had almost agreed with his reform program. No other decision so clearly illustrated the new approach in the activity of the Supreme Court as it was done in the case of "United States vs. Carolene Products Co." (1938). In this specific case, a company was charged with violating the Non-Fat Milk Act of 1923, which prohibited interstate sale of milk containing fat and oil, considering them to be a "purposely spoiled food product, harmful to health". Authorized by the company "Carolene Products", they argued that the law illegitimately restricted the freedom of contract. Disregarding the argument about property rights, the Court ruled the law valid as it considered it to be a fair instrument of state regulation necessary for protection of public health. The decision itself would have been a common and simple confirmation of established police powers.

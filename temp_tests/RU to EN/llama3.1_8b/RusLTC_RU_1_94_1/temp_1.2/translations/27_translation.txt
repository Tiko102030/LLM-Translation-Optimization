For the first time he became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of property located downstream filed a lawsuit for compensation. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his home. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded:

The defendants had the same right with regard to their territory... [the principle "use your own so as not to harm another"] should be limited in reasonable limits so that a person is not deprived of the pleasure of using their property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or damage to others... There was a law providing for certain inconveniences of this kind, such that a person who first built a dam or mill on any public or navigable river eventually acquires exclusive rights, at least over a certain distance, regardless of whether they own the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, would be deprived of the part of the profit it could obtain as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, so that both old and new residents enjoyed the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of long-established residents, who were prohibited from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. But, at the same time, by removing established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits on indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. The transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" depended in no way on J. Locke's ideas about sacred private property.

Caldwell brought an action for damages suffered by him during the repair of a street, as a result of which the foundation of his home in Boston was damaged. His attorney cited quotations from English laws, common law precedents, and this principle to secure compensation. But the defense party rejected the use of the English precedent with instrumental exposition of the case. It explained that, owing to the circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim" as brought by Caldwell "be allowed, then we shall have to inspect the correctness of repairs on all highways throughout our country". This argument found an echo among judges who rejected Caldwell's claim for compensation. Then Massachusetts State Court adopted the state's proposal to interpret the case market-wise with the intention of avoiding similar subsequent damage claims, which was a decidedly bad news for people whose property directly adjoins public edifices. "Those buying properties near traffic roads," stated the court, "should take into account future expansion or narrowing of streets and roads." In such an eventuality the amount of compensation would be doubled compared to original expenses incurred. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case "Lansing against Smith Quackenbos" a claim for damages was brought before the State of New York, resulting from docks falling in price after constructing new harbor in Albany.

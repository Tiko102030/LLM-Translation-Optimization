Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same natural law which gives us property in one way also limits the size of that property. "God gave us everything abundantly" - this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did he give it to us? For enjoyment. Man has the right to turn his labor into his property as much as he can use it for any necessities of life, before this object will spoil. And that which goes beyond these limits exceeds his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for destruction or ruin. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a reserve of wealth allowed some people to monopolize "unequal and disproportionate ownership of land," possess power over others. Therefore, restrictions on accumulation of property are necessary in order to guarantee all individuals access to the riches of society. This part of Locke's theory of property contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, subsequently other authors including Thomas Paine, a well-known American radical thinker, connected themselves with these reasonings.

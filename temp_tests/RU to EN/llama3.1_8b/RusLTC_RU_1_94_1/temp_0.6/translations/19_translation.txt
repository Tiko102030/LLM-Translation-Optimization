Local authorities regularly confiscated homes where immoral activities were carried out, filled in the sewers of factories that "posed a threat to public health", banned the production of hazardous chemicals and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and warehouses in the process, without paying any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of sectors of the economy, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and slaughterhouses, and others with economic significance but unrelated to healthcare issues, like the right to trade and buy, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). In cases where regulation was unable to achieve a public goal, officials would go on strike. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the street commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the territory of the fertilizer factory owned by Manhattan Fertilizing Company and began "reducing" the harm caused by the factory's activities, destroying the machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, pp. 226).

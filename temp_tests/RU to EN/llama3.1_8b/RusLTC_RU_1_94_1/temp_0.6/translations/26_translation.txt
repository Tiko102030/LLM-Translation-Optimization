Use your own property so as not to harm another... The application of this principle provides access to the consideration of social interpretation of ownership. Judge Redfield urged that the limitations of property rights be recognized because, in his words, actions performed by one owner affect others even when they occur on their own territory. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is only a norm and not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where different individuals' interests are at stake and both parties complain of interference from a neighbor (see Holmes 1894, p. 11). Questions related to property issues were re-examined in the XIX century to promote business development even at the expense of causing harm to other people's property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" was preserved but now it was used to protect entrepreneurs' right to act according to the requirements of passive neighbors who want their property left untouched. Now, the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of wrongful actions against someone else's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own property.

The Civil War served as the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. However, reformers wanted more. For example, defender of workers' rights George McNair warned that America must "infuse our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to train conductor C.F.Brayce: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes which trust companies are trying to impose on us today. The result of the Civil War in 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, but now it's workers who are nothing more than slaves of combined trust companies" (see Fonner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Furthermore, the Civil War contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. Large sums of money were required for military operations, as well as the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus, which once again proved that a significant financial and industrial capital was needed to support government activities. The iron processing and steel industry, with favorable tariffs on its products, was the only one able to provide the developing network of railways.

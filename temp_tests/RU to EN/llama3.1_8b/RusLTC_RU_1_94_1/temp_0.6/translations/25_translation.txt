The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case of "Calder v. Marsh" to dismiss Quakenbush's demands. The court claimed: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily affect, more or less, the comfort and property of people; and when the preserved damage has a remote causal link and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, but rather the amount of the damage should be considered as part of the price paid for future benefits of this public improvement." The court concluded: "this case is based on the principle that the common good must prevail over private interests". State support for private entrepreneurship

Without prohibiting the government from improving the state at the expense of adjacent property owners, the precedent law, as in the situation of "Lansing v. Calder", transferred to some individuals the obligation to cover expenses related to achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Subsequently, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case of "Torrey v. Railroad Company 'Rutland and Burlington'" (1855), Vermont State Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of all people's lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace, and protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another'" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle provides access to a social interpretation of property.

Recording his speech for the court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that presenting "the condition regarding permission to use the land" was not an "exaction" according to the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general land-use "does not serve the supposed purpose of protecting the ability of people to visually access the beach" and thus is “unconstitutional, and restitution must occur”. In conclusion Scalia spoke about the new standard: 

Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying cause for the limitation on construction, the new goal is transformed into something else entirely different from what it was before. Now the main task of the government is to obtain general land-use rights without paying any compensation.... if the condition provided in the land-use permit is not considered a prohibition on building and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the limitation on construction will not be a valid land-use rule, but rather "a genuine extortion". This time Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's call to reconsider the legislative body's view. They argued that "the proper criterion for consideration... is whether the state can rationally decide whether the measure adopted can achieve a public goal." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation.

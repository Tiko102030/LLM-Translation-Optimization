Property rights of Horwitz (1992), Demsetz (1964); Dillon (1895) are considered as manifestations of the individuality of property owners. On one hand, there is a right to control economic activity and determine public goals. On the other hand, there is a right of the majority to protect property owners from the effects of external influence of polluters. However, such an overall comparison of property rights concepts can be understood incorrectly because these rights primarily limit the rights of other people. Property rights give people government-approved privileges for monopolistic management of resources and the opportunity to address authorized police bodies in order to restrict the activities of other people. Therefore, property significantly limits some benefits due to the fact that state authorities more often support property owners than others. So it is not a dispute about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather discussions of property regulation require choosing whose property needs to be protected and whose property rights have an advantage in fighting for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so unclear because legal questions are repeatedly discussed on various levels of government within the structure of the US legal system. According to the federalism system, one Federal Government is formed, and a separate legal system is established for each state. Also, at each level of management there is a clear division of executive, legislative and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure compliance with the norms of the Constitution in which they find their interpretation.

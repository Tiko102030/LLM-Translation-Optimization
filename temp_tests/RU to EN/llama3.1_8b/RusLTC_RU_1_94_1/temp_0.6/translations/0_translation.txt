INTRODUCTION: The Concept of Property in Economic Theory and Public Practice
Society owns property.  Economies differ as to what definition they give to the concept of property, and it is often difficult to draw boundaries. Some believe that the right to ownership includes objects such as land and trees, including the most ephemeral ones like living nature and running water. Others supplement this concept with the assertion that the right to ownership also extends to objects such as ideas (patents), while some people also include a human being in this category. Societies also differ depending on the rights held by property owners; sets of privileges related to responsibility for actions taken towards other individuals. These rights prevent us from considering law as providing either "greater" or "lesser" protection of property, because every use affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, as it involves human relationships: I have a right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be completely free. For example, when polluting the environment, neighbors' rights to clean air and water are significantly reduced; laws that prohibit pollution limit the rights of this property but protect those of neighboring property owners. Society on one hand does not prohibit the owner from using his land and water resources even if it may cause damage to neighboring property.

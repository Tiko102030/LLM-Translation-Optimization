Despite constitutional guarantees regarding property, states continued to redistribute wealth. In fact, the 13th Amendment to the United States Constitution enshrines the principle of property confiscation. Pursuant to this amendment, uncompensated confiscation of slave owners' property redistributed almost $3 billion, half of all southern states' wealth. As a result, emancipation in America became one of the most significant examples of property confiscation in history. Government intervention into private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby only reaffirming the widespread opinion that government has a moral obligation to promote economic development and support low-income citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited food trade, regulated wages and employment opportunities, and restricted the activities of liquor vendors, bars, and prostitutes (see Handlin and Handlin 1947; Bergin 1989; Novak 1996; Lorie 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the power to confiscate private property by force in order to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

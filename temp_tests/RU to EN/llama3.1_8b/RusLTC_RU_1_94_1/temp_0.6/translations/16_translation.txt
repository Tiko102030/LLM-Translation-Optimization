Local authorities used the right to compulsory expropriation of private property in order to achieve public goals at the expense of owners of private property. Initially devised by Dutch scholar Hugo Grotius in 1625, the term "right to compulsory expropriation of private property" was recorded in English law with J. Locke, who laid it down as a basis for "natural rights," as a coincidence of the will of all the population which had granted all powers to state legislative bodies (see Eagle 1996, p. 8). American judges and interpreters of the law greatly expanded the concept based solely on instrumental considerations. Denying any sanctity of private property rights, the Vermont State Constitution of 1791 stated: "Private property must be subordinated to public use when necessity requires it" (see Eli 1998, p. 33; author's notes).

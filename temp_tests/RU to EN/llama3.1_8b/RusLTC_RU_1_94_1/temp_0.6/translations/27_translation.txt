For the first time, it became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Mulligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the owner of the property located downstream filed a claim for damages. The dam upstream collapsed and flooded his house. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: 

The defendants had the same right to their territory ... [the principle "use your own so as not to harm another"] must be limited in reasonable limits so that a person does not lose the enjoyment of using their property simply because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There was a law providing for certain inconveniences of this character, such that a person who could first build a dam or mill on any public or navigable river would ultimately acquire exclusive rights, at least up to a certain distance, regardless of whether he owns the adjacent shores or not.... The community, whose interests should not be forgotten, will be deprived of that part of the profit it could have obtained as a result of competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, so that both old and new residents had the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents who were forbidden from limiting the actions of entrepreneurs. However, at the same time, by eliminating existing monopolistic authority and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits regarding indirect losses, these cases only undermined existing property rights. It turns out that the transformation of the rule "use your own so as not to harm another" in no way depended on J. Locke's ideas about sacred private property. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of them.

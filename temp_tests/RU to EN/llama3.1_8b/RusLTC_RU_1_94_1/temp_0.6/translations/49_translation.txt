According to the court's ruling: "The landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to change the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe upon the rights of other people and which could not be achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that zoning regulations for environmental protection "must be reasonable," but "we consider it justified to prevent a violation of public rights by introducing restrictions on private property use in its original purpose." There was no "exemption" of property as there was in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was because "the use of property without changing the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law". Property rights advocates achieved greater success in their lawsuit regarding "exemptions" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's counsel, Antonin Scalia, the Court had made a series of decisions as early as 1987. For example, in the case of "First Evangelical Lutheran Church v. County of Los Angeles" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the county flood control agency prohibited the restoration of a damaged by flooding church tent campsite. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a ruling depriving an owner of using their property" is necessary in order for it "to give them the right to compensation for damages".

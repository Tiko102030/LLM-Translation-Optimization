In the landmark case of Jacobs (1885), the New York State Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars on leased premises, because it "interfered with the owner's ability to earn profit and freely use his property," and thus "deprived him of his property and personal liberty." By comparing the concepts of individual freedom and property rights protection, Judge Earl stated that in the broad sense of the word, and as understood in our country, liberty means not only freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of a person to use their abilities by all legal means to live and work... Therefore, any laws that weaken or restrict these rights are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislative body claimed that the decision was based on the state's police powers, and the measures taken were necessary to "improve the public health system in the country." For defenders of government regulation, there was no good sign that the court would consider this requirement, which it eventually concluded lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law actually "did not concern itself with issues of public health at all," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police regulations, one cannot arbitrarily intrude upon personal rights and private property." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of unprecedented judicial activism in defense of property rights. For example, in the case of Godcharles v. Wigeman (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment by lawful money (not company stock).

This part of Locke's theory on property contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation, which were later joined by other authors, including Thomas Paine, a well-known American radical thinker. Modern defenders of sacred property rights have reason to lament Locke's very popular conclusions in society. Accusing Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, Richard Epstein, for example, acknowledges that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for the defense of property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). Considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it may also be a socially neutral aspect: accumulating results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. However, recognizing social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, i.e., the rights of one person can harm others, such as in cases of resource monopolization previously accessible to all.

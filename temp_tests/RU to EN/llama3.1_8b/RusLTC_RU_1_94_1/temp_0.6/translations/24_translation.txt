Calender filed a claim for damages caused to him during the repair of the street, as a result of which the foundation of his home in Boston was damaged. His attorney brought quotes from English laws, common law precedents and this principle to achieve compensation. But the defense side rejected the use of an English precedent with instrumental statement of case. They explained that due to circumstances developed in America it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim", which Calender presented, "is satisfied, then we would have to check the correctness of repairs for all highways in our country". This argument found resonance with judges who rejected Calender's claim for compensation. Then the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's proposal for market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was incredibly bad news for people whose property directly adjoined public facilities. "Those buying property next to a road", submitted the court, "- should take into account future expansion or contraction of the street or road". Since in such a case the amount of compensation would double compared to the original sum of expenses. Similar conclusions were made in New York in 1828. In the case of "Lansing v Smith and Quackenbush", a claim was filed against the state of New York for damage due to the fact that docks fell in price after building a new bay in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach, used in the case of "Calender v Marsh" of the Massachusetts state to reject Quackenbush's demands.

However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional constraints on government regulation. For example, in Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required compensation to be paid to a property owner in South Carolina for damage to their property after a new coastal zone management law suspended construction on their land. Again expressing distrust of elected lawmakers, Scalia warned, as he had in Nollan, that judges must carefully review ordinances to safeguard against "the risk that private property will be used under the guise of reducing serious harm to society." The Court further refined its new standards in Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were directed to shift part of the burden of proof from property owners to government.

The Supreme Court of the United States also indicated that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property is based on public benefit and instrumental considerations, and in the case of 1875 "Kohl v. United States" decided that the right to compulsory expropriation of private property is a "result of political necessity, and it cannot be separated from sovereign power". The right to compulsory expropriation of private property was one example of the use of government authority to promote the development of a "well-organized society", to increase the quality of "public benefit" or "welfare" through ordinances dealing with interventions into the rights of owners. These ordinances reflected the principles of common law such as: "the welfare of the people shall be the supreme law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). Frequently cited in court decisions, these main principles serve as "common law programs for managing a well-regulated society", providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property aimed at reflecting the mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

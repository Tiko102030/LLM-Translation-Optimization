Plaintiffs claimed that, by invalidating existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional clause on contracts, which also contravened the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment to protect citizens from government interference. Recently issued to protect civil rights of freed slaves in southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment was now considered an inalienable civil right and a constitutional guarantee of property that protected private property rights from government interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the Court's 1877 members. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee on property, the Court affirmed and even expanded the powers of past regulation. Speaking about what "powers are inherent in any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government can regulate the behavior of its citizens towards each other and how people should use their property, but only when it is necessary for the common good." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Hale's statement on England's chief justice that, when property "affects public interests, it by law ceases to be a matter of private regulation alone."

The US Supreme Court did not rush to criticize state regulation, but Justice Stephen Field questioned the ideas about police powers as early as 1872 in the case "New Orleans Butchers' Benevolent Association v Crescent City Live-Stock Landing & Slaughter-House" (1872). The given case arose after the Louisiana legislative body restricted slaughterhouse operations in New Orleans. Since public health was not threatened, this law was similar to ordinances previously established in many other cities. Citing a decision by the Chief Justice of the Massachusetts Supreme Court, Shaw, in the case "Commonwealth v Algar" (1851), the Louisiana Supreme Court accepted the law, stating that "the loss of private rights in this matter has no significance in view of the overall profit and commercial turnover of the commercial organization involved".

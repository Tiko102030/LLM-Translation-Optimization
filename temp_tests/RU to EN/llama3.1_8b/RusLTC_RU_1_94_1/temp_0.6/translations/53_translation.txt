Conclusion: The process of developing property rights Despite Justice Scalia's opinion, the Fourth footnote remains an existing law. As it was brought up for discussion, the Supreme Court approved almost all legislation regulating property and recognized that the powers of elected members of the legislative body should be "as great as the economic needs of the country." At the same time, the Supreme Court established itself as a guardian of private rights and civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment. To them it equated the right to pollute their territory or discriminate based on gender, racial, or religious grounds, which are now above all property owners' claims. Considering property as a social institution, the Court implied that property rights stand on two pillars: the essence of harm and property as an instrument, not a sacred right. Recognizing the dual nature of losses, courts consider any claimed "right" to property as one of several competing "rights", with all of them needing to be balanced. After approval of Holmes' principle of balancing interests, the Court should consider each claimed "right" to property in instrumental implementation, since the alternating of interests would lead to achieving more global public goals. Instrumental, competing "rights" to property can never be absolute, and never should be an end in itself.

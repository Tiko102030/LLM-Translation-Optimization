— And the tea? — Tea, if you like. — Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately took charge, poured some, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast and got up again onto the divan. As before, he enveloped the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began to administer tea from a teaspoon, blowing on it ceaselessly and especially vigorously, as if this blowing was the main and salvific point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite enough strength to sit up on the divan without any outside help, not only to hold his hands enough to keep the spoon or cup, but even possibly to walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even still not fully understanding, and in the meantime to listen and find out what was happening here? However, he could not overcome his aversion: after taking ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed away the spoon, and fell back onto the pillow.

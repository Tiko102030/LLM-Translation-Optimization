But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolayevsky Bridge he was forced to wake up again completely as a result of one rather unpleasant occurrence for him. A carriage driver with a whip struck him hard on the back because he almost ran under the horses, despite the fact that the driver had yelled at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, leaping to the railing (he was walking in the middle of the bridge where they ride, not walk), snarled and clicked his teeth spitefully. Of course, laughter echoed all around. — And there's a case! — Some beggar or other will do it on purpose and run under the wheels; and you're to blame for it. — They make their living that way, sir, they make their living...[21 - They make their living that way...] — In newspapers of that time, it was often reported about St. Petersburg beggars who made a business out of running on purpose under the carriage's wheels in order to receive compensation for injuries (see, e.g., "The Voice", 1865, #65). But at the moment when he stood by the railing and still looked after the departing carriage with a senseless and spiteful gaze, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was shoving money into his hands.

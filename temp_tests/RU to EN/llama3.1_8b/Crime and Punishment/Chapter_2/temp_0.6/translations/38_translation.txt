Where did these feelings come from? On the other hand, if his room were suddenly filled with his first and closest friends, he wouldn't have found a single human word to say to them; his heart would be so empty. A dismal feeling of anguishing, endless isolation and alienation suddenly became conscious in his soul. It was not the meanness of his confessions to Ilya Petrovich, nor the officer's triumph over him that suddenly turned his heart upside down. Oh, what does it matter to him now about his own baseness, about all these ambitions, officers, German women, lawsuits, offices, and so on? If he were even sentenced to be burned at the stake in this moment, he wouldn't have stirred, and perhaps not even listened carefully to the verdict. Something was happening to him that was completely unfamiliar, new, sudden, and never before experienced. He didn't understand it, but he clearly felt with all the force of feeling that he couldn't address these people, the office clerks in the quarter, in any way, as if they were not his own brothers and sisters, but simply quarter officers; even then, there would be no need for him to address them, or indeed to live at all. He had never experienced a similar strange and terrifying feeling before this moment. And what's most torturous is that it was more of an experience than a consciousness, more of a sensation than a concept; the most direct, the most tormenting sensation he had ever felt in his life. The clerk began dictating to him the usual form of such a response, i.e., I'm unable to pay, I promise to do so sometime, I won't leave the city, I won't sell or give away my property, and so on.

Further, in the courtyard's recess, peered out from behind the fence a low corner of a charred stone shed, clearly part of some workshop. Here, undoubtedly, was some kind of establishment, a carriage or locksmith shop, or something similar; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust clouded. "If only I could throw it somewhere and leave!" he thought suddenly. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he strolled through the gates and happened to see, right near the gates, a gutter (as often arranged in such houses where many factory workers, trade union members, cab drivers, etc., live) attached to the fence; above the gutter, on the fence itself, was scribbled with chalk the usual sharp remark: "Here trespassing is forbidden." So it's good that there's no suspicion that he went and stopped. "Everything here is so conveniently arranged all at once, let's just throw it somewhere in a heap and leave!"

Glancing around again, he already stuck his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where the distance was as wide as an arshin, he noticed a large uncarved stone, weighing about one and a half poods, leaning directly against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear pedestrians scurrying by, of which there are always many here; but no one behind the gates could see him unless someone from the street happened to enter, which, however, was very likely, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grasped its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and turned the stone over. Under the stone a small depression formed; immediately he started throwing everything from his pocket into it.

This was some especially disheveled person with an immobile idea in their gaze. "You wouldn't learn anything from this because it doesn't matter to them," thought Raskolnikov. — Go there, to the clerk, he said and pointed ahead with his finger, showing him the very last room. He entered that room (the fourth one in order), cramped and packed tightly with people – a crowd of people slightly better dressed than those in the other rooms. Between the visitors were two ladies. One was in mourning, poorly dressed, sitting at a table opposite the clerk and writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and red-faced, with spots, a noticeable woman, and extremely lavishly dressed, with a brooch on her chest, the size of a teacup saucer, stood off to one side waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed the clerk his summons. The latter glanced briefly at it, said "wait" and continued working with the lady in mourning. He took a freer breath.

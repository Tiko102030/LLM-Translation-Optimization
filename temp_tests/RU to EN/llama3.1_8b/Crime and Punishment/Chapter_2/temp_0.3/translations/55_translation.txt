He would have just spat on someone and bitten them if anyone had spoken to him...

He stopped suddenly when he stepped out onto the embankment of the Little Neva, on Vasilyevsky Island, near a bridge. "Here's where he lives, in this house," he thought. "What is it, but I've come here myself! The same old story as before... But very, very interesting: did I come here or was I just passing by and happened to stop? Doesn't matter; I said... three days ago... that I would go see him the next day, well, I'll go! As if I couldn't go in now..."

He went up to Razumikhin's place on the fifth floor. He was home, in his little room, and at this moment he was working, writing, and himself let Raskolnikov in. They hadn't seen each other for four months. Razumikhin sat in his tattered dressing gown, in slippers on one foot, disheveled, unshaven, and unwashed. A look of surprise appeared on his face.

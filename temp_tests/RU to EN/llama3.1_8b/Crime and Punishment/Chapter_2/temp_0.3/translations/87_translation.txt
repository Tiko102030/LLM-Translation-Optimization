— And the tea? — Tea, if you like. — Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately gave orders, poured some, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast and got up again onto the divan. Still holding the sick man's head with his left hand, he lifted him and began to feed him tea from a teaspoon, blowing on the spoon continuously and especially energetically, as if in this process of blowing the spoon was the main and most vital point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite enough strength to lift himself up and sit on the divan without any external help, and not only hold his hands enough to hold a spoon or cup, but even walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for the time being, to lie low, to pretend, if necessary, even still not quite understanding, and meanwhile listen in and find out what is happening here? Anyway, he couldn't overcome his revulsion: after taking ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away, and fell back onto the pillow.

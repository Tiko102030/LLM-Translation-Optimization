But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolsky Bridge he was forced to come to his senses once again due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. A coachman from one of the carriages had given him a sharp lash on the back with his whip because he almost got under the horses' hooves, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, jumping away to the railing (he didn't know why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where they drive, not walk), maliciously hissed and clicked his teeth. Of course, laughter echoed all around. — And it's your fault! — Some kind of beggar. — It's known that a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; and you're responsible for him. — They make their living that way, sir. — They make their living that way... But at the moment when he stood by the railing and still looked after the departing carriage with a senseless and malicious gaze, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping money into his hands.

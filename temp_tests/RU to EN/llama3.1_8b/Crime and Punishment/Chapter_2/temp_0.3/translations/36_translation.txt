—"Please have mercy, Captain," he began very freely, addressing Nikodim Fyodorovich suddenly, "take my situation into account... I'm even willing to ask them for forgiveness if I've made any mistakes on my part. I'm a poor and sick student, depressed (he said it himself: 'depressed') by poverty. I used to be a student because now I can no longer support myself, but I'll get the money... My mother and sister live in the Nizhny Novgorod province. They'll send me some, and I'll... pay up. My landlady is a kind woman, but she's so angry that I've lost my lessons and haven't paid for four months, not even to eat... And I don't understand at all what this promissory note is! Now she's demanding payment from me on the basis of this loan, what am I supposed to do?!"

—"But this isn't our business..." was again noted by the clerk...

—"Wait, wait, I completely agree with you," Rascolnikov picked up again, addressing not the clerk but Nikodim Fyodorovich, but trying to address both Ilia Petrovich as well, although he stubbornly pretended to be rummaging through papers and disdainfully ignored him—"Allow me to explain my side of things... I've been living with her for about three years now, since I arrived from the provinces and before that... Well, why shouldn't I admit it? From the very beginning, I gave her a verbal promise that I would marry her daughter. This was a girl... well, she even pleased me... although I wasn't in love with her... In short, youth is to blame... or rather, I want to say that my landlady used to lend me money frequently and I lived a somewhat carefree life... I was very reckless...

—"We don't need such intimate details from you, beggar," Ilia Petrovich roughly interrupted him, but Rascolnikov stopped him with passion, although it suddenly became extremely difficult for him to speak. —"But please, allow me to tell you the rest of the story... how things were and... in my turn... even though this is unnecessary, I agree with you that telling you is pointless,"—but a year ago this girl died from typhus, and I remained a tenant as before, and when our landlady moved to her current apartment, she said to me... and said kindly... that she was completely confident in me and everything... but that perhaps I wouldn't like to give her this promissory note for 115 rubles, which she considered my debt. Please: she explicitly said that as soon as I gave her this document, she would start lending me money again as much as I wanted, and that she would never use this document herself... until I pay up myself. And now, when I've lost my lessons and have nothing to eat, she's sending it to collection... What am I supposed to say?

—"All these sensitive details, beggar, are none of our business," Ilia Petrovich brutally cut him off—"You need to give a statement and an undertaking, and what you were in love with and all those tragic events have nothing to do with this."

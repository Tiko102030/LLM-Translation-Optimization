But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still looked back with a senseless and angry expression at the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands. He looked up: an elderly merchant woman, with a headscarf and cowhide boots, and with her a girl, in a hat and with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for Christ's sake." He took it, and they walked by. Two grivnas. By their clothes and appearance, they could easily have taken him for a beggar, a real street money collector, and he would likely owe the whip blow that had moved them to pity. He clutched the two grivnas in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face towards the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water almost blue, which is rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be better outlined from any point than from here, from the bridge, not twenty paces away from the chapel, shone brightly, and through the clear air it was possible to clearly make out even its smallest ornament. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; now one anxious and not quite clear thought occupied him exclusively.

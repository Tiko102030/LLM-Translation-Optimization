Further, in the depth of the yard, a corner of a low, soot-stained stone shed was visible behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, no doubt, there was some establishment, a carriage or locksmith's shop, or something of that kind; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust was scattered. "I wish I could just throw it away and leave!" he suddenly thought to himself. Not noticing anyone in the yard, he strode through the gates and saw at this moment, right by the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, cabmen, etc.) attached to the fence, and above the gutter, immediately on the fence, was written with chalk the usual sharpness: "Here standing is forbidden." So it's good that he didn't arouse any suspicion of having gone in and stopped. "Everything here is so mixed up and can be thrown away somewhere in a heap and leave!"

Glancing around once more, he had already stuffed his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where all the distance was about an arshin wide, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing roughly one and a half pounds, leaning directly against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear people shuffling by, which is always numerous here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street happened to come in, which, however, was quite possible, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength and turned the stone over. Under the stone a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything out of his pocket into it.

— Oh, for mercy's sake, captain, he began quite brazenly, suddenly addressing Nikodim Fomich,— get involved in my situation… I am even ready to ask them to forgive us if we have done anything wrong on our part. I'm a poor and sick student, crushed by poverty. I was a student because now I can no longer support myself, but I will receive money... I have a mother and sister in — j province. They'll send me some, and I… will pay. My landlady is a kind woman, but she's become so angry that I've lost my lessons and haven't paid for the fourth month, not even sending me to eat... And I don't understand at all what this bill of exchange is! Now she demands payment from me in exchange for this loan document, what can I say?—

— But this isn't our business… — again noticed the clerk…

— Allow me, allow me, I completely agree with you, but permit me to clarify,— Rascolnikov picked up again, addressing not only Nikodim Fomich, but also trying to address Il'ia Petrovich as well, although he stubbornly pretended to be digging through papers and looked at him disdainfully,— permit me to explain from my side that I've been living with her for about three years, since my arrival in the province and before... before... well, why shouldn't I even confess it now? From the very beginning, I gave her an oral promise that I would marry her daughter, a completely free promise... She was a girl... well, she suited me, although I wasn't in love with her... one word, youth, that is, I want to say that my landlady did me many favors back then and I lived somewhat recklessly...

— We don't ask for such intimate details, benefactor, and there's no time for it,— Il'ia Petrovich coarsely interrupted him with a triumphant air, but Rascolnikov stopped him with zeal, although he found it incredibly difficult to speak suddenly. — But permit me, permit me to tell you part of the story... how it all happened and... in my turn... even though that's unnecessary, I agree with you that we should only give a brief account,— but last year this girl died of typhus, and I remained her lodger as before, and when our landlady moved to her current apartment, she told me... and said it friendly... that she completely trusts me and everything... but that maybe I wouldn't want to give her this loan document for 115 rubles, which she considered my debt. Permit me: she said exactly that she would again credit me as much as needed if only I gave her this document, and that she would never—those were her exact words—use this paper until I pay off myself... And now, when I've lost my lessons and have nothing to eat, she's even putting it up for collection... What can I say?

— All these sensitive details, benefactor, don't concern us,— Il'ia Petrovich brutally cut in again,— you need to give a review and a guarantee, and what you were in love with her and all those tragic events are of no consequence.

But at that moment, as he stood at the railing and still looked back senselessly and spitefully at the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was inserting some money into his hands. He glanced over: an elderly businesswoman, with a kerchief and goatskin boots, and with her a girl, in a hat and with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, old man, for Christ's sake." He took it, and they passed by. Two grivnas. By their clothing and appearance, they could have taken him for a beggar, a true money-collector on the street, and for giving him an entire two grivnas he probably deserved a blow from a whip that pitied them. He put the two grivnas into his hand, walked ten steps, and turned back to the Neva in the direction of the palace. The sky was clear with not the slightest cloud, and the water was almost blue, which is rarely seen on the Neva. The dome of the church, which from no point is better outlined than from here, from the bridge, within twenty paces of the chapel, shone brightly, and through the clean air it was possible to clearly distinguish even each of its ornaments. The pain from the whip's blow had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; only one anxious and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

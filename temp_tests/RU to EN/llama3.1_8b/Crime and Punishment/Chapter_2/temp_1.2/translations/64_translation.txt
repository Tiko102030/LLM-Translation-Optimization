But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolayevsky Bridge he had to come to his senses for a second time due to one rather unpleasant situation for him. A coachman from a carriage tightly lashed him with a whip across the back because he nearly got under the horses' hooves, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The lash's blow so infuriated him that, jumping away to the parapet (it is unknown why he was walking right in the middle of the bridge where people ride, not walk), he sullenly screeched and chattered his teeth. Of course, around there laughter resounded everywhere. — Well done! — One fellow shouted out. — The sort of person who tries to get money by pretending to be hurt; he'll deliberately get under a carriage's wheels when he's drunk. You'll be responsible for him. — That's the business they're in, respectable folk, that's the business... But at that very moment, as he was standing against the parapet and still foolishly and spitefully looking after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping some money into his hand.

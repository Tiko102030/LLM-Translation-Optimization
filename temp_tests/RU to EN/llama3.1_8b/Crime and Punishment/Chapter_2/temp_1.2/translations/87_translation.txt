"Do you want some tea?" "Tea, perhaps." "Pour it over, wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately took charge, poured some, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast and sat back on the divan again. Still he wrapped his left hand around the sick man's head, raised him up and started to give him tea from a teaspoon, continuously and especially with great zeal blowing on the spoon as if the most crucial and salvific point of recovery lay in this act of blowing. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist despite feeling very sufficient strength to sit up on the divan without any outside help, and not only hold his hands well enough to keep a teaspoon or cup, but even possibly walk. But by some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for now, hide out, pretend if necessary, even still not fully understanding what's going on, yet listen in and pry out what is happening here? Anyway, he couldn't overcome his aversion: after taking ten teaspoons of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away and fell back onto the pillow again. Underneath his heads were actually genuine pillows – fluffy ones with clean covers; he noticed this too and took it into account."

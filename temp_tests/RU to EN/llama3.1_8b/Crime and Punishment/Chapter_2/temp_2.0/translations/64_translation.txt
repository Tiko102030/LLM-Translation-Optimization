But Raskolnikov was already on the street. On Nikolayevsky Bridge he had to fully wake up again as a result of one very unpleasant occurrence for him. He was hit hard with a whip on his back by a cab driver from one carriage, because he almost ran into the horses, despite the fact that the driver yelled at him three or four times. The blow from the whip enraged him so much that he jumped to the parapets (he don't know why he walked right in the middle of the bridge where people are driven by, not walked) and with a fierce click of his teeth he hissed and snarled through his clenched teeth. Laughing all around, of course, - Is there any fun?! — Some thief, that's what you are! - What a scoundrel! One knows these cases: if some drunkard is caught on purpose running under the wheels; and then you will have to answer for it. — They thrive off those things, sir! — People around him would sometimes brag about Petersburg beggars who thrived off running under carriages on purpose in order to receive compensation from their injuries (see "Glasnost" 1865, No. 65). But at the moment when he was standing by the parapets and still stared with an empty and angry look after the receding carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt that someone had handed him money.

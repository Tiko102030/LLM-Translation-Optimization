And suddenly, to the greatest amazement, he vaguely heard his landlady's voice. She was howling, squealing, and wailing, hurrying along, talking so quickly that you couldn't make out what she was saying, imploring something – certainly that they should stop beating her because they were mercilessly beating her on the staircase. The sound of the beater had become so appalling with anger and fury that it was now barely audible as a gasp, but still he, the beater, was also speaking something, quickly and incoherently, stammering and spluttering. Suddenly Rascolnikov quivered like a leaf; he recognized this voice – it was Ilya Petrovich's voice! Ilya Petrovich is here, beating the landlady! He is kicking her with his legs, pounding her head against the steps – this is clear from the sounds, wails and thuds. What’s the matter with him? Did the lights just go out or something? It was heard how people gathered from all floors on the staircase, voices and shouts were rising up, footsteps echoed, doors creaked, they ran down. “But why, but how is it possible!” he repeated, seriously wondering if he had gone completely mad. But no, he's hearing it all too clearly!

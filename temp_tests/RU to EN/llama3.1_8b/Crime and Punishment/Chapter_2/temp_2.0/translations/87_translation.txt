— Is it a tea for me? — A cup of tea, if you wish. — Pour it! Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately arranged things, poured it, then filled another teacup, discarded his breakfast and sat back on the divan again. Once more he wrapped his left arm around the sick man's head, lifted him up and began to feed him tea from a teaspoon, continuously and especially enthusiastically blowing on the spoon as though this blowing was the main and most salvific point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and didn't resist, despite feeling sufficiently strong to lift himself up and sit down on the divan without any external assistance; not only did he have enough strength in his hands to hold the spoon or cup, but maybe even to walk. But suddenly, for some strange, almost bestial cleverness, it occurred to him to conceal his powers at the time, hide away, pretend, if needed, not quite understanding what's going on. In any case, he didn't overcome his aversion: after ten sips of tea with a spoon, he suddenly freed himself from under the hand, irritably pushed the spoon aside and rolled back onto the pillow again.

— And tea? — Tea, perhaps. — Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately gave orders, poured some, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast and sat back on the divan again. Still holding the sick man's head with his left hand, he lifted him up and began to feed him tea from a teaspoon, blowing constantly and especially enthusiastically into the spoon as if in this process of blowing the main and saving point of recovery lay. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite enough strength to lift himself up and sit on the divan without any external help, and not only hold his hands well enough to grasp the spoon or cup, but even walk. But by some strange, almost bestial cunning it suddenly occurred to him to hide his forces for a time, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, to be even not quite understanding, yet to listen in and find out what was happening here? However, he could not overcome his aversion: after swallowing ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away, and fell back onto the pillow again.

But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolaevsky Bridge, he was forced to come to his senses once again due to a very unpleasant incident for him. A coachman from one of the carriages struck him with a whip on the back because he almost got under the horses' hooves, despite the fact that the coachman had yelled at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, jumping away to the railing (it's unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge where the carriages go and not people), maliciously gnashed his teeth and clicked his jaws. Of course, laughter was heard all around. — It's on you! — Some scoundrel is at it again. — It's known that some drunkard will deliberately jump under the wheels; and you'll be responsible for him. — That's their trade, sir, that's their trade... But in that moment, when he stood by the railing and was still looking after the departing carriage with a senseless and angry expression, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.

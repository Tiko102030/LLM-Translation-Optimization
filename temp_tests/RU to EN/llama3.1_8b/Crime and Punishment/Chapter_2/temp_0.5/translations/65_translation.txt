But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still looked back with a meaningless and angry gaze at the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was shoving money into his hands. He looked around: an elderly merchantwoman in a headscarf and goat-hair boots, and with her a girl, wearing a hat and carrying a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for Christ's sake." He took the money, and they walked by. It was two grivnas. By their clothes and appearance, they might have taken him to be a beggar, a real street collector of coins, and at such a large sum he, no doubt, should have been beaten with a whip which had moved them to pity. He clutched the two grivnas in his hand, walked ten paces, and turned his face towards the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was clear, without even the slightest cloud, and the water almost blue, as it so rarely is on the Neva. The cupola of the cathedral, which can be seen from no other point better than from here, from the bridge, not yet twenty paces away from the chapel, shone brightly, and through the clean air one could distinctly see even each of its ornaments. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; now only a restless and not quite clear thought occupied him exclusively.

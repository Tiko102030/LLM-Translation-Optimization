Further in the yard, peeking out from behind a fence was a low, charred, stone shed corner, obviously part of some workshop. Here, surely, there was some kind of establishment, a carriage or locksmith shop, or something like that; everywhere, almost from the very gates, coal dust was scattered. "I wish I could throw it all away and leave!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the yard, he walked through the gates and just saw, right next to the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses, where there are many factory workers, artisans, cabmen, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written with chalk the usual sharpness: "Here standing here forbidden to spit". So it's good that there's no suspicion that he went in and stopped. "Everything is so cluttered all at once and can be thrown somewhere into a pile and leave!"

He glanced around again, already sticking his hand into his pocket, when suddenly, right next to the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where all the distance was about an arm's width, he noticed a large uncut stone, weighing approximately one and a half poods, leaning directly against the stone street wall. Behind this wall was a street, a sidewalk; you could hear pedestrians shuffling by - there are always many here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone from the street came in, which, however, could very well happen, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed it firmly at the top with both hands, gathered all his strength, and turned the stone over. Under the stone a small depression formed; immediately he began throwing everything from his pocket into it.

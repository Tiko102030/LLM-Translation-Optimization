He said, "Will you have some tea?" and I replied, "Yes, thank you." "Fill it up," he said. "Wait a moment, let me fill it for you myself; sit down at the table." He immediately began to arrange things, poured a glass of tea, then filled another one, tossed aside his own breakfast and returned to sit on the sofa again. Holding the sick man's head with his left hand, he lifted him up and began feeding him a teaspoon of tea, blowing constantly on it as if this blowing process were the very foundation of his recovery.

Raskolnikov remained silent and didn't resist despite feeling sufficiently strong to lift himself up onto the sofa without any outside help, not only holding his hands well enough to hold the spoon or cup but possibly even walking. But for some strange, almost savage reason a thought suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength temporarily, hide it away, perhaps even pretending to be even less understanding than he actually was and thus listen in and try to figure out what was going on here. However, he wasn't able to overcome his revulsion: having drunk ten spoons of tea, he suddenly freed himself from his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away and fell onto the pillow again.

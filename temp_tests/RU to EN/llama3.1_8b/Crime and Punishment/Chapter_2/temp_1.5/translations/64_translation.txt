But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolsky Bridge, he was forced to wake up completely again as a result of a very unpleasant case that befell him. A cab driver in one of the carriages had given him a good lashing with his whip across the back for almost getting run over by the horses, despite the fact that the cab driver had shouted at him three or four times already. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, jumping to the railing (for no reason at all did he walk along the very middle of the bridge where carriages travel and not pedestrians), spitefully grated his teeth and clicked his jaws. Around them, of course, people were laughing loudly.— Well done! —someone yelled—A tramp who does such things on purpose; and then you'll be the one to blame for him. They prey on us with such tactics, the gentlemen preying on us with such tactics... [21— Such predators...—It was often reported in the newspapers of that time about St. Petersburg beggars who profited by willfully jumping under the wheels of a carriage to receive an allowance for their injuries (see, for example, “Golos”, 1865, #65).] But at the very moment when he stood by the railing and still looked after the departing carriage in a senseless and enraged manner, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was shoving money into his hands.

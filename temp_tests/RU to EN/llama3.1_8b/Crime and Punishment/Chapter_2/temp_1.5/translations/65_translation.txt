But at that very moment, as he stood at the railings and still looked after the retreating carriage with a meaningless and malevolent gaze, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands. He glanced over: an elderly merchantwoman, in a head kerchief and cowhide boots, and a girl beside her, wearing a hat and carrying a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, Father, for Christ's sake." He took the money, and they passed by. It was two grivnas. By their dress and appearance, they might easily take him to be a beggar, a genuine street panhandler, and he would likely deserve the whip that they were so merciful about. He stuffed the two grivnas into his hand, walked ten paces, and turned back to face the Neva River in the direction of the palace. The sky was completely clear, with not even the faintest cloud to be seen, and the water was almost blue – a rare occurrence on the Neva. The cupola of the church, which can be clearly seen from any point, shone brightly as it could be seen from here, on the bridge, about twenty paces before the chapel; and through the clear air you could even make out each detail of its decorations. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; only a worried and not very clear idea was now exclusively occupying him.

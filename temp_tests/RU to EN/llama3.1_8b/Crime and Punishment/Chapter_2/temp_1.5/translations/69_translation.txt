But no, he was hearing it all too clearly! But then they would certainly come to him now, if so, "because... after all this is just the same... because of yesterday... Lord!" He wanted to lock himself up in his room, but his hand didn't rise... and anyway, what's the use? Fear had wrapped his soul in ice, tormented him, petrified him... But finally, after that endless wailing, which had lasted for a good ten minutes, began to subside. The landlady was moaning and shrieking, Ilya Petrovich was still threatening and cursing... But at last it seemed as though he too had calmed down; he's silent now; "has he really gone! Lord!" Yes, and the landlady goes out too, still sobbing and crying... and her door slammed shut. The crowd disperses into the flats from the staircase - they gasp, argue, exclaim to one another, sometimes raising their voices to a shout, sometimes lowering them to a whisper. There must have been a lot of people; practically the whole house had gathered. "But God, can this be possible! And what's the point of him coming here at all!"

Raskolnikov was exhausted and collapsed onto a divan, but he couldn't close his eyes; for half an hour he lay in such anguish, in such unbearable feeling of boundless horror that he had never experienced before. Suddenly a bright light filled his room: Nastasya came in with a candle and a tray with soup. Glancing at him attentively and noticing that he wasn't asleep, she put the candle on the table and began to unpack the things she had brought: bread, salt, a plate, a spoon.

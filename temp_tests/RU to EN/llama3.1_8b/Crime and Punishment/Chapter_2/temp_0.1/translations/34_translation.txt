Louisa Ivanovna with a benevolent smile began to squat down in all directions and, squatting, reached the doors; but at the doors she bumped her backside into one of the prominent officers, with an open fresh face and with excellent thick fair mustaches. This was Nikodim Fomich himself, the quarter's overseer. Louisa Ivanovna hastened to sit down almost to the floor and with frequent small steps, jumping, flew out of the office. — Again thunder, again thunder and lightning, tornado, hurricane! — Nikodim Fomich kindly and affably addressed Ilya Petrovich, — again they have disturbed my heart, again it has boiled over! I even heard it from the stairs. — What? — with noble carelessness said Ilya Petrovich (and not what, but somehow: "Da-a sh-ta-a!"), moving with some papers to another table and dramatically flinging his shoulders with each step, wherever he stepped, there his shoulder was; — look here, you see: the gentleman writer, that is, the student, who used to be, doesn't pay money, issued IOUs, doesn't clean the apartment, constant complaints come in about them, and they have taken it upon themselves to make a complaint that I lit a cigarette among them! They are whining, but look here: take a glance at them: there they are in their most attractive appearance now. — Poverty is not a vice, comrade, well, what can you do?

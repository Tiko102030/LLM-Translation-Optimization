He would have just spat on someone, bitten him, it seemed, if anyone had spoken to him...

He stopped suddenly when he came out onto the embankment of the Little Neva, on Vasilyevsky Island, near a bridge. "Here he lives in this house," he thought. "What is it, but I myself came here! Again the same story as before... But very, however, interesting: did I come here or just happened to pass by? It doesn't matter; I said... three days ago... that I would go see him the next day, well, and I will go! As if I couldn't go in now..."

He went up to Razumikhin's place on the fifth floor. He was at home, in his little room, and was writing at this moment, and himself let Raskolnikov in. They hadn't seen each other for four months. Razumikhin sat in his tattered dressing gown, in slippers with one foot bare, disheveled, unshaven, and unwashed. A look of surprise appeared on his face.

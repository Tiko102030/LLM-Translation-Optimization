But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still looked back with a senseless and angry expression at the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping money into his hands. He looked around: an elderly merchant's wife, in a headscarf and goat-hair boots, and with her a girl, in a hat and with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for Christ's sake." He took the money, and they walked by. It was two grivnas. By their clothes and appearance, they could have taken him for a beggar, for a real street coin-collector, and he probably deserved a whip-lashing from them for taking the whole two grivnas. He clutched the two grivnas in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face towards the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water almost blue, which is rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be drawn better from any point than from here, from the bridge, not twenty paces away from the chapel, shone brightly, and through the clear air it was possible to clearly see even each of its ornaments. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; one anxious and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

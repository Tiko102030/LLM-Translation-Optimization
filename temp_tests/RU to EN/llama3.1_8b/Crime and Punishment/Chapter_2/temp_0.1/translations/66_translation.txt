The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; one anxious and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked far off for a long time and intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to university, it often happened to him, perhaps ten times, that on his way home he would stop exactly at this same spot, gaze intently at this truly magnificent panorama, and each time almost be amazed by one obscure and insoluble impression of his own. An inexplicable chill always blew over him from this magnificent panorama; the spirit was mute and deaf to him in this lush picture... He marveled every time at his gloomy and enigmatic impression and put off solving it, not trusting himself, for the future. Suddenly he remembered now all these old questions and perplexities of his, and it seemed to him that he had not forgotten them by chance. It even struck him as strange and wonderful that he had stopped at the same spot as before, as if he really imagined that he could think about the same things now as before, with the same themes and pictures that interested him... still so recently. Even a little bit funny it seemed to him, and at the same time his chest constricted with pain. In some depth, below, somewhere just visible underfoot, it seemed to him now that all this past was, and old thoughts, and old problems, and old themes, and old impressions, and the whole panorama, and he himself, and everything... It seemed as if he were flying upwards somewhere and everything disappeared from his eyes. Making one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a two-kopeck coin clenched in his fist. He relaxed his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung it back and forth, and then threw it into the water; after that, he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had cut himself off from everything and everyone with scissors just now.

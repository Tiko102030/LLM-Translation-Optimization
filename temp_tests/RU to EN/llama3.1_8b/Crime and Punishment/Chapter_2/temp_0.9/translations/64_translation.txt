But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolayevsky Bridge he had to wake up again for a moment due to a rather unpleasant incident that befell him. A coachman of a carriage struck him firmly with his whip on the back because he almost got run over by horses, despite the fact that the coachman shouted at him three or four times. The blow from the whip enraged him so much that he, jumping away to the parapet (for some reason he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where vehicles go and pedestrians don't), angrily gnashed his teeth and clicked his jaws. Around, of course, laughter resounded: — And it's your fault! — You're a troublemaker, you're always seeking out trouble! — You deliberately jump under the wheels; anyone can get drunk and do that on purpose. And you'll be held responsible for it. — That's how they make their living, esteemed one; that's how they make their living...[21 — How they make their living… — In newspapers of those times, there were often stories about St. Petersburg beggars who made a business out of deliberately running under the wheels of an equipage to receive compensation for injuries (see, e.g., "Golos", 1865, No. 65).] But at that very moment when he stood by the parapet and was still stupidly and sullenly watching the carriage moving away in the distance, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping some money into his hand.

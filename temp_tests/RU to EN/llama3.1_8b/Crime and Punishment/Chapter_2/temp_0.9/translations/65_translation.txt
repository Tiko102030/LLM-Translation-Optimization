But at that very moment, as he stood at the railing and was still aimlessly and spitefully looking back at the departing carriage, rubbing his spine, he suddenly felt someone slipping some money into his hands. He looked up: an elderly merchant woman, with a kerchief on her head and goatskin boots, and with her a girl, in a hat and carrying a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, dear sir, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. The money was two grivnas. By their clothes and appearance, they could easily have taken him for a beggar, for an actual coin collector on the street, and with the offering of the entire two grivnas, he probably had to receive a blow from the whip that moved them to pity. He folded his hands over the two grivnas, took ten steps, and turned his face towards the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was clear without even a single cloud, and the water almost blue - a rare sight on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which is not depicted better from any point than looking at it from here, from the bridge, not reaching twenty steps to the chapel, shone brightly, and through the clean air one could distinctly make out every detail of its ornamentation. Pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; only a perturbing and not very clear thought occupied him now exclusively.

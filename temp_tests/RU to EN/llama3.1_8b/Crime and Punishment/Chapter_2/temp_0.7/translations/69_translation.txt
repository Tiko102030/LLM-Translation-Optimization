But no, he hears it all too clearly!... But then, of course, they'll come to him now, if so, "because… yes, all this is from the same... because of yesterday... Lord!" He wanted to lock himself in, but his hand didn't rise... and what's the use anyway? Fear, like ice, encased his soul, tormented it, petrified it... But finally, that whole racket, which had been going on for a good ten minutes, began to gradually subside. The landlady was wailing and moaning, Ilya Petrovich was still threatening and swearing... But finally, it seemed, he calmed down; he's not making a sound anymore; "has he really left! Lord!" Yes, the landlady is leaving too, still wailing and crying... and her door has slammed shut. And the crowd is dispersing from the staircase to the apartments, gasping, arguing, calling out to one another, now raising their voices to a shout, now lowering them to a whisper. There must have been many of them; almost the whole house had gathered. "But God, can all this be possible! And why did he come here at all!"

Raskolnikov collapsed onto the divan in despair, but couldn't close his eyes yet; he lay there for half an hour in such suffering, in such unbearable feeling of boundless horror that he'd never experienced before. Suddenly a bright light illuminated his room: Nastasya came in with a candle and a bowl of soup. Looking at him attentively and noticing that he wasn't asleep, she put the candle on the table and began to unpack what she had brought: bread, salt, a plate, a spoon.

But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still looked back with a meaningless and angry expression at the departing carriage, massaging his spine, he suddenly felt someone pushing money into his hands. He looked up: an old merchant's wife in a kerchief and goat-horned boots, and beside her a girl in a hat and a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, Father, for Christ's sake." He took it, and they walked by. The sum was two grivnas. They looked at him as if he were a beggar, a real street money collector, and the fact that they gave him a whole two-grivna note would probably have earned them a blow with a whip that had moved their hearts to pity. He clutched the two grivnas in his hand and walked ten paces, then turned his face towards the Neva River, towards the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water almost blue, which is rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which can be drawn no better from any point than looking at it from here, from the bridge, not twenty paces short of the chapel, shone brightly, and through the clean air one could clearly see even each of its decorations. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the blow; now only one anxious and not entirely clear thought occupied him exclusively.

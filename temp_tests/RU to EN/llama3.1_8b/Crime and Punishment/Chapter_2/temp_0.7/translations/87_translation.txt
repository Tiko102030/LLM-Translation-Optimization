— And the tea? — Tea, perhaps. — Pour some. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately arranged things, poured some, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast and sat back down on the divan. He still held the sick man's head in his left hand, lifted him up, and began to feed him tea from a teaspoon, blowing repeatedly and particularly energetically on the spoon as if this blowing process was the most crucial and life-saving part of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist despite feeling quite enough strength to lift himself and sit on the divan without any outside help, and not only hold his hands well enough to grasp a spoon or cup, but even walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his powers for the time being, retreat, pretend, if necessary, even still not quite understanding, and in the meantime listen and find out what's going on here? However, he couldn't overcome his aversion: after taking ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed the spoon away, and fell back onto the pillow. Under his heads were indeed real pillows – down-filled ones with clean cases; he noticed this too and took it into account.

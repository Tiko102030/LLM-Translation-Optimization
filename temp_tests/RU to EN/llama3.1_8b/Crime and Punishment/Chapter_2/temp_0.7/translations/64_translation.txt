But Raskolnikov had already come out onto the street. On Nikolaevsky Bridge, he was forced to wake up one more time as a result of a very unpleasant incident for him. A coachman from a carriage struck him with a whip on his back because he almost got under the horses' hooves, despite the fact that the coachman had yelled at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that he, jumping to the railing (he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages ride, not pedestrians), snarled and snapped his teeth viciously. Naturally, laughter echoed all around: "And what's new! — A vagrant who's looking for trouble. — It's well-known, a drunkard will do it intentionally and run under the wheels; and you're the one responsible for him. — They make their living that way, sir, they make their living that way..." But in that moment, as he stood by the railing and still looked after the departing carriage with an absurd and spiteful expression, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slip some money into his hands.

But Raskolnikov had already stepped out onto the street. On Nikolayevsky Bridge, he was again made to fully wake up due to a rather unpleasant occurrence for him. A cab driver from one of the carriages struck him with his whip on the back because he had almost gotten under the horses' hooves, despite the fact that the cab driver had yelled at him three or four times. The blow from the whip infuriated him so much that, jumping to the parapet (he was walking down the middle of the bridge, where vehicles pass through, not pedestrians), he nastily gritted his teeth and clicked them. Of course, all around there were people laughing. — And what for! — A swindler like him. — You know how it is, some fellow will intentionally jump under the wheels; that's what they're in business for; and you'll be responsible for covering up. — They prey on us, sir, they prey on us… [21 They preys on us... — At the time of publication, there were often reports in newspapers about St. Petersburg beggars who would intentionally jump under the wheels of carriages to get compensation for their injuries (see e.g. "Golos", 1865, no. 65).] But at the very moment when he was standing by the parapet and still senselessly looking after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt that someone was shoving money into his hand.

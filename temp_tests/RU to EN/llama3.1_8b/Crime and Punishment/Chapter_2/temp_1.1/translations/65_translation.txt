But at that moment, as he stood by the railings and still mindlessly and spitefully looked back at the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone thrusting money into his hands. He glanced around: an elderly merchant's wife in a kerchief and cowhide boots, and with her a girl, wearing a hat and carrying a green parasol, likely her daughter. "Take it, father, for Christ's sake." He took the money and they passed by. The money was two rubles and a half. By their clothing and appearance, they could easily take him for a beggar, a genuine street money collector, and with such a large sum he probably owed them an apology, which moved them to pity. He stuffed the two rubles and a half into his hand, took ten steps and turned back to face the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was clear, without even a small cloud, while the water was almost blue, something that rarely happened on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be depicted better from any point than from here, from the bridge, not more than twenty paces away from the church, shone and through the pure air one could see each ornament on it distinctly. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; one restless and not altogether clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

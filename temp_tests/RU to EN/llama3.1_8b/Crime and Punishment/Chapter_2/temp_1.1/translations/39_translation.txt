The clerk began to dictate the usual response in such a case: I am unable to pay, I promise it will be done someday, I will not leave the city, I won't sell or give away my property, and so on.—You can't write even that, your pen is slipping from your hands,— observed the clerk, peering at Raskolnikov with curiosity. — Are you ill?—Yes... My head is spinning... Go on talking! — Go on; sign it. The clerk took back the paper and busied himself with others. Raskolnikov handed over the pen, but instead of standing up and leaving, he placed his two elbows on the table and clenched his hands around his head. It was as if a nail was being driven into his skull. A strange idea suddenly occurred to him: get up now, go over to Nikodim Fomich, tell him everything from yesterday, every last detail, then go with them to the apartment and point out their things, in the corner, in a hole. The urge was so strong that he had already gotten up from his seat to carry it out.

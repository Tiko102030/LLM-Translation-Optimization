All of the above applies only to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions. For them, it is indeed impossible to guarantee anything and it is not possible to fully automate the training of neural networks. However, alongside with iterative training algorithms there are non-iterative algorithms that have a very high level of stability and allow completely automating the learning process. Checking the adequacy of training Even in the case of successful-looking training at first, the network does not always learn what its creator wanted it to. There is a known case where a network was trained for recognizing tanks by photos, however later it turned out that all tanks were photographed on one and the same background. As a result, the network 'learned' to recognize this type of landscape, instead of 'learning' to recognize tanks. Thus, the network 'understands' not what is required from it, but what is easiest to generalise. Quality testing of neural network training should be done with examples that did not participate in its training. At the same time, the number of test examples must be as much higher than better is the quality of the training. If the errors of a neuron net have a probability close to one billionth, then and for confirming this probability one billion test examples are required.

Further, in the recess of the courtyard, a low, soot-stained, stone barn corner peered out behind the wall, clearly part of some workshop. This must have been some kind of place, a carriage workshop or a blacksmith's, or something like that; everywhere, almost from the gates, a lot of soot-dust blackened the area. "Now that would be a good place to dump them and leave!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he stepped through the gate and just then saw, close to the gate, a gutter fixed against the wall (as is often done in such houses where there are many factory workers, craftsmen, and other types of laborers), and above the gutter, directly on the wall, was inscribed in chalk the usual acerbic notice in such cases: "Cart is prohibited here." That would be fortunate enough that no suspicion arose that he had come and stopped. "Everything could be tossed in somewhere here and leave!"  

Looking around again, he had already stuck his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right by the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire space was a span wide, he noticed a large unshaped stone, roughly one and a half pud in weight, resting directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear passersby scurrying about, which there was always a good number of; but no one could see him from the gates unless someone came in from the street, which, nevertheless, could very well happen, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grasped the top of it firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength and overturned the stone. Underneath the stone, a small hollow was formed; immediately he began to toss everything from his pockets into it.

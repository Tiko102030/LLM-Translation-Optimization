And where did these feelings come from? On the contrary, now, if by some chance the room were suddenly filled not with his quarter friends, but with his very first and dearest friends, then, it seems, he would still not be able to address them with a single human word, so deeply had his heart suddenly emptied. A dark sense of suffering, endless loneliness and alienation suddenly consciously struck his soul. It was neither the baseness of his confessions to Ilyi Petrovich, nor the baseness and the captain's triumph over him that suddenly turned his heart upside down. Oh, what concern does it now matter to him about his own weakness, about all these ambitions, captains, Germans, grievances, offices and so forth, and so forth! Even if he were sentenced to be burned at that very moment, he would still not move, not even probably listen carefully to the sentence. Something entirely unfamiliar, new, abrupt, and unprecedented was happening to him. It was not that he understood it, but he clearly felt, with the full force of his perception, that he could no longer address these people, whether they were quarter officials or even if they were all his own relatives and siblings, but not the quarter captains—he would have had absolutely no need to address them, and not in any case of life; he had never before experienced such a strange and terrifying feeling. And what was most agonizing of all—the feeling was more of a feeling than a consciousness, than a notion; an immediate, agonizing feeling more intense than any he had ever experienced in his life. The scribe began to dictate to him the usual form of such a statement, namely, I can't pay, I promise then (sometime), I won't leave the city, I won't sell or give away my property, and so forth.

It was some especially disheveled man with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't learn anything from him because he doesn't care," thought Raskolnikov. "Go there, to the clerk," said the clerk, and pushed his finger forward, pointing to the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), a small and crowded one filled with people—not quite as well dressed as in those rooms. Between the visitors were two women. One, in mourning, poorly dressed, was sitting at the table opposite the clerk, writing something under his dictation. The other woman, very plump and ruddy red, with spots, a noticeable woman, and dressed extremely lavishly, with a brooch on her chest, as large as a tea saucer, stood aside waiting for something. Raskolnikov gave the clerk his summons. The clerk glanced at it briefly, said, "Wait," and continued working with the mourning lady. He breathed more freely.

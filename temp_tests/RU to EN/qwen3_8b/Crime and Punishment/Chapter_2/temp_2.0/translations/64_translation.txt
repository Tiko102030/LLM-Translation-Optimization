But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. At the Nikolayevsky Bridge, he had to fully regain consciousness due to a rather unpleasant incident for him. The coachman of one carriage whipped him hard on the back, for having narrowly missed being trampled by the horses, despite the coachman having shouted at him three or four times. The blow from the whip so enraged him that he leaped to the guardrails (it's unclear why he was walking in the very center of the bridge, where carriages drive, not pedestrians), and angrily gnashed his teeth. Of course, laughter was echoing around him. "And let them do their thing!"—some scoffing. "It's known that drunkards will deliberately run under wheels; you have to take responsibility for them."—That's their trade, honorable sir, that's their trade…[21 — That's their trade… — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about St. Petersburg poor folks who earned a living by deliberately running under carriage wheels to obtain assistance for injuries (see, for example, «Glas», 1865, № 65).] But at that moment, while he was standing by the guardrails, still senselessly and nastily watching the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone pushing money into his hands.

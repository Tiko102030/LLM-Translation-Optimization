But at that very moment, as he stood by the railings, still blindly and spitefully staring after the retreating carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone pushing money into his hands. He looked around: an elderly lady in headgear and goatskin boots, with her daughter in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, sir, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. Two kopecks. By their clothing and appearance, they could very easily have mistaken him for a beggar, for a real coin-collector on the streets; and the offering of an entire two kopecks, he probably should have expected a whip-swing to chastise them, but instead they were mercifully lenient. He clenched the two kopecks in his hand, walked ten paces, and turned his face towards the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is extremely rare on the Neva. The dome of the church, which could not be best seen from any point except from here looking out from the bridge, twenty paces away from the chapel, glowed bright enough to be distinctly seen, as if through clear air one could clearly discern even each of its ornaments. The pain from the whip had quieted down, and Raskolnikov had already forgotten about the blow; but now a restless, yet not quite clear thought occupied him wholly.

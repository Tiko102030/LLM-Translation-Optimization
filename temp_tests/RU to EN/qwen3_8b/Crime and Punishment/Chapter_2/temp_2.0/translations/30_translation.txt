—Ilya Petrovich!— began the scribe solicitously, but stopped to wait, since the enraged lieutenant couldn't be restrained except by holding his hands, something he knew from his own experience. As for the tall lady, at first she trembled violently at the thunder and lightning; but strangely enough: the more furious the outbursts, the more amiable her expression became, and the more enchanting her smile turned toward the furious lieutenant. She marched in place and kept bowing continuously, eagerly waiting for her moment to speak up and finally did. —There won't be any noise or fights here, Captain, — she suddenly burst out, as if scattering peas, with a strong German accent, though speaking fluent Russian— and there won't be any scandal at all, they came drunk, and I'll tell you everything, Captain, and I'm not to blame… I have a noble house, Captain, and noble manners, Captain, and I always, always wanted no scandal. They came completely drunk and then asked for a lute three times, and then one raised his legs and started playing the fortepiano with his foot, and that's very bad in a noble house, and he broke the fortepiano completely, and there's absolutely no manners here, and I told him. He took a lute and started pushing everyone from behind with it. And then I quickly called the butler and Karl came, he took Karl and struck him in the face, and Henrietta also got struck in the face, and I got slapped five times on the cheek. And that's very indecent in a noble house, Captain, and I shouted. He opened the window on the canal and started shouting from the window, like a little pig, and that's a disgrace.

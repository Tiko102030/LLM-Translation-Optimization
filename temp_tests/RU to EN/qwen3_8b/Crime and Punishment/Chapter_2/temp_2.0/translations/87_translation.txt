— Some tea? — Very well, some tea. — Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit down at the table. He ordered it immediately, poured it, then poured another cup, tossed his breakfast aside, and sat again on the sofa. He wrapped his left hand around the patient's head, lifted him up, and began feeding him tea with a spoon, blowing continuously and particularly diligently upon the spoon, as though the very essence of the recovery lay in this act of blowing. Rakholnik stayed silent and did not resist, despite feeling within himself more than enough strength to rise up and sit on the sofa without any help, and not only that, but even to hold the spoon or cup, and perhaps even to walk. But on account of some strange, almost beast-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a while, to hide himself, to pretend if necessary, perhaps even a little before he fully comprehended, while in reality listening and finding out what was going on? Nevertheless, he could not overcome his revulsion: after sipping ten spoonsful of tea, he suddenly freed his head, disdainfully pushed the spoon away and collapsed again on the pillow. Under his head, there were now indeed real pillows—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this, too, and took it into consideration.

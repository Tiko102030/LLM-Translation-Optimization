—"All these sensitive details, my good sir, are none of our concern," Ilia Petrovich cut off haughtily, "you must give your statement and obligation; as for what you happened to be in love and all these tragic places, it has nothing to do with us." —"Well, you... are harsh," muttered Nicodemus Fomich, sitting down at the table and also beginning to sign. Some inexplicable shame had come over him. —"Write down," said the clerk to Raskolnikov. —"What is there to write?" asked the latter unusually curtly. —"I'll dictate to you," said the clerk. Raskolnikov felt as though the clerk had grown more careless and contemptuous toward him since his confession; yet strangely enough, he suddenly felt utterly unconcerned about what any opinion anyone else might hold, and this change had occurred in an instant, in one moment. If he had wanted to think a little, he would certainly have been amazed at how he could talk like that to them a minute ago, and even thrust his feelings upon them. And where did these feelings come from?

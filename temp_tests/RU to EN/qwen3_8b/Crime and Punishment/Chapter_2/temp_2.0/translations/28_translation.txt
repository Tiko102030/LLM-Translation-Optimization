The clerk looked at him with a compassionate, sorrowful smile, mixed with some triumph, as if examining a new recruit who had just begun being fired upon: "So, what's it like now, how do you feel?" But what concern was it now to him about loan paperwork, about collections! Did it even deserve any worry, any attention at all? He stood, read, listened, answered, even asked questions on his own, but all of it was mechanical. It was the triumph of self-preservation, salvation from the crushing danger—this filled his entire being at that moment, without anticipation, without analysis, without future uncertainties and riddles, without doubts and without questions. This was the moment of total, direct, purely animal happiness. But precisely at this moment something akin to thunder and lightning happened in the office. The captain, still shaken by the lack of respect, blazing with passion, evidently determined to support the injured pride, threw himself at the unfortunate "puffy lady," who had been looking at him since he entered with the most stupidly glib smile. "So, you, such-and-such and so- and-so," he suddenly shouted at the top of his lungs (the mourning lady had already left), "what's been happening to you during the past night?"

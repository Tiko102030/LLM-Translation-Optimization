The pain from the lashes had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; now only a restless and rather unclear thought occupied his mind entirely. He stood for a long time, gazing intently into the distance; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to university, it was often—the most often when returning home—that he would perhaps stop here a hundred times, stare intently at this really splendid panorama, and each time almost惊奇 at a single obscure and insoluble impression of his. An inexplicable coldness always came from this splendid panorama; this lush picture was full of a silent and heavy spirit… Each time he marveled at his own gloomy and mysterious impression and postponed solving it, distrusting himself, to the future. Suddenly, he sharply remembered these previous questions and puzzlements of his, and it seemed to him that he had not unexpectedly recalled them now. It seemed strange and wonderful to him that he had stopped at the same spot again as before, as if he really imagined he could now think about the same things as before, and be interested in the same topics and pictures as before—just as recently. Even almost foolishly it seemed to him at the same time, and a painful feeling tightened his chest. In some depth, below, somewhere slightly visible underfoot, he now felt all this former past, these previous thoughts, these previous tasks, these previous themes, these previous impressions, and this entire panorama, and himself, and everything, everything… It seemed to him that he was flying somewhere upward and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making an involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a two-kopeck coin clenched in his fist. He relaxed his hand, glanced intently at the coin, waved his arm, and threw it into the water; then turned around and went home. It seemed to him that he had somehow cut himself off from everything and everyone at this moment with scissors.

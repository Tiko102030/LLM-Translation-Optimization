The plump lady bounced up from her seat at the sight of him, and with some special delight began to sit down again; but the officer paid her not the slightest attention, and she no longer dared sit in his presence. He was a corporal, assistant quartermaster, with reddish sideburns curling horizontally in both directions and an extraordinarily small face, nothing particularly remarkable except for some cheekiness. He glared at Raskolnikov, sideways and partly out of disapproval: his dress was too ragged, and despite all his lowering himself, his posture was still not suitable for such clothing. Raskolnikov, by his inconsiderate attitude, looked at him too straight and too long, so that the officer even got offended. "What's the matter with you?" he shouted, probably startled that such a ragged person didn't retreat at his lightning glance. "I was summoned… by order…", answered Raskolnikov in an awkward manner. "It's about the case of recovering money from them, from the student," hurried on the clerk, detaching himself from the paper. "Ah-ha!" he said, and passed Raskolnikov the notebook, pointing to a place in it,—"Read! 'Money?'"

The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a restless and somewhat unclear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and gazed into the distance for a long time; this place was particularly familiar to him. When he went to the university, it usually happened—most often, when returning home—that he would stop precisely at this same spot, look intently at this truly magnificent panorama, and each time almost marvel at a single indistinct and unsolvable impression of his own. An inexplicable cold always seemed to come from this magnificent panorama; this rich picture was full of a silent and heavy spirit… He marveled each time at his gloomy and mysterious impression and postponed solving it, not trusting himself, to the future. Now suddenly, sharply, he recalled these former questions and uncertainties of his, and it seemed to him that he had not accidentally recalled them now. It seemed strange and strange to him that he had stopped at the same spot again, as before, as though he had indeed imagined that he could think about the same thing now, as before, and be interested in the same themes and pictures as before… just so recently. It almost seemed funny to him at the same time, and yet it pressed his chest painfully. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible under his feet, it seemed to him that all this former past, these former thoughts, these former tasks, these former themes, these former impressions, and this entire panorama, and himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as though he was flying away somewhere upward and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double-rouble coin clenched in his fist. He unclenched his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung his arm, and threw it into the water; then he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as though he had somehow cut himself off from everything and everyone in this moment with scissors.

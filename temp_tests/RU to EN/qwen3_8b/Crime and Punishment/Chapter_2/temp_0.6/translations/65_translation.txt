But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully looked after the receding carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt that someone was shoving money into his hands. He looked: an old woman, with a headpiece and goat-skin shoes, and with her a girl in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they walked past. A two-grivna coin. By her dress and appearance, they could very well have taken him for a beggar, a real collector of alms on the street, and in giving him an entire two-grivna coin, he probably deserved a whip, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two-grivna coin in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is so rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be outlined better from any point than from here, from the bridge, not twenty steps away from the chapel, shone brightly, and through the clear air, one could clearly see even each of its decorations. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the strike; one restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to once again fully come to his senses due to a very unpleasant incident. The coachman of a carriage whipped him hard on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge where horses go, not pedestrians), and he gnashed his teeth maliciously. Laughter echoed around him. "To business!" someone said. "Somehow someone's going to get burned." "It's known, drunkards will deliberately run under wheels; but you'll have to answer for them." "That's their trade, sir, that's their trade…" [21 – "That's their trade…" – In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg beggars who deliberately ran under carriages in order to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still mindlessly and spitefully watching the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.

—"A cup of tea?" — "Very well, a cup of tea." — "Pour it." "Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table." He immediately ordered the tea to be poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat down again on the sofa. He still held the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began to feed him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the very act of blowing were the main and most healing part of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite capable of lifting himself up and sitting on the sofa without any help, and not only of using his hands sufficiently to hold the spoon or cup, but even, perhaps, of walking. But for some strange, almost animal-like trickery, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a while, hide himself, pretend, if necessary, even though he didn't quite understand yet, and at the same time listen and find out what was going on? Nevertheless, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, petulantly pushed away the spoon, and fell back onto the pillow. Under his head, indeed, there were now real pillows—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.

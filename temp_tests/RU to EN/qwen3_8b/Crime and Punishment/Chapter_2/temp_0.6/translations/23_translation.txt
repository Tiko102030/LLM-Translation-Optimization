The plump lady sprang up from her seat at the sight of him, and with some peculiar enthusiasm began to sit down again; but the officer paid her not the slightest attention, and she no longer dared sit down in his presence. He was a sublieutenant, assistant to the quarter-master, with red-gold whiskers sprouting horizontally in both directions and with exceedingly small facial features, expressing nothing in particular except some audacity. He glanced at Raskolnikov sideways and partly with indignation: his attire was too shabby, and despite all his humility, his posture still betrayed his clothes. Raskolnikov, by his inconsiderate look, had stared at him too directly and for too long, so that the officer even felt offended. "What do you want?" he shouted, probably surprised that such a ragged fellow didn't flinch from his lightning glance. "He was summoned… by order…," Raskolnikov replied in a mumbled fashion. "It's about the case of collecting money from them, from the student," hastened the clerk, detaching himself from the papers. "Here you are!" and he tossed Raskolnikov the notebook, indicating the place in it,—"Read! 'Money?'

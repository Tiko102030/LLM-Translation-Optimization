He was some particularly disheveled person with a fixed idea in his gaze. "You won't learn anything from this, because it's all the same to him," Raskolnikov thought. "Go there to the clerk," said the scribe, pushing forward with his finger to indicate the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), cramped and crowded with people—crowds, somewhat better dressed than in those rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One in mourning, poorly dressed, sat at the table opposite the clerk and was writing under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and flushed red, with spots, a noticeable woman, and dressed unusually flamboyantly, with a brooch on her chest the size of a tea plate, stood aside and waited for something. Raskolnikov handed his summons to the clerk. The clerk glanced at it, said, "Wait," and continued with the mourning lady. He breathed more freely.

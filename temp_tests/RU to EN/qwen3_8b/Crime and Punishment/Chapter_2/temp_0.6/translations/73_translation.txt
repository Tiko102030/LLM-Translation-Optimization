He remembered only that he had taken a sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came amnesia. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of amnesia throughout his illness; it was a feverish condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Much later he recalled many things. Sometimes it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him, wanting to take him away somewhere, and there was much arguing and quarreling about him. At other times, suddenly, he found himself alone in the room, everyone had left, and they feared him, opening the door only occasionally, just a little, to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves about something, laughing and taunting him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he distinguished also another person, very evidently familiar to him, but who exactly—he could not guess, and he longed for this, even weeping. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had already been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something he could not forget—tormented, suffering, recalling, groaning, falling into a frenzy or an unbearable, intolerable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but someone always stopped him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and amnesia.

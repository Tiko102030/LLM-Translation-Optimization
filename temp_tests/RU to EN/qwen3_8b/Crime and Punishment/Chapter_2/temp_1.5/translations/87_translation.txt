— A cup of tea? — Well, a cup of tea then. — Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit down at the table. He immediately took charge, poured it, then poured another cup, tossed his breakfast aside, and sat back down on the couch. He continued to support the sick man's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and started feeding him with a teaspoon, continuously and particularly diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the very act of blowing were the main and saving part of the recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite enough strength within himself to lift up and sit on the couch without any assistance, and not only to manage his hands sufficiently to hold the spoon or cup, but even possibly to walk. Yet, for some strange, almost beast-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a time, to hide, to feign, if necessary, even a half-understood state, while at the same time listening and finding out what was happening here? In any case, he couldn't overcome his aversion: having sipped ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, petulantly pushed the spoon aside, and collapsed again onto the pillow. Indeed, under his head were now real pillows—feather ones with clean pillowcases; he noticed this as well and took it into consideration.

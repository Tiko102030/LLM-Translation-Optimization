But at that moment, as he stood by the balustrade and still senselessly and angrily looked after the retreating carriage, rubbing his back, suddenly he felt that someone was slipping money into his hands. He looked: an elderly woman, in a headpiece and goatskin boots, and with her a girl in a hat with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. Two kopecks. By his dress and appearance, they could easily have taken him for a beggar, a real collector of alms on the street, and perhaps for the whole two kopecks he was due a whipping, which moved them to compassion. He clutched the two-kopeck coin in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is so rarely the case on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be viewed better from any point than from here, from the bridge, twenty steps short of the chapel, shone so brilliantly that through the clear air, one could distinctly see even each of its decorations. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov forgot about the strike; a single restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

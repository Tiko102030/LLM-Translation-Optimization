— Ilyà Petrovich! — began the scribe solicitously, but stopped to wait, since there was no other way to restrain the boiling corporal than by holding his hands, which he knew from his own experience. As for the stout lady, at first she trembled from thunder and lightning; but strange to say: the more numerous and fierce the curses, the more agreeable her countenance became, and her smile grew ever more enchanting, directed toward the furious corporal. She stomped in place and continuously knelt, impatiently waiting for her turn to speak, and finally got it. — There was no noise or fight at all, Captain Sir, — she suddenly chattered, as if popping peas, with a strong German accent, although briskly in Russian, — and no scandal whatsoever, they came drunk, and I'll tell you everything, Captain Sir, and I'm not to blame… I have a noble house, Captain Sir, and noble manners, Captain Sir, and I always, always wanted no scandal. They came entirely drunk, and then asked for three more rounds, and then one raised his legs and began to play the fortepiano with his feet, and that's entirely unbecoming in a noble house, and he completely broke the fortepiano, and there's absolutely no affectation here, and I said so. He took a round and started pushing everyone from behind with the round. And then I quickly called the servant and Karl came, he took Karl and hit him in the eyes, and Henrietta also had her eyes hit, and me five times on the cheek. And that's very unbecoming in a noble house, Captain Sir, and I shouted. He opened the window of the ditch and started to yell from the window, like a little pig, and that's a disgrace.

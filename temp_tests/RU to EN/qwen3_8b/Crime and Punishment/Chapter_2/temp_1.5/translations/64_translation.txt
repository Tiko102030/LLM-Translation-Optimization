But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nicholas Bridge, he had to fully regain consciousness again due to a rather unpleasant incident. The carriage driver of one of the sleighs whips him hard on the back for almost hitting him with the horse, despite the driver having shouted at him several times. The whip strike so enraged him that he jumped to the balustrade (it was unclear why he had been walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages drive, not pedestrians), and growled angrily, clicking his teeth. Laughter was heard all around. — "To business!" — someone shouted. "Somehow someone will be burned." — "It's well known that drunkards will intentionally run under the wheels; you'll have to answer for that." — "That's their business, sir, that's their business…" [21 — "That's their business…" — In newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg's poor who deliberately ran under carriage wheels to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the balustrade, still senselessly and wrathfully watching the receding sleigh, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping some money into his hands.

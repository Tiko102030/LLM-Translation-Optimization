— Well, she is my mistress, isn't she? — Well, then what about it? The clerk looked at him with a condescending smile of pity, yet also a touch of triumph, like a novice just beginning to be fired upon: "So, what's it like now? How do you feel?" But what business of his was it now with borrowed writing, with the claim! Did it even warrant the slightest worry, let alone the faintest attention! He stood, read, listened, answered, even asked questions, but all of this was mechanical. Triumph in self-preservation, salvation from the pressing danger—this filled his entire being at that moment, without forethought, without analysis, without future puzzles or revelations, without doubts or questions. It was a moment of complete, immediate, purely animal joy. But precisely at that moment, in the office, something like thunder and lightning occurred. The lieutenant, still shaken by the indignity, burning with resentment, obviously eager to support the wounded ambition, pounced on the unfortunate "wreath-laden lady," who had been looking at him since he entered with the most foolishly smug smile. — So you, with your airs and graces, — he suddenly yelled at the top of his lungs (the wreath-laden lady had already left), — what happened last night?

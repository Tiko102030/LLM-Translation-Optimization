He remembered only that he had taken a sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup on his chest. Then there came amnesia. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of amnesia throughout the illness: it was a feverish condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Later, he remembered many things. It seemed to him that people were gathering around him, wanting to take him away somewhere, and there was much arguing and fighting about him. At other times he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone having left and fearing him, opening the door occasionally to peer at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves, laughing, and taunting him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he distinguished another person, very much like someone he knew, but he could not figure out who it was, and he longed for this, even crying. At times it seemed to him that he had been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about that he had completely forgotten; instead, every minute he remembered that he had forgotten something that could not be forgotten—tormented, suffering, recalling, moaning, sinking into rages or into a terrifying, unbearable fear. At such times he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but always someone forced him to stay, and he would again fall into helplessness and amnesia.

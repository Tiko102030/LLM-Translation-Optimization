It was some particularly disheveled person with an unmoving idea in his gaze. "You won't learn anything from this, because he doesn't care," thought Raskolnikov. "Go there, to the clerk," said the scribe, and with a finger pushed forward, pointing to the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), narrow and densely packed with people—somewhat more neatly dressed than in those rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One, in mourning dress, poorly dressed, was seated at the desk facing the clerk, writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and ruddy, with spots, a noticeable woman, dressed unusually elaborately, with a brooch on her chest as large as a tea saucer, stood in the corner, waiting for something. Raskolnikov handed his summons to the clerk. The clerk glanced at it briefly, said, "Wait," and continued working with the mourning lady. He breathed easier.

Further, in the recess of the yard, a corner of a low, soot-stained, stone shack peeked out behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, presumably, was some kind of establishment, a cart shop or blacksmith's, or something of the sort; everywhere, almost from the very gates, a lot of sooty dust was darkened. "That would be a place to throw something and escape!"—it suddenly occurred to him. Not noticing anyone in the yard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, right near the gates, a gutter fixed against the fence (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, craftsmen, carriage drivers, and the like), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was inscribed in chalk the usual witty sign in such cases: "Here, carts are prohibited." Therefore, it was already good that there was no suspicion he had entered and stopped. "All at once, throw everything into a heap somewhere and escape!"

After looking around again, he had already put his hand in his pocket, when suddenly, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was a span wide, he noticed a large uncarved stone, roughly about a pood and a half in weight, resting directly against the stone city wall. Behind this wall was the street and the sidewalk; one could hear pedestrians shuffling around, which here were always quite a few; but no one could see him from the gates, unless someone came from the street, which, however, could very well happen, so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, gripped its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small depression formed under the stone; he immediately started throwing everything from his pockets into it.

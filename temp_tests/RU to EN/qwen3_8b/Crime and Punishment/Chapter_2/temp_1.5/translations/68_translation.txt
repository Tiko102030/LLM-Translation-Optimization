And there, to the greatest surprise, he suddenly caught the sound of his mistress's voice. She was howling, shrieking, and wailing, rushing, hastening, pouring out words so fast that it was impossible to make out what she was saying—she was undoubtedly pleading, of course, for them to stop hitting her, because they were brutally beating her on the stairs. The voice of the attacker had become so terrifying from wrath and fury that it was reduced to a mere rasp, yet the attacker himself was also speaking something, incoherently, hurrying, stumbling over words. Suddenly Rakolyakov trembled like a leaf—he recognized this voice; it was Ilya Petrovich! Ilya Petrovich was here, hitting his mistress! He was hitting her with his feet, knocking her head against the steps—this was clear, audible by the sounds, the screams, the blows! What was this? Had the world turned upside down? One could hear how crowds were gathering on all the floors, along the entire staircase; voices, exclamations, footsteps, doors slamming, people converging. “But why, why, and how could this be!”—he repeated, seriously thinking he had gone mad. But no, he was too clearly hearing it...

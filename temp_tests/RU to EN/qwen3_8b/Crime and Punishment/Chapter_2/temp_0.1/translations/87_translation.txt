—"A cup of tea?" — "A cup of tea, perhaps." — "Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table." He immediately took charge, poured the tea, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back down on the sofa. He continued to hold the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began feeding him with a teaspoon, pouring tea continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the entire main and saving point of recovery lay in this act of blowing. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite strong enough to lift himself up and sit on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to control his hands sufficiently to hold the spoon or cup, but even perhaps to walk. Yet, for some strange, almost beast-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a while, to hide, to pretend, if necessary, even though he didn't quite understand yet, but at the same time to listen and find out what was going on. In any case, he could not overcome his aversion: after drinking ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, pushed the spoon away capriciously, and collapsed back onto the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.

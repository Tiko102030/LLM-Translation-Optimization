But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a very unpleasant incident for him. The coachman of a carriage whipped him hard on the back for almost running over the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The blow from the whip so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it's unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), and he maliciously ground and clamped his teeth. Laughter echoed around him, of course. — And let's get to work! — Some kind of fire. — It's known that drunkards will deliberately run under the wheels; and you have to take responsibility for them. — That's their trade, sir, that's their trade…[21 — That's their trade… — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg beggars who made a trade of deliberately running under carriages to receive assistance for their injuries (see, for example, «Golos», 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and maliciously watching the retreating carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.

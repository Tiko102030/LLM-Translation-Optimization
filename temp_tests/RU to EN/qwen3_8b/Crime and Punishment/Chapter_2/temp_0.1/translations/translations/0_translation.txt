The core of "Crime and Punishment" is the psychological history of the crime and its moral consequences. However, the main character, Rodion Raskolnikov, is an unusual criminal. His crime—the murder of the usurer Alena Ivanovna—he commits under the influence of the system of ideas he created and suffered through, viewing this crime as a unique social-psychological experiment intended to confirm not only in his own eyes but also in the eyes of others the correctness of his theoretical conclusions. Therefore, the psychological analysis of the criminal's state before and after the murder is inseparably intertwined in the novel with the analysis of Raskolnikov's philosophical theory, which Dostoevsky presents as "the sign of the times," expressing the ideological and moral turmoil characteristic of a significant number of young people in his era—above all, from the middle-class, democratic milieu. Raskolnikov is a gifted, intelligent, and honest youth, forced to leave his studies due to lack of funds. His mother, the widow of a provincial official, lives on a modest pension after her husband's death, sending most of it to her son. Raskolnikov's sister, Dunya, was forced to become a governess in the household of wealthy landowners in order to help her mother and brother, where she suffers humiliation and mistreatment. Raskolnikov lives in a cramped room, resembling a coffin, on the fifth floor of a multi-story Petersburg house near the Sennaya Square, constantly observing the lives of the poor and the merchant population of Petersburg. He painfully realizes that not only he himself, but thousands of others are inevitably doomed to an early death, poverty, and lack of rights under the existing order—and this gives rise to a constant, deep mental struggle striving to find a way out of the unjust situation. At the same time, Raskolnikov is painfully proud, unsociable, and full of awareness of his own exceptionalism.

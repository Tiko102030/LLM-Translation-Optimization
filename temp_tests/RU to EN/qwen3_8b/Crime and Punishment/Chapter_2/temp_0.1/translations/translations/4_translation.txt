From the lips of the unhappy and beloved girl, Raskolnikov, to his own surprise, hears words at first incomprehensible to him, yet irresistibly true in their inner truth, about how the morality of "extraordinary people," who had previously impressed him so much, calmly building their calculations on the blood of others without remorse, in the end is no different from the inhuman morality openly proclaimed and daily practiced by the ruling classes, who have elevated violence and crime to a daily, "normal" law of life. In Raskolnikov's eyes, the true bearers of beauty and morality are now not those who place themselves above ordinary people, but rather those, like Sonya, who amidst hunger and humiliation retain in their souls faith in life and humanity, moral steadfastness and unshakable resolve, deep aversion to violence, evil, and crime in any form.

But at the same time, Raskolnikov is painfully proud, uncommunicative, and fully conscious of his own exceptionalness. He is not used to the company of other people, avoids it, and shuns it. Having withdrawn from everyone, "like a turtle into its shell," the novel's hero tries to solve the questions that arise in his mind from the consciousness of the injustice of the surrounding social life, seeking answers on his own. Raskolnikov deeply suffers from hunger and poverty, from the humiliation of his mother and sister; he passionately wants to help them. However, his own sufferings and the sorrow of his loved ones are not the main cause of his torments and his crime. "If only I had killed him just because I was hungry... I would be happy now," he says after carrying out his terrible plan. The hero of "Crime and Punishment" does not act under the influence of an immediate, unconscious impulse; he is a thinking person, one accustomed to analyzing his impressions and actions. Reflecting on the causes of existing inequality and injustice, Raskolnikov comes to the conclusion that there has always been a distinction between two classes of people. While the vast majority of people have always and everywhere silently and obediently submitted to the established order of things, lacking the strength to rise against it, from time to time in the history of humanity, a few "extraordinary" people have appeared—Lycurgus, Muhammad, Napoleon—who were its true driving forces. Destined by nature to be the "masters of fate," they courageously rose against the existing order, and in doing so, boldly violated generally accepted moral norms, not hesitating before violence and crime in order to impose their will upon humanity.

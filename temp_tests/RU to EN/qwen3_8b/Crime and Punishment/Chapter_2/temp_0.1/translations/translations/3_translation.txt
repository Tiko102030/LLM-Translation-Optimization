Inequality between the strong and the weak, between predators and the oppressed, forming the foundation of class society and the state, remains for Raskolnikov in his reflections as an eternal model of any human community. Raskolnikov reasons abstractly, according to the principle: "Thus was — thus will be," and therefore his protest turns into its opposite. The contradiction between the few who have the right to dictate their will to others, even by means of violence and crime, and the majority of people deprived of elementary human rights and condemned to serve as passive objects of their actions is elevated by the hero of the novel into an immutable law of life established from time immemorial and not subject to repeal. This contradiction between Raskolnikov's deep and sincere protest against social oppression and inequality, and his own hero's assertion of the right of a single strong individual to build their life on the blood and bones of others constitutes the basis of the tragic conflict in "Crime and Punishment." Throughout the development of the novel, Dostoevsky forces the hero to personally experience that his rebellion against the existing inhumanity itself carries an inhuman character, leading not to rise and flourishing, but to suppression and moral annihilation of the individual — and the hero of the novel is gradually forced to feel this, first by the voice of his heart, to which his mind continues to resist, and then by his entire moral being. Raskolnikov manages to commit the planned murder. However, the tragic "experiment" carried out by the hero leads to a result different from what he expected. Through his own experience and through the examples of other people, subjects of his observations — Luzhin, Svidrigailov, Sonya Marmeladov — Raskolnikov step by step realizes that the morality of "extraordinary" people, which seemed to him at first as an arrogant uprising against the existing order of things, is beyond his capacity. And it is not a matter of Raskolnikov's weakness compared to Mohammed and Napoleon, as he initially imagined (they "could," but he could not!). From the lips of the unfortunate and beloved girl, Raskolnikov, to his surprise, hears words initially incomprehensible to him, but irresistible in their inner truth, about the fact that the morality of "extraordinary" people, calmly building their calculations on the blood of others without remorse, in the end is no different from the inhuman morality openly proclaimed and daily practiced by people from the ruling classes, who have elevated violence and crime into a daily, "normal" law of life.

But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully looked after the receding carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shove money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman who sells goods, in wooden clogs, and with her a girl in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, Father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. Two kopecks. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real beggar for alms on the street, and the act of giving him a whole two kopecks, he probably deserved a whip, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two kopecks in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is very rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be seen better from any point than from here, from the bridge, twenty steps away from the chapel, shone brilliantly, and through the clear air, one could clearly see even every ornament of it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the blow; now only a restless and somewhat unclear thought occupied him entirely.

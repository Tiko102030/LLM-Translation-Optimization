— Well, isn't she my mistress? — Well, so what if she's the mistress? The clerk looked at him with a condescending smile of pity, along with a touch of triumph, as if looking at a novice just beginning to be bombarded: "So, how do you feel now?" But what was there now for him to do with borrowed writing, with the penalty! Was it worth even the slightest worry, let alone any attention! He stood, read, listened, answered, even asked questions himself, but all this was mechanical. The triumph of self-preservation, salvation from the pressing danger—this filled his entire being at that moment, without foresight, without analysis, without future uncertainties and guesses, without doubts and without questions. It was a moment of complete, immediate, purely animal joy. But at that very moment, something like thunder and lightning occurred in the office. The lieutenant, still shaken by the disrespect, burning with anger, obviously wanting to support the wounded ambition, launched himself at the unfortunate "plump lady," who had been looking at him with the most foolish smile since he had entered. — So you, such and such a one, — he suddenly shouted at the top of his voice (the widow had already left), — what happened during the past night?

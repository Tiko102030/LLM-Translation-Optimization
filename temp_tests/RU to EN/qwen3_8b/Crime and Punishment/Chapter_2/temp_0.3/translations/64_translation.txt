But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to once again fully come to his senses due to a very unpleasant incident. The coachman of a carriage whipped him hard on the back for almost stepping on the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), maliciously gnashing and chattering his teeth. Laughter echoed around him. "Well, let's get to it!" someone said. "Some drunkard is going to deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to take responsibility for him." "That's their trade, esteemed sir, that's their trade..." [21 - "That's their trade..." — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about St. Petersburg beggars who deliberately ran under carriages to receive aid for their injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and maliciously watching the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.

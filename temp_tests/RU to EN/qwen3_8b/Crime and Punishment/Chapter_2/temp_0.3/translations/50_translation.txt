Further, in the recess of the courtyard, a low, soot-stained, stone shed peeped out from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, probably, was some sort of establishment, a carriage shop or a blacksmith's, or something of the kind; everywhere, almost from the very gates, thick with sooty dust. "That would be a good place to throw something and disappear!" he suddenly thought. Noticing no one in the courtyard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, carriage drivers, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark: "Here, a cart is forbidden to stop." That was good, then, that he had come in and stopped without arousing suspicion. "Everything could be thrown down here at once and then disappear!" 

Looking around once more, he had already stuck his hand into his pocket when, suddenly, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was about a sazhene wide, he noticed a large, rough-hewn stone, about one and a half pud in weight, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear pedestrians scurrying about, which there were always quite a few; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone came from the street, which, in fact, could very well happen, and so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, seized its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small depression formed under the stone; immediately he began throwing everything from his pockets into it.

This was some particularly disheveled person with an unshakable idea in his gaze. "You'll learn nothing from him, since he doesn't care," thought Raskolnikov. "Go there to the scribe," said the clerk, and with a finger he pushed forward, indicating the very last room. He entered this room (the fourth in order), a cramped and densely packed one—crowded with people, somewhat better dressed than in those rooms. Among the visitors were two ladies. One, in mourning, poorly dressed, was seated at the table opposite the scribe, writing something under his dictation. The other lady, very plump and ruddy, with freckles, a noticeable woman, and extremely ostentatiously dressed, with a brooch on her chest as large as a tea saucer, stood aside and waited for something. Raskolnikov handed his summons to the scribe. The scribe glanced at it briefly, said, "Wait," and continued working with the mourning lady. He breathed more freely.

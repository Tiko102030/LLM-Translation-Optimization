And where did these feelings come from? On the contrary, now, if the room were suddenly filled not with his quarter friends, but with his closest friends, then, it seems, he would still have no human word for them, his heart having suddenly emptied. A dark sense of tormenting, endless solitude and alienation suddenly consciously struck his soul. Not the baseness of his confessions to Ily Petrovich, nor the baseness and the poruchik's triumph over him suddenly turned his heart upside down. Oh, what business does he have now with his own baseness, with all these ambitions, poruchiks, Germans, grievances, offices, and so on and so forth! Even if he were sentenced to be burned at this very moment, he would still not move, and perhaps would not even listen attentively to the verdict. Something completely unfamiliar, new, sudden, and unprecedented was happening to him. Not that he understood it, but he clearly felt, with all the force of his feeling, that he could no longer address these people, whether in the quarter office or even if they were all his own brothers and sisters, and not quarter poruchiks; then, even so, he would have had absolutely no need to address them, and in no case in life; he had never before experienced such a strange and terrible sensation. And what was most tormenting of all—this was more a feeling than a consciousness, than a concept; a direct, most tormenting feeling among all the feelings he had ever experienced in his life. The clerk began dictating to him the standard form of a statement in such a case, that is, I cannot pay, I promise then (sometime), I will not leave the city, I will neither sell nor give away my property, and so on.

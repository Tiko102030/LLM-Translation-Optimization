—"A cup of tea?" — "Very well, a cup of tea." — "Pour it." "Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table." He immediately arranged this, poured the tea, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat down again on the sofa. He still wrapped his left hand around the patient's head, lifted him up, and began to feed him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the main and saving point of recovery lay in this act of blowing. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite strong enough to raise himself up and sit on the sofa without any help, and not only to hold the spoon or cup in his hands, but even perhaps to walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a while, to hide, to pretend, if necessary, even as if not yet fully understanding, while at the same time listening and finding out what was going on here? In any case, he did not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, pushed the spoon away petulantly, and fell back onto the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he also noticed this and took it into consideration.

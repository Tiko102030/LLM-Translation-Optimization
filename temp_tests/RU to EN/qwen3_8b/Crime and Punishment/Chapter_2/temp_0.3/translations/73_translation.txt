He remembered only how he had taken a sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came confusion. III

He was not entirely in a state of confusion throughout the illness: it was a feverish condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Later, he remembered much. At times, it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him and wanted to take him away somewhere, and there was much arguing and quarreling about him. At other times, he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone having left and fearing him, opening the door only occasionally to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves, laughing and teasing him. He often remembered Natasha beside him; he distinguished another person, very familiar to him, but could not figure out who it was, and longed for it, even weeping. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had already been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about that he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something he could not forget—tormented, suffering, recalling, groaning, falling into rage or an unbearable, dreadful fear. Then he would try to get up and run away, but someone would always stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and confusion.

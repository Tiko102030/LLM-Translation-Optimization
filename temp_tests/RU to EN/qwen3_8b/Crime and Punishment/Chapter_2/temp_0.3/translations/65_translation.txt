But at that moment, as he stood by the balustrades and still senselessly and angrily looked after the retreating carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone tucking money into his hands. He looked: an old woman in a headscarf and goat-skin boots, and with her a girl in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for Christ's sake." He took it, and they walked past. Two kopecks. By his dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real collector of alms on the street, and probably he was due a whip for receiving the whole two kopecks, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two kopecks in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is so rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which can be seen best from this spot, from the bridge, twenty steps away from the chapel, gleamed, and through the clear air, one could clearly see even every ornament. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the strike; a single restless and not entirely clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

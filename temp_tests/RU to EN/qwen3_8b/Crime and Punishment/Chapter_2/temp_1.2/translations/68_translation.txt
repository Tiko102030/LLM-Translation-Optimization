And to his greatest astonishment, he suddenly recognized the voice of his mistress. She was howling, screaming, and wailing, rushing, hurrying, spilling out words so fast that they couldn't be understood, pleading—obviously, to stop beating her, because she was being mercilessly beaten on the staircase. The voice of the attacker had become so terrifying from hatred and fury that it was barely a rasp, yet the attacker himself was also speaking incoherently, hurriedly, gasping and struggling to get words out. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf—he recognized this voice; it was Il'yas Petrovich's voice. Il'yas Petrovich was here, beating his mistress! He was kicking her, hitting her head against the steps—this was clearly audible, by the sounds, the screams, and the blows! What was this? Had the world turned upside down? One could hear people gathering on all the floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, footsteps, doors slamming, people gathering. "But for what, for what, and how could this be possible!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking that he had gone mad. But no, he heard it too clearly...

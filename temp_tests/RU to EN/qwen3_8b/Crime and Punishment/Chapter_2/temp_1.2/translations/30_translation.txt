—Ilya Petrovich!— began the clerk, solicitously, but stopped to wait a moment, since there was no way to restrain the boiling corporal except by holding his hands, something he knew from his own experience. As for the plump lady, at first she trembled from the thunder and lightning; but strange thing: the more numerous and stronger the curses became, the more amiable her expression grew, and the more charming her smile became, directed at the furious corporal. She shuffled in place, continuously knelt down, eagerly waiting for her turn to speak, and finally did. —There's no shouting or fighting here, sir captain— she suddenly chattered, as if spilling peas, with a strong German accent, though speaking Russian briskly— and no, no scandal at all, they came drunk, and I'll tell you everything, sir captain, and I'm not to blame… I have a noble house, sir captain, and noble manners, sir captain, and I always, always wanted no scandal. They came completely drunk, and then asked for three bottles again, and then one lifted his legs and started to play the fortepiano with his foot, and that's very bad in a noble house, and he broke the fortepiano, and there's completely, completely no manner here, and I told him. He took a bottle and started to push everyone from behind with it. And then I quickly called the servant and Karl came, he took Karl and hit him in the eye, and Henriette also had her eye hit, and me five times on the cheek. And that's very indecent in a noble house, sir captain, and I shouted. He opened the window to the gutter and started to scream from the window, like a little pig, and that's a disgrace.

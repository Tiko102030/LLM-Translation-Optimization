The plump lady sprang up from her place upon seeing him, and with some peculiar excitement began to sit down again; but the officer paid her no slightest attention, and she no longer dared sit in his presence. He was a poruchik, an assistant quartermaster, with reddish whiskers sticking out horizontally on both sides, and with extraordinarily small features, nothing in fact except some impudence. He looked at Raskolnikov sidelong and partly with disdain: his clothes were too shabby, and despite his lowered posture, his bearing was still not quite right. Raskolnikov, through inconsiderateness, looked at him too straight and too long, so that even he was offended. "What do you want?" he shouted, probably surprised that such a ragged fellow didn't hide from his lightning glance. "I was called… by a summons…" Raskolnikov replied in a vague manner. "It's about a case of collecting money from them, from the student," hastened the clerk, breaking away from the papers. "Ah-ha!" he said, and tossed Raskolnikov the notebook, indicating the place in it,—"Read! 'Money?'"

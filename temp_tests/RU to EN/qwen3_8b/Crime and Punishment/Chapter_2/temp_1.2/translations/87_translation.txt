—"A cup of tea?" — "A cup of tea, then." — "Pour it." "Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit at the table." He immediately ordered, poured the tea, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat down again on the sofa. He still wrapped his left hand around the patient's head, lifted him up, and began feeding him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the very act of blowing were the main and saving point of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, although he felt within himself quite enough strength to sit up and remain on the sofa without any outside assistance, and not only to control his hands enough to hold the spoon or cup, but even, perhaps, to walk. But for some strange, almost animal-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a while, to hide, to feign helplessness, if necessary, even though he did not fully understand yet, but at the same time to listen and find out what was happening here? Nevertheless, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, petulantly pushed the spoon away, and collapsed again on the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows—down-filled with clean pillowcases—under his head; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.

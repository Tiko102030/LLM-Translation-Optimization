But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to regain full awareness once again due to a very unpleasant incident for him. The coachman of one of the sleighs heavily whipped him on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped back to the railing (he didn't know why he was walking in the middle of the bridge where cars drive, not pedestrians), viciously grinding his teeth and clenching his jaws. Laughter was echoing around him. — And let's get to work! — Some drunkard. — It's well known that drunkards will deliberately run under the wheels; you have to take responsibility for them. — That's their trade, esteemed sir, that's their trade…[21 — That's their trade… — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg beggars who deliberately ran under the wheels of carriages in order to receive assistance for injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still mindlessly and angrily watched the departing sleigh, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.

But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and maliciously stared after the receding carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman who sold goods, in a headscarf and clumsy shoes, and with her a girl, in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they walked past. A double grivna. By their dress and appearance, they could very well have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street collector, and the offering of an entire double grivna probably meant he deserved a whip's stroke, which moved them to pity. He clutched the double grivna in his hand, walked ten steps, and then turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is very rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which can be seen best from no other point than from here, from the bridge, twenty paces away from the chapel, sparkled, and through the clear air, one could clearly discern even every single ornament on it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the blow; now only a restless and not entirely clear thought occupied him exclusively.

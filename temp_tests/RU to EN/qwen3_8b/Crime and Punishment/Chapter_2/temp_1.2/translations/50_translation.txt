Further, in the recess of the courtyard, protruded from behind the fence a low, soot-stained, stone shed, obviously part of some workshop. Here, probably, was some sort of establishment, a carriage-making or blacksmithing workshop, or something along those lines; everywhere, almost from the gates, the dust was blackened with soot. "That's a place to toss it and disappear!" he thought to himself. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he walked through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter fastened against the fence (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, craftsmen, coachmen, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark in such cases: "Here carts are forbidden to stop." That was good, there was no suspicion that he had come in and stopped. "Everything could be thrown here at once and then just disappear!" 

Looking around again, he had already shoved his hand into his pocket when, suddenly, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was an arshin wide, he noticed a large, uncut stone, about one and a half pud in weight, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and he could hear pedestrians bustling around, as there were always quite a few of them here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone came from the street, which, in fact, could very easily happen, so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grasped its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small depression formed beneath the stone; immediately he began throwing everything from his pockets into it.

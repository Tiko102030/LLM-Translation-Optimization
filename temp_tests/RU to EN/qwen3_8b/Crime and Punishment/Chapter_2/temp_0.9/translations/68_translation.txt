And here, to his greatest astonishment, he suddenly caught his mistress's voice. She was howling, screaming, and wailing, hurrying, rushing, spilling words so fast that one couldn't make out what she was pleading about—certainly about being stopped from being beaten, because she was being cruelly beaten on the stairs. The voice of the one beating her had become so terrible from fury and madness that it was only a rasp, yet still the one beating her was saying something, too, speaking quickly, incoherently, rushing, gasping. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf: he recognized this voice; it was Ilya Petrovich's voice. Ilya Petrovich is here and beating his mistress! He is beating her with his feet, knocking her head against the steps—this is clear, one can hear it by the sounds, the screams, the blows! What is this, has the world turned upside down, or what? One could hear how crowds were gathering on all floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, people coming up, knocking, doors slamming, gathering. "But for what, for what, and how can this be possible!" he repeated, seriously thinking that he was completely mad. But no, he was too clearly hearing it!

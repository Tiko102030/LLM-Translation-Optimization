The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a single restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked into the distance for a long and intense time; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to the university, he usually—most often, returning home—found himself stopping precisely at this same spot, gazing intently at this truly magnificent panorama and being almost surprised each time by a single unclear and unsolvable impression of his own. An inexplicable coldness always seemed to blow upon him from this magnificent panorama; this rich picture was full of a silent and deaf spirit… Each time he marveled at his own gloomy and enigmatic impression and postponed solving it, not trusting himself, to the future. Now suddenly, he sharply remembered these previous questions and doubts of his, and it seemed to him that he had not accidentally recalled them now. It seemed strange and strange to him that he had stopped at the same spot again, as before, as if indeed imagining that he might now think about the same things, and be interested in the same themes and pictures as before… just not so long ago. It almost seemed funny to him at the same time, and yet it pressed painfully on his chest. In some depth, below, somewhere just barely visible under his feet, it seemed to him now all this previous past, and previous thoughts, and previous tasks, and previous themes, and previous impressions, and this entire panorama, and himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as if he was flying away somewhere upwards, and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double-grivna coin tightly gripped in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung his arm, and threw it into the water; then turned around and went home. It seemed to him that he had somehow cut himself off from everyone and everything at that moment, as if with scissors.

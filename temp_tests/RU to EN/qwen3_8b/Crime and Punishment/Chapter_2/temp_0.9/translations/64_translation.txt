But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a very unpleasant incident. A coachman whipped him hard on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he jumped to the railing (it's unclear why he was walking in the middle of the bridge, where horses go, not pedestrians), growled fiercely, and gnashed his teeth. Laughter was heard all around. — "To work!" — someone said. — "Somehow they get burned. — It's known, drunkards will deliberately run under wheels; but you have to answer for them." — "That's their trade, sir, that's their trade…" [21 — "That's their trade…" — In newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg poor people who made a living by deliberately running under carriages to receive compensation for injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and angrily watching the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone pushing money into his hands.

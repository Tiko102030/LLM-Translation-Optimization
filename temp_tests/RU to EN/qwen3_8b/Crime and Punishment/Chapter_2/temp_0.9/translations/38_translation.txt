And where did these feelings come from? Quite the opposite, now, if suddenly the room had been filled not with his quarterly acquaintances, but with his closest friends, then, it seems, he would have found not a single human word for them, his heart having suddenly grown so empty. A dark sense of tormenting, endless loneliness and alienation suddenly consciously struck his soul. Not the baseness of his heart's confessions to Ilya Petrovich, nor the baseness and the captain's triumph over him had suddenly turned his heart upside down. Oh, what business now has he with his own baseness, with all these ambitions, captains, Germans, grievances, offices, and so on and so forth! Even if he had been sentenced to be burned at this very moment, he would not have moved then, perhaps even not listened attentively to the verdict. Something entirely unfamiliar, new, sudden, and unprecedented was happening to him. Not that he understood it, but he clearly felt, with all the force of his feeling, that he could no longer address these people, whether they were quarter's officers or even if they were all his own brothers and sisters, and not quarter's officers—then, even in that case, there would have been absolutely no need for him to address them, and never under any circumstances in life; he had never before experienced such a strange and terrible feeling. And what was most agonizing of all—this was more a feeling than a consciousness, than a concept; a direct, agonizing feeling, the most tormenting feeling of all he had ever experienced in his life. The clerk began dictating to him the standard form of such a statement, that is, I cannot pay, I promise then (whensoever), I will not leave the city, I will neither sell nor give away my property, and so on.

He remembered only that he had taken a sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came amnesia. III

He, however, was not entirely lost to amnesia throughout his illness; it was a feverish condition, with delirium and half-consciousness. Much later he recalled many things. At times it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him and wanted to take him away somewhere, and there was much argument and quarreling about him. At other times he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone had left, and they feared him; they opened the door only occasionally, slightly, to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves about something, laughing and teasing him. Natasha often appeared to him by his side; he distinguished also another person, very much like someone he knew, but he could not figure out who exactly and pined for this, even weeping. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about all of that he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something he could not forget—tormented, suffering, recalling, groaning, falling into frenzy or into a terrible, unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but always someone would stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and amnesia.

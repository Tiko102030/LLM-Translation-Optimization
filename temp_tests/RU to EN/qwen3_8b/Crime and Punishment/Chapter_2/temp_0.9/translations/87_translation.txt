—"A cup of tea?" — "A cup of tea, please." — "Pour it." "Wait, I'll pour it for you; sit at the table." He immediately took charge, poured, then poured another cup, discarded his breakfast, and sat back on the sofa. He continued to hold the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him, and began feeding him with a teaspoon, blowing continuously and especially diligently on the spoon, as if the main and saving point of recovery lay precisely in this act of blowing. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, even though he felt he had quite sufficient strength to sit up and remain on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to control his hands enough to hold the spoon or cup, but even possibly to walk. But due to some strange, almost feral cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a while, to hide, to pretend, if necessary, even as if not fully understanding, while at the same time listening and finding out what was happening here. However, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, capriciously pushed away the spoon, and collapsed again onto the pillow. Under his head, there were now real pillows—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into consideration.

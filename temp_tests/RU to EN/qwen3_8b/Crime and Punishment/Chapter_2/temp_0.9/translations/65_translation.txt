But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully looked after the retreating carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone tucking money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman in a headpiece and ox boots, and with her a girl in a hat and with a green parasol, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for Christ's sake." He took it, and they walked past. It was a two-kopeck coin. By his clothes and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real collector of alms on the street, and perhaps he was due a whip for receiving the whole two kopecks, which moved them to pity. He clutched the two-kopeck coin in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which is very rare on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be viewed better from any point than from here, from the bridge, twenty steps away from the chapel, gleamed brightly, and through the clear air, one could clearly see even each of its ornaments. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the strike; one restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively.

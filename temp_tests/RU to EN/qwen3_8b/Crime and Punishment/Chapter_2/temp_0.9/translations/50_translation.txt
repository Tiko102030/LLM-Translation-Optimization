Further, in the recess of the courtyard, a corner of a low, sooty, stone barn peeped out from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, surely, was some sort of establishment, a cartwright's or a blacksmith's, or something of the sort; everywhere, almost from the gates, soot dust was blackening the area. "What a place to throw something and escape!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter fastened against the fence (as is often arranged in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, coachmen, and the like), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark in such cases: "Here stands a cart prohibited." So that was good, there was no suspicion that he had come in and stopped. "Everything can be thrown down here at once and escape!" 

Looking around once more, he had already tucked his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right against the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was a cubit wide, he noticed a large, uncut stone, roughly weighing about one and a half pud, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and he could hear pedestrians scurrying about, of which there were always quite a few here; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone came from the street, which, however, could very well happen, so he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small depression formed under the stone; immediately he began throwing everything from his pockets into it.

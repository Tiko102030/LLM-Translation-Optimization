The plump lady sprang up from her seat upon seeing him, and with some peculiar excitement began to sit down; but the officer paid her not the slightest attention, and she no longer dared to sit down in his presence. He was a lieutenant, assistant to the quartermaster's overseer, with horizontally sticking-out red-gold whiskers on both sides and extremely small facial features, nothing, in fact, particularly notable except for a certain boldness. He looked at Raskolnikov slantwise and partly with indignation: his clothes were too shabby, and despite all his humility, his posture was still ill-suited to his attire; Raskolnikov, by carelessness, had looked at him too directly and for too long, so that the officer even got offended. "What do you want?" he shouted, probably surprised that such a raggedy person did not even think of hiding from his lightning-fast glance. "Required… by a summons…" Raskolnikov answered somewhat awkwardly. "This is about a debt collection case from them, from the student," hurried the clerk, detaching himself from the papers. "Here you are!"— and he tossed Raskolnikov the notebook, indicating the place in it,—"read it! 'Money?'

—"A cup of tea?" — "A cup of tea, please." — "Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit at the table." He immediately arranged, poured, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back on the sofa. He continued to encircle the patient's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began to feed him with a teaspoon, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the main and saving point of recovery lay in this blowing process. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite feeling quite enough strength to lift himself up and sit on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to control his hands enough to hold the spoon or cup, but even perhaps to walk. But due to some strange, almost beast-like cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to hide his strength for a while, to conceal himself, to pretend, if necessary, even as if not yet fully understanding, while at the same time listening and finding out what was happening here? Nevertheless, he could not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, petulantly pushed away the spoon, and collapsed again onto the pillow. Under his head, there were indeed real pillows—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he also noticed this and took it into consideration.

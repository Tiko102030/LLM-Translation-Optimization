Further, in the recess of the courtyard, a low, soot-stained, stone shed peeped out from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, probably, was some sort of establishment, a carriage workshop or a blacksmith's, or something of the sort; everywhere, almost from the very gates, there was a blackened layer of soot dust. "That would be a fine place to throw something and disappear!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter fitted against the fence (as is often the case in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, coachmen, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark in such cases: "Here stands the carriage prohibited." So that was good, there was no suspicion that he had come in and stopped. "Everything could be thrown down here at once and then disappear!" 

Looking around once more, he had already shoved his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right against the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was a cubit wide, he noticed a large, rough-hewn stone, roughly perhaps a peck and a half in weight, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear passersby scurrying about, which there were always plenty of; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone came from the street, which, however, could very well happen, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grasped its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small hollow formed under the stone; immediately he began throwing everything from his pockets into it.

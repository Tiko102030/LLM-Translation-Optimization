And lo and behold, to his greatest astonishment, he suddenly recognized the voice of his landlady. She was howling, screaming, wailing, rushing, hastening, spewing out words so fast that they couldn't be understood—pleading, obviously, to stop beating her, because she was being mercilessly beaten on the stairs. The voice of the aggressor had become so terrifying from hatred and fury that it was only a rasp, yet the aggressor was also speaking, albeit incoherently, hurriedly, gasping, and stumbling over words. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf—he recognized this voice; it was Ilyi Petrovich. Ilyi Petrovich was here, beating his landlady! He was beating her with his feet, striking her head against the steps—this is clear, one can hear it by the sounds, the screams, the blows! What is this? Has the world turned upside down? One could hear how crowds were gathering on all the floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, doors slamming, footsteps, and people converging. "But why, why, and how could this be!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking that he had completely lost his mind. But no, he was hearing it too clearly...

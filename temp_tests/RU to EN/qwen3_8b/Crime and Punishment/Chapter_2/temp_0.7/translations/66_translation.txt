The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood there, gazing into the distance for a long time, intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to the university, it usually happened—more often than not, when returning home—that he would stop exactly at this same spot, stare intently at this truly magnificent panorama, and each time almost wonder at a single obscure and unsolvable impression of his own. An inexplicable coldness always seemed to emanate from this magnificent panorama; this rich picture was full of a silent and deaf spirit… Each time he marveled at his own gloomy and enigmatic impression, postponing the resolution of it, distrusting himself, to the future. Now suddenly, sharply, he recalled these previous questions and bewilderments of his, and it seemed to him that he hadn't accidentally recalled them now. It seemed strange and weird to him that he had stopped at the same spot again, just as before, as if he had truly imagined that he could think about the same things now as before, and be interested in the same old themes and pictures, as he had done… just so recently. It almost seemed funny to him at the same time, and yet it constricted his chest painfully. In some depth, down below, somewhere barely visible under his feet, it seemed to him now all that old past, those old thoughts, those old tasks, those old themes, those old impressions, and this entire panorama, himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as if he was flying away somewhere upwards, and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double-grivna clutched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung his arm, and threw it into the water; then he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had somehow cut himself off from everyone and everything in that moment with scissors.

But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a very unpleasant incident. A coachman of a carriage had whipped him heavily on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite having shouted at him several times. The whip's blow enraged him so much that he jumped to the railing (it was unclear why he had been walking in the middle of the bridge, where carriages drive, not pedestrians), and he gnashed his teeth angrily. Laughter echoed around him. "To business!" someone said. "Some drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll have to answer for him." "That's how they make a living, honorable sir, that's how they make a living…" [21 – "That's how they make a living…" – In newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg poor people who made a living by deliberately running under carriage wheels to receive compensation for injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing, still senselessly and angrily watching the retreating carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.

He remembered only that he had taken a sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came confusion. III

He was, however, not entirely in a state of amnesia throughout the illness; it was a febrile condition, with delirium and semi-consciousness. Much later he recalled many things. At one moment it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him, wanting to take him somewhere, and there was much argument and quarreling about him. At another moment he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone having left and fearing him; they would occasionally open the door slightly to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves, laughing, and mocking him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he could distinguish another person, very apparently familiar to him, but he could not for the life of him remember who it was, and he pined for this, even crying. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had been lying there for a whole month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about that he had completely forgotten; instead, he was constantly aware that he had forgotten something he could not forget—tormented, suffering, recalling it, groaning, falling into a frenzy or a terrible, unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but someone would always stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and confusion.

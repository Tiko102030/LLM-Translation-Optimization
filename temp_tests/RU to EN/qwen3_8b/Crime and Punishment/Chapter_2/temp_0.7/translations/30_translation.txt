—Ilya Petrovich!— the scribe began solicitously, but stopped to wait, since there was no other way to restrain the boiling corporal except by holding his hands, which he knew from his own experience. As for the stout lady, at first she trembled from thunder and lightning; but strange thing: the more numerous and fierce the curses became, the more amiable her demeanor grew, and the more enchanting her smile became, directed at the furious corporal. She shuffled in place and kept bowing continuously, eagerly waiting for her turn to speak, and finally got it. —There's no noise or scuffle here, sir captain— she suddenly chattered, as if spilling peas, with a strong German accent, though fluently Russian— and no, no scandal at all, they came drunk, and I'll tell you everything, sir captain, and I'm not to blame… I have a noble home, sir captain, and noble manners, sir captain, and I always, always wanted no scandal. They came completely drunk and then asked for three more drinks, and then one raised his legs and started playing the fortepiano with his foot, and that's very bad in a noble home, and he broke the fortepiano entirely, and there's absolutely no manners here, and I said so. He took the stick and started pushing everyone from behind with it. And then I quickly called the butler and Karl came, he took Karl and hit him in the eye, and Henrietta also got hit in the eye, and I got my cheek hit five times. And that's very impolite in a noble home, sir captain, and I shouted. He opened the window and started yelling from it, like a squealing pig; and that's a disgrace.

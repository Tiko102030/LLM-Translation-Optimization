But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully stared after the departing wheelchair, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands. He looked around: an old woman in a headscarf and goat shoes, with a young girl in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. It was a double grivna. By her dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street collector, and the act of giving a whole double grivna, he probably should have received a whip's strike, which moved them to pity. He clutched the double grivna in his hand, walked about ten steps, and then turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which rarely occurs on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which can't be seen better from any point than from here, from the bridge, not yet twenty paces from the chapel, shone brilliantly, and through the clear air, one could distinctly see even every ornament of it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the strike; now only a restless and somewhat unclear thought occupied him exclusively.

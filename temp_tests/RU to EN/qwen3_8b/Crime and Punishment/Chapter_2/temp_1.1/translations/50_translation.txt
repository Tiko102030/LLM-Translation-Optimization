Further, in the recess of the courtyard, a low, sooty, stone shed peeped out from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. There, probably, was some sort of establishment, a carriage workshop or a blacksmith's, or something along those lines; everywhere, almost from the very gates, the sooty dust had darkened. "What a place to throw something away and leave!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he walked through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter attached to the fence (as is often the case in such houses where there are many factory workers, craftsmen, coachmen, etc.), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark: "Here, carts are forbidden." That meant it was already good that there was no suspicion, that he had come in and stopped. "Everything can be thrown down here at once and then leave!" 

After looking around once more, he had already put his hand into his pocket when suddenly, near the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire distance was an arshin wide, he noticed a large, uncut stone, approximately, perhaps, one and a half puds in weight, lying directly against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear pedestrians scurrying about, which there were always quite a number of; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone came from the street, which, however, could very well happen, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, seized the top of it firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and turned the stone over. A small hollow formed under the stone; immediately he began to toss everything from his pocket into it.

But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. A coachman of one of the sleighs whipped him heavily on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so angered him that, jumping back to the railing (it's unclear why he was walking in the very center of the bridge, where horses go, not pedestrians), he gnashed and chattered his teeth in fury. Laughter echoed around him. — "To business!" — someone said. "Somehow, a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; you'll be held responsible for that." — "That's their trade, esteemed sir, that's their trade..." [21 - "That's their trade..." — In the newspapers of that time, it was often reported about St. Petersburg beggars who deliberately ran into carriages to obtain assistance for their injuries (see, for example, "Golos," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and angrily looked after the retreating sleigh, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone shoving money into his hands.

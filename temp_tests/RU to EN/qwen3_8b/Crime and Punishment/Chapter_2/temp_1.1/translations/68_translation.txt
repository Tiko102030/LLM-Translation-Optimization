And lo, to his greatest astonishment, he suddenly made out the voice of his landlady. She was wailing, screaming, and weeping, rushing, hurrying, spewing out words so fast that they couldn't be deciphered, pleading—clearly, about being stopped from being beaten, for she was being unmercifully beaten on the stairs. The voice of the one beating her had become so terrifying from hatred and fury that it was barely more than a gurgle, yet even he, the one beating her, was saying something, too, speaking hurriedly, indistinctly, urgently, gasping. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf: he recognized this voice—it was Ilya Petrovich. Ilya Petrovich was here, beating his landlady! He was beating her with his feet, knocking her head against the steps—this is clear, one can hear it by the sounds, the screams, the blows! What is this—has the world turned upside down, or what? One could hear how crowds were gathering on all floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, doors slamming, footsteps approaching. "But for what, for what, and how can this be possible!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking that he had completely lost his mind. But no, he was too clearly hearing it...

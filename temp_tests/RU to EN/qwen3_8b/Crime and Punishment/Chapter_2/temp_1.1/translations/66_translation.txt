The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a restless and rather unclear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and gazed into the distance for a long time, intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he walked to the university, he often, in fact, most frequently when returning home, would stop exactly at this same spot, look intently at this truly magnificent panorama, and each time almost wonder at a certain unclear and unsolvable impression of his own. An inexplicable chill always came from this magnificent panorama; for him, this rich picture was full of a silent and deaf spirit… Each time he marveled at his own gloomy and mysterious impression and postponed solving it, distrusting himself, to the future. Now suddenly he sharply recalled these previous questions and doubts of his, and it seemed to him that he had not accidentally remembered them now. It seemed strange and amazing to him that he had stopped at the same place again, as before, as though indeed imagining that he could think about the same thing now as before, and be interested in the same themes and pictures as before, which he had been interested in just so recently. Even slightly amusing, at the same time, it felt strange and heavy in his chest. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible under his feet, he now sensed all this previous past, these old thoughts, these old tasks, these old themes, these old impressions, and this whole panorama, himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as if he was flying upward somewhere and everything was disappearing from his sight… Making an involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a two-kopeck coin clenched in his fist. He unclenched his hand, looked intently at the coin, raised his arm, and threw it into the water; then he turned around and went home. It seemed to him as if he had somehow cut himself off from everything and everyone in this moment with scissors.

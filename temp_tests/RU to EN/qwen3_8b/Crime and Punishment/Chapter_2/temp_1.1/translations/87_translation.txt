— A cup of tea? — I'll have a cup. — Pour it. Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table. He immediately ordered the tea, poured it, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat back down on the sofa. He continued to hold the sick man's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began to feed him with a teaspoonful of tea, continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the very act of blowing were the main and most vital part of recovery. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite strong enough to lift himself up and sit on the sofa without any outside help, and not only to hold the spoon or cup in his hands, but even perhaps to walk. Yet, for some strange, almost beast-like reason, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a while, to hide, to pretend, if necessary, even as if he hadn't fully understood yet, and at the same time to listen and find out what was happening here? In any case, he could not overcome his aversion: after drinking ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, pouted, pushed the spoon away, and collapsed again onto the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled ones with clean pillowcases; he noticed this too and took it into account.

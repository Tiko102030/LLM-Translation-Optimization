He remembered only that he had taken a single sip of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came amnesia. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of amnesia throughout his illness; it was a feverish condition, with delirium and half-consciousness. Later, he recalled much. At one moment, it seemed to him that a crowd had gathered around him and wanted to take him away somewhere, with much argument and quarrels about him. At another moment, he suddenly found himself alone in the room, with everyone having left and fearing him, and only occasionally, briefly, they opened the door to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves about something, laughing and mocking him. He often remembered Nastasya by his side; he distinguished also another person, very apparently familiar to him, but who exactly he could not guess, and he pined for this, even weeping. Sometimes it seemed to him that he had already been lying there for a month; at another time, that it was still the same day. But of that—of all that—he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something that could not be forgotten—tormented, suffering, recalling, groaning, descending into frenzy or a terrible, unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but someone would always stop him by force, and he would fall back into helplessness and amnesia.

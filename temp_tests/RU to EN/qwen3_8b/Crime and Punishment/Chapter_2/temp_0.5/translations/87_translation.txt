—"A cup of tea?" — "Perhaps a cup of tea." — "Pour it." "Wait, I'll pour it myself; sit down at the table." He immediately took charge, poured the tea, then poured another cup, threw away his breakfast, and sat down again on the sofa. He continued to support the sick man's head with his left hand, lifted him up, and began to feed him with a teaspoon, pouring tea continuously and especially diligently blowing on the spoon, as if the main and saving point of recovery lay in this act of blowing. Raskolnikov remained silent and did not resist, despite the fact that he felt quite strong enough to lift himself up and sit on the sofa without any help, and not only to hold the spoon or cup in his hands, but even perhaps to walk. But for some strange, almost beastly cunning, it suddenly occurred to him to conceal his strength for a time, to hide, to pretend, if necessary, even though he didn't quite understand yet, and at the same time to listen and find out what was going on. However, he did not overcome his aversion: after sipping ten spoonfuls of tea, he suddenly freed his head, pushed the spoon away petulantly, and collapsed again on the pillow. Indeed, there were now real pillows under his head—down-filled with clean pillowcases; he also noticed this and took it into consideration.

But at that moment, as he stood by the railing and still senselessly and spitefully stared after the departing carriage, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone tucking money into his hands. He looked around: an old widow in a headpiece and goatskin boots, and with her a young woman in a hat and with a green umbrella, probably her daughter. "Take it, father, for the sake of Christ." He took it, and they passed by. It was a double grivna. By their dress and appearance, they could easily have mistaken him for a beggar, a real street collector, and in giving him an entire double grivna, he, probably, deserved a whip's stroke, which moved them to pity. He clutched the double grivna in his hand, walked ten steps, and turned his face toward the Neva, in the direction of the palace. The sky was cloudless, and the water was almost blue, which rarely happens on the Neva. The dome of the cathedral, which cannot be outlined better from any point than from here, from the bridge, twenty steps away from the chapel, shone brilliantly, and through the clear air, one could distinctly see even every ornament of it. The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten the strike; a restless and not quite clear thought now occupied him entirely.

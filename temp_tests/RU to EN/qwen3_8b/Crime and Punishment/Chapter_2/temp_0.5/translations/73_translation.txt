He remembered only how he took a swig of cold water and spilled it from the cup onto his chest. Then came oblivion. III

He, however, was not entirely in a state of oblivion throughout the illness: it was a feverish condition, with delirium and half-consciousness. Later, he recalled much. At times, it seemed to him that a crowd was gathering around him, wanting to take him somewhere, and there was much arguing and quarreling about him. At other times, he suddenly found himself alone in the room, everyone had left, and they feared him, opening the door only occasionally to look at him, threatening him, whispering among themselves, laughing, and taunting him. He often remembered Natasha by his side; he distinguished another person, very much like someone he knew, but who exactly—he could not guess, and he longed for it, even weeping. At times, it seemed to him that he had already been lying there for a month; at other times, that it was still the same day. But about that—about that he had completely forgotten; instead, he remembered every minute that he had forgotten something that could not be forgotten—tormented, suffered, recalling, groaning, descending into fury or a terrible, unbearable fear. Then he would try to get up, wanting to run away, but always someone would stop him by force, and he would again fall into helplessness and oblivion.

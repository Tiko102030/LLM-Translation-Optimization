But Raskolnikov was already stepping out onto the street. On the Nikolaevsky Bridge, he had to fully come to his senses again due to a rather unpleasant incident. A coachman of one of the sleighs whipped him hard on the back for almost hitting the horses, despite the fact that the coachman had shouted at him three or four times. The whip's blow so enraged him that he dashed toward the balustrades (it was unclear why he was walking in the very center of the bridge, where carriages go, not pedestrians), and snarled, gnashing his teeth. Laughter echoed around him. — "To work, then!" — someone said, as if mocking. — "Somehow, a drunkard will deliberately run under the wheels; and you'll be held responsible for him." — "They make a living by that, esteemed sir, they make a living by that…" [21 — "They make a living by that…" — In newspapers of that time, it was often reported about Petersburg poor people who made a living by deliberately running under carriages to receive compensation for injuries (see, for example, "Voice," 1865, No. 65).] But at that moment, as he stood by the balustrades, still senselessly and spitefully watching the departing sleigh, rubbing his back, he suddenly felt someone slipping money into his hands.

And lo and behold, to the greatest amazement, he suddenly recognized the voice of his mistress. She was howling, screaming, and wailing, rushing, hurrying, spilling out words so fast that it was impossible to make sense of what she was saying—clearly pleading, of course, to stop beating her, since she was being mercilessly beaten on the stairs. The voice of the attacker had become so terrifying from hatred and fury that it was barely more than a rasp, yet still, the attacker was saying something, too, in a rush, indistinct, hurried, and gasping. Suddenly Raskolnikov trembled like a leaf: he recognized this voice; it was Ilyi Petrovich. Ilyi Petrovich was here, beating his mistress! He was kicking her with his feet, hitting her head against the steps—this was clear, audible from the sounds, the screams, the blows! What was this—has the world turned upside down? One could hear how crowds were gathering on all the floors, along the entire staircase, voices, exclamations, doors slamming, people rushing down. "But why, why, and how could this be!"—he kept repeating, seriously thinking that he had gone mad. But no, he heard everything too clearly...

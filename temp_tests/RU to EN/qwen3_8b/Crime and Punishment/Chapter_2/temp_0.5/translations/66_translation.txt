The pain from the whip had subsided, and Raskolnikov had forgotten about the blow; a restless and somewhat unclear thought now occupied him exclusively. He stood and looked into the distance for a long time, intently; this place was especially familiar to him. When he went to the university, it usually happened—more often than not, when returning home—that he would stop precisely at this same spot, gaze intently at this truly magnificent panorama, and each time almost marvel at one obscure and unsolvable impression of his own. An inexplicable coldness always seemed to emanate from this magnificent panorama; it was full for him of a silent and heavy spirit… Each time he marveled at his gloomy and enigmatic impression and postponed solving it, distrusting himself, to the future. Now suddenly he sharply recalled these former questions and doubts of his, and it seemed to him that he had not accidentally remembered them now. It seemed strange and wondrous to him that he had stopped at the same spot again, as before, as though he had indeed imagined that he could think about the same things now as before, and be interested in the same themes and pictures as before… just so recently. It almost seemed funny to him at the same time, and yet it pressed painfully on his chest. In some depth, below, somewhere barely visible under his feet, it seemed to him now all this former past, these former thoughts, these former tasks, these former themes, these former impressions, and this entire panorama, and himself, and everything, everything… It seemed as though he was flying somewhere upward and everything was vanishing from his eyes… Making one involuntary movement with his hand, he suddenly felt a double-grivna clenched in his fist. He opened his hand, looked intently at the coin, swung his arm, and threw it into the water; then he turned around and went home. It seemed to him that he had somehow cut himself off from all and everything with scissors at this moment.

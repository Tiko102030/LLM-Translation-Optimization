Further, in the recess of the courtyard, a low, soot-stained, stone shed peeped out from behind the fence, obviously part of some workshop. Here, probably, was some kind of establishment, a carriage or blacksmith's shop, or something of the sort; everywhere, almost from the very gates, there was a black coating of soot. "What a place to throw something away and disappear!" he suddenly thought. Not noticing anyone in the courtyard, he stepped through the gates and just then saw, close to the gates, a gutter fixed against the fence (as is often the case in such houses where there are many factory workers, artisans, coachmen, and so on), and above the gutter, right on the fence, was written in chalk the usual sharp remark: "Here carts are forbidden to stop." That was good, then, that no suspicion had arisen that he had come in and stopped. "Everything can be thrown down here at once and then escape!"

Looking around once more, he had already shoved his hand into his pocket when suddenly, right at the outer wall, between the gates and the gutter, where the entire space was a yard wide, he noticed a large, uncut stone, about a pound and a half in weight, lying right against the stone wall of the street. Behind this wall was the street, the sidewalk, and one could hear pedestrians scurrying about, which there were always quite a few; but no one behind the gates could see him, unless someone came in from the street, which, however, could very well happen, and therefore he had to hurry. He bent down to the stone, grabbed its top firmly with both hands, gathered all his strength, and overturned the stone. A small hollow formed under the stone; immediately he began throwing everything from his pockets into it.

Examining more closely the limits of public authority in a democratic society, Wait acknowledged several restrictions on governmental regulation. "For the use of regulated property, you may receive compensation," and Wait argued that the final decision on compensation should be made by the legislative body. "It is presumed that any law," he stated, "is constitutional. Judges cannot declare which laws are unconstitutional unless it is clearly evident. If there is doubt, the expressed will of the legislative body should be supported by a majority." Acknowledging that this power could be abused, he urged citizens to seek compensation for damages "at polling stations, not in court." Defenders of property rights were shocked by the explicit reading of police powers in the Mann case. Judge Stephen Field thought otherwise and stated that "the principle on which the majority's opinion relies... undermines private property rights." "If it becomes law," he warned, "then all property and all commercial activity in the state will be under the control of the majority members of its legislative body." The Mann case decision showed that property rights are now also one of society's interests, another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer require any constitutional guarantee.

"Use your own so as not to harm another." Application of this principle ensures access to the consideration of the social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his view, actions of one owner affect others even if they occur on their own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" is merely a norm, not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests of different people are involved and where both parties complain of a neighbor's interference with their territory (see Holmes, 1894, p. 11). Issues concerning the subject of property were reconsidered in the nineteenth century to promote entrepreneurial activity, even at the expense of causing harm to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm another" remained, but now it was used to protect entrepreneurs' rights to act according to the requirements of passive neighbors who wish their property to remain undisturbed. Now, the concept of "harm" was considered not as a series of wrongful acts concerning another's property, but as the inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for damage caused by the use of their property. This approach first became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805).

As a result, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Governmental intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widespread belief that government has a moral obligation to promote a thriving economy and care for the poor. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited the trade of food products, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and restricted the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Henderson and Henderson 1947; Virgin 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right to compulsory transfer of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

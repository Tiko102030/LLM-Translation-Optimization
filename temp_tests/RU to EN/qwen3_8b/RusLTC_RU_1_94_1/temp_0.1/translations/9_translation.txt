Citing the Bible, Locke argues that the same natural law which in this way grants us property also limits the extent of that property. "God has given us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did he give us this? For our enjoyment. A person has the right to convert as much of a thing into their property as they can use for their own needs before it is spoiled. Anything beyond these limits exceeds their share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," gaining power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee everyone access to the wealth of society. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these considerations, including the renowned radical American thinker, Thomas Paine.

In the Jacobs case (1885), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in leased premises, because it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use his property," thus "depriving him of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and protection of property rights, Justice Earl stated that freedom, in a broad sense, and as understood in our country, means not only the right to be free from actual servitude... but also the right of a person to use his abilities in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or restrict these rights... are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislature claimed that the ruling was based on the state's police powers, and the measures taken were necessary to "improve the nation's health system." This was not favorable for defenders of state regulation, as the court later concluded that the requirement lacked the necessary amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law "did not actually relate to health issues," and Justice Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police powers, one cannot arbitrarily intrude into personal rights and private property." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of unprecedented judicial activism in the protection of property rights. For example, in the "Godcharles v. Waideman" (1866) case, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law was a violation of the equal rights of employer and employee, more than that, it was an insulting attempt to subject an unskilled worker to legislative oversight," because it prevented workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

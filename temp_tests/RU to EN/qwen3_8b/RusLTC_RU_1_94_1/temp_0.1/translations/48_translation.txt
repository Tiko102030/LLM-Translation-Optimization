However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote of Justice Stone's opinion, in which the majority of the Court equated the concept of property rights with the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases concerning economic legislation, the Court intends to apply the principle of the presumption of constitutionality of laws, and selected members of the legislative body will be responsible for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative actions. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there will be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religious choice. Environmental protection and countermeasures against the "eminent domain" procedure. After issuing the decision in the Caroline Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected legislative members to have broad rights in choosing objects of social policy, even though they restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy implemented in this context. The case "Djast v. Marinette County" of 1972 in Wisconsin clearly illustrates how the judicial power easily applied the position from the Caroline Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Djast filed a lawsuit regarding the repeal of regulations preventing them from occupying the designated swampy areas for building homes. The Wisconsin Supreme Court rejected the family's claims and ruled that legislative activity is a lawful use of police powers. Later, it wanted to approve a broader list of restrictions on property rights.

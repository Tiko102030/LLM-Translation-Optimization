Chancellor of New York Kent declared in 1826 that "every person possesses such an amount of freedom for acquiring, using, and disposing of his or her property that it may be compared to a tolerable state of property and mutual rights of others... Although, thus, property is protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as much as may be necessary to prevent abuse of rights which may lead to violation of the rights of another person or of the whole society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the 19th century. For example, in 1837, the legislative body in the state of Illinois did not have three pages to list all the powers that would be possessed by the city administration of Chicago concerning property-related issues. In addition to regulation and licensing of waterborne transportation, and restrictions on "spinning a wheel, playing ball, or launching paper kites... capable of irritating people," new cities acquired the power to "compel the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candlestore, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, privy, textile factory, or any other old and undesirable building or house to clean, remove, or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3–6).

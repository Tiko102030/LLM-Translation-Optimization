Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law. Instead of relying in his speech on the opinion of the majority of the legislative assembly members regarding natural rights and the beliefs of John Locke, he accused them of copying "Social Statics of Spencer." Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that more signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than the protection of the sacred notion of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another social goal. This was not the first time that Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "The Common Law" (1881), he discussed in detail that the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "Law does not even seek to compensate a person for all the harm caused to him... Anyone may found any business, and subsequently, to survive in competitive struggle, he will poach customers from his competitor, and possibly even drive him bankrupt." In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is acceptable according to the principles of "such a policy which has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. /think).

The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could have developed the expanding network of railways. At the top of the entire financial pyramid was a newly established centralized banking system, which was based in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Irritated workers and farmers saw the true aims of the bankers and owners of the iron and railway companies, whose ownership was precisely sponsored by government agencies. They wanted new laws that would expand public authority in matters of regulating property rights. They achieved remarkable success by obtaining the passage of a law establishing an eight-hour workday and a law setting railroad rates. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, leading to the transfer of these bills for consideration by the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed the business circles of the state, which were worried about the growing interest in the ideas of popular radicalism of that time. The plaintiffs argued that the law, by voiding existing contracts, violated the contracts clause of the Constitution, thereby also violating the newly reissued Fourteenth Amendment regarding the protection of citizens' rights from state interference.

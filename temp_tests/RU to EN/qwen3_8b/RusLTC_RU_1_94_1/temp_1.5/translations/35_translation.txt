In the Jacobson case (1885), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars within leased premises, because it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use his property," thus "denying him his property and personal freedom." Equating the concepts of personal liberty and protection of property rights, Judge Earl stated that in a broad sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of a person to utilize his abilities in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or restrict these rights... are violations of his fundamental right to freedom. The Legislative Assembly claimed that the ruling was based on the state's police powers, and that the measures taken were necessary to "improve the nation's health system." This was not promising for defenders of governmental regulation, as the court later concluded that the requirement lacked the necessary amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law "did not actually relate to health issues at all," and Judge Earl notified the legislative body that "under the guise of police power, there can be no arbitrary intrusion into personal rights and private property." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an unprecedented judicial activity in the protection of property rights. For example, in the case "Godcharles v. Waidman" (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (not company stocks). The court argued that "the law is a violation of the equal rights of the employer and employee, and more so, it is an insulting attempt to place an unskilled worker under legal guardianship," because it hinders workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

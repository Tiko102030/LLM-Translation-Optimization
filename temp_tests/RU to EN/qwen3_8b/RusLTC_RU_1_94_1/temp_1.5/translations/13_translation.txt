English property was valued to an even lesser extent: debts to English creditors were, without lengthy discussion, annulled; some states, including Virginia, closed the cases designated for hearing on claims asserted by British subjects (see Elly 1998, p. 35). Widespread confiscation of property from opponents of independence from England did not serve as a good omen for property security in the new republic. The main rebels fought to curb what they regarded as "excess democracy" (see Tomlinson 1993, p. 60). Ensuring property security, "the protector of any right" became a concern for the creators of the first constitutions of all states (see Elly 1998, p. 35).

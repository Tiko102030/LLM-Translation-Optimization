But after these icons of liberalism retired in the 1990 and 1991 years, the Court introduced additional limitations on governmental regulation. For example, in the case of Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required that property owner in South Carolina be compensated for the damage caused to his property after a new law on "Coastal Line Organization" suspended construction on his land. Again expressing distrust of the elected legislators, Scalia warned, as in the Nolan case, that judges must closely examine the enactments in order to protect themselves from "the risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes." The Court improved its new standards in the case of Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from the property owners to the government. The case was filed when the City of Tigard, Oregon administration, stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only if she gave up part of her land for the creation of a public park, pedestrian and bike paths in order to reduce traffic congestion. Writing for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration "did not dispute the fact that the construction of pedestrian and bike paths at Dolan's expense was a consequence of the city administration's need to improve transportation infrastructure." The rulings in the Nolan and Dolan cases signify the emergence of a new era of intensified property regulation. This may lead to property rights once again returning to the level they were at before the New Deal.

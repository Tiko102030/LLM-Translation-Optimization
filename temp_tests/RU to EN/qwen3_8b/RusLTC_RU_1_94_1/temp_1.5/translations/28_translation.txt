The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed some property in comparison to another property. According to one of the historians of law, Willis Hall, this approach is one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America—"the liberation of all human creative energy." Despite the fact that the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on the entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. Theoretically, if the project's cost were higher than the amount of the caused damage, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the caused damage. However, in practice, all the burden is shifted to the victim's side, which in turn more significantly contributes to the increase in the number of projects by entrepreneurs, while the entire risk and operational costs fall on the neighbors' shoulders. If property rights were enforced to a greater extent legally, then high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by reducing the costs of risk and operational expenses for entrepreneurs, this new principle has further contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

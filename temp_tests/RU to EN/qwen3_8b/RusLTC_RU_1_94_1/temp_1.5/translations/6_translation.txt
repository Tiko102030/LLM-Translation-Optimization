There is an "inalienable" right of property of the laborer, because: although land and all lower creatures belong collectively to all people, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, by the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever a person takes from the state in which nature created and preserved the object, he combines it with his labor and adds to it something that belongs personally to him, thereby making it his property... For since this labor is the laborer's inalienable property, no one other than himself can have a right to what he once attached his labor to, at least in those cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it is to use someone else's labor, i.e., to enslave a person. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, property owners form society and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

The Supreme Court of Louisiana adopted a law stating that "private rights losses in this case are irrelevant due to the overall profits and trade turnover of this commercial entity" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230-31). The butchers of New Orleans brought their case to the U.S. Supreme Court, where their interests were represented by John A. Campbell, the predecessor in office of Field, a Democrat and assistant war secretary (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court prior to the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the core principles of labor law to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the city of New Orleans ordinances had brought us back to a time when "the persistent gaze of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure that regulatory measures would be expanded, and that income, property, and power would be redistributed.

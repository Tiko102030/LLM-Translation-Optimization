The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and therefore the attorneys feared the growing pressure that a further expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power would entail. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's particular opinion left a stronger impression than Judge Miller's, which was supported by a majority of votes and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field asserted that fundamental rights to free labor had been violated in the state of Louisiana. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's prescriptions regarding that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the primary part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." Field required twenty years to comprehend the court's decision in the case of "Slotter-House and Mann," before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case of "Allgeier v. State of Louisiana," the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not have a license to engage in commercial activity in Louisiana, which essentially constituted a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation existed in the case of Jacobs, and Justice Rufus W. Peckham of the Supreme Court argued that a person's freedom included not only the absence of "physical restraint," but also encompassed the right to "work where he wishes; earn a living in any lawful manner; pursue his preferred profession and, for this purpose, enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned objectives."

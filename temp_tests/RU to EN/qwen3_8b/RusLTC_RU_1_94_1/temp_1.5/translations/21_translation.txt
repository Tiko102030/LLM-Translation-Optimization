Lemuel Shaw, a representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court, supported the legislative authority dealing with issues of regulating property within Boston Harbor and prohibiting construction outside of this wharf, and spoke on the rights of the police as follows: We believe this to be an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every owner of property, regardless of whether they possess an exclusive right to it or not, manages it with all the implied responsibility, so that its use should not cause harm to other people who also have the right to enjoy the use of their own property, nor to society as a whole. All property in this community... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules applicable to it may be necessary for achieving the common good and general welfare. Rights to property, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to the action of appropriate restrictions necessary to confirm that others are not harmed by the use of this property, and to the action of established legal rules, since legislative authority... may assume that this is a necessary and appropriate resolution. In conclusion, Shaw added: "It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits of its manifestation." Indeed, by using universal, typical phrases such as "public interest," "internal structure of the state," "implied right," and "obligation of the supreme authority to protect all," Shaw demonstrated that law enforcement agencies may possess a broader set of tools to achieve their objectives. Southern state governments resorted to the aid of police to enforce laws limiting the use of property by slaves, for example, prohibiting slaveowners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they established unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation, which limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activities in certain types of professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection for the property and labor of African Americans once again demonstrates the understanding of property at that time. (See Cohen 1991; Lichtenthaler 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955).

Regarding this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public goals, were immediately revoked once they hindered the fulfillment of these goals. In the early 19th century, government authorities striving to stimulate industry and trade launched a campaign for the construction of roads and railroads, canals. During the construction of the state's infrastructure, indirect costs, expenses related to damaging adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved property had to be borne. According to the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would bear responsibility for indirect costs, significantly increasing the financing costs of inventions. Changing the meaning of this principle, i.e., avoiding responsibility for the consequences of their actions regarding adjacent property by independent entrepreneurs, would drastically reduce the costs of constructing state infrastructure. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calender v. Marsh," establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calender filed a lawsuit for damages caused to him during street repairs, resulting in damage to the foundation of his house in Boston.

Calhoun filed a claim for damages caused during street repair, which resulted in damage to the foundation of his home in Boston. His attorney cited English laws, common law precedents, and this principle in order to obtain compensation. However, the defense rejected the use of the English precedent with an instrumental presentation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances in America, the road needed to be repaired, and "if such a claim," which Calhoun had brought, "were granted, then it would be necessary to examine the correctness of repairs of all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who rejected Calhoun's claim for compensation. Subsequently, the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's offer of a market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was far from good news for people whose property was directly adjacent to public structures. "Those who purchase property near a road," the court stated, "must take into account future expansion or reduction of the street or highway." For, in such a case, the amount of compensation would have doubled compared to the original cost of repairs. Similar conclusions were reached in New York in 1828, in the case of "Lansing v. Smith Walter Quackenbush," where a claim was brought against the state of New York for damages resulting from the decline in the price of docks after the construction of a new harbor in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the case of "Calhoun v. Marsh" in Massachusetts to dismiss Quackenbush's claims.

This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation. Later, other authors joined these arguments, including the well-known American radical Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reasons to regret the widespread societal conclusions of Locke. For example, Richard Epstein, who accused Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, acknowledges that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the perspective of relations between people and objects, it can function as a socially neutral aspect: the accumulation of the results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is accessible to others. However, acknowledging the social parameters of property allows for a distributional aspect, that is, the rights of one person may harm others, for example, in the case of monopolization of resources previously available to all.

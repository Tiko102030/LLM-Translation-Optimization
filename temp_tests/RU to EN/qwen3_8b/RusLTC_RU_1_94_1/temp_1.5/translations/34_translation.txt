In the late 19th century, expressing opposition to the opinion of "a large army of dissatisfied people," attorneys and judges intended to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tidemann, they demanded limiting the entire scope of state power by establishing boundaries for the authority of police. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to the definition of private property. Even before the beginning of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. Pipple" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared a law prohibiting the sale of liquor unconstitutional. After the law went into effect, everyone who had sold that liquor lost their property. At the same time, the procedure for taking possession of property did not follow the legal order established by statute. For the first time, realizing that the concept of "statutory order" protected property, the court ruled that "legislative authority cannot entirely abolish commercial activity in any of its forms, thus subjecting property to complete disappearance."

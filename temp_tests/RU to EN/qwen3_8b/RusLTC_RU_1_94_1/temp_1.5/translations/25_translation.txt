The New York court utilized the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Calender v. Marsh" to dismiss Quakenbush's claims. The court asserted: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily, to some extent, affect the comfort and property of people; and in the case where the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of the damage itself should be considered as part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." The court concluded: "This case is based on the principle that the public good must prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise
The precedent, as in the situation "Lancing v. Calender," does not prohibit the government from improving the state at the expense of adjacent property owners, but it places the obligation on certain individuals to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horowitz 1977, p.70). Such instrumental norms later became private cases. For example, in the case "Torp v. Rutland and Ballston Railroad Company" (1855), Vermont Supreme Court Chief Justice Isaac Redfield supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to injure another' (Latin: sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas)." The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property.

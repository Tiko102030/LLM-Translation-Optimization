Referring to the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature that in this way grants us property also limits the extent of this property. "God gave us everything in abundance" — this is the voice of reason, confirmed by inspiration. But why did He give us this? For our enjoyment. A person has the right to transform, through their labor, as much as they can use for the necessities of their life before the item spoils. And anything beyond these limits exceeds their share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's disappointment, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," to wield power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to ensure everyone's access to society's wealth. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to the emergence of discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; subsequently, other authors joined these considerations, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

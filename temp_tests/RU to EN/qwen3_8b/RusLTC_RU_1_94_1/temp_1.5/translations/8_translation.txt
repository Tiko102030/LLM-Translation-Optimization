A particular form of ownership as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke himself does not defend property rights as resolutely as one might infer from the well-known passage cited above (see Horn 1990; Ketsnelson 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he refers to the issue of the right of Stuart monarchs of England to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament. But beyond this issue, Locke acknowledges a social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke argued that each person should have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be limited to ensure that all can share the bounty of nature, leaving "sufficient and in the same state" for others. Referring to the Bible, Locke claims that the same law of nature, which in this way gives us property, limits the extent of this property as well.

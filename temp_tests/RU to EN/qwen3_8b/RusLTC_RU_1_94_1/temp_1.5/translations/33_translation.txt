The ruling in the Mann case showed that ownership rights had also become one of society's interests, another instrument for achieving social goals, and no constitutional guarantee was anymore required. Against the backdrop of increasing social unrest, this approach increasingly frightened conservatives and provoked greater emotional reactions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events at Haymarket in 1886, the distinguished legal scholar Christopher Tideman (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of the anointed of the Lord did not permit limiting police powers. The spread of non-intervention policy reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled everywhere in the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to determine what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied and their obvious power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the conditions of universal suffrage, conservatives feared more than ever that a more despotic and more unwise autocracy might appear than any that had existed before. In the late 19th century, attorneys and judges who opposed the views of "the numerous army of dissatisfied" intended to sanctify private property.

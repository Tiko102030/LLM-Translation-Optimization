For the first time, he became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the state of New York (1805), where the downstream landowner initiated a claim for damages. The upstream dam collapsed, flooding his home. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: "The defendants had the same rights regarding their territory... [the principle of 'use your own so as not to harm others'] should be limited to a reasonable extent to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely due to some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law that provided for some inconvenience of this nature, such that a person who could first build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river ultimately acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose a portion of the profits they could have gained through competitive struggle. This approach democratized property rights, resulting in both long-time and new residents possessing the same privileges. Thus, wealth was redistributed to entrepreneurs at the expense of long-time residents being prohibited from restricting the actions of entrepreneurs. At the same time, by removing the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from lawsuits over indirect damages, these cases only undermined existing property rights. Therefore, the transformation of the principle 'use your own so as not to harm others' had nothing to do with the ideas of sacred private property introduced by J. Locke. The new approach was neither a general criticism of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights.

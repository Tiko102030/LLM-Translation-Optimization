Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to neighbors' property. On the other hand, it can support the affected neighbors and limit the activity of the owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of loss of clean air and water or the cost of payment that would have to be paid to the firm to stop it from discharging waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer without these privileges, because they would have to reduce pollution levels or pay neighbors for the right to pollute. Some question the fact that property rights significantly influence income distribution, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase asserted that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries leads to the same efficient social outcome, the same connection between the production of goods and services. Should those polluting the environment buy the right from their neighbors, or should neighbors pay those polluting the environment to stop doing so? Environmental pollution will occur where the costs of reducing its negative effects are greater than the value of clean air to neighbors, and it will be stopped when the costs of reducing the negative effects are less. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient result regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

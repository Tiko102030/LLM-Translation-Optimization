Property owned or used by African Americans did not possess the same degree of inviolability as that of property owned or used by European-Americans. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, African Americans in the southern states gained the right to own property, however, their rights could change depending on the political climate in the state and the degree of participation of African Americans in it. The same instrumental approach that the governments of the northern states used to limit property rights regarding clean air and water was also used in the southern states to maintain African Americans' dependence on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public goals, only the goals differed. Redistribution of property rights for the purpose of stimulating economic growth
By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to the instrumental approach that he had previously used in the Court to invalidate contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge Company. He did this in order to manage the only bridge across the Charles River connecting Boston and Cambridge on his own (see the case Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge Company encountered some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... invested in the construction of railways and canals along the lines of express highways... We would be forced to regress a century back to the inventions of that era and remain stagnant as a result."

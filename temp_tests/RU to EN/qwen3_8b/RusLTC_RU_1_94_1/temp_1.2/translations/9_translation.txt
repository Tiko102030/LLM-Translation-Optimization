Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature which in this way grants us property also limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly" – this is the voice of reason, confirmed by inspiration. But why did He give us this? For enjoyment. A man has a right to appropriate as much of the property as he can use for the necessities of his life before the thing is spoiled. Anything that goes beyond these limits exceeds his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "unproportional and unequal ownership of land," to wield power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee access to societal wealth for all. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limits on individual accumulation; subsequently, other authors joined these arguments, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

Regarding this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public objectives, were immediately suspended as soon as they hindered the accomplishment of these goals. In the early 19th century, state authorities seeking to stimulate industry and trade launched a campaign to build roads, canals, and railroads. When constructing state infrastructure, indirect costs were incurred, including expenses related to damaging adjacent property and losses in the value of unimproved property. According to the legal principle "sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas" (Latin: "use your own property so as not to harm another"), investors would bear responsibility for indirect costs, significantly increasing the expenses of financing inventions. By altering the meaning of this principle—i.e., by exempting independent entrepreneurs from liability for the consequences of their actions regarding adjacent property—the costs of constructing state infrastructure would sharply decrease. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calender v. Marsh," establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calender filed a lawsuit seeking compensation for damages caused during street repairs, which resulted in the damage of the foundation of his house in Boston.

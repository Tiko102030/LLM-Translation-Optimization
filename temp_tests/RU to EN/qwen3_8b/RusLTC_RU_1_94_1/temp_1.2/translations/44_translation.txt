In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal Co. v. Holmes, Holmes more accurately played the role of the Trojan Horse rather than the victor—defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "expropriation principle" of property on the basis of establishing a balance between competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should be constantly "reasonably" used, but the notion of what constitutes "reasonably" remains still vague. If actions go "too far," in such a case, compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it is the judges, taking into account the existence of public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public possess absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully assess and balance the competing interests of both parties. Property owners, in turn, should not antagonize everyone against themselves. Holmes claimed that "the government will hardly be able to continue its activities further if, to some extent, the issue of property rights is not resolved."

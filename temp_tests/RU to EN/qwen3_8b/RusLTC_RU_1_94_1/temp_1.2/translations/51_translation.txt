In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of a "condition regarding the issuance of a land use permit" is not an "expropriation" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general land use "does not serve the purported purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "thus is invalid, and compensation must be provided." In conclusion, Scalia spoke of the new standard: Because there is no connection between the condition and the cause of the construction restriction, the new purpose transforms into something entirely different, unlike what it was before. Now, the government's primary task is to obtain general land use rights without paying any compensation... if the condition specified in the land use permit is not regarded as a construction prohibition and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the construction restriction will not be a valid land use regulation but merely "present extortion." This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall issued a separate opinion. They rejected Scalia's request to reconsider the legislators' opinion. They argued that "the appropriate criterion for consideration... is whether the state can rationally determine whether the adopted measure can achieve the public purpose." But after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional limitations on governmental regulation.

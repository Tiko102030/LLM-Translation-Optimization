The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's special opinion left a stronger impression in memory than Justice Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the fundamental rights to free labor were violated in the state of Louisiana. Referring to Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's prescriptions that, since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the essential part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." Field required twenty years to understand the court's decision in the case of "Sloter-House and Mann" before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case of "Allgeyer v. Louisiana," the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not hold a license to conduct commercial activities in Louisiana, which amounted to a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation was presented in the case of Jacobs, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn a living by any lawful means; to pursue his chosen profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives."

Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law. Instead of relying on the majority opinion of the legislature's members regarding natural rights and John Locke's convictions, he accused them of copying "Social Statics" by Spencer. Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice, which rather signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than the protection of a sacred notion of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another societal goal. This was not the first time that Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "Common Law" (1881), he thoroughly discussed how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the assumptions of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "Law does not even attempt to reimburse a man for all the injury he has suffered... Any one may start a business, and later, in order to survive in the competitive struggle, he will attract his competitor's customers, and perhaps thereby even ruin him."

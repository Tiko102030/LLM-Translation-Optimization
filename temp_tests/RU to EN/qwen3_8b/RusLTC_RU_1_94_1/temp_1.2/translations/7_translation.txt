These views were prevalent in the American republic, and Locke's arguments continued to be cited by judges of the late nineteenth-century Supreme Court, who opposed public regulation of property rights (see Elly 1998). Others argue about property from the perspective of instrumentalism. For them, property rights should be protected, that is, these rights serve as an incentive for entrepreneurs (see Northh 1981). However, it is convincing that such an instrumental approach does not sanctify property; it does not make it an end in itself, an indubitable right surpassing all others. Instead, the issue of property has now become paramount, and the instrumental approach undermines the protection of all property rights.

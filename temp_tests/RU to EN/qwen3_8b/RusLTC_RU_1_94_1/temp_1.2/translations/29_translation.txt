The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. Reformers, however, sought even more. For example, labor rights advocate George MacNeil warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to the words of railroad conductor K.F. Breasly: "The Declaration of Independence was not created with the purposes that today trust companies try to impose upon us. The result of the Civil War of 1861 was the emancipation of black slaves, yet now workers are nothing more than slaves of consolidated trust companies" (see Fonner 1998, pp. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. Conducting military operations required substantial sums of money, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed the need for significant financial and industrial capital capable of supporting government activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could supply the expanding network of railroads.

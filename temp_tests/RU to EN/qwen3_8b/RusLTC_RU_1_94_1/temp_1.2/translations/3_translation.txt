On the other hand, the right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public objectives, and protect property owners from the consequences of the actions of polluters is considered. However, such a general comparison of property rights can be misunderstood, since these rights, to a greater extent, limit the rights of other people. Property rights grant individuals legally sanctioned privileges for the monopolistic control of resources, the ability to appeal to authorized police authorities to restrict the activities of other people. Therefore, property rights significantly limit some advantages because government authorities, to a greater extent, support property owners rather than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a debate about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather the discussion of property regulation that forces us to make a choice about whose property needs to be protected and whose property rights hold the advantage in the struggle for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so confusing because legal issues have been repeatedly discussed at various levels of government within the structure of the legal system of the United States. Thus, according to the system of federalism, one Federal Government is formed, while each state has its own legal system. Also, at each level of administration, there is a clear separation of executive, legislative, and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure the observance of constitutional norms, in which they find their interpretation.

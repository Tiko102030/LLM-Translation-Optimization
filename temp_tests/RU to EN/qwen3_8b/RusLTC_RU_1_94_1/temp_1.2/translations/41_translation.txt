It appears that the basis of the aforementioned case lies in John Locke's views on absolute property rights. The Mann case was also mentioned, with the decision being issued by Justice Wait of the Supreme Court. However, attention should be drawn to the previously discussed statement: "this is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., once again consciously re-established an instrumental view of property rights, declaring the previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. In the case of Lochner, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Beginning his speech with the idea that "the decision was based on the fundamentals of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy most of the country's population," he added: "The right of each person to act as they wish for as long as they desire, provided that they do not infringe upon the right of others to do the same, does not conform to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal management, or the laws of any of the states or other municipal institutions that take their money to achieve goals that seem necessary, regardless of whether they want it or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Herbert Spencer's Social Static. Unlike how Lochner despotically protects intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require achieving a balance. Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully selected words to initiate a new case for an alternative law."

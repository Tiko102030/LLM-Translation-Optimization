Chancellor Kent of New York declared in 1826 that "each person possesses such an amount of freedom for acquiring, using, and disposing of one's property that it may be compared with an acceptable state of property and mutual rights of others... Although thus property is protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the method and model of its use to the extent necessary to prevent abuse of rights that may lead to violation of the rights of another person or of society as a whole." To protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed wide powers throughout the 19th century. For instance, in 1837, the legislative body in the state of Illinois did not have enough space on three pages to list all the powers that the administration of Chicago would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transport and restricting "spinning a hoop, playing ball, or launching paper kites... capable of annoying people," the new city acquired the power to "compel the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle shop, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, outhouse, textile factory, or any other dilapidated, repulsive building or house to clean, remove, or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3–6).

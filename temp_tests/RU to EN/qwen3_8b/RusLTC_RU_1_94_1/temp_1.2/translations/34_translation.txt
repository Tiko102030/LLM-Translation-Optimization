In the late 19th century, expressing opposition to the opinion of "the multitude of dissatisfied people," attorneys and judges aimed to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tidemann, they demanded limiting the full extent of governmental power by establishing boundaries for the police's authority. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to determining private property. Even before the start of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. Pipel" (1856), the Appellate Court of New York State declared a law prohibiting the sale of liquor unconstitutional. After the law went into effect, all those who had sold this liquor had their property seized. However, the procedure of property confiscation did not proceed according to the legal order established by law. For the first time, the court recognized that the concept of "legal order" protected property, and ruled that "legislative power cannot entirely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thereby subjecting private property to complete extinction."

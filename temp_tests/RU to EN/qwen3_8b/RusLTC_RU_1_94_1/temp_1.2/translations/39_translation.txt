A similar situation arose in the Jacobus case, where Justice Rufus W. Peckham of the Supreme Court argued that individual freedom included not only the absence of "physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he desires; to earn his livelihood in any lawful manner; to pursue his chosen profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives." After the court's decision in the Allgeier case, the peak of judicial activity in articulating the rationale occurred eight years later in the "Lochner v. New York" case (1905), where the Court declared New York's law unconstitutional because it reduced the working hours of bakers. In his opinion before the Court, Justice Peckham emphatically stated that "the law compulsorily interferes with the right of contract between employer and employee," which constitutes a violation of "the general right to enter into contracts relating to the operations of his business," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Acknowledging that the police power regarding regulating property is justified since it protects "health or moral behavior," Peckham asserted that there was "no necessary justification for intervention in the individual's freedom or his right to enter into a free contract by reducing the working hours for the position of a baker." In conclusion, Peckham referred to this as a matter of which of the two evils is lesser: the state's power to enact laws or the individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limit of police power has been reached and fully addressed in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner New Deal could logically be defined based on other judicial decisions that affirmed property rights.

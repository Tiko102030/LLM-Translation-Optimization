The New York court applied an instrumental approach used in the Massachusetts case "Calhoun v. Marsh" to dismiss Quakenbush's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily, more or less, affect the comfort and property of people; and in the case where the preserved damage has a distant causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court stated: "This case is based on the principle that the public good should prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise  
Without prohibiting the government from improving the state's infrastructure at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedential law, as in the case of "Lansing v. Calhoun," shifted the obligation to some people to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case "Torp v. The Rutland and Buryington Railroad Company" (1855), Chief Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks, because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property.

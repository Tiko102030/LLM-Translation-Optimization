Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory wastewater into sewers, prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, thereby destroying private housing and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to health, such as drug production and cattle slaughter, and others of economic significance not directly related to health issues, such as the rights to trade and purchase, to sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). When regulation proved unable to achieve public goals, officials resorted to sabotage. For example, in 1872, the city marshal and twenty-five armed police entered the premises of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and began to "reduce" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking away all necessary parts (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

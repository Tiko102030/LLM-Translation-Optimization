"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938) and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of the judicial decisions of the 1930s, which became the cause of the spread of judicial pragmatism and the instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," in which the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, approved the state's actions concerning the regulation of property. The Court upheld the law of the state of Minnesota; this law imposed a moratorium on the forced sale of mortgaged property under conditions of an established adverse economic situation. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal accuracy as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information. He added that the Court should interpret this article only "in accordance with the state's reserved power to protect the vital interests of its residents." "The effectiveness of legislative action should not be evaluated by whether its direct or indirect impact on the contract terms, but rather by whether the solution presented was lawful and whether it was achieved through reasonable and appropriate methods." This reflects a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. The economic situation in the country may be the result of how a temporary limitation on the enforcement of contract conditions is reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, and thus, it does not exceed the state's reserved power to protect the vital interests of society. Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unlawful restriction of the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority. In this regard, whether the legislative body conducts a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not concern itself with.

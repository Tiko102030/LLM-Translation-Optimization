The ruling in the Mann case showed that property rights were now one of the main interests of society, another instrument for achieving social goals, and no constitutional guarantee was required anymore. Against the backdrop of increasingly growing social unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked greater emotional reactions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman 1999). After the events on Haymarket Street in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the right of God’s anointed did not allow limiting the police's powers. The spread of the policy of non-interference reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum swings again in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights is declared to be beyond the law, and now it is needed as if it were a panacea for any social evil threatening society's prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unrestrained throughout all civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak against the cunning of the stronger, to determine what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous discontented army and their obvious power in enforcing their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the conditions of universal suffrage, conservatives feared even more that a despotism more oppressive and more unreasonable than any that had existed before might emerge. In the late nineteenth century, when expressing opposition to the views of "the numerous discontented army," attorneys and judges intended to sanctify private property.

In the Jacobson case (1885), the New York Appellate Court declared a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in leased premises unconstitutional, because it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use his property," thereby "depriving him of his property and personal freedoms." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and protection of property rights, Justice Earl stated that, in a broad sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only the right to be free from actual servitude... but also the right of a person to use his abilities by all legal means to live and work... Therefore, any law that weakens or restricts these rights... is a violation of his fundamental rights to freedom. The legislative assembly argued that the ruling was based on the state's police powers, and that the measures taken were necessary to "improve the nation's healthcare system." For defenders of state regulation, it was not promising that the court considered this requirement, and subsequently, it concluded that it lacked the necessary amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law "in fact did not relate to health issues at all," and Justice Earl notified the legislative body that "under the pretext of police powers, no arbitrary interference with personal rights and private property is allowed." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of unprecedented judicial activism in the protection of property rights. For example, in the "Godcharles v. Wyman" case (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law is a violation of equal rights of the employer and the worker, more than that, it is an insulting attempt to subject an unskilled worker to legislative oversight," because it hindered workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

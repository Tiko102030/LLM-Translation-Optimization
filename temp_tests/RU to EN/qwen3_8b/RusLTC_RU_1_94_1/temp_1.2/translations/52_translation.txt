But after these icons of liberalism retired in the 1990s and 1991s, the Court introduced additional restrictions on state regulation. For example, in the case of Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council (1992), the Court required that a property owner in South Carolina be compensated for the damage caused to his property after a new "Coastal Zone Management Act" temporarily halted construction on his land. Again expressing distrust toward elected legislators, Scalia warned, as in the case of Nolan, that judges must carefully examine the regulations to protect themselves from the "risk that private property, under the guise of reducing serious harm to society, will be used for public purposes." The Court refined its new standards in the case of Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994), where courts were instructed to shift part of the burden of proof from the property owners to the government. The case was brought when the City of Tigard, Oregon administration, stated that it would approve Florence Dolan's plan to expand her plumbing store only if she donated part of her land for creating a public park, pedestrian and bike path to reduce traffic congestion. Writing for the Court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist argued that the city administration "did not dispute the fact that constructing pedestrian and bike paths at Dolan's expense was a result of the city administration's need to improve transportation infrastructure." The rulings in the Nolan and Dolan cases signal the beginning of a new era of enhanced scrutiny of property regulation. This may lead to property rights returning to the level they were at before the New Deal.

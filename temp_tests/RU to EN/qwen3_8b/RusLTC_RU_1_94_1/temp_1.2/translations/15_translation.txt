Consequently, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Governmental intervention in private property matters did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widely held notion of the government's moral obligation to foster a thriving economy and support the poor citizens. For example, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, restricted food trade, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and also restricted the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Henderson & Henderson 1947; Bergin 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the right to compulsory condemnation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

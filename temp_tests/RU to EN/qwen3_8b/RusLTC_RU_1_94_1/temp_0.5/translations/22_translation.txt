Property owned or used by African Americans did not possess the same degree of inviolability as that of property owned or used by European Americans. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, African Americans in the southern states gained the right to own property; however, their rights could change depending on the political climate of the state and the extent of African American participation in it. The same instrumental approach that the governments of the northern states used to limit property rights regarding clean air and water was also used in the southern states to maintain African Americans' dependence on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public goals, although the goals differed. Redistribution of property rights to stimulate economic growth
By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice Roger Taney of the Supreme Court of the United States of America in 1837 resorted to an instrumental approach that he had used in the Court to annul the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge company. He did this to manage the sole bridge over the Charles River connecting Boston and Cambridge (see the case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge company experienced some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... that had been invested in the construction of railroads and canals along the expressway lines... We would be thrown back a century to the inventions of that era and would be forced to remain motionless."

Society, on one hand, does not prohibit a property owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause harm to neighbors' property. On the other hand, it may support the affected neighbors and limit the activities of the property owner. This makes property rights distributive. When property owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of the loss of clean air and water or the cost of paying the company to stop dumping waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer without these privileges, as they would have to reduce pollution levels or purchase permission from neighbors to pollute. Some question the significant impact that property rights have on income distribution, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase argued that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social outcome, the same coordination of production of goods and services. Should those who pollute the environment purchase the right to do so from their neighbors, or should neighbors pay those who pollute the environment to stop doing so? Pollution will occur where the costs of reducing its negative consequences exceed the value of clean air to neighbors, and it will cease when the costs of reducing the negative impact are lower. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could sustain the developing railway network. At the apex of the entire financial pyramid was a recently established centralized banking system, founded in New York and created during the war (see Benzel 1984, 1990). Incensed workers and farmers saw the true aims of bankers and owners of the iron and railway companies, whose ownership was directly sponsored by governmental authorities. They desired new laws that would expand the public's authority in regulating property. They achieved remarkable success in securing the enactment of a law establishing an eight-hour workday and a law setting railroad rates. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, and as a result, these bills were sent for consideration to the Supreme Court. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case "Mann v. State of Illinois" greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of the popular radicalism of that time. The plaintiffs claimed that the law violated the clause of the Constitution regarding contracts by voiding existing agreements, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from state interference.

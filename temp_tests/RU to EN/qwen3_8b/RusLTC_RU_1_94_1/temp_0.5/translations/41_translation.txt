It appears that the above-mentioned case is based on John Locke's views regarding absolute property rights. The case also mentioned the final decision in the Mann case, which was handed down by Chief Justice Waite of the Supreme Court. However, attention must be paid to the last statement discussed earlier: "this is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again consciously re-established an instrumental vision of property rights, declaring the previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. Holmes had already expressed his opinion in the Lochner case. Beginning his speech with the assertion that "the decision was rendered according to the principles of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy a large part of the country's population," he added: "The right of each person to act as he wishes for as long as he wishes, provided, of course, that he does not infringe upon the right of others to do the same, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal administration, or the laws of any of the states or other municipal institutions that take his money to achieve goals that seem necessary, regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike Lochner's despotic protection of intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require achieving a balance. After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

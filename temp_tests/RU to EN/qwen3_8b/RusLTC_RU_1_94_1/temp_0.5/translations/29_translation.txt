The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. Reformers, however, wanted more. For example, labor rights defender George McNeil warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to the words of a railroad conductor, K.F. Breyse: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that today's trust companies are trying to impose on us. The outcome of the 1861 Civil War was the emancipation of Black slaves, however, today workers are nothing but slaves of the consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124–125). Moreover, the Civil War contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. The conduct of military operations required large sums of money, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed that a significant amount of financial and industrial capital was needed to sustain government operations. The ironworking and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could provide the expanding network of railroads.

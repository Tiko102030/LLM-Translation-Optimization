According to the court ruling: "A landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of soil to use it for purposes that infringe upon the rights of others and that could not have been achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that environmental protection zoning rules "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights by imposing limitations on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "eminent domain" taking, as in the Pennsylvania Coal Co. case. This was because "the use of property without altering the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding "eminent domain" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's lawyer, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already issued several decisions by 1987. For example, in the case "First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the county's flood control agency prohibited the restoration of a church's flood-damaged camping site. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a regulation depriving the owner of the use of their property must be necessary to 'give them the right to compensation for damages.'"

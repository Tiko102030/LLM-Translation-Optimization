Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory wastewater into sewers, which "endangered human health," prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private housing and storage facilities without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance not related to health issues, such as the right to trade and purchase, to sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84-94). When regulation failed to achieve the public goal, officials began to take strike action. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the city commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the territory of the fertilizer plant owned by the firm "Manhattan Fertilizing," and began to "mitigate" the harm caused by the plant's activities, destroying the machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, pp. 226).

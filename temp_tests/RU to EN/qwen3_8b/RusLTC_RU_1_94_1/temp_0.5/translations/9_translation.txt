Referring to the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature which in this way gives us property also limits the extent of this property. "God has given us all abundantly"—this is the voice of reason confirmed by inspiration. But for what purpose did he give us this? For our enjoyment. A man has a right to make his property by his labor as much as he can use for the necessities of his life before the thing is spoilt. Whatever goes beyond this limit exceeds his share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoil or destruction. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a means of preserving wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportionate and unequal ownership of land," gaining power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee access to the wealth of society for all. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation. Later, other authors joined these considerations, including the renowned radical American thinker, Thomas Paine.

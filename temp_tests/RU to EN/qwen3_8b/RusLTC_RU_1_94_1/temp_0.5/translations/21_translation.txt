Representative Lemuel Shaw of the Massachusetts Supreme Court supported the legislative authority dealing with the regulation of property within the Boston Harbor and prohibiting construction of buildings beyond this wharf, and regarding the rights of the police, he said the following: "We believe that this is an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every property owner, regardless of whether he possesses an exclusive right to it or not, manages it with the full responsibility implied thereby, so that its use should not cause harm to other people who also have the right to derive pleasure from the use of their own property, nor to society as a whole. All property in this community... is completely or partially owned by the government, and general rules applicable to it may be necessary for the purpose of achieving the public good and general welfare. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to the action of appropriate restrictions necessary to confirm that others have not suffered from the use of this property, and to the action of legal rules, since the legislative authority... may assume that this is a necessary and reasonable solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: 'It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits of its manifestation.' Indeed, by using universal, typical phrases such as 'public interest,' 'internal structure of the state,' 'implied right,' and 'obligation of the supreme authority to protect all,' and 'public good and general welfare,' Shaw established that the police authorities may have a broader set of tools to achieve their goals. Southern state governments resorted to the help of the police to enact laws limiting the use of property by slaves, for example, prohibiting slave owners from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they established unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation, which limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activity in certain types of professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of property and labor rights for African Americans once again demonstrates the understanding of property at that time. (See Coen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955).

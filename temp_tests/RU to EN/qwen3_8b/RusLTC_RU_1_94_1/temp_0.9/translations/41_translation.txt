It appears that the above-mentioned case is based on John Locke's views regarding absolute property rights. The case also referred to the final decision in the Mann case, which was handed down by Chief Justice Waite. However, it is important to note the last statement considered earlier: "this is merely a question of degree." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., once again consciously established an instrumental view of property rights, which declared the previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. Holmes had already expressed his opinion in the Lochner case. Beginning his speech by stating, "the decision was rendered according to the principles of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy most of the country's population," he added: "The right of each person to act as he wishes for as long as he wishes, provided, of course, that he does not infringe upon the right of others to do the same, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, postal service management, or the laws of any of the states or other municipal institutions that take his money to achieve objectives that seem necessary, regardless of whether he wants them or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. Unlike how Lochner despotically protects intensive property rights, Holmes believed that competing interests require achieving a balance. After losing the Lochner case, he carefully chose his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law.

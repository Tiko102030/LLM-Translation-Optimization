In the 1875 case "Kohler v. United States," it stated that the right to the compulsory taking of private property is "a result of political necessity and cannot be separated from sovereign power." The right to the compulsory taking of private property was one example of the use of governmental power to promote the development of a "well-ordered society," increase the level of the "public good" or "welfare" through regulations concerning interference with property owners' rights. These regulations reflected principles of common law such as: "the welfare of the people shall be the supreme law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est) and "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas). These fundamental principles, often cited in court decisions, serve as "principles of common law administration in a well-ordered society," providing a legal basis for comprehensive state regulation of property in order to reflect mutual interests and rights of all citizens (see Novak 1996, p. 42).

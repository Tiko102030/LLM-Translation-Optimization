"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938), and "United States v. Darby" (1941), were prototypes of judicial decisions of the 1930s, which, in fact, became the reason for the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the judicial decision of 1934 in the case of "Home Building & Loan Ass’n v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, approved the state's actions regarding the regulation of property. The Court upheld the law of the state of Minnesota; this law imposed a moratorium on the redemption of mortgaged property under an adverse economic situation established by legislation. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered "with literal precision as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, the interpretation of which is possible only with additional information." He added that the Court should interpret this article "only in accordance with the state's reserved right to protect its citizens' vital interests." "The efficiency of the legislative body's activities should not be assessed by whether its influence on the contract terms is direct or indirect, but rather by whether the decision was legally grounded and whether it was achieved by reasonable and appropriate methods." This reflects a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:
The country's economic situation may be the result of how a temporary limitation on the enforcement of contract terms is reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of the article, and thus does not exceed the state's reserved right to protect vital societal interests. ... Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unlawful restriction of the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority. ... At the same time, whether the legislative body conducts a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not interfere.

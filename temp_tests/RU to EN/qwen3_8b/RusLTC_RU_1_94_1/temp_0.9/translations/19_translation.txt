Local authorities regularly seized homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory waste into sewage systems, prohibited the production of hazardous chemicals, and extinguished fires, thereby destroying private residences and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, as well as other economically significant areas not directly related to health issues, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials would initiate strikes. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the city commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the territory of the fertilizer production plant owned by the firm "Manhattan Fertilizing" and began to "reduce" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary components with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

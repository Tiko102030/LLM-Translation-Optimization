Calender filed a claim for damages caused by the repair of the street, which resulted in damage to the foundation of his house in Boston. His attorney cited English laws, common law precedents, and this principle to seek compensation. However, the defense rejected the use of English precedents with an instrumental presentation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim, which Calender filed, 'is granted, then it would be necessary to review the correctness of repairs for all highways in our country.'" This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Calender's claim for compensation. Then, the Massachusetts state court adopted the state's proposal for a market interpretation of the case to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was extremely bad news for people whose property was directly adjacent to public structures. "Those who purchase property adjacent to a roadway," the court stated, "should take into account future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." For in such a case, the compensation amount would have doubled compared to the initial cost of repairs. Similar conclusions were reached in New York in 1828, in the case "Lansing vs. Smith Walter Quakenbush," where a claim was filed against the state of New York for damages due to the decline in dock prices after the construction of a new harbor in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Calender vs. Marsh" to reject Quakenbush's claims.

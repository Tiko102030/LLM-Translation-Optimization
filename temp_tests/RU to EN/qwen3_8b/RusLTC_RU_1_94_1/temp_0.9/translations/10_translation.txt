This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation. Later, other authors joined these considerations, including the renowned American radical Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reasons to regret such popular societal conclusions by Locke. For example, Richard Epstein, who accused Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, acknowledges that Locke created "not the slightest difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it can also serve as a socially neutral factor: the accumulation of one's own labor results does not diminish the significance of what is accessible to others. However, acknowledging the social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, that is, the rights of one person may harm another, for example, in cases of monopolizing resources previously accessible to all.

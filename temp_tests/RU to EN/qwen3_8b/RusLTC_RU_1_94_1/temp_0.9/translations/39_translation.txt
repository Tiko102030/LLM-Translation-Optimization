A similar situation was in court in the Jacobson case, and Judge Rufus W. Peckham of the Supreme Court argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn a living by any lawful means; to pursue the profession he loves and, for this purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned goals." After the court's decision in the Olmstead case, the peak of judicial activity in articulating the rationale was established eight years later in the case of "Lochner v. New York" (1905), where the Court declared the New York law unconstitutional because the law reduced the working hours of bakers. Appearing before the Court, Judge Peckham firmly stated that "the law necessarily infringes upon the right to enter into a contract between employer and employee," which constitutes a violation of "the general right to enter into a contract relating to the operation of his business," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Acknowledging the fact that police actions regarding regulating property are justified because they protect "health or moral conduct," Peckham argued that in this case, "there is no necessary justification for interfering with the freedom of a person or his right to enter into a free contract by reducing the working hours for the position of a baker." In conclusion, Peckham stated that this was merely a question of which of the two evils is lesser: the state's power to enact laws or the individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limits of police authority have been reached and fully addressed in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner era can logically be defined based on other judicial decisions that affirmed property rights.

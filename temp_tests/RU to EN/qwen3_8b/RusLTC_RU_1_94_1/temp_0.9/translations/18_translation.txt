Governor Kent of New York declared in 1826 that "each person possesses such an amount of freedom for acquiring, using, and disposing of their property that it can be compared with a tolerable state of property and mutual rights of others… Although, thus, property is protected… the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use to the extent necessary to prevent abuse of rights that may lead to violation of the rights of another person or society as a whole." In order to protect public welfare and take into account the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the entire 19th century. For instance, in 1837, the legislative body in the state of Illinois did not have three pages to enter into a catalog all the powers that the city of Chicago administration would have regarding property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing waterborne transportation and limiting "spinning of the ring, playing ball, or launching paper kites… capable of annoying people," the new city acquired the power to "force the owner or tenant of any grocery store, wine cellar, candle store, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, privy, sewing factory, or any other dilapidated, repulsive building or house to clean, move, or demolish it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3–6).

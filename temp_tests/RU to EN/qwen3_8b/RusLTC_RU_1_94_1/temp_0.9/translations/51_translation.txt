In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of "conditions related to the issuance of a land-use permit" does not constitute an "exaction" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement of general land-use rights "does not serve the alleged purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "is therefore an improper taking, and compensation should be provided." In conclusion, Scalia discussed the new standard: "Because there is no connection between the condition and the underlying purpose of the construction restriction, the new purpose becomes something else, unlike what it was before. Now, the government's main task is to obtain general land-use rights without any compensation... if the condition specified in the land-use permit is not considered a prohibition on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the construction restriction will not be a proper regulation of land use, but rather a 'present taking.' This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall filed a separate opinion. They rejected Scalia's request to reconsider the position of the legislative body's members. They argued that 'the proper standard for review... is whether the government can rationally determine whether the adopted measure can achieve the public purpose.' However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional limitations on government regulation.

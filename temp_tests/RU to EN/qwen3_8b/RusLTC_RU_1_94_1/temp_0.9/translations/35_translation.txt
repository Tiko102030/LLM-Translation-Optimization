In the Jacobson case (1885), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars within leased premises, because it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use his property," thus "depriving him of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal liberty and protection of property rights, Justice Earl stated that, in a broad sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only freedom from actual slavery... but also the right of a person to use his abilities in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, any laws that weaken or restrict these rights... constitute a violation of his fundamental right to freedom. The legislature claimed that the decision was based on the state's police powers and that the measures taken were necessary to "improve the country's health system." For advocates of state regulation, it was not promising that the court considered this requirement; in fact, the court concluded that it lacked sufficient evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law "did not actually relate to health issues at all," and Justice Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police powers, arbitrary intrusion into personal rights and private property cannot be justified." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an unprecedented judicial activism in protecting property rights. For example, in the case "Goddard v. Widjman" (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal tender (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law violated the equal rights of employer and employee, more than that, it was an insulting attempt to subject unskilled labor to legislative oversight," because it hindered workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

After losing the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law. Instead of relying on the majority's opinion of the legislators' views on natural rights and J. Locke's beliefs, he accused them of copying Spencer's "Social Statics." Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that more likely signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than the protection of the sacred notion of free labor. According to the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another social goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "General Law" (1881), he elaborated that the concept of absolute rights contradicts the foundations of a competitive market economy. As Holmes stated: "Law does not even aim to compensate a person for all the harm inflicted upon him... Anyone may establish any firm, and later, to survive in competitive struggle, he will poach his competitor's clients, and perhaps thereby even ruin him."

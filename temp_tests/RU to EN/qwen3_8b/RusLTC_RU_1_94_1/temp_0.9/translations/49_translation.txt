According to the court's ruling: "The owner of the land has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of the soil in order to use it for purposes that infringe on the rights of others and that could not have been achieved with its original composition." The court recognized that zoning rules for environmental protection "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing the violation of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "expropriation" of property, as occurred in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was because "the use of property without altering the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding "expropriations" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Under the leadership of Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already issued several decisions in 1987. For example, in the case "First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the flood control district prohibited the restoration of a flood-damaged church camp. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "a regulation that deprives the owner of the use of their property" is necessary to "grant them the right to compensation for damages."

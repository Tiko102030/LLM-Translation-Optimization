Citing the Bible, Locke asserts that the same natural law that grants us property in this manner also limits the extent of that property. "God has given us all abundantly" — this is the voice of reason, confirmed by inspiration. But why did He give us this? For our enjoyment. A person has the right to convert a thing into their property through their labor, as much as they can use it for the necessities of their life before the item is spoiled. And what goes beyond these limits exceeds their share and belongs to others. Nothing was created by God for spoilage or destruction. However, to Locke's dismay, the invention of money as a store of wealth allowed some to monopolize "disproportional and unequal ownership of land," and to wield power over others. Therefore, limitations on the accumulation of property are necessary to guarantee everyone access to society's wealth. This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions on natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; subsequently, other authors joined these considerations, including the renowned American radical, Thomas Paine.

The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general confirmation of these rights. Instead, through redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed certain types of property in comparison with other types of property. According to one legal historian, Willard Hurst, this approach is one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America — "the freedom of the creative energy of man." Despite the fact that the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. In theory, if the cost of a project were higher than the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the damage caused. However, in practice, the entire burden is transferred to the injured party, which only further encourages the number of projects undertaken by entrepreneurs, while all the risk and operational costs are placed on the neighbors. If property rights were more strictly enforced legally, high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by economizing on the costs of risk and operational expenses for entrepreneurs, the new principle has even more significantly contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

The plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated the constitutional provisions on contracts, which also violated the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Recently enacted to protect civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an integral civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights that protected private property rights from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. Rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee of property rights, the Court affirmed and even expanded past regulatory powers. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the behavior of its citizens toward each other and how people ought to use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice of England Hale's statement that when property "touches the interests of society, it is already by law no longer subject to private regulation alone." Remembering the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (lat. salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: "When an owner uses his property in a way that creates an interest for the public, in fact he himself wants that, and until that interest disappears, he must accept that now society controls his property, since it is necessary for the increase of public welfare. If he does not want society to control his property, he must simply cease using it." Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

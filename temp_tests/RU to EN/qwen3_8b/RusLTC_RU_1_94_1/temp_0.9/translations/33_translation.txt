The Mann case decision showed that property rights were now one of society's key interests, becoming another instrument for achieving social goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantees. Against the backdrop of increasing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked greater emotional reactions among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events at Haymarket Square in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tidemann (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of kings had not allowed limitations on police powers. The spread of non-intervention policies reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are uncontrolled in all civilized worlds. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to determine what wages a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied and their obvious power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the development of universal suffrage, conservatives feared even more that an autocracy more despotic and less reasonable than any that had existed before might emerge. In the late 19th century, attorneys and judges who opposed the opinion of the "numerous army of dissatisfied" intended to sanctify private property.

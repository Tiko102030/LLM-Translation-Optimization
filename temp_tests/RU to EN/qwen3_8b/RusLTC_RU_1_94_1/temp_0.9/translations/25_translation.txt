The New York court employed an instrumental approach used in the Massachusetts case "Calhoun v. Marsh" to dismiss Quakenbush's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement must necessarily, more or less, affect the comfort and property of people; and when the damage sustained has a remote causal connection and is predictable, no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the amount of damage itself should be considered part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." In conclusion, the court held: "this case is based on the principle that the public good must prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise  
Not prohibiting the government from carrying out state improvements at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedent law, as in the case of "Lansing v. Calhoun," shifted the obligation to some individuals to cover the costs associated with achieving public goals (see Horwitz 1977, p. 70). Later, such instrumental norms became private cases. For example, in the case "Torp v. Railroad Company 'Rutland and Ballston'" (1855), Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to fence their tracks, because "the protection of the state's public order implies the protection of the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and the protection of all property in the state according to the principle 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas).' The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property.

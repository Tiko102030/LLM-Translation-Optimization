The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now also one of society's interests, becoming another tool for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. Amid growing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and provoked stronger reactions from those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events at Haymarket Square in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tideman (1971 [1886]) warned that the authority of the anointed of God did not allow limiting police powers. The spread of non-intervention policy reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum swings once again in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled throughout the civilized world. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the more powerful, to determine what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous army of dissatisfied and their evident power to enforce their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the conditions of universal suffrage, conservatives feared even more that a despotism more oppressive and less reasonable than any that had existed before might emerge. In the late 19th century, when opposing the views of the "numerous army of dissatisfied," advocates and judges intended to sanctify private property.

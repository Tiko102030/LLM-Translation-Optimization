The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5 to 4, but Campbell's arguments found favor with the judges, and attorneys therefore feared growing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures and the redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Justice Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion left a stronger impression on the public than Justice Miller's majority opinion, which was later supplemented by arguments supporting the police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the fundamental rights to free labor had been violated in the state of Louisiana. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's teachings that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the essential part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." Field required twenty years to fully grasp the court's decision in the case "Slotter-House and Mann" before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case "Allgeier v. State of Louisiana," the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company without a license to conduct commercial activity in Louisiana, which essentially constituted a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation arose in the Jacob case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham demonstrated that personal freedom included not only the absence of "physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he chooses; to earn a livelihood by any lawful means; to pursue the desired profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives."

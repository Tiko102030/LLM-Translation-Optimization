There exists an "inalienable" right of property of the laborer, because: Although the earth and all lower creatures belong in common to all mankind, nevertheless ... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, according to the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever a person may take from that state in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and appends to it something that belongs to him personally, thereby making it his property... For since this labor is the inalienable property of the laborer, no one else can have a right to that to which he once attached it, at least in those cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it is to use someone else's labor, that is, to enslave a person. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, owners form society and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

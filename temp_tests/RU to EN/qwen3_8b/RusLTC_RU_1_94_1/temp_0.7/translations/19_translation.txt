Local authorities regularly confiscated houses used for immoral activities, dumped factory waste into sewers, prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private homes and storage facilities without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, as well as others with economic significance not related to health issues, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, pp. 84–94). When regulation failed to achieve its public objective, officials began to resort to force. For example, in Jersey City in 1872, the police commissioner and twenty-five armed officers entered the premises of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and began "reducing" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

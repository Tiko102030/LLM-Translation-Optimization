Regarding this case, as with all American law, property rights, initially necessary to achieve public objectives, were immediately revoked as soon as they hindered the fulfillment of these objectives. In the beginning of the 19th century, governmental authorities aiming to stimulate industry and trade launched a campaign for the construction of roads and railways, canals. During the construction of the state's infrastructure, indirect costs, expenses related to damage to adjacent property, and losses in the value of unimproved property had to be borne. According to the legal principle "use your own so as not to harm another" (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas), investors would bear responsibility for indirect costs, significantly increasing the expenses of financing inventions. When the meaning of this principle was changed, i.e., when independent entrepreneurs avoided responsibility for the consequences of their actions regarding adjacent property, the costs of constructing state infrastructure would sharply decrease. The transformation of this principle began in 1823 in the case "Calender v. Marsh," establishing a precedent for the state of Massachusetts. Calender filed a claim for damages caused to him during street repair, as a result of which the foundation of his house in Boston was damaged.

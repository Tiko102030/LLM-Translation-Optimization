British property was assessed to an even lesser extent: debts to English creditors were annulled without much deliberation; some states, including Virginia, closed cases designated for hearings on claims asserted by British subjects (see Elie 1998, p. 35). Widespread confiscation of property from opponents of independence from England was not a good omen for property security in the new republic. The main rebels fought to curb what they considered as "excess democracy" (see Tomlinson 1993, p. 60). Ensuring property security, "the defender of any right" became a concern for the creators of the first state constitutions (see Elie 1998, p. /think).

The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, through the redistribution of rights, it merely affirmed some form of property in comparison to another form of property. According to one legal historian, Willard Hurst, this approach represents one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America—"the freedom of the creative energy of man." Although the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on the entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. Theoretically, even if the cost of a project exceeded the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of the damage caused. However, in practice, the entire burden is shifted onto the injured party, which only to a greater extent encourages the number of projects undertaken by entrepreneurs, while all the risks and operational costs are placed on the neighbors. If property rights were enforced to a greater legal extent, high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by reducing the costs of risk and operational expenses for entrepreneurs, the new principle has further facilitated the development of entrepreneurship.

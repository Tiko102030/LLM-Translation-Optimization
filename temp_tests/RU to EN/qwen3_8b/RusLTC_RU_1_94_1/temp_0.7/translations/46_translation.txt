"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938), and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of judicial decisions from the 1930s, which, in turn, contributed to the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in response to the country's economic instability, approved state actions regarding property regulation. The Court upheld the Minnesota statute, which imposed a moratorium on the right of redemption of mortgaged property under conditions of an established adverse economic situation. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, whose interpretation is possible only with additional context. He added that the Court should interpret this article "only in accordance with the reserved state power to protect the vital interests of its residents." "The effectiveness of legislative action should not be evaluated by whether its direct or indirect impact on the contract terms, but by whether the decision was lawfully made and whether it was achieved through reasonable and appropriate methods." This reflects a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that: "The economic situation in the country may be the result of how a temporary restriction on the enforcement of contractual terms is reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of the clause, thus not exceeding the reserved state power to protect the vital interests of society.... Since this clause of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unlawful restriction of the state's protectionist policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority.... At the same time, whether the legislative body conducts a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not concern itself with that."

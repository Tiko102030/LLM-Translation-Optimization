A similar situation arose in the Jacobus case, where Justice Rufus W. Peckham of the Supreme Court argued that personal freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn a living by any lawful means; to pursue his chosen profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned objectives." After the court's decision in the Allgeier case, the peak of judicial activism in the articulation of motives occurred eight years later in the case "Lochner v. New York" (1905), where the Court declared New York's law unconstitutional, as it reduced working hours for bakers according to that law. Appearing before the Court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law inevitably interferes with the right to enter into a contract between employer and employee," which constitutes a violation of "the general right to enter into contracts relating to the operations of his business," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Acknowledging that the actions of the police in regulating property are justified because they protect "health or moral conduct," Peckham asserted that in this case, "there is no necessary justification for interfering with personal freedom or his right to enter into a free contract by reducing the working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham stated that this was merely a question of which of the evils is lesser: the state's power to enact laws or the individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limits of police authority have been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner era could logically be defined based on other judicial decisions that affirmed property rights.

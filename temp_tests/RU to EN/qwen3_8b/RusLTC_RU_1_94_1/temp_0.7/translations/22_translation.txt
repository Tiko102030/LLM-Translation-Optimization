After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, African Americans from the Southern states gained the right to own property, however, their rights could change depending on the political climate of the state and the level of African American participation in it. The same instrumental approach that the governments of the Northern states used to limit property rights regarding clean air and water was also used in the Southern states to maintain African Americans' dependence on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined to achieve public goals, only the goals differed. Redistribution of property rights in order to stimulate economic growth
By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through the redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney in 1837 resorted to the instrumental approach that he had used in the Court to invalidate the contractual rights of the Charles River Bridge company. He did this in order to independently manage the sole bridge across the Charles River connecting Boston and Cambridge (see the case "Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge"). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge company experienced some difficulties. The company could lose "millions of dollars... that had been invested in the construction of railroads and canals on the lines of the express highways... We would be sent back a century to the inventions of that era and would be forced to remain motionless."

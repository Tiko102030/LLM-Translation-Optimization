Society, on one hand, does not prohibit a property owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to the property of neighbors. On the other hand, it may support the affected neighbors and limit the activities of the given property owner. This makes property rights distributive. When property owners have the right to pollute the environment, this reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of the loss of clean air and water or the cost that would need to be paid to the firm to stop it from discharging waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer without these privileges, because they would have to reduce pollution levels, or they would have to buy permission to pollute from their neighbors. Some question the fact that property rights significantly influence the distribution of income, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase claimed that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear definition of boundaries will lead to the same efficient social outcome, the same combination of production of goods and services. Should those polluting the environment buy the right to do so from their neighbors, or should neighbors pay those polluting the environment to stop doing so? Environmental pollution will occur where the cost of reducing its negative consequences is greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will cease when the cost of reducing the negative effect becomes lower. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

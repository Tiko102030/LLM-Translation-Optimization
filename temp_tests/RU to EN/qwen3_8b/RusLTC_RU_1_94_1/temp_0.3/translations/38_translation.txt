The butchers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure regarding the expansion of regulatory measures, redistribution of income, property, and power. Today, Judge Stephen J. Field's dissenting opinion left a stronger impression than Judge Miller's opinion, which was supported by a majority vote and later supplemented by arguments in favor of police powers. Like Campbell, Field argued that the fundamental rights to free labor had been violated in the state of Louisiana. Citing Adam Smith, he based his defense of property rights on John Locke's teachings that since free labor is the ultimate source of property, property is "the essential part of freedom... in the American sense of the word." It took Field twenty years to fully understand the court's decision in the case of "Sloter-House and Mann" before his views were adopted by the Supreme Court. In 1897, in the case of "Allgeier v. State of Louisiana," the Court declared the state law unconstitutional. According to this law, a person could not obtain an insurance policy from a company that did not hold a license to conduct commercial activities in the state of Louisiana, which in essence constituted a violation of the freedom guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment. A similar situation arose in the Jacobson case, and Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that freedom of a person included not only the absence of "his physical restraint" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn a living in any lawful manner; to pursue his chosen profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful achievement of the aforementioned objectives."

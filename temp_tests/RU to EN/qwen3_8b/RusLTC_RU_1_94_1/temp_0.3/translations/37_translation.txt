The Louisiana Supreme Court adopted a law stating that "losses of private rights in this case are irrelevant in light of the overall profit and commercial turnover of this business entity" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230-31). The butchers of New Orleans brought their case to the United States Supreme Court, where their interests were represented by John A. Campbell, Field's legal predecessor, a Democrat and assistant war minister (see Foner 1988, pp. 530). Working in the Supreme Court prior to the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the core principles of labor legislation to persuade the Court to protect a citizen's right to property ownership. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the city of New Orleans' rulings had returned us to a time when "the persistent eye of government followed the butcher to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The butchers of New Orleans lost their case 5-4, but Campbell's arguments resonated with the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure for expanded regulatory measures, with income, property, and power being redistributed.

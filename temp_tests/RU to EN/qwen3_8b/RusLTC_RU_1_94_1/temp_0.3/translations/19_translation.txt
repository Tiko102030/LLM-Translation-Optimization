Local authorities regularly confiscated homes where immoral activities took place, dumped factory wastewater into sewers, banned the production of hazardous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private residences and storage facilities without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as pharmaceutical production and livestock slaughter, as well as other economically significant areas not directly related to healthcare, such as the rights to trade and purchase, to sell and produce (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). When regulation failed to achieve public goals, officials resorted to strikes. For example, in 1872 in Jersey City, the city commissioner and twenty-five armed police entered the territory of the Manhattan Fertilizing fertilizer plant and began "reducing" the harm caused by the plant's activities, destroying machinery and taking all necessary components (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

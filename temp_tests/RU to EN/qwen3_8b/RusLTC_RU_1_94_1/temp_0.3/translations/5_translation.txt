The new paradigm of property rights acknowledges various definitions of property, however, it still considers "property rights" as relations between humans and objects (see Barceló 1997; Liebknecht 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As relations between humans and objects, property exists without human society; for instance, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Just as relations between humans and objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it serves as a reward for labor, which forms the non-human material world.

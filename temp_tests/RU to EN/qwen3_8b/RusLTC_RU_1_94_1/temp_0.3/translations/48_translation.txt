However, the most significant aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, which stated that the majority of the Court equated the concept of property rights with the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights. According to Stone, in cases concerning economic legislation, the Court intends to apply the principle of the presumption of constitutionality of laws, and the elected members of the legislative body will be accountable for evaluating economic regulations and restrictions on property rights. Since such rules are established for instrumental reasons, the Court does not consider it necessary to balance economic interests with legislative actions. Stone, on the contrary, warned that there will be a thorough investigation into violations of personal freedoms, such as freedom of speech or freedom of religious choice. Environmental protection and the expropriation procedure. After issuing the decision in the Caroline Products case, the Supreme Court allowed elected democratic members of the legislative body broad rights in selecting objects of social policy, even though they had restricted the actions of property owners. Environmental protection was one of the main goals of the policy pursued in this context. The case "Just v. Marinette County" from 1972 in Wisconsin clearly illustrates how the judicial power easily applied the position from the Caroline Products case. Ronald and Katherine L. Just filed a lawsuit regarding the repeal of regulations preventing them from occupying the designated swampy areas for building homes. The Wisconsin Supreme Court rejected the Just family's claims and ruled that legislative activity is a lawful use of police powers. Later, it wanted to approve a broader list of property rights restrictions.

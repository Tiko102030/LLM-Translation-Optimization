Later, he sought to approve a broader list of property rights restrictions. According to the court ruling: "A landowner has no absolute and unlimited right to alter the natural composition of soil for purposes that violate the rights of others and that could not be achieved with its original composition." The court acknowledged that zoning regulations for environmental protection "must be reasonable," but "we assume that preventing violations of public rights by imposing restrictions on the use of private property for its original purposes is justified." There was no "taking" of property, as in the Pennsylvania Coal case. This was because "the use of property without altering the natural composition of its soil is permitted by law." Property rights advocates achieved greater success in litigation regarding "takings" after Ronald Reagan became president in 1980. Led by Reagan's attorney, Antonin Scalia, the Court had already issued several decisions in 1987. For example, in the case of "First Anglican Evangelical Lutheran Church v. Los Angeles County" (1987), the Court required the Los Angeles County administration to pay compensation after the county's flood control district prohibited the restoration of a flood-damaged church campsite. The Court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an order depriving the owner of using their property is necessary to 'give them the right to compensation for damages.'"

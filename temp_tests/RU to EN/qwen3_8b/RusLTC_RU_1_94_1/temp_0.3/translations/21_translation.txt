Lemuel Shaw, a representative of the Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts, supported the legislative authority dealing with the regulation of property within Boston Harbor and prohibiting construction beyond the wharf. Regarding the rights of police power, he stated as follows: "We consider this to be an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where every property owner, regardless of whether they possess exclusive rights to it or not, manages it with the full responsibility implied, so that its use should not cause harm to other individuals who also have the right to enjoy the use of their own property, nor to society as a whole. All property in this community... is either fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules applicable to achieve the public good and general welfare may be applied to it. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to the necessary restrictions to ensure that no one else is harmed by the use of this property, and to the rules established by law, since legislative authority... may assume that this is a necessary and appropriate solution. In conclusion, Shaw added: 'It is much easier to feel and understand life and the sources of its power than to establish boundaries or limits of its manifestation.' Indeed, by using universal, typical phrases such as 'public interest,' 'internal structure of the state,' 'implied right,' and 'the obligation of the highest authority to protect all,' and 'general welfare and general well-being,' Shaw established that the police authorities may possess a broader set of tools to achieve their objectives. Southern state governments resorted to the assistance of police power to enact laws limiting the use of property, for example, prohibiting slave owners from teaching their enslaved people to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they imposed unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as implementing a policy of strict racial segregation, which limited the sale of property to African Americans or their employment in certain types of professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of property and labor for African Americans once again demonstrates the understanding of property at that time. (See Cohen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955.)

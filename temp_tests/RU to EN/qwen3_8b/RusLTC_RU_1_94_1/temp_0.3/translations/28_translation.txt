The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights. Instead, through redistribution of rights, it merely asserted some form of property in comparison to another form of property. According to one historian of law, Willard Hurst, this approach represents one of the working principles of the legal order of the United States of America—"the release of the full creative energy of mankind." Although the burden of paying compensation to neighbors for indirect damages remains on the entrepreneurs, they still have the opportunity to proceed with profitable projects. In theory, if the cost of a project were higher than the amount of damage caused, its profitability would still increase, even if the entrepreneur had to pay the amount of damage caused. However, in practice, all the burden is shifted to the injured party, which only to a greater extent encourages the number of projects undertaken by entrepreneurs, while all the risk and operational costs are placed on the shoulders of neighbors. If property rights were respected to a greater legal extent, high operational costs would hinder the realization of entrepreneurs' plans. Thus, by reducing the costs of risk and operational expenses for entrepreneurs, this new principle has further contributed to the development of entrepreneurship.

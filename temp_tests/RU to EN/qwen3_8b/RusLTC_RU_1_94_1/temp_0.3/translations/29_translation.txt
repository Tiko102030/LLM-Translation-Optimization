The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which liberated the country from slavery. Reformers, however, sought even more. For example, labor rights advocate George McNeil warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to the words of a railroad conductor, K.F. Breyse: "The Declaration of Independence was not created with the purposes that today the trust companies try to impose upon us. The result of the 1861 Civil War was the emancipation of black slaves, yet today, workers are nothing but slaves of the consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. Conducting military operations required substantial financial resources, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed the necessity of significant financial and industrial capital capable of supporting government activities. The ironworking and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones able to supply the developing network of railroads.

The plaintiffs argued that by repealing existing contracts, the law violated the constitution's contract clause, which also violated the recently enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Enacted recently to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment is now an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights that protected private property from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "the government may regulate and the behavior of its citizens towards each other, and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice Hale, stating that when property "touches the interests of society, it is already by law no longer subject to private regulation alone." Remembering the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when a property owner uses their property in such a way that the public develops an interest in it, in fact, he himself desires this, and until that interest disappears, he must simply accept that now society controls his property, since it is necessary for the purpose of increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to control his property, he must simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

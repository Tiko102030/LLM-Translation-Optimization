There exists an "inalienable" right of property of the laborer, because: Although the earth and all lower creatures belong to all people in common, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, according to the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever a person takes from the state in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and attaches to it something that belongs to him personally, thereby making it his own property... For since this labor is an inalienable property of the laborer, no one else can have a right to what he has once attached it to, at least in those cases when a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone else's labor, i.e., making a person a slave. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, owners form societies and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

INTRODUCTION: The Concept of Property in Economic Theory and Social Practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ based on the definition they provide for the concept of property, and often, the boundaries can be difficult to define. Some believe that property rights extend to such items as land and trees, including the most ephemeral objects such as living nature and flowing water. Others expand this concept by asserting that property rights also apply to such objects as ideas (patents), and some even include humans themselves as property. Societies also differ based on the rights that property owners possess; a set of privileges related to responsibility for actions taken towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because every use of it affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, since it involves human relationships; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be fully free. For example, when the environment is polluted, neighbors' rights to clean air and water significantly decrease; laws prohibiting pollution of the environment limit the rights of the given property but protect the neighbors' property. Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using their land and water resources, even if it may cause damage to the property of neighbors.

The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts by voiding existing agreements, thereby also violating the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference. Recently enacted to protect civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property rights, protecting private property from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court members. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property rights, the Court upheld and even expanded the power of past regulation. Stating that "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and the behavior of its citizens towards one another, and how people ought to use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice Hale of England's statement that when property "touches the interests of society, it is by law no longer subject to private regulation alone." Recalling the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when an owner uses his property in a manner that gives rise to public interest in it, in fact he himself desires this, and until that interest disappears, he must accept that society now controls his property, since this is necessary for the increase of public welfare. If he does not wish society to control his property, he must simply cease its use. Examining more closely the boundaries of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

A similar situation arose in the Jacobus case, where Supreme Court Justice Rufus W. Peckham argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "physical limitation" but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn his living by any lawful means; to pursue his chosen profession and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts which may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives." Following the court's decision in the Allgeyer case, the peak of judicial activity in articulating the rationale was established eight years later in the case "Lochner v. New York" (1905), where the Court declared New York's law unconstitutional because it reduced working hours for bakers. Appearing before the Court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law necessarily infringes upon the right of contract between employer and employee," which constitutes a violation of "the general right to enter into contracts relating to the operations of his business," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Acknowledging that police actions regarding the regulation of property are justified because they protect "health or moral conduct," Peckham asserted that in this case "there is no necessary justification for interfering with human freedom or his right to enter into a free contract by reducing working hours for the position of baker." In conclusion, Peckham stated that this was merely a question of which of the two evils is lesser: the state's power to enact laws or the individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limits of police power have been reached and fully considered in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner New Deal can logically be defined based on other judicial decisions that affirmed property rights.

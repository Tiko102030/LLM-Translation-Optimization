The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the full power of the national government, which freed the country from slavery. Reformers, however, wanted even more. For example, labor rights advocate George Macneil warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to the words of the train conductor, K.F. Breyse: "The Declaration of Independence was not created with the purposes that today trust companies try to impose upon us. The outcome of the 1861 Civil War was the emancipation of Black slaves, yet today, workers are nothing other than slaves of consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, pp. 99, 124–125). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. Conducting military operations required substantial funds, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed the need for significant financial and industrial capital capable of supporting government activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones able to supply the developing network of railways.

The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights were now one of society's interests, another instrument for achieving public goals, and no longer required any constitutional guarantee. In the context of increasingly growing societal unrest, this approach increasingly alarmed conservatives and caused a greater outburst of feelings among those who feared that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events that took place on Haymarket Street in 1886, the prominent legal scholar Christopher Tideman (1971 [1886]) warned that the divine right of the policeman did not allow limiting the police's authority. The spread of the hands-off policy reduced the number of "government violations of human rights and freedoms." But now:
the political pendulum is swinging back in the opposite direction... Government intervention in human rights has been declared lawful, and now it is needed as though it were a panacea for any social evil threatening societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are uncontrolled throughout all civilized society. The state is called upon to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to establish what wage a worker should receive for his labor, and how many hours he should work daily...
Considering these unusual demands of the numerous army of the discontented and their evident power in implementing their ideas about a civil state in the civilized world, especially under the conditions of universal suffrage, conservatives feared even more that such an autocracy might emerge, more despotic and more unreasonable than any that had existed before. In the late 19th century, expressing opposition to the opinion of the "army of the discontented," lawyers and judges aimed to sanctify private property.

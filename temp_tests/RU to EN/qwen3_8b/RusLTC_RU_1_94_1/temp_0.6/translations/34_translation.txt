In the late 19th century, expressing opposition to the opinion of "the multitude of discontented soldiers," attorneys and judges aimed to sanctify private property. Frequently citing Tidemann, they demanded the limitation of the full extent of state power by establishing boundaries for the authority of the police. State courts took the initiative in defining new foundations for a sacred, rather than instrumental, approach to determining private property. Even before the beginning of the Civil War, in the case of "Wainhamer v. Pipple" (1856), the New York Appellate Court declared a law prohibiting the sale of liquor unconstitutional. After the law took effect, all those who had sold this liquor lost their property. At the same time, the procedure for seizing property did not follow the legal order established by law. For the first time, the court recognized that the concept of "legal order" protected property, and ruled that "legislative power cannot entirely abolish commercial activity in any of its manifestations, thereby subjecting property to complete disappearance."

In this regard, in the case of Pennsylvania Coal v. Holmes, Holmes more acted as a Trojan Horse rather than as a winner-defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the "principle of taking" on the basis of balancing competing interests. Compensation for damages will depend on circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should constantly be "reasonably" used, but the notion of what "reasonably" means remains vague. If actions go "too far," in such cases, compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it will be the judges, taking into account the existence of public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what "too far" means. Neither private property owners nor the public have absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully assess and balance the competing interests of both sides. Property owners, in turn, should not set all against themselves. Holmes asserted that "the government will hardly be able to continue its activities further if, to some extent, the issue of property rights is not resolved."

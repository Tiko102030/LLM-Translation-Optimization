The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to recover damages." The court's decision in the case of the First Anglican Church, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, was not based on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the core principles of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nolan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nollans, who owned a coastal parcel of land situated between public parks to the north and south, wanted to build a new three-bedroom house in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other houses in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only under the condition that the Nollans agreed to grant a common right of use over the territory between the line of high tide and the dike behind their home. This way, people could easily access the parks located both to the north and south by simply strolling leisurely along the sand. The commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger house would contribute to "filling the territory," which "people would subconsciously no longer realize that they are near the shore and that they have the full right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

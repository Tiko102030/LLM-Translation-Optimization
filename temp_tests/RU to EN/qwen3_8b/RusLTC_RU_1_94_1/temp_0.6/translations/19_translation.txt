Local authorities regularly confiscated homes used for immoral activities, dumped factory wastewater that "endangered human health," prohibited the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, destroying private residences and storage facilities without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as drug production and livestock slaughter, and others with economic significance unrelated to health, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sale and production (see Novak 1996, pp. 84–94). When regulation failed to achieve the public goal, officials began to strike. For example, in 1872, the city commissioner and twenty-five armed police officers entered the territory of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and began to "reduce" the harm caused by the plant's operations, destroying machinery and taking all necessary parts with them (see Novak 1996, p. 226).

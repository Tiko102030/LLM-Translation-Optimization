The Supreme Court of Louisiana adopted a law stating that "losses of private rights in this case are of no significance in light of the overall profit and trade turnover of this commercial enterprise" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230–31). The meatpackers of New Orleans brought their case before the U.S. Supreme Court, where their interests were represented by John A. Campbell, legal predecessor of Field, a Democrat and assistant secretary of war (see Foner 1988, p. 530). While working in the Supreme Court prior to the war, Campbell ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the core principles of labor law to persuade the Court to protect a citizen's right to property. Citing the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the city of New Orleans' rulings had returned us to a time when "the government's nagging eye followed the meatpacker to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The meatpackers of New Orleans lost their case five to four, but Campbell's arguments found resonance among the judges, and attorneys therefore feared the growing pressure that expanding regulatory measures would lead to the redistribution of income, property, and power.

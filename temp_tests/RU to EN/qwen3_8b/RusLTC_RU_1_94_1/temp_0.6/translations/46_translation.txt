"United States v. Carolene Products" (1938), and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of judicial decisions from the 1930s, which, in turn, became the cause of the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Assn. v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, approved the state's actions regarding the regulation of property. The Court upheld the law of the state of Minnesota; this law imposed a moratorium on the right of redemption in foreclosed property under conditions of an adverse economic situation established by law. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, whose interpretation is possible only with additional context. He added that the Court should interpret this article only "in accordance with the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of its residents." "The efficiency of legislative action should not be evaluated by whether it directly or indirectly affects the terms of the contract, but rather by whether the decision was legally justified and whether it was achieved by reasonable and appropriate methods." This indicates a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. The economic situation in the country may be the result of how a temporary limitation on the enforcement of contractual terms is reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of the article, and thus will not exceed the reserved right of the state to protect the vital interests of society. Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unlawful restriction of the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in such a way as not to violate legislative authority.

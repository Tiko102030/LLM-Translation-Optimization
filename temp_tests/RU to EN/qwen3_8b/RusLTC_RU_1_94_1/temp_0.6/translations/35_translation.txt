In the Jacobs case (1885), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in leased premises, because it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use his property," thus "depriving him of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of personal freedom and property rights, Justice Earl stated that, in a broad sense, and as understood in our country, freedom means not only the right to be free from actual servitude... but also the right of a person to use his abilities in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, all laws that weaken or restrict these rights... constitute violations of his fundamental right to freedom. The legislature claimed that the decision was based on the state's police powers, and the measures taken were necessary to "improve the country's public health system." It was not favorable for proponents of governmental regulation that the court considered this requirement, and later it concluded that it lacked the necessary amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law "did not in fact relate to health issues," and Justice Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police powers, one cannot arbitrarily intrude into personal rights and private property." The Jacobs case marked the beginning of an unprecedented judicial activism in the protection of property rights. For example, in the case "Godcharles v. Widjman" (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (rather than company stock). The court asserted that "the law was a violation of the equal rights of employer and employee, more than that, it was an insulting attempt to subject an unqualified worker to legislative guardianship," because it hindered workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

In reality, the Thirteenth Amendment codifies in the U.S. Constitution the principle of property confiscation. Under this amendment, confiscation of slave property without compensation redistributed almost $3 billion, half of all the wealth of the Southern states. As a result, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Government intervention into private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. Throughout the colonial and pre-war period, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again affirming the widely held belief that the moral duty of government is to promote a prosperous economy and support the welfare of the poor. For instance, most local authorities established transportation tariffs and bread prices, regulated food trade, controlled wage levels and employment opportunities, and restricted the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Hendlin and Hendlin, 1947; Berger, 1989; Novak, 1996; Lori, 1999; Levi, 2000). Local authorities also used the right of compulsory alienation of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

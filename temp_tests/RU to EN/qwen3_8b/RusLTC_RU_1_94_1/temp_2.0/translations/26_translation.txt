Use your own so as not to harm others. Applying this principle ensures access to the social interpretation of property. Judge Redfield called for recognizing limitations on property rights because, in his view, actions by one owner affect others even when carried out on their own property. However, the principle "use your own so as not to harm others" is merely a norm, not a rule; it should not be followed in most cases where conflicting property interests are involved and where both parties accuse the neighbor of trespassing on their territory (see Holmes, 1894, p. 11). Issues concerning the subject of property were reevaluated in the 19th century to promote entrepreneurship, even at the expense of causing damage to others' property. Thus, the principle "use your own so as not to harm others" remained but was now used to protect entrepreneurs' right to act according to the requirements of passive neighbors who wanted their property to remain untouched. Now, the concept of "harm" was not considered as a series of wrongful acts concerning another's property, but rather as the inability of owners to use their own. The new approach allowed entrepreneurs not to pay compensation for harm caused by using their property. It first became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" in the state of New York (1805).

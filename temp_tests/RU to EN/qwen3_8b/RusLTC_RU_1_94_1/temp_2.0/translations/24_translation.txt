Calender filed a claim for damages caused by the repair of the street, which damaged the foundation of his home in Boston. His attorney cited English laws, common law precedents, and this principle in order to seek compensation. However, the defense rejected the use of English precedent with an instrumental portrayal of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a claim," which Calender had filed, "were to be accepted, then it would be necessary to examine the correctness of repair of all highways in our country." This argument resonated with the judges, who dismissed Calender's claim for compensation. Then, the state of Massachusetts court adopted the state's offer for a market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent claims for damages, which was far from good news for people whose property directly adjacent to public structures. "Those who purchase property adjacent to a roadway," the court said, "must take into consideration the future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." For in such a case, the amount of compensation would have doubled compared to the original cost. Such conclusions were drawn in New York in 1828 in the case "Lancing v. Smith Walter Quakenbush" where a claim was brought against the state of New York for damages caused by the drop in the value of docks after the construction of a new harbor in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the "Calender v. Marsh" case of the state of Massachusetts to reject Quakenbush's demands.

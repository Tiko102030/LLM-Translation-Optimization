Rejecting any privileged status of private property, Hughes concluded that:
The economic conjuncture in the country may be the result of how a temporary restriction on the compulsory execution of contract conditions will be reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of its article, thus not exceeding the reserved state right to protect vital societal interests...
Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and entirely unlawful restriction of the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in a manner that does not violate legislative authority...
In this regard, whether the policy conducted by the legislative body is reasonable or unreasonable, the Court will not concern itself with it. Already in 1934, when the Court was ready to abandon evaluating economic legislation and examining cases related to property rights, the legislative body already then proclaimed the necessity of "protecting vital societal interests." After Roosevelt's re-election as president in 1936 and his attempts to "move" the Court in 1937, the Court had practically fully agreed with his reform program. No other decision has so clearly illustrated the new approach in the Court's operations as the case "United States v. Carolene Products Co. (1938)."
In this specific case, the company was sued under the 1923 "Skim Milk Laws," which prohibited the interstate sale of milk containing fat and oil, treating it as the sale of "intentionally ruined food product harmful to health." The company's counsel argued that the law unreasonably restricted freedom of contract. Rejecting the arguments supporting property rights, the Court considered the law valid, since it regarded it as a fair instrument of state regulation necessary to protect public health. The decision itself would have merely been an ordinary, simple affirmation of the established police power. However, the most important aspect of this case was not the decision itself, but the fourth footnote by Justice Stone, according to which the majority of the Court shared the concept of property rights and the bundle of sacred civil liberties guaranteed by the Bill of Rights.

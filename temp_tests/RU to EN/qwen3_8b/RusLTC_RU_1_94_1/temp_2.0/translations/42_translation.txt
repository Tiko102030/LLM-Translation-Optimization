Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to initiate a new case for an alternative law. Instead of relying on the majority opinion of the legislative assembly members regarding natural rights and John Locke's convictions, he accused them of copying "Social Statics of Spencer." Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that more likely signifies the end of productivity and prosperity rather than the protection of a sacred notion of free labor. According to the instrumental approach's provisions, property rights become susceptible to the bases of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another social objective. This was not the first time that Holmes had challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "The Common Law" (1881), he thoroughly discussed how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the preconditions for creating a competitive market economy. As Holmes said: "The law even does not strive to reimburse the man for the entire damage inflicted upon him... Any one may establish any sort of business, and then, in order to survive in a competitive struggle, he will attract the clients of his competitor, and perhaps thereby even ruin him." In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy that has nothing in common with morality" (Holmes 1881, p.).

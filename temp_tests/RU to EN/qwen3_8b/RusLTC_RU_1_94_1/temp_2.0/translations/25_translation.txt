The New York court applied an instrumental approach used in the Massachusetts case of "Calendary v. Marsh" to dismiss Quin's claims. The court stated: "Any significant public improvement inevitably and more or less affects the comfort and property of people; and when the damage sustained has a remote causative connection and is predictable, then no compensation is recognized for the damage, and the sum of the damage itself should be considered part of the price paid for the future benefits of this public improvement." The court concluded: "This case is based on the principle that the public good must prevail over private interests." State support for private enterprise  
Not prohibiting the government from undertaking improvements at the expense of adjacent property owners, precedent law, as in the case of "Lansing v. Calendary," imposed an obligation on some people to bear costs related to the achievement of public goals (see Horowitz 1977, p. 70). In the future, such instrumental norms became private cases. For instance, in the case of "Tor v. the Rutland and Ballingdon Railroad Company" (1855), Chief Justice Isaac Redfield of the Vermont Supreme Court supported a law requiring railroad companies to protect their tracks, because "the protection of the state's public order implies protecting the lives, limbs, health, comfort, and peace of all people, and protecting all property in the state under the principle of 'use your own so as not to harm another' (lat. sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas).' The application of this principle ensures access to a social interpretation of property rights.

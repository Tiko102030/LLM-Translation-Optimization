The ironworking and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could ensure the developing network of railways. At the pinnacle of the entire financial pyramid stood a recently centralized banking system, which was founded in New York and established during the war (see Bensel, 1984, 1990). Incensed workers and farmers saw the real aims of the bankers and owners of ironworking and railway companies, whose ownership was precisely sponsored by government agencies. They demanded new laws that would expand the public's authority in matters of regulating property. They achieved remarkable success in securing the enactment of a law establishing an eight-hour workday and a law setting railroad rates. There were also objections from many large entrepreneurs, leading to the transmission of these bills to the Supreme Court for review. Thus, in the 1880s, the Supreme Court still approved comprehensive government regulation of property rights. The Supreme Court's 1877 decision in the case of Mann v. State of Illinois greatly alarmed business circles in the state, which were concerned about the growing interest in the ideas of the popular radicalism of that time. The plaintiffs argued that the law, by voiding existing contracts, violated the Constitution's contract clause, which also invalidated the recently enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens' rights from governmental interference.

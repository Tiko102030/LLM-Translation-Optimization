This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation; later, other authors joined these arguments, including the notable American radical, Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reasons to regret the widely accepted conclusions of Locke's theory. For instance, Richard Epstein, criticizing Locke for his inability to interpret the Bible, acknowledges that Locke created "no slightest difficulty" in justifying property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property in terms of relationships between people and objects, it can function as a socially neutral factor: the accumulation of the results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is available to others. However, recognizing the social parameters of property allows for a distributional aspect, that is, the rights of one individual may harm another, for example, in the case of monopolizing resources previously accessible to all. Just like social relations, property theory becomes subject to various controversies (see Friedrich Hayek 1998, p. [page number]).

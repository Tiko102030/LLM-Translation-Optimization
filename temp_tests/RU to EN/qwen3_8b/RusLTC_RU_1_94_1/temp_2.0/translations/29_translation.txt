The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which liberated the country from slavery. Reformers, however, desired even more. For example, George MacNeail, defender of working people, warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. As K.F. Breazeale, a conductor, put it: "The Declaration of Independence was created not for the purposes that today trust companies are trying to impose upon us. The result of the 1861 Civil War was the emancipation of black slaves, yet now workers are none other than slaves of consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Moreover, the Civil War also contributed to the unification of workers in opposition to these new demands. Large sums of money were needed for warfare, and the consequent expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed that a significant amount of financial and industrial capital was required to sustain governmental activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones capable of providing the growing railroad network.

Society, on the one hand, does not prohibit an owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to neighbors' property. On the other hand, it may support the harmed neighbors and restrict the activity of this owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the rights to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of lost clean air and water or the cost of paying a firm to stop discharging waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be much poorer if they did not have these privileges, because they would have to reduce pollution levels or pay neighbors for the right to pollute. Some doubt that property rights have a significant impact on income distribution, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase claimed that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear establishment of boundaries leads to the same efficient social outcome, the same efficient combination of production of goods and services. Should those polluting the environment buy the rights from their neighbors, or should the neighbors pay the polluters to stop doing so? Pollution will occur where the costs of reducing its negative consequences exceed the value of clean air to neighbors, and it will cease when the costs of reducing the negative effect are less. A free trade market will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

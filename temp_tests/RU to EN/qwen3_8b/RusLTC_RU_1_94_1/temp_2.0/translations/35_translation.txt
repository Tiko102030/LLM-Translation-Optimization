In the case of Jacobson (1885), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting cigar manufacturing within leased premises, arguing that this "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profit and freely use his property," thereby "depriving him of his property and personal freedom." Comparing the concepts of freedom of the individual and property rights, Justice Erl emphasized that, in a broad sense, "freedom as understood in our country refers not only to freedom from actual slavery, but also to the right of a person to use his abilities through all lawful means to live and work." Therefore, all laws weakening or restricting these rights are violations of his fundamental freedom of liberty. The Legislature maintained that the decision was based on the state's police powers and that the actions were necessary to "improve the public health system in the country." However, for proponents of state regulation, the fact that the court had examined this issue did not bode well, and ultimately the court concluded that the claim lacked the necessary evidence. The Court ultimately determined that "the law in fact did not relate to health issues at all," and Justice Erl reminded the legislative body that "under the pretense of police instructions, one cannot arbitrarily intrude on private rights and personal property." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of an unprecedented judicial activism protecting property rights. For example, in the case "Godcharles v. Wainwright" (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law that demanded payment in legal currency (rather than company stock). The Court asserted that "this law constitutes an infringement upon equal rights of employer and employee, not to mention an insulting attempt to subject an unskilled worker to legislative oversight," since this hampers workers from selling their labor under any conditions they are willing to accept.

The decision in the Mann case showed that property rights had become one of society's main interests, another tool for the attainment of social goals, and no constitutional guarantees were required anymore. Against the backdrop of growing societal unrest, this approach began to alarm conservatives even more and triggered a stronger wave of emotions among those fearing that expanded police powers would undermine all property rights (see Friedman, 1999). After the events on Haymarket Street in 1886, the distinguished legal scholar Christopher Tideman (1971 [1886]), warned that the right of the "anointed of God" had failed to restrain the powers of the police. The spread of the non-intervention policy reduced the number of 'violations of human rights and freedoms by the government.' But now:
the political pendulum is swinging again in the opposite direction... government intervention in human rights has been declared unlawful, and now it is needed as though it were a universal cure for any social ills that threaten societal prosperity. Socialism, communism, and anarchism are unbridled throughout the civilized world. The state is to protect the weak from the cunning of the stronger, to set how much workers should be paid for their labor, and how many hours a day they should work...
Considering these unusual demands from the numerous discontented and their evident power in enforcing their ideas of a citizen state in the civilized world, especially in light of the expanding suffrage, conservatives grew even more frightened that such an autocracy could emerge, more despotic and more ill-considered than any before. In the late 19th century, when speaking out against the opinion of "the numerous army of the discontented," attorneys and judges intended to consecrate private property.

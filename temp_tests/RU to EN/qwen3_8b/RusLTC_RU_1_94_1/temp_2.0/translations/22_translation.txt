Property owned or used by African Americans did not possess the same degree of immunity as property owned or used by individuals of European descent. After the end of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, Negroes from the southern states gained the right to own property; however, their rights could change depending on the political climate in the state and the level of African American participation in it. The same instrumental approach that the governments of the Northern states used to limit property rights regarding clean air and water was also used by Southern states to maintain African Americans' dependence on whites. In both cases, property rights were defined for achieving public goals, though the goals differed. Redistributing property rights with the purpose of stimulating economic growth
By sponsoring the construction of public infrastructure through redistribution of property rights, Chief Justice of the United States Supreme Court Roger Taney resorted to the instrumental approach he used at the Court to annul the contract rights of the Charles River Bridge company in 1837. He did it to manage the sole bridge across the Charles River connecting Boston and Cambridge (see case Charles River Bridge v. Warren Bridge). The defense side of the Charles River Bridge company experienced some challenges. The company could lose 'millions of dollars... that had been invested in construction of railways and canals on the lines of express highways... We would be thrown back a century to the inventions of that era and would be forced to remain stagnant'.

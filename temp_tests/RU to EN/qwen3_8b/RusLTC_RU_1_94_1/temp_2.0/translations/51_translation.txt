Delivering the opinion to the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that the imposition of "a condition regarding the grant of land use permit" does not constitute a "taking" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general land use "fails to serve the purported purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "thus is an unconstitutional taking and compensation should follow." In conclusion, Scalia discussed the new standard: "Because there is no connection between the condition and the state's interest in restricting development, the new purpose transforms into something altogether different than it was before. Now, the government's main task is to secure general land use rights without paying any compensation... If the condition set forth in the land use permit is not considered a restriction on development and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on development is not a legitimate regulation of land use but rather simply 'present takings.' " This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall filed a concurrence. They rejected Scalia's requirement to reconsider the lawmaking body's stance. They argued that 'the proper standard for review... is whether the state can rationally determine whether the measure can achieve the public purpose.' However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court imposed further restrictions on government regulation.

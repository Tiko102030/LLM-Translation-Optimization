Local authorities regularly confiscated residences where unlawful activities were carried out, dumped factory sewage into waterways that "threatened public health," banned the production of dangerous chemicals, and extinguished fires, thereby destroying private residences and warehouses without providing any compensation. Officials gained access to dozens of economic sectors, including those related to healthcare, such as the production of medicines and the slaughter of cattle, as well as others of economic significance unrelated to health matters, such as the rights to trade and purchase, sell and produce (see Novak 1996, p. 84-94). In cases when regulation was unable to achieve the public objective, officials began resorting to coercive measures. For example, in 1872, in Jersey City, the police commissioner and twenty-five armed officers entered the premises of the Manhattan Fertilizing Company's fertilizer plant and began to "reduce" the harm caused by the plant's activities, destroying machinery and taking away all necessary equipment (see Newak 1996, p. 226).

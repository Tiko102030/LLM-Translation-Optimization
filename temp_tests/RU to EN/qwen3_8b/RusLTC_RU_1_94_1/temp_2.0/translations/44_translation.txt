In this regard, in the case of Holmes v. Pennsylvania, Holmes more closely resembled a Trojan Horse rather than a victor—defender of sacred property rights. Instead of considering property rights as natural rights, Holmes justified the 'principle of taking' of property based on establishing a balance between competing interests. Compensation for losses will depend on the circumstances and will be an optional procedure. According to Holmes, property should be constantly 'reasonably' used, but the notion of what constitutes 'reasonable' remains nebulous. If actions go 'too far,' compensation for damages will be necessary; however, it is the judges, taking into account the presence of public needs and special circumstances, who will determine what constitutes 'too far.' Neither private property owners nor the public possess absolute rights; in each case, judges must carefully assess and balance the competing interests of both parties. Property owners should not, in turn, have all against them. Holmes argued that 'the government will scarcely be able to continue its activities further if the issue of property rights is not resolved to some extent.'

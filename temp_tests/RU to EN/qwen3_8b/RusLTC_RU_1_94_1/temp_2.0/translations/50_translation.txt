The court informed the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance taking away the owner's use of their property" was necessary in order "to give them the right to seek compensation for damages." The court's decision in the First Anglican Church case, supported by liberal judges Brennon and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the key points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case of the same year, "Nollan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different situation because it established a new standard regarding the old rules. Thus, holding a beachfront lot situated between public parks to the north and south, the Nollans wanted to build a three-bedroom home in place of their aging bungalow, similar to other homes in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only under the condition that the Nollans consented to granting a general right of use over the land between the tideline and the dike behind their home. In this way, people would be able to easily reach the parks located both to the north and south by simply leisurely walking on the sand. The Commission explained its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling the area," which "people would not consciously realize is nearby, and that they have the full right to spend time there" (see Igl 1996, p. 255).

The Louisiana Supreme Court adopted a law stating that "losses of private rights in this case are insignificant in view of the overall profit and trade volume of this commercial entity" (see Novak 1996, pp. 230-31). The New Orleans meatpackers brought their case before the U.S. Supreme Court, where their interests were represented by John A. Campbell, a predecessor of Field, a Democrat and assistant to the Secretary of War (see Foner 1988, p. 530). Working in the Supreme Court before the war, Campbell had ruled in the Dred Scott case that African Americans could not be citizens of the United States. Fifteen years later, he used the principles of labor law to convince the Court to protect a citizen's right to property. Drawing a parallel to the struggle against feudalism, Campbell argued that the city's resolutions had brought the situation back to a time when "the persistent gaze of the government followed the meatpacker to the slaughterhouse and the baker to the bakery." The New Orleans meatpackers lost their case 5-4, but Campbell's arguments resonated among the judges, and thus attorneys feared the growing pressure for the expansion of regulatory measures, the redistribution of income, property, and power.

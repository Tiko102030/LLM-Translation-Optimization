In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "a policy with nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Intent, and Design," Holmes based his argument on the aforementioned view of the harm caused by competition. Thus, he aimed to demonstrate the general necessity of an instrumental approach and the "balance of interests principle" particularly in situations where judges weigh competing rights instead of relying on syllogistic reasoning (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130-35). At the 1903 Supreme Court session, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glue Co. v. United States Glue Co." "In modern society," he stated, "each part is closely connected to another, and if something affects one of them, the rest are more or less affected. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the legislative authority comes to an end, it would be insufficient merely to show that some progress has been made in the formulation of law, i.e., when it will be permissible to intervene in commercial or already existing contracts."

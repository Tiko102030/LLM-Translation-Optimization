Chancellor Kent of New York declared in 1826 that "every person possesses such an amount of freedom to acquire, use, and dispose of their property which may be compared with a reasonable state of property and mutual rights of others... Although, thus, property is protected... the legislative body has the right to prescribe the manner and model of its use as much as may be necessary to prevent abuse of rights, which could lead to violation of the rights of another person or society as a whole." In order to protect public welfare and consider the interests of all people, local authorities and regional governments assumed broad powers throughout the entire 19th century. For example, in 1837, the legislative body of the state of Illinois did not have three pages to record in the catalog all the powers that the administration of the city of Chicago would have with respect to property issues. In addition to regulating and licensing water transportation and limiting the "operation of the wheel, ball games, or the launching of paper kites... which could annoy people," the new city obtained the power that "forced the owner or lessee of any grocery store, winery, candle store, soap factory, tannery, stable, barn, outhouse, textile factory, or any other dilapidated, repulsive building or house to clean, remove, or destroy it" (see Novak 1996, pp. 3-6).

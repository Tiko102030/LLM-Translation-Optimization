The plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional provision on contracts by repealing existing agreements, thereby also violating the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment protecting citizens from governmental interference. Originally enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the Southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment had now become an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property protecting private property rights from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court justices. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee regarding property, the Court upheld and even expanded the powers of prior regulation. Stating that 'powers belong to any sovereign authority,' the Court decided that 'the government may regulate the conduct of its citizens toward each other and how people must use their property, but only when necessary for the general welfare.' Chief Justice Waite quoted Lord Chief Justice of England, Hale, in saying that 'when property involves societal interests, it by law ceases to be subject only to private regulation.' Recalling the principle 'the welfare of the people is the supreme law' (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: 'when a property owner uses his property in a way that creates an interest for the public, in fact he himself desires this, and until that interest fades, he must merely accept that now society controls his property, since it is necessary for the purpose of increasing public welfare. If, however, he does not wish the public to control his property, he must simply cease using it.' By examining more thoroughly the limits of public authority in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations of governmental regulation.

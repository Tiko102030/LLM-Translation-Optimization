There exists an "indisputable" right of property for the laborer, because: although the earth and all lower creatures belong jointly to all mankind, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands, according to the strictest account, belong to him. Whatever a man takes from the state in which nature made and preserved the object, he combines it with his labor and adds to it something that belongs personally to him, thus making it his property... For since this labor is the laborer's indisputable property, no one else can have a right to what he has once joined it to, at least in those cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke consecrates property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; to use it means to use another's labor, i.e., to convert a person into a slave. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, property owners form societies and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

It appears that the basis of the aforementioned case lies in John Locke's views regarding absolute property rights. The case also mentioned the final decision in the Mann case, handed down by Chief Justice Waite. However, it is important to note the last statement discussed earlier: "This is merely a matter of degree." This limitation, written by Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, once again deliberately re-established an instrumental vision of property rights, declaring previous fifty years of judicial practice unconstitutional. In the case of Lochner, Holmes had already expressed his opinion. Beginning his statement by pointing out that "the decision was rendered according to the principles of economic theory, which, however, does not satisfy the majority of the country's population," he added:
The right of each person to act at his own will for as long as he desires, provided of course that this does not infringe the right of others to do the same, does not correspond to the fundamental postulates of legal schools, the postal service administration, or the laws of any of the states or another municipal institution that takes his money to achieve the goals that seem necessary, regardless of whether he wishes it or not. The Fourteenth Amendment does not take into account Herbert Spencer's Social Statics. In contrast to Lochner's despotic protection of intensive property rights, Holmes considered that competing interests require achieving a balance. Having lost the case of Lochner, he carefully selected his words in order to start a new case under an alternative law.

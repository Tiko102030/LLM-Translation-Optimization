The Civil War marked the beginning of a new era in American political life, demonstrating the power of the national government, which liberated the country from slavery. Reformers, however, sought even more. For example, the labor rights defender George McNeil warned that America must "imbue our industrial system with republican principles" or risk losing its democracy. According to the words of a railroad conductor, K.F. Breassie: "The Declaration of Independence was not created for the purposes that today trust companies are trying to impose upon us. The result of the 1861 Civil War was the liberation of black slaves, but today, workers are no different than slaves of the consolidated trust companies" (see Foner 1998, p. 99, 124-5). Furthermore, the Civil War contributed to workers uniting and opposing these new demands. The conduct of military operations required large sums of money, and the closely related expansion and centralization of the government apparatus once again confirmed the necessity of significant financial and industrial capital capable of sustaining government activities. The iron and steel industries, whose products were subject to preferential tariffs, were the only ones that could supply the expanding network of railroads.

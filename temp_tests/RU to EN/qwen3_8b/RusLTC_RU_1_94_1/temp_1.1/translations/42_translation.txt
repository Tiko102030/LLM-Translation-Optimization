Having lost the Lochner case, he carefully selected his words to launch a new case for an alternative law. Instead of basing his speech on the majority opinion of the legislative assembly members regarding natural rights and on the beliefs of J. Locke, he accused them of copying "Social Statics of Spencer." Thus, he characterized their defense of property rights as an instrumental choice that rather signifies the end of productivity and prosperity, rather than the protection of a sacred notion of free labor. According to the principles of the instrumental approach, property rights become vulnerable to the foundations of another economic theory, another instrumental vision, or the choice of another public goal. This was not the first time Holmes challenged the existing model of legal justification used to protect property rights. In his book "General Jurisprudence" (1881), he thoroughly discussed how the concept of absolute rights contradicts the premises of creating a competitive market economy. According to Holmes: "Law even does not attempt to reimburse the person for all the harm caused to him... Anybody may start any firm, and later, to survive in competitive struggle, he will attract his competitor's clients, and possibly even ruin him in the process." In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is acceptable in accordance with the principles of "such a policy that has nothing to do with morality" (Holms 1881, p.)

Lemuel Shaw, a representative of the Massachusetts Supreme Court, supported the legislative authority dealing with issues of regulating property within Boston Harbor and prohibiting construction of buildings beyond that wharf, and spoke of police powers as follows: "We consider this to be an established norm arising from the very essence of a well-organized civil society, where each property owner, regardless of whether he possesses an exclusive right to it or not, manages it with the entire implied responsibility so that its use should not cause harm to other persons who have the same right to enjoy the use of their own property, nor to society as a whole. All property in this society... is fully or partially owned by the government, and general rules applicable to it may be necessary for the purpose of achieving the general welfare and universal prosperity. Property rights, like all other social and customary rights, are subject to the action of appropriate limitations necessary to ensure that no one else is harmed by the use of this property, and to the action of established legal rules, since the legislative authority... may assume that this is a necessary and appropriate measure. In conclusion, Shaw added: "Much easier to feel and understand life and its sources than to establish boundaries or limits of its manifestations." Indeed, by employing universal, typical phrases such as "public interest," "internal structure of the state," "implied right," and "obligation of the supreme authority to protect all," Shaw established that police agencies may possess a broader set of tools in achieving their objectives. Southern states resorted to the assistance of police power to introduce laws limiting the use of property rights, for example, prohibiting slaveholders from teaching their slaves to read. Later, after the abolition of slavery, they established unusual restrictions on private property rights, such as introducing a policy of strict racial segregation, which limited the sale of property to African Americans or their labor activities in certain types of professions. The application of laws providing unequal protection of property and labor rights for African Americans once again demonstrates the understanding of property at that time. (see Coen 1991; Lichtenstein 1996; Weiner 1978; Woodward 1955).

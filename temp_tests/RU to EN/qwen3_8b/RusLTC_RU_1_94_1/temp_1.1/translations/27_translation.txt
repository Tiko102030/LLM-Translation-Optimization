He first became a precedent in the case "Palmer v. Malligan" of the State of New York (1805), where the owner of property situated downstream filed a claim for damages. A dam located upstream collapsed and flooded his home. Speaking on behalf of the court, Judge Livingston concluded: "the defendants had the same right concerning their territory... [the principle 'use your property so as not to cause harm to others'] must be limited within reasonable limits to prevent depriving a person of the enjoyment of their property merely because of some foolish inconvenience or harm to others... There existed a law providing for some inconveniences of this kind; thus, a person who could initially build a dam or a mill on any public or navigable river eventually acquired an exclusive right, at least for a certain distance, regardless of whether they owned the adjacent shores or not... The public, whose interests should not be forgotten, would lose a portion of the profits it could have gained through competitive rivalry. This approach democratized property rights, resulting in both long-standing and new residents possessing the same privileges. Thus, there was a redistribution of wealth to entrepreneurs at the expense of established residents being prohibited from restricting entrepreneurial actions. However, at the same time, by eliminating the established monopolistic power and freeing entrepreneurs from litigation regarding indirect damages, these cases actually undermined existing property rights. Therefore, the transformation of the principle 'use your property so as not to cause harm to others' was in no way dependent on the ideas about sacred private property introduced by John Locke. The new approach was neither a general critique of property rights nor a general affirmation of these rights.

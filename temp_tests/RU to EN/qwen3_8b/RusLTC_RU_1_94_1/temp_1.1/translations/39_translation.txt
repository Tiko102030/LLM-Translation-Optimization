A similar situation arose in the Jacobus case, where Justice Rufus W. Peckham of the Supreme Court argued that human freedom included not only the absence of "his physical limitations," but also encompassed the right "to work where he wishes; to earn his living by any lawful means; to pursue the profession he loves and, for that purpose, to enter into all contracts that may be proper, necessary, and important for the successful attainment of the aforementioned objectives." After the court's decision in the Allgeyer case, the peak of judicial activity in articulating the rationale was established eight years later in the case "Lochner v. New York" (1905), where the Court declared the New York law unconstitutional, since the law had reduced the working hours of bakers. Appearing before the Court, Justice Peckham firmly stated that "the law necessarily infringes upon the right to enter into a contract between an employer and employee," which constitutes a violation of "the general right to enter into a contract that relates to the operation of his business," which, in turn, "is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution." Acknowledging that police actions concerning the regulation of property are justified, as they protect "health or moral behavior," Peckham argued that in this case "there is no necessary justification for interfering with human freedom or his right to enter into a free contract by reducing the working hours for the position of a baker." In conclusion, Peckham stated that this is merely a question of which of the two evils is lesser: the state's power to enact laws or an individual's right to personal freedom and the freedom to enter into contracts. We believe that the limits of police authority have been reached and fully examined in this case. The concepts of property, instrumentalism, and the Lochner era can logically be defined based on other judicial decisions that affirmed property rights.

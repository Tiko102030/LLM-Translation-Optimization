In the precedent case of Jacobson (1885), the New York Appellate Court declared unconstitutional a law prohibiting the manufacture of cigars in leased premises, as it "interfered with the owner's ability to obtain profits and freely use their property," thereby "denying them their property and personal freedoms." Judge Earl, comparing the concepts of personal freedom and protection of property rights, stated that freedom, in a broad sense as understood in our country, means not only freedom from actual servitude... but also the right of a person to use their abilities in all lawful ways to live and work... Therefore, any laws that weaken or restrict these rights... are violations of his fundamental rights to freedom. The Legislative Assembly claimed that the ruling was based on the state's police powers, and the measures taken were necessary to "improve the country's health system." For defenders of state regulation, it was not promising that the court considered this requirement, and subsequently, it concluded that it lacked the necessary amount of evidence. In conclusion, it was established that the law "did not actually relate to health issues," and Judge Earl informed the legislative body that "under the pretext of police powers, arbitrary intrusion into personal rights and private property cannot be tolerated." The Jacobson case marked the beginning of unprecedented judicial activism in protecting property rights. For example, in the case of "Gothcharlz v. Widjmen" (1866), the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declared unconstitutional a law requiring payment in legal money (rather than company stock). The court argued that "the law was a violation of equal rights between employer and employee, more than that, it was an insulting attempt to subject unskilled laborers to legislative oversight," as it prevented workers from selling their labor under any conditions they were willing to accept.

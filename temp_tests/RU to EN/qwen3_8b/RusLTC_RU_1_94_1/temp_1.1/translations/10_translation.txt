This part of Locke's property theory contributed to discussions about natural rights as limitations on individual accumulation. Later, other authors joined these considerations, including the prominent American radical, Thomas Paine. Modern defenders of sacred property rights had reasons to regret the widespread popularity of Locke's conclusions. For example, Richard Epstein, who accused Locke of being incompetent in interpreting the Bible, acknowledges that Locke did not create "the slightest difficulty" for defending property rights (see Epstein 1985, p. 10). When considering property from the perspective of relationships between people and objects, it can serve as a socially neutral factor: the accumulation of the results of one's own labor does not diminish the significance of what is accessible to others. However, recognizing the social parameters of property allows for a distributive aspect, that is, the rights of one person may harm others, for example, in the case of monopolization of resources previously available to all.

INTRODUCTION: The Concept of Property in Economic Theory and Social Practice
Society possesses property. Economic systems differ based on the definitions they provide for the concept of property, and often, the boundaries are difficult to define. Some believe that the right to property extends to such objects as land and trees, including the most transient ones such as living nature and flowing water. Others expand this concept by asserting that the right to property also applies to such objects as ideas (patents), and some even include humans as property. Societies also differ based on the rights that property owners possess; the set of privileges regarding responsibility for any actions towards other people. These rights prevent us from considering the law as providing "greater" or "lesser" protection for property, because every use of it affects the property of others. Property has dimensions, because it involves human relationships; I have the right to dispose of my property, which reduces your right to be fully free. For example, when the environment is polluted, the neighbors' right to clean air and water significantly decreases; rights prohibiting pollution limit the rights of this property, but protect the property of neighbors. Society, on one hand, does not prohibit the owner from using its land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to the property of neighbors.

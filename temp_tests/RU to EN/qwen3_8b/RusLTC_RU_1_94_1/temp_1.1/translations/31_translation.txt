Plaintiffs argued that the law violated the constitutional provision regarding contracts by repealing existing agreements, thereby also violating the newly enacted Fourteenth Amendment, which protects citizens' rights from governmental interference. Recently enacted to protect the civil rights of freed slaves in the southern states, the Fourteenth Amendment now served as an essential civil right, a constitutional guarantee of property protecting private property rights from governmental interference. However, this view was supported by a minority of the 1877 Court members. By rejecting the plaintiff's constitutional guarantee of property, the Court upheld and even expanded the authority of past regulation. Speaking of "powers belong to any sovereign authority," the Court ruled that "government may regulate and control the conduct of its citizens toward one another and how people must use their property, but only when it is necessary for the general welfare." Chief Justice Waite cited Lord Chief Justice of England, Hale, stating that "when property 'touches the interests of society, it is, by law, no longer subject solely to private regulation.'" Remembering the principle "the welfare of the people is the supreme law" (Latin: salus populi suprema lex est), Waite added: when a property owner uses their property in a manner that creates an interest for the public, in fact, he himself desires this, and until the interest disappears, he must accept that society now manages his property, since it is necessary for increasing public welfare. If he does not want society to manage his property, he should simply cease using it. Considering more closely the boundaries of public power in a democratic society, Waite acknowledged several limitations on governmental regulation.

The new paradigm of property rights recognizes various definitions of property, however, it still considers "property rights" as relations between people and objects (see Barceló 1997; Liebkenn 1989). Thus, property rights exist independently of other social circumstances. As relations between people and objects, property exists without human society; for instance, Robinson Crusoe also has property rights. Just as relations between people and objects they create, property acquires moral legitimacy and economic significance because it is a reward for labor, which forms the inhuman material world. As John Locke says, "in the beginning, labor gave the right to property" (see J. Locke 1952, p. 27 [par. 45]).

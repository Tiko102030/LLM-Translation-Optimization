In conclusion, Holmes added that the harm caused by competition is permissible in accordance with the principles of "such a policy that has nothing to do with morality" (Holmes 1881, p. 115, 128). In his 1894 essay "Privilege, Criminal Intent, and Design," Holmes based his argument on the aforementioned understanding of the harm caused by competition. Thus, he aimed to show the general necessity of an instrumental approach and the "principle of balancing interests," especially in situations where judges weigh competing rights rather than resorting to syllogistic reasoning (Horwitz 1992, pp. 130–35). At the 1903 Supreme Court session, Holmes applied this approach in the case "Diamond Glu Company v. United States Glu Company." "In modern society," he stated, "each part of it is closely connected to the other, and if something affects one of them, the rest will more or less feel it. Therefore, until everything is prohibited and the legislative authority comes to an end, it will not be enough simply to show that some progress has been made in the development of law, that is, when it will already be possible to interfere in commercial or existing transactions."

"United States v. Carolin Products" (1938), and "United States v. Darby" (1941) served as prototypes of judicial decisions from the 1930s, which, in turn, contributed to the spread of judicial pragmatism and an instrumental approach to property rights. Most notably was the 1934 decision in "Home Building & Loan Association v. Blaisdell," where the Supreme Court, in light of the country's economic instability, upheld the state's actions regarding property regulation. The Court affirmed the law of the state of Minnesota, which imposed a moratorium on the forced sale of mortgaged property under an established adverse economic situation. Speaking on behalf of the Court, Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes stated that the constitutional article limiting state regulation should not be considered with literal precision as any mathematical formula, but it remains one of the main articles of the Constitution, whose interpretation is only possible with additional context. He added that the Court should interpret this article "in accordance with the reserved state power to protect the vital interests of its residents." "The efficacy of the legislative body's actions should not be evaluated by whether its influence on the contract terms is direct or indirect, but rather by whether the solution presented is lawful and whether it was achieved by reasonable and appropriate means." This indicates a social and instrumental approach to defining property, which, in turn, was transformed into a constitutional principle. Rejecting any privileged status for private property, Hughes concluded that:
The country's economic situation may be a result of how temporary limitations on the enforcement of contract terms are reconciled with the true meaning and purpose of the article, and thus do not exceed the reserved state power to protect the vital interests of society. ... Since this article of the contract is not an unconditional and completely unlawful restriction of the state's protective policy, the legislative body must make decisions in a way that does not violate legislative authority. ... In this regard, whether the legislative body implements a reasonable or unreasonable policy, the Court will not concern itself with that.

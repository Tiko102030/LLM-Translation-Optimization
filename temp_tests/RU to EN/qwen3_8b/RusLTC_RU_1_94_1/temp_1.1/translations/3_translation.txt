On the other hand, the right of the majority to control economic activity, determine public goals, and protect property owners from the consequences of the actions of polluters is considered. However, such a general comparison of property rights can be misunderstood, as these rights, to a greater extent, limit the rights of other people. Property rights grant individuals government-sanctioned privileges of monopolistic control over resources and the opportunity to appeal to authorized police authorities to limit the activities of other people. Thus, property rights significantly limit some advantages because government authorities largely support property owners rather than other citizens. Therefore, it is not a debate about the principles of natural freedom and justice, but rather discussions on property regulation that force a choice about whose property needs protection and whose property rights have the advantage in the struggle for them. The discussion of democratic rights and their regulation has become so confusing because legal issues in the structure of the United States legal system have been repeatedly discussed at various levels of government. Thus, according to the federalism system, a single Federal Government is formed, while each state has its own legal system. Also, at each level of government, there is a clear separation of executive, legislative, and judicial powers, each of which is intended to respect and ensure the observance of constitutional norms, which they interpret.

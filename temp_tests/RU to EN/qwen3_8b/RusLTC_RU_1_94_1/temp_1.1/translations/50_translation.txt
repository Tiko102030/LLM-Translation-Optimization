The court notified the Los Angeles County administration that "an ordinance depriving the owner of the use of their property" was necessary to "grant them the right to compensation for damages." The court's decision in the First Anglican Church case, supported by liberal judges Brennan and Marshall, did not rest on constitutional principles but merely affirmed the main points of the Pennsylvania Coal case. However, the case from the same year, "Nollan v. California Coastal Commission" (1987), was a completely different scenario, as it established a new standard regarding old rules. Thus, the Nollans, who owned a coastal parcel of land between public parks to the north and south, wanted to replace their dilapidated bungalow with a three-bedroom home similar to other houses in the area. The California Coastal Commission issued the necessary permit only if the Nollans agreed to allow a common right of way across the area between the high tide line and the dike behind their home. Thus, people could easily reach the parks located both to the north and south by simply strolling leisurely along the sand. The Commission justified its requirement by stating that a larger home would contribute to "filling the territory," which "people would subconsciously fail to realize that the beach is nearby and that they have the full right to spend time there" (see Iggle 1996, p. 255).

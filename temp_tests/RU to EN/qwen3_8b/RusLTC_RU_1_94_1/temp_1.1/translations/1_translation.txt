Society, on one hand, does not prohibit an owner from using their land and water resources, even if this may cause damage to neighboring property. On the other hand, it may support the affected neighbors and limit the activities of that owner. This makes property rights distributive. When owners have the right to pollute the environment, it reduces the real income of neighbors by the amount of the loss of clean air and water or the cost of the payment that must be made to the company to stop it from discharging waste into the environment. Factories actively polluting the environment would be significantly poorer without these privileges, as they would have to reduce pollution levels or pay neighbors for the right to pollute. Some question the extent to which property rights significantly influence income distribution, while others argue in favor of its efficiency. In his famous 1960 article, Ronald Coase claimed that regardless of the distribution of property rights, any clear definition of boundaries leads to the same efficient social outcome, the same combination of production and services. Should those polluting the environment purchase the right to do so from their neighbors, or should neighbors pay those polluting the environment to stop doing so? Pollution will occur where the cost of reducing its negative consequences is greater than the value of clean air for neighbors, and will cease when the cost of reducing the negative effect is lower. A market of free trade will lead to the same efficient outcome regardless of the distribution of property rights and regardless of who owns the rights to air and water.

In fact, the Thirteenth Amendment enshrined in the United States Constitution the principle of confiscation of property. According to this amendment, confiscation without compensation of slave property redistributed nearly three billion dollars, half of the total wealth of Southern states. Thus, the emancipation of slaves in America became one of the most significant examples in history of property confiscation. Government intervention in private property affairs did not end with these confiscations. During the colonial and pre-war periods, regional governments and local authorities regularly intervened in private property matters, thereby once again confirming the widespread belief in the moral duty of government to aid in the development of a thriving economy and to care for the poor citizens. For example, most local authorities set transportation tariffs and bread prices, limited trade in food products, regulated wage levels and employment opportunities, and restricted the activities of alcohol sellers, bars, and prostitutes (see Hendlin and Hendlin 1947; Berджин 1989; Novak 1996; Lori 1999; Levi 2000). And local authorities used the power of compulsory transfer of private property to achieve public goals at the expense of private property owners.

Local authorities used the right of compulsory acquisition of private property to achieve public objectives at the expense of private property owners. The term "right of compulsory acquisition of private property," first coined by the Dutch scholar Hugo Grotius in 1625, was incorporated into English law by John Locke, who placed it at the foundation of "natural law," as the consent of the entire population, which transferred all powers to the state's legislative bodies. (See Igl 1996, p. 8). American judges and legal interpreters expanded the concept entirely based on instrumental considerations. Denying any inviolability of private property rights, the 1791 Vermont Constitution stated: "Private property must be subject to public use when necessity demands it" (see Elie 1998, p. 33; author's notes).

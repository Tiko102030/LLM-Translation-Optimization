In his opinion for the Court, Justice Antonin Scalia acknowledged that imposing "conditions related to the issuance of a land-use permit" does not constitute a "taking" under the Fifth Amendment. The requirement for general land use "does not serve the presumed purpose of protecting the public's visual access to the beach" and "is therefore unconstitutional, and compensation must be provided." In conclusion, Scalia spoke of the new standard: "Because there is no connection between the condition and the cause of the construction restriction, the new purpose transforms into something entirely different from what it was before. Now, the government's primary goal is to obtain general land-use rights without paying any compensation... if the condition set forth in the land-use permit is not considered a prohibition on construction and does not serve the same governmental purpose, then the restriction on construction will not be a valid land-use regulation but merely 'a present taking.' This time, Justices Brennan and Marshall filed a dissenting opinion. They rejected Scalia's request to review the opinion of the legislative body. They argued that 'the proper standard for review... is whether the government can rationally determine whether the adopted measure can achieve a public purpose.' However, after these icons of liberalism retired in 1990 and 1991, the Court introduced additional restrictions on government regulation."

There exists an "inalienable" right of property of the laborer, because: Although the earth and all lower creatures belong collectively to all people, nevertheless... the labor of his body and the work of his hands belong to him according to the strictest account. Whatever a person takes from the state in which nature created and preserved this object, he combines it with his labor and attaches to it something personally belonging to him, thereby making it his own property... For since this labor is an inalienable property of the laborer, no one other than himself can have a right to what he once attached it to, at least in those cases where a sufficient quantity of the same quality remains for the common use of others. Thus, Locke sanctifies property as a physical manifestation of past labor. Property is an important natural right; using it means using someone else's labor, that is, reducing a person to servitude. From this it follows that property rights owe nothing to society; nevertheless, property owners form society and governments to protect their property (see Smith 1999).

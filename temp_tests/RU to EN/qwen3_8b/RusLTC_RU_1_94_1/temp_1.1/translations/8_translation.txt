A certain form of property as an instrument may be convenient, but it will never be necessary. However, Locke himself does not defend property rights as resolutely as one might infer from the well-known passage quoted above (see Horn 1990; Kecskés 1998, p. 77). In this passage, he refers to the question of the right of the Stuart English monarchs to impose taxes without the consent of Parliament. However, beyond this issue, Locke acknowledges a social definition of property, according to which property rights must be limited by other natural rights. Locke argued that each person should have as much as they can use. Accumulation must be limited to ensure that all can share the bounty of nature, leaving "enough and as good" for others. Referring to the Bible, Locke asserts that the same law of nature, which in this way grants us property, also limits the extent of this property.

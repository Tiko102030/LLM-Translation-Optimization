Callender filed a lawsuit seeking compensation for damages caused to his house's foundation during street repairs in Boston, which resulted in the foundation's damage. His attorney cited English laws, common law precedents, and this principle in an attempt to secure compensation. However, the defense rejected the use of English precedents with an instrumental presentation of the case. They explained that due to the circumstances prevailing in America, it was necessary to repair the road, and "if such a lawsuit," which Callender had filed, "were to be granted, then we would have to examine the correctness of repairs of all highways in our country." This argument found favor with the judges, who dismissed Callender's claim for compensation. Then, the Massachusetts state court accepted the state's proposal for a market interpretation of the case in order to avoid similar subsequent damage claims, which was decidedly bad news for people whose property directly abutted public structures. "Those who purchase property adjacent to a road," the court stated, "must take into account the future expansion or narrowing of the street or road." For in such a case, the compensation amount would have doubled compared to the initial cost. Similar conclusions were reached in New York in 1828 in the case "Lansing versus Smith Walter Quakenbush," where a lawsuit was filed against the state of New York for damages resulting from the drop in the value of docks after the construction of a new harbor in Albany. The New York court used the instrumental approach employed in the Massachusetts case "Callender versus Marsh" to dismiss Quakenbush's claims.

Other networks (such as Kohonen networks), as well as networks trained with a teacher, repeatedly examine the dataset, with one full pass through the dataset referred to as an epoch of training. In supervised learning, the set of original data is divided into two parts—the actual training dataset and test data; the principle of division may be arbitrary. The training data are presented to the network for learning, while the test data are used to calculate the network's error (test data are never used for training the network). Thus, if the error on the test data decreases, the network is indeed generalizing. If the error on the training data continues to decrease while the error on the test data increases, this indicates that the network has stopped generalizing and is simply "memorizing" the training data. This phenomenon is called network overfitting or overfitting. In such cases, training is typically halted. During the training process, other issues may arise, such as paralysis or the network becoming trapped in a local minimum of the error surface. It is impossible to predict in advance the occurrence of any particular problem or to provide definitive recommendations for resolving them. All the above applies only to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions.

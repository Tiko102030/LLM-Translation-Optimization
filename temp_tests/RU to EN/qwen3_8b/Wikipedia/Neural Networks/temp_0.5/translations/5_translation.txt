All the above applies only to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions. For them, nothing can be guaranteed, and it is impossible to fully automate the training of neural networks. However, along with iterative training algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms that have very high stability and allow for fully automating the training process. Testing the adequacy of training

Even in the case of seemingly successful training, the network does not always learn exactly what the creator intended. There is a known case where a network was trained to recognize tank images from photographs, but later it was discovered that all the tanks were photographed against the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of "learning" to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what was required of it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be conducted on examples that were not part of its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be greater the higher the quality of training. If the errors of the neural network have a probability close to one billionth, then confirming this probability requires a billion test examples.

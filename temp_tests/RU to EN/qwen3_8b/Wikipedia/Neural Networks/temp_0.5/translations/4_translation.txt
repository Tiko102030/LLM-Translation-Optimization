Other networks (such as Kohonen networks), as well as networks trained with a teacher, repeatedly process the dataset multiple times, with one complete pass through the dataset referred to as an epoch of training. In supervised learning, the set of original data is divided into two parts—the actual training set and test data; the principle of division may be arbitrary. Training data is fed to the network for learning, while test data is used to calculate the network's error (test data is never used for training the network). Thus, if the error on the test data decreases, the network is indeed generalizing. If the error on the training data continues to decrease while the error on the test data increases, it means the network has stopped generalizing and is simply "memorizing" the training data. This phenomenon is called network overfitting or overfitting. In such cases, training is usually stopped. During training, other problems may arise, such as paralysis or the network getting stuck in a local minimum of the error surface. It is impossible to predict in advance the occurrence of any particular problem, nor can one provide definitive recommendations for resolving them. All the above applies exclusively to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions.

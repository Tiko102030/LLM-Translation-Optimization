Forecasting

A neural network's ability to forecast directly follows from its capacity for generalization and the identification of hidden dependencies between input and output data. After training, the network is capable of predicting future values of a sequence based on several previous values and (or) certain existing factors at the current moment. Forecasting is only possible when previous changes to some extent truly determine the future. For example, forecasting stock prices based on previous week's data may be successful (or may not be), while forecasting tomorrow's lottery results based on data from the past 50 years is almost certainly unlikely to yield any results. Approximation

Neural networks can approximate continuous functions. The generalized approximation theorem has been proven: using linear operations and cascade connections, one can obtain a device capable of computing any continuous function with a predetermined accuracy. This means that the nonlinear characteristic of a neuron can be arbitrary: from sigmoidal to arbitrary wave packet or wavelet, sine, or polynomial. The choice of nonlinear function may affect the complexity of a specific network, but with any nonlinearity, the network remains a universal approximator, and with the correct choice of structure, it can sufficiently accurately approximate the functioning of any continuous automaton. Data Compression and Associative Memory

The ability of neural networks to identify relationships between different parameters allows for expressing data of high dimensionality in a more compact form, if the data are closely interrelated. The reverse process—reconstructing the original data set from partial information—is called (auto)associative memory.

Forecasting

The ability of a neural network to forecast is directly derived from its ability to generalize and identify hidden dependencies between input and output data. After training, the network is capable of predicting a future value of a sequence based on several previous values and (or) some existing factors at the current moment. Forecasting is possible only when previous changes to some extent truly determine the future. For example, forecasting stock prices based on prices from the past week may be successful (or may not be), whereas forecasting tomorrow's lottery results based on data from the last 50 years almost certainly yields no results. Approximation

Neural networks can approximate continuous functions. The general approximation theorem has been proved: using linear operations and cascade connections, one can create a device that computes any continuous function with a given accuracy. This means that the nonlinear characteristic of a neuron can be arbitrary: from sigmoidal to arbitrary wave packets or wavelets, sine waves, or polynomials. The choice of nonlinear function may affect the complexity of a specific network, but with any nonlinearity, the network remains a universal approximator and, with the correct choice of structure, can sufficiently accurately approximate the functioning of any continuous automaton. Data Compression and Associative Memory

The ability of neural networks to detect relationships between different parameters allows for expressing data of large dimensionality more compactly, if the data is closely interrelated. The reverse process — reconstructing the original dataset from partial information — is called (auto)associative memory.

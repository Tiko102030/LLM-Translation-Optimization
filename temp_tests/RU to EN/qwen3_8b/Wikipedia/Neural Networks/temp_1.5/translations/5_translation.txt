All the above refers exclusively to iterative algorithms for searching neural network solutions. For them, one cannot guarantee anything and one cannot fully automate neural network training. However, along with iterative training algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms with very high stability, allowing for complete automation of the training process. Checking the adequacy of training

Even in the case of seemingly successful training, the network does not always learn exactly what its creator intended. There is a known case when a network was trained to recognize tank images from photographs, but later it turned out that all tanks were photographed against the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of "learning" to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what was required of it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be carried out using examples that were not involved in its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be greater the higher the quality of training. If the errors of the neural network have a probability close to one in a billion, then confirming this probability would require a billion test examples.

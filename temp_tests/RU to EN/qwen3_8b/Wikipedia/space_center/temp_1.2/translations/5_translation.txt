All the above applies only to iterative algorithms for finding neural network solutions. For these algorithms, nothing can be guaranteed, and fully automating the training of neural networks is not possible. However, along with iterative training algorithms, there exist non-iterative algorithms that possess very high robustness and allow for fully automating the training process. Checking the adequacy of training

Even in the case of seemingly successful training, the network does not always learn exactly what the creator intended. There is a known case where a network was trained to recognize tank images from photographs, but later it was discovered that all the tanks were photographed against the same background. As a result, the network "learned" to recognize this type of landscape instead of "learning" to recognize tanks. Thus, the network "understands" not what was required of it, but what is easiest to generalize. Testing the quality of neural network training must be conducted using examples that were not part of its training. At the same time, the number of test examples should be greater the higher the quality of training. If the errors of the neural network have a probability close to one in a billion, then a billion test examples are needed to confirm this probability.
